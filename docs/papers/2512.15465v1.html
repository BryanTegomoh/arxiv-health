Female anatomies disguise ECG abnormalities following myocardial infarction: an AI-enabled modelling and simulation study

Published: 2025-12-17

Summary

This AI-enabled modeling and simulation study quantifies how torso-heart anatomical factors contribute to sex-differences in ECG biomarkers following myocardial infarction. It found that female anatomies, characterized by smaller ventricles, a more superior cardiac position, and different ventricular orientation, lead to reduced ST-elevation, shorter QRS duration, and shortened T-peak-to-end interval due to increased distances and altered angles between infarcts and ECG electrodes.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for addressing sex-biased outcomes in cardiovascular diagnosis and risk stratification, particularly for myocardial infarction. By identifying anatomical contributors to ECG biomarker differences, it can improve the accuracy of ECG interpretation in female patients, potentially preventing missed diagnoses or inappropriate treatments.

AI Health Application

The paper uses AI-enabled multiscale modeling and simulation to construct a large cohort of torso-ventricular anatomies from clinical MRI data. This AI-augmented approach allows for the simulation of ECGs across various anatomical variabilities and electrophysiological stages (healthy, ischemic, infarcted) to quantify the impact of anatomical sex differences on ECG biomarkers. The AI is used to mechanistically understand complex physiological interactions, leading to a blueprint for future work in tailoring clinical guidelines based on unique patient anatomy, thereby reducing diagnostic bias in healthcare.

Methodology

The study employed AI-augmented multiscale modeling and simulation based on clinical magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) data and ECGs. A cohort of 1720 torso-ventricular anatomies (50% female) was constructed from UK Biobank MRIs of healthy and post-MI participants. 8600 ECG simulations were performed across healthy, acutely ischemic, and infarcted electrophysiological stages, quantifying the effect of cardiac size, position, and orientation on ECG biomarkers.

Key Findings

Female anatomies have smaller ventricles, a more superior cardiac position, and different ventricular orientation, leading to increased distances between the infarct and ECG electrodes (relative to cardiac size) and larger angles between the infarct normal and ECG lead axes. These anatomical factors reduce ST-elevation, cause low-amplitude late depolarisation/repolarisation tails to be missed, shortening QRS duration (QRSd) and T-peak-to-end interval (TpTe). Cardiac position and orientation disproportionately affect TpTe more than QRSd.

Clinical Impact

The findings could lead to more accurate interpretation of ECGs in female patients, reducing misdiagnosis or delayed treatment of myocardial infarction. This mechanistic understanding supports the development of sex-specific or anatomically tailored ECG diagnostic criteria and clinical guidelines, ultimately reducing biased outcomes in cardiovascular medicine and enhancing personalized patient care.

Limitations

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract. However, as an AI-enabled modeling and simulation study, validation in prospective human clinical trials would be a necessary next step to confirm these computational findings.

Future Directions

The study provides a blueprint for further quantifying the impact of anatomical sex differences on functional markers. Future work will focus on tailoring clinical guidelines to consider unique patient anatomy, aiming to reduce current diagnostic biases and improve outcomes in myocardial infarction patients.

Abstract

The electrocardiogram (ECG) is modulated by torso-heart anatomy, and this challenges patients' diagnosis and risk stratification. This study aims to quantify how torso-heart anatomical factors affect sex-differences in ECG biomarkers in acute and chronic myocardial infarction (MI). We exploit the perfect control of AI-augmented multiscale modelling and simulation, based on clinical magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) data and ECGs for model construction and validation. A cohort of 1720 torso-ventricular anatomies (50% female) was constructed from MRIs of healthy and post-MI participants in the UK Biobank study. 8600 ECG simulations were performed considering anatomical variability and 3 electrophysiological stages (healthy, acutely ischemic, and infarcted). The effect of cardiac size, position, and orientation on each ECG biomarker was quantified. Female anatomies had larger distances between the infarct and ECG electrodes (relative to cardiac size), and larger angles between the infarct normal and ECG lead axes, both primarily caused by their more superior cardiac position. This reduced ST-elevation and caused low-amplitude late depolarisation and repolarisation tails to be missed, shortening QRS duration (QRSd) and T-peak-to-end interval (TpTe). The position and orientation of the heart impacted TpTe more severely than QRSd. AI-enabled mechanistic modelling and simulation identify smaller ventricles, superior cardiac position, and different ventricular orientation as key anatomical contributors of shorter QRS and T waves, and lower ST-elevation, in female versus male anatomies. This provides a blueprint for quantifying the impact of anatomical sex differences on functional markers and enables future work in tailoring clinical guidelines considering unique patient anatomy to reduce biased outcomes.
