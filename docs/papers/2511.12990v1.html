Brain Networks Flow-Topology via Variance Minimization in the Wasserstein Space

Summary

This paper introduces a novel framework for robustly analyzing topological variability in weighted brain networks by combining combinatorial Hodge decomposition with Wasserstein variance minimization. The method quantifies topological disparity using the 2-Wasserstein distance between persistence diagrams, demonstrating superior noise suppression and heightened sensitivity to genuine structural differences compared to traditional raw-edge weight analysis. It was successfully applied to differentiate functional brain networks between cannabis users and non-users in an ADHD dataset.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a more robust and sensitive method for identifying subtle, genuine structural differences in brain networks, which is critical for understanding disease pathogenesis, discovering novel biomarkers for neurological and psychiatric conditions, and assessing the impact of substance use on brain function.

Methodology

The framework involves decomposing network signals (flows) using combinatorial Hodge theory. Topological data analysis is then applied to generate persistence diagrams from these decomposed components. The 2-Wasserstein distance is used to quantify the topological disparity between these diagrams. A variance minimization test statistic measures the reduction in dispersion when comparing within-group to between-group differences in the Wasserstein space. The method was validated through simulations and applied to functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) data from the Multimodal Treatment of ADHD dataset.
