Summary

This study systematically evaluated machine learning models to assess smoking-related health risk using routine health screening data, aiming for earlier identification of health decline. The Random Forest model emerged as the most accurate (AUC 0.926), reliably distinguishing individuals based on health biomarkers, with blood pressure, triglycerides, liver enzymes, and serum creatinine identified as key predictive indicators.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for preventive medicine by offering an advanced, data-driven approach to detect early physiological signs of smoking-related health decline, potentially enabling timely clinical interventions and personalized risk management before severe, irreversible disease progression occurs.

AI Health Application

The paper uses machine learning models (Random Forest, XGBoost, LightGBM) for health risk assessment, specifically to classify current smoking status based on health screening biomarkers. This application helps in the early identification of individuals exhibiting physiological signs of smoking-related health decline, facilitating timely medical intervention and personalized health management within a clinical or public health setting. It also employs SHAP analysis for clinical interpretability, identifying specific health markers crucial for prediction.

Methodology

The study employed a cross-sectional design, analyzing health screening data from 55,691 individuals. Key Findings

The Random Forest model achieved superior performance with an AUC of 0.926, demonstrating its high accuracy in distinguishing individuals with smoking-related health decline. SHAP analysis revealed that blood pressure levels, triglyceride concentrations, liver enzyme readings, and serum creatinine were the most significant biomarkers contributing to the model's predictions, indicating their strong association with declining health in smokers.

Clinical Impact

The findings offer a practical, interpretable machine learning tool that could be integrated into routine health screenings to identify individuals at high risk for smoking-related health issues earlier. This can facilitate proactive clinical interventions, personalized counseling, and targeted monitoring based on the identified key biomarkers, potentially mitigating the progression of smoking-related diseases.

Limitations

The study utilized a cross-sectional design focused on classifying *current smoking status* based on existing health screening biomarkers, and therefore does not predict *future disease development* or long-term health outcomes.

Abstract

Smoking continues to be a major preventable cause of death worldwide, affecting millions through damage to the heart, metabolism, liver, and kidneys. However, current medical screening methods often miss the early warning signs of smoking-related health problems, leading to late-stage diagnoses when treatment options become limited. This study presents a systematic comparative evaluation of machine learning approaches for smoking-related health risk assessment, emphasizing clinical interpretability and practical deployment over algorithmic innovation. We analyzed health screening data from 55,691 individuals, examining various health indicators, including body measurements, blood tests, and demographic information. We tested three advanced prediction algorithms - Random Forest, XGBoost, and LightGBM - to determine which could most accurately identify people at high risk. This study employed a cross-sectional design to classify current smoking status based on health screening biomarkers, not to predict future disease development. Our Random Forest model performed best, achieving an Area Under the Curve (AUC) of 0.926, meaning it could reliably distinguish between high-risk and lower-risk individuals. Using SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations) analysis to understand what the model was detecting, we found that key health markers played crucial roles in prediction: blood pressure levels, triglyceride concentrations, liver enzyme readings, and kidney function indicators (serum creatinine) were the strongest signals of declining health in smokers.
