Summary

This paper advances infectious disease modeling by introducing chemotaxis-inspired Partial Differential Equation (PDE) models, which more realistically capture airborne infection spread towards susceptible populations compared to traditional reaction-diffusion models. It provides an epidemiologically interpretable framework, including a novel spatially-aware basic reproduction number ($R_0$), and demonstrates improved accuracy in simulating spatiotemporal disease dynamics in real-world scenarios like the COVID-19 outbreak in Lombardy.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to public health as it provides a more accurate and sophisticated modeling framework for predicting the spatial spread of airborne infectious diseases. Such improved models can better inform and optimize public health interventions, resource allocation, and policy decisions during outbreaks, ultimately leading to more effective disease control.

Methodology

The study develops and mathematically analyzes Partial Differential Equation (PDE) models that include a chemotaxis-type term to represent the spatial propagation of airborne infectious diseases. It involves the derivation of epidemiologically interpretable parameters, such as a spatially-aware basic reproduction number ($R_0$), and addresses numerical challenges through the introduction of a stabilization scheme. The model's performance is evaluated via computational simulation studies in specific geographical regions (Lombardy and Georgia) and compared against traditional pure reaction-diffusion PDE models using real-world outbreak data.

Abstract

Partial differential equation (PDE) models for infectious diseases, while less common than their ordinary differential equation (ODE) counterparts, have found successful applications for many years. Such models are typically of reaction-diffusion type, and model spatial propagation as a diffusive process. However, given the complex nature of human mobility, such models are limited in their ability to describe airborne infectious diseases in human populations. Recent work has advocated for the inclusion of an additional chemotaxis-type term as an alternative; spatial propagation of infection fronts is assumed additionally to flow from low-to-high concentrations of susceptible populations. The present work extends the study of such models by providing an epidemiologically interpretable analysis, directly connecting model behavior to information readily available to policymakers. In particular, we derive a spatially-aware basic reproduction number, which accounts for spatial heterogeneity in population density. Furthermore, we discuss several important aspects concerning the numerical solution of the model, including the introduction of a stabilization scheme. Finally, we perform a series of simulation studies in the Italian region of Lombardy (severely affected by the COVID-19 outbreak in 2020) and in the US state of Georgia, in which we demonstrate the model's potential to better capture important spatiotemporal dynamics observed in real-world data compared to pure reaction-diffusion models.
