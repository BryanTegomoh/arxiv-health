Performance Evaluation of Transfer Learning Based Medical Image Classification Techniques for Disease Detection

Published: 2025-12-04
Authors: Zeeshan Ahmad, Shudi Bao, Meng Chen

Summary

This paper comprehensively evaluates the performance of six deep convolutional neural network models (AlexNet, VGG16, ResNet18, ResNet34, ResNet50, InceptionV3) using transfer learning for disease detection on a custom chest X-ray dataset. The study finds that InceptionV3 consistently achieves superior performance across standard metrics, while demonstrating that transfer learning is largely beneficial, especially with limited data, and provides insights for model selection.

Medical Relevance

Medical image classification is crucial for early and accurate disease identification from images like X-rays, MRIs, and CT scans. Optimizing deep learning techniques with transfer learning makes advanced diagnostic tools more accessible and effective, particularly when extensive medical datasets for training are scarce.

AI Health Application

The paper evaluates AI models (deep convolutional neural networks, transfer learning) for automating or assisting in the classification of medical images (e.g., X-rays, MRIs, CT scans) to detect various diseases. This contributes to the development of AI tools for faster, more consistent, and potentially more accurate medical diagnosis and screening.

Key Points

  • The study evaluates six pre-trained deep CNN models (AlexNet, VGG16, ResNet18, ResNet34, ResNet50, InceptionV3) for medical image classification.
  • A custom chest X-ray dataset was used as the target domain for disease detection.
  • InceptionV3 consistently outperformed all other evaluated models across standard performance metrics.
  • The ResNet family demonstrated progressively better performance with increasing model depth (ResNet18 < ResNet34 < ResNet50).
  • Transfer learning (TL) was found to be beneficial, particularly in scenarios with limited data.
  • The effectiveness of TL is influenced by factors such as model architecture, dataset size, and domain similarity between the source and target tasks.
  • Uncertainty analysis and runtime comparisons were conducted, and it was shown that a well-trained feature extractor allows for efficient prediction with a lightweight feedforward model.

Methodology

The study employed transfer learning (TL) by re-using six pre-trained deep convolutional neural networks (AlexNet, VGG16, ResNet18, ResNet34, ResNet50, InceptionV3). These models were evaluated on a custom chest X-ray dataset specifically for disease detection. Performance was assessed using standard metrics, and additional analyses included uncertainty analysis and runtime comparisons to evaluate robustness and computational efficiency.

Key Findings

InceptionV3 consistently achieved the highest performance across all standard metrics for chest X-ray disease detection. The ResNet family showed a positive correlation between model depth and performance. Transfer learning was found to be beneficial, especially with limited data, though its efficacy depends on model architecture, dataset size, and domain similarity. Efficient prediction can be achieved using a lightweight feedforward model when combined with a well-trained feature extractor.

Clinical Impact

The findings provide crucial insights for clinicians and researchers in selecting optimal deep learning models for medical image classification tasks, particularly for disease detection using chest X-rays. Identifying InceptionV3 as a top performer, and understanding the factors influencing TL effectiveness, can guide the development of more accurate and computationally efficient AI-powered diagnostic tools, potentially leading to faster and more reliable disease identification in clinical settings.

Limitations

The extent of improvement from transfer learning depends on several factors, including the specific model architecture, the size of the target dataset, and the similarity between the source and target domains. While beneficial in most cases, particularly with limited data, TL's universal applicability or maximal benefit is not guaranteed without considering these contextual factors.

Future Directions

The study contributes to understanding TL in medical image classification and provides insights for selecting appropriate models based on specific requirements. This implies future research could focus on tailoring model selection based on specific clinical needs, dataset characteristics, and exploring further optimization strategies for feature extractors in medical imaging.

Abstract

Medical image classification plays an increasingly vital role in identifying various diseases by classifying medical images, such as X-rays, MRIs and CT scans, into different categories based on their features. In recent years, deep learning techniques have attracted significant attention in medical image classification. However, it is usually infeasible to train an entire large deep learning model from scratch. To address this issue, one of the solutions is the transfer learning (TL) technique, where a pre-trained model is reused for a new task. In this paper, we present a comprehensive analysis of TL techniques for medical image classification using deep convolutional neural networks. We evaluate six pre-trained models (AlexNet, VGG16, ResNet18, ResNet34, ResNet50, and InceptionV3) on a custom chest X-ray dataset for disease detection. The experimental results demonstrate that InceptionV3 consistently outperforms other models across all the standard metrics. The ResNet family shows progressively better performance with increasing depth, whereas VGG16 and AlexNet perform reasonably well but with lower accuracy. In addition, we also conduct uncertainty analysis and runtime comparison to assess the robustness and computational efficiency of these models. Our findings reveal that TL is beneficial in most cases, especially with limited data, but the extent of improvement depends on several factors such as model architecture, dataset size, and domain similarity between source and target tasks. Moreover, we demonstrate that with a well-trained feature extractor, only a lightweight feedforward model is enough to provide efficient prediction. As such, this study contributes to the understanding of TL in medical image classification, and provides insights for selecting appropriate models based on specific requirements.
