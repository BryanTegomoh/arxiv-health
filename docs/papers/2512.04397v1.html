Performance Evaluation of Transfer Learning Based Medical Image Classification Techniques for Disease Detection

Summary

This paper conducts a comprehensive evaluation of six pre-trained deep convolutional neural networks (AlexNet, VGG16, ResNet18, ResNet34, ResNet50, and InceptionV3) using transfer learning for disease detection on a custom chest X-ray dataset. The study demonstrates that InceptionV3 consistently achieves superior performance, while highlighting the benefits of transfer learning, especially with limited data, and offers insights for model selection in medical image classification.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and automated classification of medical images is crucial for early and reliable disease detection, which can significantly improve patient outcomes. This research directly contributes to developing robust AI tools that can assist clinicians in diagnosing diseases like those detectable from chest X-rays, especially in resource-constrained environments or with limited data.

AI Health Application

The AI application described is medical image classification for automated or assisted disease detection and diagnosis. Specifically, it evaluates deep learning models for classifying medical images (e.g., chest X-rays) to identify the presence of diseases.

Key Points

  • The study addresses the infeasibility of training large deep learning models from scratch in medical image classification by utilizing transfer learning (TL).
  • Six pre-trained deep CNN models (AlexNet, VGG16, ResNet18, ResNet34, ResNet50, InceptionV3) were evaluated on a custom chest X-ray dataset for disease detection.
  • InceptionV3 consistently outperformed all other tested models across standard evaluation metrics for medical image classification.
  • The ResNet family showed a progressive improvement in performance with increasing model depth (ResNet18 < ResNet34 < ResNet50).
  • Transfer learning was found to be generally beneficial, particularly in scenarios with limited data, but its effectiveness is influenced by factors like model architecture, dataset size, and domain similarity.
  • Uncertainty analysis and runtime comparisons were also performed to assess model robustness and computational efficiency.
  • The research indicates that a well-trained feature extractor combined with a lightweight feedforward model can achieve efficient and accurate predictions.

Methodology

The study employed transfer learning, leveraging six pre-trained deep convolutional neural networks (AlexNet, VGG16, ResNet18, ResNet34, ResNet50, InceptionV3) as feature extractors. These models were fine-tuned or used in a feature extraction setup on a custom chest X-ray dataset to classify medical images for disease detection. Performance was evaluated using standard metrics, complemented by uncertainty analysis for robustness and runtime comparisons for computational efficiency.

Key Findings

InceptionV3 demonstrated superior performance across all metrics, making it the most effective model for this specific task. The ResNet architecture exhibited a positive correlation between depth and classification accuracy. VGG16 and AlexNet performed reasonably but with lower accuracy compared to deeper models. The study also highlighted that transfer learning is generally advantageous, especially with limited data, and that a lightweight feedforward model can provide efficient predictions when combined with a strong pre-trained feature extractor.

Clinical Impact

This research provides valuable guidance for clinicians and researchers in selecting appropriate deep learning models for medical image classification tasks, particularly for chest X-ray analysis. The superior performance of InceptionV3 and the efficiency of lightweight prediction models could lead to faster, more accurate, and more accessible AI-driven diagnostic tools, potentially aiding in earlier disease detection and improving healthcare delivery, especially where expert radiologists are scarce.

Limitations

The extent of improvement from transfer learning depends on several factors, including model architecture, dataset size, and the domain similarity between the source and target tasks. While beneficial with limited data, the study implies that the advantages might be less pronounced with very large, domain-specific datasets.

Future Directions

The paper does not explicitly state future research directions, but implied directions include exploring transfer learning benefits with larger and more diverse medical datasets, investigating other advanced deep learning architectures, and further refining lightweight prediction models for clinical deployment.

Abstract

Medical image classification plays an increasingly vital role in identifying various diseases by classifying medical images, such as X-rays, MRIs and CT scans, into different categories based on their features. In recent years, deep learning techniques have attracted significant attention in medical image classification. However, it is usually infeasible to train an entire large deep learning model from scratch. To address this issue, one of the solutions is the transfer learning (TL) technique, where a pre-trained model is reused for a new task. In this paper, we present a comprehensive analysis of TL techniques for medical image classification using deep convolutional neural networks. We evaluate six pre-trained models (AlexNet, VGG16, ResNet18, ResNet34, ResNet50, and InceptionV3) on a custom chest X-ray dataset for disease detection. The experimental results demonstrate that InceptionV3 consistently outperforms other models across all the standard metrics. The ResNet family shows progressively better performance with increasing depth, whereas VGG16 and AlexNet perform reasonably well but with lower accuracy. In addition, we also conduct uncertainty analysis and runtime comparison to assess the robustness and computational efficiency of these models. Our findings reveal that TL is beneficial in most cases, especially with limited data, but the extent of improvement depends on several factors such as model architecture, dataset size, and domain similarity between source and target tasks. Moreover, we demonstrate that with a well-trained feature extractor, only a lightweight feedforward model is enough to provide efficient prediction. As such, this study contributes to the understanding of TL in medical image classification, and provides insights for selecting appropriate models based on specific requirements.
