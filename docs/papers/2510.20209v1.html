Assessing the Feasibility of Early Cancer Detection Using Routine Laboratory Data: An Evaluation of Machine Learning Approaches on an Imbalanced Dataset

arXiv ID: 2510.20209v1
Published: 2025-10-23
Authors: Shumin Li
Categories: cs.LG, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This study evaluated the feasibility of early cancer detection in dogs using machine learning on routine laboratory data, addressing challenges like non-specific biomarkers and severe class imbalance. While a statistically detectable cancer signal was found (AUROC=0.815), the optimal model demonstrated poor clinical classification performance (F1=0.25, PPV=0.15), indicating that routine lab data alone are insufficient for reliable early cancer diagnosis or rule-out.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to veterinary medicine, particularly oncology, by exploring low-cost, accessible screening methods for early cancer detection in dogs. Understanding the limitations of routine lab data for this purpose is critical for guiding future diagnostic development and avoiding misallocation of resources.

AI Health Application

The research develops and evaluates machine learning models for early cancer risk classification using routine laboratory data. This constitutes an AI application aimed at creating accessible screening tools for health monitoring and disease detection, specifically in the context of 'computational veterinary oncology'.

Key Points

  • The study addressed the significant challenge of developing accessible early cancer detection tools for dogs using low-cost routine laboratory data.
  • A comprehensive benchmark evaluation involved 126 analytical pipelines, combining various machine learning models, feature selection methods, and data balancing techniques on the Golden Retriever Lifetime Study (GRLS) cohort.
  • The optimal model, a Logistic Regression classifier with class weighting and recursive feature elimination, achieved moderate ranking ability (AUROC = 0.815; 95% CI: 0.793-0.836).
  • Despite the moderate AUROC, clinical classification performance was poor, with an F1-score of 0.25 and a Positive Predictive Value (PPV) of 0.15.
  • A high Negative Predictive Value (NPV) of 0.98 was observed, but insufficient recall (0.79) precluded its use as a reliable rule-out test.
  • Interpretability analysis with SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) revealed that predictions were primarily driven by non-specific features like age and markers of inflammation and anemia.
  • The study concluded that the cancer signal in routine lab data, while statistically detectable, is too weak and confounded by normal aging or other inflammatory conditions for clinically reliable discrimination, establishing a performance ceiling for this data modality in isolation.

Methodology

A comprehensive benchmark evaluation was performed on data from the Golden Retriever Lifetime Study (GRLS) cohort, utilizing 126 distinct machine learning pipelines. Each pipeline comprised various machine learning models (e.g., Logistic Regression), feature selection methods (e.g., recursive feature elimination), and data balancing techniques (e.g., class weighting). Data were partitioned at the patient level to prevent leakage, and interpretability was assessed using SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP).

Key Findings

A statistically detectable cancer signal was identified in routine lab data, achieving a moderate AUROC of 0.815 for the optimal model. However, clinical classification performance was poor (F1-score = 0.25, PPV = 0.15). While a high Negative Predictive Value (0.98) was noted, insufficient recall (0.79) rendered it unreliable for ruling out cancer. Predictions were predominantly based on non-specific features such as age and markers of inflammation and anemia.

Clinical Impact

This study establishes that routine laboratory data alone, despite their accessibility and low cost, are insufficient for clinically reliable early cancer detection or rule-out in dogs. While a high NPV is promising, the low recall and PPV mean that such a test would generate too many false positives to be clinically useful for diagnosis, and too many false negatives to confidently rule out disease. This guides future research towards multi-modal data integration rather than refining single-modality approaches.

Limitations

The primary limitation is the inherent non-specificity of individual biomarkers in routine laboratory data and the severe class imbalance in screening populations. The study also acknowledges that grouping diverse cancer types and including post-diagnosis samples in the dataset might have confounded the signal. The finding that the cancer signal is too weak and confounded by normal aging or other inflammatory conditions highlights a fundamental limitation of this data modality in isolation.

Future Directions

The study strongly suggests that meaningful progress in computational veterinary oncology will necessitate the integration of multi-modal data sources. This implies combining routine lab data with other information such as genetic markers, imaging data, advanced proteomics, or metabolomics to achieve clinically reliable cancer discrimination.

Abstract

The development of accessible screening tools for early cancer detection in dogs represents a significant challenge in veterinary medicine. Routine laboratory data offer a promising, low-cost source for such tools, but their utility is hampered by the non-specificity of individual biomarkers and the severe class imbalance inherent in screening populations. This study assesses the feasibility of cancer risk classification using the Golden Retriever Lifetime Study (GRLS) cohort under real-world constraints, including the grouping of diverse cancer types and the inclusion of post-diagnosis samples. A comprehensive benchmark evaluation was conducted, systematically comparing 126 analytical pipelines that comprised various machine learning models, feature selection methods, and data balancing techniques. Data were partitioned at the patient level to prevent leakage. The optimal model, a Logistic Regression classifier with class weighting and recursive feature elimination, demonstrated moderate ranking ability (AUROC = 0.815; 95% CI: 0.793-0.836) but poor clinical classification performance (F1-score = 0.25, Positive Predictive Value = 0.15). While a high Negative Predictive Value (0.98) was achieved, insufficient recall (0.79) precludes its use as a reliable rule-out test. Interpretability analysis with SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) revealed that predictions were driven by non-specific features like age and markers of inflammation and anemia. It is concluded that while a statistically detectable cancer signal exists in routine lab data, it is too weak and confounded for clinically reliable discrimination from normal aging or other inflammatory conditions. This work establishes a critical performance ceiling for this data modality in isolation and underscores that meaningful progress in computational veterinary oncology will require integration of multi-modal data sources.
