Assessing the Feasibility of Early Cancer Detection Using Routine Laboratory Data: An Evaluation of Machine Learning Approaches on an Imbalanced Dataset

arXiv ID: 2510.20209v1
Published: 2025-10-23
Authors: Shumin Li
Categories: cs.LG, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This study evaluated the feasibility of early cancer detection in dogs using routine laboratory data and machine learning, specifically on the Golden Retriever Lifetime Study cohort. While a moderate ranking ability was achieved (AUROC=0.815), the optimal model demonstrated poor clinical classification performance (F1=0.25, PPV=0.15) and insufficient recall for reliable rule-out, concluding that the cancer signal in such data is too weak and confounded for clinical utility in isolation. The work establishes a performance ceiling for this data modality and highlights the need for multi-modal data integration.

Medical Relevance

This research provides critical insights into the limitations of using routine, non-specific laboratory data for early cancer detection in veterinary medicine. It sets a realistic benchmark for current capabilities, preventing over-reliance on single-modality approaches and directing future efforts towards more complex, multi-modal diagnostic strategies to achieve clinical utility.

AI Health Application

The AI application involves using various machine learning models and data processing pipelines to classify cancer risk and detect early cancer signals from routine laboratory data. This aims to develop an accessible, low-cost screening tool for early disease detection, which is a direct application of AI in health diagnostics and personalized medicine.

Key Points

  • The study addressed the challenge of accessible early cancer detection in dogs using routine, low-cost laboratory data from the Golden Retriever Lifetime Study (GRLS) cohort.
  • A comprehensive benchmark evaluation was conducted, comparing 126 analytical pipelines encompassing various machine learning models, feature selection methods, and data balancing techniques, with patient-level data partitioning to prevent leakage.
  • The optimal model, a Logistic Regression classifier with class weighting and recursive feature elimination, achieved a moderate ranking ability (AUROC = 0.815; 95% CI: 0.793-0.836).
  • Despite moderate AUROC, the model showed poor clinical classification performance with an F1-score of 0.25 and a Positive Predictive Value (PPV) of 0.15.
  • A high Negative Predictive Value (NPV = 0.98) was achieved, but insufficient recall (0.79) precluded its use as a reliable rule-out test for cancer.
  • Interpretability analysis (SHAP) revealed predictions were driven by non-specific features such as age and general markers of inflammation and anemia, rather than specific cancer indicators.
  • The study concluded that the statistically detectable cancer signal in routine lab data is too weak and confounded by normal aging or other inflammatory conditions for clinically reliable discrimination, establishing a performance ceiling for this data modality in isolation.

Methodology

The study employed a comprehensive benchmark evaluation comparing 126 machine learning pipelines. These pipelines consisted of various ML models, feature selection methods (e.g., recursive feature elimination), and data balancing techniques (e.g., class weighting) applied to routine laboratory data from the Golden Retriever Lifetime Study (GRLS) cohort. Data were partitioned at the patient level to prevent leakage, and the analysis accounted for real-world constraints such as grouping diverse cancer types and including post-diagnosis samples. Interpretability analysis was performed using SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) on the optimal model.

Key Findings

The optimal model, a Logistic Regression classifier with class weighting and recursive feature elimination, achieved an AUROC of 0.815 (95% CI: 0.793-0.836), indicating moderate ranking ability. However, its clinical classification performance was poor, reflected by an F1-score of 0.25 and a Positive Predictive Value (PPV) of 0.15. While a high Negative Predictive Value (NPV) of 0.98 was achieved, insufficient recall (0.79) prevented its use as a reliable rule-out test. SHAP analysis revealed that predictions were largely driven by non-specific features like age and general markers of inflammation and anemia, rather than specific cancer indicators.

Clinical Impact

The study's findings indicate that machine learning models built solely on routine laboratory data are currently insufficient for reliable early cancer detection or as a rule-out test in veterinary clinical practice, despite a statistically detectable signal. The poor clinical classification performance (low PPV, F1-score, and insufficient recall) highlights that these non-specific biomarkers cannot adequately discriminate early cancer from normal aging or inflammatory conditions. This sets a realistic performance ceiling for such single-modality approaches, informing veterinarians and researchers about the current limitations of these diagnostic tools.

Limitations

The primary limitation identified is that the cancer signal within routine laboratory data, even with advanced machine learning, is too weak and highly confounded. The predictive features identified by SHAP (age, inflammation, anemia markers) are non-specific, making it challenging for the model to reliably distinguish early cancer from common physiological changes or other inflammatory conditions. This inherent non-specificity limits the clinical utility of this data modality when used in isolation.

Future Directions

The study explicitly recommends that meaningful progress in computational veterinary oncology for early cancer detection will necessitate the integration of multi-modal data sources. This suggests combining routine laboratory data with other, more specific diagnostic information, such as advanced imaging, genetic markers, or specialized proteomic/metabolomic panels, to overcome the current limitations of non-specific biomarkers and achieve clinically reliable discrimination.

Abstract

The development of accessible screening tools for early cancer detection in dogs represents a significant challenge in veterinary medicine. Routine laboratory data offer a promising, low-cost source for such tools, but their utility is hampered by the non-specificity of individual biomarkers and the severe class imbalance inherent in screening populations. This study assesses the feasibility of cancer risk classification using the Golden Retriever Lifetime Study (GRLS) cohort under real-world constraints, including the grouping of diverse cancer types and the inclusion of post-diagnosis samples. A comprehensive benchmark evaluation was conducted, systematically comparing 126 analytical pipelines that comprised various machine learning models, feature selection methods, and data balancing techniques. Data were partitioned at the patient level to prevent leakage. The optimal model, a Logistic Regression classifier with class weighting and recursive feature elimination, demonstrated moderate ranking ability (AUROC = 0.815; 95% CI: 0.793-0.836) but poor clinical classification performance (F1-score = 0.25, Positive Predictive Value = 0.15). While a high Negative Predictive Value (0.98) was achieved, insufficient recall (0.79) precludes its use as a reliable rule-out test. Interpretability analysis with SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) revealed that predictions were driven by non-specific features like age and markers of inflammation and anemia. It is concluded that while a statistically detectable cancer signal exists in routine lab data, it is too weak and confounded for clinically reliable discrimination from normal aging or other inflammatory conditions. This work establishes a critical performance ceiling for this data modality in isolation and underscores that meaningful progress in computational veterinary oncology will require integration of multi-modal data sources.
