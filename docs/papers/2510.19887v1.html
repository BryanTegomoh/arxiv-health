Compressing Biology: Evaluating the Stable Diffusion VAE for Phenotypic Drug Discovery

Summary

This paper systematically evaluates the Stable Diffusion Variational Autoencoder (SD-VAE) for reconstructing high-dimensional Cell Painting microscopy images, a crucial step in phenotypic drug discovery. It demonstrates that SD-VAE effectively preserves phenotypic signals with minimal loss, thereby validating its suitability for microscopy workflows. The study also shows that general-purpose feature extractors like InceptionV3 can perform as well as or better than specialized models in biologically relevant retrieval tasks, simplifying future computational pipelines.

By validating the use of accessible, off-the-shelf AI models, it reduces the complexity and cost of analyzing vast microscopy datasets, potentially speeding up the identification of treatments for various diseases.

Methodology

The researchers performed a systematic evaluation of the Stable Diffusion Variational Autoencoder (SD-VAE) by applying it to reconstruct Cell Painting images from a large dataset. This dataset contained images from diverse molecular perturbations and cell types. They benchmarked the reconstruction quality using four categories of metrics: pixel-level comparisons, embedding-based metrics (comparing feature vectors), latent-space metrics (evaluating the compressed representations), and retrieval-based metrics (assessing the ability to retrieve biologically similar phenotypes). They further compared the performance of general-purpose feature extractors (e.g., InceptionV3) against specialized, bespoke models in these retrieval tasks.

Key Findings

The study's key findings are twofold: first, the Stable Diffusion VAE can effectively reconstruct high-dimensional Cell Painting images while preserving essential phenotypic signals with minimal loss. Second, general-purpose feature extractors like InceptionV3 perform comparably to or better than specialized, publicly available models in biologically relevant retrieval tasks, indicating that complex, bespoke model development may not always be necessary for effective analysis in this domain.

Abstract

High-throughput phenotypic screens generate vast microscopy image datasets that push the limits of generative models due to their large dimensionality. Despite the growing popularity of general-purpose models trained on natural images for microscopy data analysis, their suitability in this domain has not been quantitatively demonstrated. We present the first systematic evaluation of Stable Diffusion's variational autoencoder (SD-VAE) for reconstructing Cell Painting images, assessing performance across a large dataset with diverse molecular perturbations and cell types. We find that SD-VAE reconstructions preserve phenotypic signals with minimal loss, supporting its use in microscopy workflows. To benchmark reconstruction quality, we compare pixel-level, embedding-based, latent-space, and retrieval-based metrics for a biologically informed evaluation. We show that general-purpose feature extractors like InceptionV3 match or surpass publicly available bespoke models in retrieval tasks, simplifying future pipelines. Our findings offer practical guidelines for evaluating generative models on microscopy data and support the use of off-the-shelf models in phenotypic drug discovery.
