Summary

This paper presents an epidemiological model to describe the spatial dynamics of airborne infectious diseases, incorporating ambient airflow as an infection control measure. It reveals that larger pathogen-carrying droplets (~4 Œºm) are the primary drivers of infectious spread, capable of generating propagating waves or localized outbreaks, while very small droplets (~0.4 Œºm) pose minimal risk. A significant finding is the derivation of a threshold air velocity that can impair disease transmission even when the basic reproduction number R0 is high.

Medical Relevance

This research provides critical insights into the physical mechanisms governing airborne disease transmission, directly informing the design and implementation of environmental infection control strategies, particularly ventilation systems, in indoor settings to mitigate outbreak risks.

Methodology

The study employs an epidemiological model to describe the spatial dynamics of airborne disease transmission. This model incorporates the physics of droplet transport, specifically diffusive and convective processes, and integrates the effects of ambient airflow as an infection control measure. The inclusion of airflow leads to a delay equation, where the current droplet density is a function of the infectious density at a previous time, capturing the temporal aspect of pathogen dispersal.

Key Findings

Droplet size significantly impacts infectious potential: very small droplets (~0.4 Œºm) transmit negligible infection, whereas larger droplets (~4 Œºm) are potent vectors capable of driving substantial disease spread. Larger droplets can manifest as propagating infectious waves in fully susceptible populations or localized secondary outbreaks. Droplet diffusion is a weak mechanism for spatial infection spread; convective processes driven by airflow are more dominant. Crucially, a specific threshold air velocity exists, which, if met or exceeded, can effectively disrupt and impair disease transmission, even when the intrinsic transmissibility (basic reproduction number R0) is high (greater than 1).

Clinical Impact

The findings have direct implications for designing and optimizing ventilation and air purification systems in healthcare facilities, public buildings, and other indoor environments to mitigate airborne transmission risks. Specifically, they highlight the importance of targeting airflow dynamics to reduce the concentration and spread of infectious larger droplets and provide a basis for establishing minimum effective air velocities for infection control, thereby enhancing patient and staff safety during outbreaks.

Abstract

Disease outbreaks, such as those of Severe Acute Respiratory Syndrome in 2003 and the 2009 pandemic A(H1N1) influenza, have highlighted the potential for airborne transmission in indoor environments. Respirable pathogen-carrying droplets provide a vector for the spatial spread of infection with droplet transport determined by diffusive and convective processes. An epidemiological model describing the spatial dynamics of disease transmission is presented. The effects of an ambient airflow, as an infection control, are incorporated leading to a delay equation, with droplet density dependent on the infectious density at a previous time. It is found that small droplets ($\sim 0.4\ Œº$m) generate a negligible infectious force due to the small viral load and the associated duration they require to transmit infection. In contrast, larger droplets ($\sim 4\ Œº$m) can lead to an infectious wave propagating through a fully susceptible population or a secondary infection outbreak for a localised susceptible population. Droplet diffusion is found to be an inefficient mode of droplet transport leading to minimal spatial spread of infection. A threshold air velocity is derived, above which disease transmission is impaired even when the basic reproduction number $R_{0}$ exceeds unity.
