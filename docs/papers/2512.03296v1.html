Associating Healthcare Teamwork with Patient Outcomes for Predictive Analysis

arXiv ID: 2512.03296v1
Published: 2025-12-02
Authors: Hsiao-Ying Lu, Kwan-Liu Ma
Categories: cs.SI, cs.CY, cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces an AI-driven approach to investigate the impact of healthcare professionals' (HCPs) collaboration, captured via electronic health records (EHRs), on cancer patient survival. By modeling HCP interactions as networks and applying machine learning, the study identifies predictive signals of patient survival embedded in these collaborations and pinpoints key network traits associated with improved outcomes, validated by clinical experts.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a novel framework to quantify and leverage the dynamics of healthcare teamwork, directly linking collaboration patterns to patient survival outcomes. This can inform targeted interventions to optimize team performance and improve clinical results, particularly in complex conditions like cancer where multi-disciplinary care is crucial.

AI Health Application

The research uses machine learning techniques and network analysis on EHR-mediated healthcare professional interactions to predict cancer patient survival. It identifies key collaboration traits associated with improved outcomes, providing a practical AI-driven workflow to assess and improve team-based healthcare delivery and support data-informed interventions.

Key Points

  • Addresses the overlooked role of human collaboration in influencing cancer treatment outcomes.
  • Utilizes electronic health record (EHR) data to capture and model HCP interactions as networks.
  • Applies machine learning techniques to detect predictive signals of patient survival within these collaboration networks.
  • Ensures generalizability of the predictive models through cross-validation.
  • Explains predictions by identifying specific 'key network traits' of collaboration linked to improved patient outcomes.
  • Validates the relevance of the identified crucial collaboration traits through clinical experts and existing literature.
  • Proposes a practical workflow for leveraging digital traces of collaboration and AI to assess and improve team-based healthcare delivery.
  • Suggests the approach is potentially transferable to other domains involving complex collaboration and offers actionable insights for data-informed interventions.

Methodology

The study models electronic health record (EHR)-mediated healthcare professional (HCP) interactions as networks. Machine learning techniques are then applied to these networks to detect predictive signals correlating collaboration patterns with patient survival outcomes. Model generalizability is ensured through cross-validation, and predictions are explained by identifying key network traits.

Key Findings

The research successfully identified predictive signals of patient survival embedded within HCP collaboration networks derived from EHR data. Critically, it pinpointed specific 'key network traits' of collaboration that are associated with improved patient outcomes, with these traits subsequently validated by clinical experts and existing literature.

Clinical Impact

This work offers actionable insights for healthcare delivery by identifying effective collaboration patterns, enabling data-informed interventions to improve team-based care. It could lead to better training, process optimization, or resource allocation strategies to enhance teamwork, thereby potentially improving patient survival and treatment efficacy, particularly in cancer care.

Abstract

Cancer treatment outcomes are influenced not only by clinical and demographic factors but also by the collaboration of healthcare teams. However, prior work has largely overlooked the potential role of human collaboration in shaping patient survival. This paper presents an applied AI approach to uncovering the impact of healthcare professionals' (HCPs) collaboration-captured through electronic health record (EHR) systems-on cancer patient outcomes. We model EHR-mediated HCP interactions as networks and apply machine learning techniques to detect predictive signals of patient survival embedded in these collaborations. Our models are cross validated to ensure generalizability, and we explain the predictions by identifying key network traits associated with improved outcomes. Importantly, clinical experts and literature validate the relevance of the identified crucial collaboration traits, reinforcing their potential for real-world applications. This work contributes to a practical workflow for leveraging digital traces of collaboration and AI to assess and improve team-based healthcare. The approach is potentially transferable to other domains involving complex collaboration and offers actionable insights to support data-informed interventions in healthcare delivery.
