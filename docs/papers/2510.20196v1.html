A Structured Review and Quantitative Profiling of Public Brain MRI Datasets for Foundation Model Development

arXiv ID: 2510.20196v1
Published: 2025-10-23
Authors: Minh Sao Khue Luu, Margaret V. Benedichuk, Ekaterina I. Roppert, Roman M. Kenzhin, Bair N. Tuchinov
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00

Summary

This study systematically reviews 54 public brain MRI datasets, encompassing over 538,031 images, to assess their suitability for foundation model development. It uncovers significant data imbalances, image-level heterogeneity, and demonstrates that standard preprocessing, while improving within-dataset consistency, leaves substantial inter-dataset bias and covariate shift, necessitating advanced domain-adaptive strategies for generalizable models.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing AI in medical imaging by identifying fundamental challenges in existing brain MRI data, directly impacting the development of reliable and generalizable diagnostic and prognostic AI models. Addressing these issues will ensure that AI tools can perform consistently and accurately across diverse patient populations and clinical settings, leading to safer and more effective clinical applications.

AI Health Application

The research directly supports the development of AI foundation models for automated analysis, interpretation, and potentially diagnosis or prognosis from brain MRI scans. These models could enhance clinical decision support systems, improve diagnostic accuracy, facilitate early detection of neurological conditions, and personalize treatment plans by providing robust, generalized AI capabilities across diverse clinical settings and patient populations.

Key Points

  • A structured, multi-level review and quantitative profiling of 54 publicly accessible brain MRI datasets (>538,031 images) was performed.
  • Revealed strong imbalances at the dataset level, with large healthy cohorts dominating over smaller clinical populations.
  • Quantified substantial image-level heterogeneity in voxel spacing, orientation, and intensity distributions across 15 representative datasets.
  • Demonstrated that standard preprocessing steps (normalization, bias correction, skull stripping, registration, interpolation) improve within-dataset consistency but leave significant residual differences between datasets.
  • A feature-space case study using a 3D DenseNet121 confirmed measurable residual covariate shift after standardized preprocessing.
  • Concluded that harmonization alone is insufficient to eliminate inter-dataset bias, which can adversely affect representation learning.
  • Emphasized the critical need for preprocessing-aware and domain-adaptive strategies for developing generalizable brain MRI foundation models.

Methodology

The study conducted a multi-level structured review and quantitative profiling. It analyzed 54 public brain MRI datasets (>538,031 images) by characterizing modality composition, disease coverage, and scale at the dataset level. At the image level, it quantified voxel spacing, orientation, and intensity distributions across 15 representative datasets. It then quantitatively evaluated the impact of common preprocessing steps (intensity normalization, bias field correction, skull stripping, spatial registration, interpolation) and performed a feature-space case study using a 3D DenseNet121 to assess residual covariate shift.

Clinical Impact

The findings underscore the necessity for advanced data handling in medical AI development. Clinically, this means that AI models for brain MRI diagnosis, prognosis, or treatment response prediction need to be designed with explicit consideration for data variability and bias. This will lead to more robust, reliable, and deployable AI tools in neuroradiology, reducing the risk of misdiagnosis or biased performance when applied to patients from diverse sources or with different demographic profiles.

Limitations

The study implicitly highlights limitations within current public brain MRI datasets and standard preprocessing practices: the strong imbalance between healthy and clinical populations, the substantial image-level heterogeneity, and the inherent inability of current harmonization techniques to fully eliminate inter-dataset bias, resulting in persistent covariate shift.

Future Directions

The research strongly advocates for the development of 'preprocessing-aware' strategies that account for data transformations, and 'domain-adaptive' strategies to handle inter-dataset variability in the design of generalizable brain MRI foundation models. This is crucial for creating robust AI solutions applicable across diverse clinical environments.

Abstract

The development of foundation models for brain MRI depends critically on the scale, diversity, and consistency of available data, yet systematic assessments of these factors remain scarce. In this study, we analyze 54 publicly accessible brain MRI datasets encompassing over 538,031 to provide a structured, multi-level overview tailored to foundation model development. At the dataset level, we characterize modality composition, disease coverage, and dataset scale, revealing strong imbalances between large healthy cohorts and smaller clinical populations. At the image level, we quantify voxel spacing, orientation, and intensity distributions across 15 representative datasets, demonstrating substantial heterogeneity that can influence representation learning. We then perform a quantitative evaluation of preprocessing variability, examining how intensity normalization, bias field correction, skull stripping, spatial registration, and interpolation alter voxel statistics and geometry. While these steps improve within-dataset consistency, residual differences persist between datasets. Finally, feature-space case study using a 3D DenseNet121 shows measurable residual covariate shift after standardized preprocessing, confirming that harmonization alone cannot eliminate inter-dataset bias. Together, these analyses provide a unified characterization of variability in public brain MRI resources and emphasize the need for preprocessing-aware and domain-adaptive strategies in the design of generalizable brain MRI foundation models.
