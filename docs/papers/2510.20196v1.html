A Structured Review and Quantitative Profiling of Public Brain MRI Datasets for Foundation Model Development

Summary

This study systematically reviews and quantitatively profiles 54 publicly available brain MRI datasets (over 538,031 entries) to assess their suitability for foundation model development. It uncovers significant imbalances between healthy and clinical cohorts, substantial heterogeneity in image characteristics, and persistent inter-dataset variability even after standardized preprocessing. The research confirms measurable residual covariate shift in feature space, highlighting that current harmonization methods are insufficient to eliminate inter-dataset bias.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing AI in clinical neuroimaging by identifying fundamental data challenges that hinder the development of robust, generalizable, and clinically reliable brain MRI foundation models. Addressing these issues is paramount for creating AI tools that can accurately diagnose, monitor, and prognose neurological conditions across diverse patient populations and scanner environments.

AI Health Application

The research directly supports the development of robust and generalizable AI foundation models for brain MRI analysis. These models, once developed, would serve as powerful tools for various clinical applications, including automated disease detection and diagnosis (e.g., Alzheimer's disease, multiple sclerosis, brain tumors), patient stratification, prediction of disease progression, quantitative assessment of neurological conditions, and guiding treatment strategies based on MRI biomarkers. The paper aims to overcome data-related challenges to make these AI applications more reliable and effective in healthcare.

Methodology

The study employed a multi-level quantitative profiling approach: 1) Dataset-level characterization of modality composition, disease coverage, and scale across 54 datasets. 2) Image-level quantification of voxel spacing, orientation, and intensity distributions across 15 representative datasets. 3) Quantitative evaluation of preprocessing variability by examining the impact of intensity normalization, bias field correction, skull stripping, spatial registration, and interpolation on voxel statistics and geometry. 4) A feature-space case study using a 3D DenseNet121 model to measure residual covariate shift after standardized preprocessing.

Key Findings

Public brain MRI datasets are characterized by strong imbalances, with large healthy cohorts outnumbering smaller clinical populations. Significant heterogeneity exists in fundamental image properties like voxel spacing and intensity distributions. While standard preprocessing improves within-dataset consistency, substantial residual differences persist between datasets. Furthermore, even after standardized preprocessing, a deep learning model (3D DenseNet121) reveals measurable residual covariate shift in feature space, confirming that harmonization alone is insufficient to eliminate inter-dataset bias.

This research has significant implications for the clinical deployment of AI in neuroimaging. By highlighting the pervasive data variability and bias, it underscores why AI models trained on current public data may lack generalizability and robustness when applied to diverse clinical settings (e.g., different hospitals, scanners, patient demographics). The findings will guide the development of more robust, preprocessing-aware, and domain-adaptive AI models, leading to more reliable diagnostic and prognostic tools for neurological disorders and ultimately enhancing patient care by enabling more accurate and equitable medical AI applications.

Abstract

The development of foundation models for brain MRI depends critically on the scale, diversity, and consistency of available data, yet systematic assessments of these factors remain scarce. In this study, we analyze 54 publicly accessible brain MRI datasets encompassing over 538,031 to provide a structured, multi-level overview tailored to foundation model development. At the dataset level, we characterize modality composition, disease coverage, and dataset scale, revealing strong imbalances between large healthy cohorts and smaller clinical populations. At the image level, we quantify voxel spacing, orientation, and intensity distributions across 15 representative datasets, demonstrating substantial heterogeneity that can influence representation learning. We then perform a quantitative evaluation of preprocessing variability, examining how intensity normalization, bias field correction, skull stripping, spatial registration, and interpolation alter voxel statistics and geometry. While these steps improve within-dataset consistency, residual differences persist between datasets. Finally, feature-space case study using a 3D DenseNet121 shows measurable residual covariate shift after standardized preprocessing, confirming that harmonization alone cannot eliminate inter-dataset bias. Together, these analyses provide a unified characterization of variability in public brain MRI resources and emphasize the need for preprocessing-aware and domain-adaptive strategies in the design of generalizable brain MRI foundation models.
