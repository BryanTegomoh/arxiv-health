A Structured Review and Quantitative Profiling of Public Brain MRI Datasets for Foundation Model Development

arXiv ID: 2510.20196v1
Published: 2025-10-23
Authors: Minh Sao Khue Luu, Margaret V. Benedichuk, Ekaterina I. Roppert, Roman M. Kenzhin, Bair N. Tuchinov
Categories: cs.CV

Summary

This study systematically reviews and quantitatively profiles 54 public brain MRI datasets (over 538,031 images) to assess their suitability for developing foundation models. It reveals significant imbalances in dataset scale and disease coverage, alongside substantial image-level heterogeneity and persistent inter-dataset variability even after standardized preprocessing. The research ultimately demonstrates that harmonization alone is insufficient to eliminate inter-dataset bias, underscoring the need for advanced preprocessing-aware and domain-adaptive strategies.

Medical Relevance

The development of robust and generalizable AI foundation models for brain MRI is crucial for advancing clinical diagnosis, prognosis, and research in neurology. This study directly addresses the fundamental data challenges that impede such development, highlighting the necessity for advanced data handling to ensure AI models are reliable and effective in diverse clinical settings.

AI Health Application

The research directly supports the development of AI foundation models for brain MRI. These models can be fine-tuned for various clinical applications, including automated detection and classification of brain diseases (e.g., tumors, stroke, multiple sclerosis, dementia), segmentation of brain structures and lesions, prediction of disease progression, and quantitative analysis of imaging biomarkers for precision medicine and drug discovery.

Methodology

The study employed a multi-level quantitative analysis: first, characterizing 54 public datasets at the dataset level (modality, disease coverage, scale); second, quantifying image-level features (voxel spacing, orientation, intensity distributions) across 15 representative datasets; third, evaluating the impact of standard preprocessing steps on voxel statistics and geometry; and finally, performing a feature-space case study using a 3D DenseNet121 to assess residual covariate shift after preprocessing.

Abstract

The development of foundation models for brain MRI depends critically on the scale, diversity, and consistency of available data, yet systematic assessments of these factors remain scarce. In this study, we analyze 54 publicly accessible brain MRI datasets encompassing over 538,031 to provide a structured, multi-level overview tailored to foundation model development. At the dataset level, we characterize modality composition, disease coverage, and dataset scale, revealing strong imbalances between large healthy cohorts and smaller clinical populations. At the image level, we quantify voxel spacing, orientation, and intensity distributions across 15 representative datasets, demonstrating substantial heterogeneity that can influence representation learning. We then perform a quantitative evaluation of preprocessing variability, examining how intensity normalization, bias field correction, skull stripping, spatial registration, and interpolation alter voxel statistics and geometry. While these steps improve within-dataset consistency, residual differences persist between datasets. Finally, feature-space case study using a 3D DenseNet121 shows measurable residual covariate shift after standardized preprocessing, confirming that harmonization alone cannot eliminate inter-dataset bias. Together, these analyses provide a unified characterization of variability in public brain MRI resources and emphasize the need for preprocessing-aware and domain-adaptive strategies in the design of generalizable brain MRI foundation models.
