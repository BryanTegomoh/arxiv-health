Deep Learning Classification of EEG Responses to Multi-Dimensional Transcranial Electrical Stimulation

arXiv ID: 2512.20319v1
Published: 2025-12-23
Authors: Alexis Pomares Pastor, Ines Ribeiro Violante, Gregory Scott
Categories: q-bio.NC, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00

Summary

This study introduces a novel framework utilizing Deep Learning to objectively classify EEG brain responses evoked by multi-dimensional transcranial electrical stimulation (TES) for assessing conscious level. They demonstrated that transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) targeting the angular gyrus elicited exceptionally reliable EEG responses, enabling a Convolutional Neural Network to achieve a 92% F1-score on unseen data, significantly outperforming human-level performance. These findings establish a robust method for consciousness measurement applicable in clinical settings.

Medical Relevance

This research offers a promising objective, non-invasive, and bedside tool for assessing conscious levels in patients with severe brain injuries, seizures, or other conditions causing impaired consciousness. It has the potential to improve diagnostic accuracy, guide prognosis, and inform treatment decisions for critically ill patients who cannot communicate or respond volitionally.

Methodology

The study involved collecting EEG data from 11 participants while applying multi-dimensional transcranial electrical stimulation (TES). Specifically, transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) was delivered to posterior cortical areas targeting the angular gyrus. Deep Learning algorithms, particularly Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), were then trained and used to classify the evoked EEG brain responses. Model performance was rigorously evaluated using F1-score on holdout data from participants never seen during the training phase.

Key Findings

The research successfully demonstrated the feasibility of using Deep Learning to classify EEG brain responses evoked by multi-dimensional TES. A critical finding was that tDCS applied to the angular gyrus elicited exceptionally reliable and distinct brain responses. The optimized CNN model achieved a 92% classification F1-score on completely unseen participant data, significantly outperforming human experts who typically achieve 60-70% accuracy on similar tasks.

This framework offers a path towards a robust, objective, and easily translatable bedside measure of conscious level, which is a major unmet need in medical practice. It could revolutionize the assessment of patients with conditions like severe traumatic brain injury, stroke, prolonged seizures, or disorders of consciousness, by providing an objective brain state indicator independent of sensory or motor functions. This could lead to earlier and more accurate diagnoses, improved prognostication, and more effective, personalized treatment strategies.

Abstract

A major shortcoming of medical practice is the lack of an objective measure of conscious level. Impairment of consciousness is common, e.g. following brain injury and seizures, which can also interfere with sensory processing and volitional responses. This is also an important pitfall in neurophysiological methods that infer awareness via command following, e.g. using functional MRI or electroencephalography (EEG). Transcranial electrical stimulation (TES) can be employed to non-invasively stimulate the brain, bypassing sensory inputs, and has already showed promising results in providing reliable indicators of brain state. However, current non-invasive solutions have been limited to magnetic stimulation, which is not easily translatable to clinical settings. Our long-term vision is to develop an objective measure of brain state that can be used at the bedside, without requiring patients to understand commands or initiate motor responses. In this study, we demonstrated the feasibility of a framework using Deep Learning algorithms to classify EEG brain responses evoked by a defined multi-dimensional pattern of TES. We collected EEG-TES data from 11 participants and found that delivering transcranial direct current stimulation (tDCS) to posterior cortical areas targeting the angular gyrus elicited an exceptionally reliable brain response. For this paradigm, our best Convolutional Neural Network model reached a 92% classification F1-score on Holdout data from participants never seen during training, significantly surpassing human-level performance at 60-70% accuracy. These findings establish a framework for robust consciousness measurement for clinical use. In this spirit, we documented and open-sourced our datasets and codebase in full, to be used freely by the neuroscience and AI research communities, who may replicate our results with free tools like GitHub, Kaggle, and Colab.
