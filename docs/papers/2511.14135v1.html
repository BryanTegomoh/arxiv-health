Fair-GNE : Generalized Nash Equilibrium-Seeking Fairness in Multiagent Healthcare Automation

arXiv ID: 2511.14135v1
Published: 2025-11-18
Authors: Promise Ekpo, Saesha Agarwal, Felix Grimm, Lekan Molu, Angelique Taylor
Categories: cs.LG, cs.AI, cs.GT, cs.MA
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces Fair-GNE, a novel multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) approach that leverages a constrained Generalized Nash Equilibrium (GNE)-seeking game framework to achieve self-enforceable and certifiably fair workload allocation in multiagent healthcare automation. Unlike existing MARL methods that rely on post-hoc reward shaping, Fair-GNE intrinsically steers group policy to an equitable collective equilibrium, significantly improving workload balance while maintaining high task success in demand-side healthcare worker settings.

Medical Relevance

Enforcing fair workload allocation among healthcare agents, whether human or automated, is paramount for consistent performance, preventing burnout, and ensuring efficient resource utilization in dynamic clinical environments like emergency care or demand-side management. This research offers a principled, self-enforceable mechanism for achieving this fairness, directly impacting the reliability and effectiveness of healthcare automation systems.

AI Health Application

The AI application involves using multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) combined with a generalized Nash equilibrium (GNE) game-theoretic framework to achieve fair workload allocation and optimize performance among multiple agents in healthcare automation systems. This is specifically demonstrated for scenarios like resuscitation, where AI can help coordinate healthcare workers or automated systems to improve efficiency and fairness in task distribution.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical gap of lacking certifiable, self-enforceable fairness in multi-agent workload allocation within learning-enabled demand-side healthcare worker settings.
  • Proposes Fair-GNE, which models Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) as a constrained Generalized Nash Equilibrium (GNE)-seeking game.
  • The GNE framework ensures that no individual agent can unilaterally improve its utility function by changing its decisions, thereby establishing a stable and equitable collective equilibrium.
  • Evaluated the hypothesis using a custom-designed, high-fidelity resuscitation simulator, demonstrating its applicability in critical healthcare scenarios.
  • Achieved a significant improvement in workload balance, measured by Jain's Fairness Index (JFI), increasing it from 0.33 (fixed-penalty baselines) to 0.89 (p < 0.01).
  • Maintained a high task success rate of 86%, indicating that fairness gains do not compromise overall system performance.
  • Demonstrates statistically significant fairness gains achieved through adaptive constraint enforcement, embedding fairness directly into the decision-making process.

Methodology

The methodology involves modeling Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) as a constrained Generalized Nash Equilibrium (GNE)-seeking game. This framework is designed for self-interested decision-makers whose actions affect others, aiming to steer the group policy to a safe, locally efficient, and equitable equilibrium. The approach incorporates adaptive constraint enforcement to achieve fairness. Evaluation was conducted using a custom-designed, high-fidelity resuscitation simulator.

Key Findings

Fair-GNE significantly improved workload balance, achieving a Jain's Fairness Index (JFI) of 0.89, compared to 0.33 for fixed-penalty baselines (p < 0.01). This was accomplished while maintaining a robust 86% task success rate. The results confirm statistically significant fairness gains through the proposed adaptive constraint enforcement within the GNE framework.

Clinical Impact

This research can lead to more robust, reliable, and ethically sound multi-agent healthcare automation systems. By ensuring fair workload distribution, it can optimize the performance of AI-powered assistants or robotic systems in clinical settings, potentially reducing human operator fatigue or burnout when integrated into human-AI teams. In critical scenarios like resuscitation, fair task allocation can improve overall team efficiency and patient outcomes by preventing overburdening of specific agents and ensuring all necessary tasks are completed optimally.

Abstract

Enforcing a fair workload allocation among multiple agents tasked to achieve an objective in learning enabled demand side healthcare worker settings is crucial for consistent and reliable performance at runtime. Existing multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) approaches steer fairness by shaping reward through post hoc orchestrations, leaving no certifiable self-enforceable fairness that is immutable by individual agents at runtime. Contextualized within a setting where each agent shares resources with others, we address this shortcoming with a learning enabled optimization scheme among self-interested decision makers whose individual actions affect those of other agents. This extends the problem to a generalized Nash equilibrium (GNE) game-theoretic framework where we steer group policy to a safe and locally efficient equilibrium, so that no agent can improve its utility function by unilaterally changing its decisions. Fair-GNE models MARL as a constrained generalized Nash equilibrium-seeking (GNE) game, prescribing an ideal equitable collective equilibrium within the problem's natural fabric. Our hypothesis is rigorously evaluated in our custom-designed high-fidelity resuscitation simulator. Across all our numerical experiments, Fair-GNE achieves significant improvement in workload balance over fixed-penalty baselines (0.89 vs.\ 0.33 JFI, $p < 0.01$) while maintaining 86\% task success, demonstrating statistically significant fairness gains through adaptive constraint enforcement. Our results communicate our formulations, evaluation metrics, and equilibrium-seeking innovations in large multi-agent learning-based healthcare systems with clarity and principled fairness enforcement.
