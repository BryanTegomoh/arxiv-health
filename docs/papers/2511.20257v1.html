Interpretable Air Pollution Forecasting by Physics-Guided Spatiotemporal Decoupling

arXiv ID: 2511.20257v1
Published: 2025-11-25
Authors: Zhiguo Zhang, Xiaoliang Ma, Daniel Schlesinger
Categories: cs.LG, cs.AI

Summary

This study introduces a novel physics-guided, interpretable-by-design spatiotemporal learning framework for accurate air pollution forecasting, addressing the trade-off between performance and interpretability. By decoupling pollutant behavior into a transport kernel and an explainable attention mechanism, the model achieves high predictive performance, outperforming state-of-the-art baselines, while offering crucial interpretability for real-world air quality management.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and interpretable air pollution forecasting is crucial for public health, enabling proactive warnings, informing preventive strategies against pollution-related diseases, and guiding policy decisions to mitigate health risks associated with poor air quality.

AI Health Application

The AI model (physics-guided spatiotemporal decoupling) is applied to forecast air pollution, which is a critical input for public health decision-making and operational air-quality management. This allows for proactive measures to mitigate the health risks associated with high pollution levels, thereby serving as an AI-powered tool for environmental health protection and preventive medicine.

Methodology

The study proposes a spatiotemporal learning framework that decomposes air pollutant behavior into two additive modules. The first module is a physics-guided transport kernel incorporating directed weights based on wind and geographical factors (advection). The second module is an explainable attention mechanism designed to learn local responses and attribute future concentrations to specific historical lags and exogenous drivers. The model's performance was evaluated against state-of-the-art baselines using a comprehensive dataset from the Stockholm region.

Key Findings

The proposed model consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines across multiple forecasting horizons. It successfully integrates high predictive performance with robust spatiotemporal interpretability, overcoming the common trade-off observed in existing air pollution forecasting models.

Clinical Impact

This interpretable air pollution forecasting model can significantly enhance real-world air quality management by providing more reliable and actionable predictions. This enables public health agencies and clinicians to issue more precise health advisories, better target interventions for vulnerable populations, and inform policy decisions for reducing air pollution exposure, thereby mitigating the burden of air pollution-related illnesses on healthcare systems and individual health.

Abstract

Accurate and interpretable air pollution forecasting is crucial for public health, but most models face a trade-off between performance and interpretability. This study proposes a physics-guided, interpretable-by-design spatiotemporal learning framework. The model decomposes the spatiotemporal behavior of air pollutant concentrations into two transparent, additive modules. The first is a physics-guided transport kernel with directed weights conditioned on wind and geography (advection). The second is an explainable attention mechanism that learns local responses and attributes future concentrations to specific historical lags and exogenous drivers. Evaluated on a comprehensive dataset from the Stockholm region, our model consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines across multiple forecasting horizons. Our model's integration of high predictive performance and spatiotemporal interpretability provides a more reliable foundation for operational air-quality management in real-world applications.
