Summary

This paper introduces a novel two-stage methodology employing voxel sparsification and Submanifold Sparse Convolutional Networks (SSCNs) for automated, high-resolution 3D segmentation of kidneys and kidney tumors in CT scans. The method achieves state-of-the-art accuracy competitive with KiTS23 challenge winners while significantly reducing computational resource demands, addressing a key bottleneck in routine quantitative clinical analyses.

Medical Relevance

Automated and accurate 3D segmentation of renal structures and tumors is paramount for improving diagnostic efficiency, facilitating precise treatment planning, and enabling routine quantitative analysis in oncology, thereby overcoming current labor-intensive manual processes that limit clinical workflow.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the automated 3D segmentation of kidneys and kidney tumors from Computed Tomography (CT) scans. This technology aims to assist clinicians in the diagnosis, staging, treatment planning, and quantitative analysis of renal cancer, by making the process faster, more accurate, and less resource-intensive.

Key Points

  • The research addresses the clinical bottleneck of time-consuming manual tumour delineation in radiological images and the computational impracticality of processing full high-resolution 3D CT scans with traditional dense convolutional neural networks.
  • A new two-stage methodology is proposed, combining voxel sparsification with Submanifold Sparse Convolutional Networks (SSCNs) to enable segmentation directly on high-resolution, native 3D inputs.
  • The method achieved state-of-the-art segmentation accuracies for renal cancer CT images from the KiTS23 challenge, demonstrating its effectiveness in a real-world clinical context.
  • Specific Dice similarity coefficients (DSC) obtained were 95.8% for kidneys + masses, 85.7% for tumours + cysts, and 80.3% for tumours alone, showing competitive performance against challenge winners.
  • Crucially, the methodology offers significant computational improvements, reducing inference time by up to 60% and VRAM usage by up to 75% compared to equivalent dense architectures.
  • These computational efficiencies were consistently observed across various CPU and GPU cards tested, highlighting the method's practical deployability.
  • By overcoming computational limitations, the method facilitates the routine performance of quantitative analyses in clinical settings, which is currently hindered by manual segmentation efforts.

Methodology

The proposed methodology consists of two stages: first, voxel sparsification to manage the large data volume of 3D CT scans, followed by the application of Submanifold Sparse Convolutional Networks (SSCNs). This approach processes high-resolution inputs using a native 3D model architecture, explicitly avoiding the downsampling or patching strategies typically required by traditional dense CNNs due to memory constraints.

Key Findings

The method achieved high segmentation accuracy, with Dice similarity coefficients of 95.8% for kidneys + masses, 85.7% for tumours + cysts, and 80.3% for tumours alone, competitive with KiTS23 challenge winners. A significant finding is the substantial computational efficiency, demonstrating up to a 60% reduction in inference time and up to a 75% reduction in VRAM usage compared to dense architectures, across various hardware configurations.

Clinical Impact

This research has the potential to profoundly impact clinical practice by automating a currently time-consuming and specialized task, thereby enabling routine quantitative analysis of kidney and tumor characteristics from CT scans. This automation can lead to faster diagnosis, more precise treatment planning, improved monitoring of disease progression, and more efficient allocation of clinical resources, ultimately enhancing patient care in renal cancer management.

Abstract

The accurate delineation of tumours in radiological images like Computed Tomography is a very specialised and time-consuming task, and currently a bottleneck preventing quantitative analyses to be performed routinely in the clinical setting. For this reason, developing methods for the automated segmentation of tumours in medical imaging is of the utmost importance and has driven significant efforts in recent years. However, challenges regarding the impracticality of 3D scans, given the large amount of voxels to be analysed, usually requires the downsampling of such images or using patches thereof when applying traditional convolutional neural networks. To overcome this problem, in this paper we propose a new methodology that uses, divided into two stages, voxel sparsification and submanifold sparse convolutional networks. This method allows segmentations to be performed with high-resolution inputs and a native 3D model architecture, obtaining state-of-the-art accuracies while significantly reducing the computational resources needed in terms of GPU memory and time. We studied the deployment of this methodology in the context of Computed Tomography images of renal cancer patients from the KiTS23 challenge, and our method achieved results competitive with the challenge winners, with Dice similarity coefficients of 95.8% for kidneys + masses, 85.7% for tumours + cysts, and 80.3% for tumours alone. Crucially, our method also offers significant computational improvements, achieving up to a 60% reduction in inference time and up to a 75\% reduction in VRAM usage compared to an equivalent dense architecture, across both CPU and various GPU cards tested.
