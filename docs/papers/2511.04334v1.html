Summary

This paper introduces a novel methodology using two-stage voxel sparsification combined with Submanifold Sparse Convolutional Networks for automated 3D segmentation of kidneys and kidney tumors in Computed Tomography (CT) scans. The approach enables high-resolution input processing with a native 3D architecture, achieving state-of-the-art accuracies competitive with KiTS23 challenge winners while significantly reducing computational resources.

Medical Relevance

Automated, accurate, and computationally efficient segmentation of kidney tumors from CT scans is crucial for streamlining renal cancer diagnosis, treatment planning, and monitoring. It enables routine quantitative analysis currently limited by manual effort, directly improving patient care pathways.

AI Health Application

This research applies advanced deep learning (submanifold sparse convolutional networks) to develop an automated tool for segmenting kidneys and kidney tumors from Computed Tomography (CT) scans. This AI application aims to assist radiologists in the precise delineation of tumors, improving the accuracy and efficiency of renal cancer diagnosis and monitoring, and enabling routine quantitative analyses in a clinical setting.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical clinical bottleneck of time-consuming manual tumor delineation in radiological images, hindering routine quantitative analyses.
  • Proposes a novel two-stage methodology combining voxel sparsification and Submanifold Sparse Convolutional Networks to overcome limitations of traditional CNNs with large 3D scans.
  • Enables automated 3D segmentation using high-resolution CT inputs and a native 3D model architecture, avoiding the need for downsampling or image patching.
  • Achieved competitive segmentation accuracies on the KiTS23 challenge dataset: 95.8% Dice for kidneys + masses, 85.7% for tumours + cysts, and 80.3% for tumours alone.
  • Demonstrates significant computational efficiency, including up to a 60% reduction in inference time and up to a 75% reduction in VRAM usage compared to an equivalent dense architecture.
  • The method's computational improvements were validated across various CPU and GPU configurations, indicating broad applicability in clinical settings.

Methodology

The methodology is divided into two stages. First, a voxel sparsification technique is applied to efficiently reduce the number of voxels that need processing, focusing computational efforts on relevant regions. This is followed by the application of Submanifold Sparse Convolutional Networks, which are designed to operate efficiently on sparse data, thus allowing the use of high-resolution, native 3D CT inputs without downsampling or requiring image patches. This sparse convolutional architecture significantly reduces computational resources compared to dense networks.

Key Findings

The method achieved high segmentation accuracy on the KiTS23 challenge dataset, with Dice similarity coefficients of 95.8% for kidneys + masses, 85.7% for tumours + cysts, and 80.3% for tumours alone, competitive with challenge winners. Crucially, it also demonstrated significant computational advantages, including up to a 60% reduction in inference time and up to a 75% reduction in VRAM usage compared to an equivalent dense architecture, across various CPU and GPU cards tested.

Clinical Impact

This methodology has the potential to significantly improve clinical workflows by automating a currently labor-intensive and specialized task in renal cancer diagnosis and treatment planning. The reduced inference time and VRAM usage make high-resolution 3D segmentation models more feasible for deployment in clinical settings with existing hardware, accelerating the process of obtaining quantitative analyses, improving diagnostic accuracy, and aiding in personalized treatment strategies and monitoring of tumor response.

Abstract

The accurate delineation of tumours in radiological images like Computed Tomography is a very specialised and time-consuming task, and currently a bottleneck preventing quantitative analyses to be performed routinely in the clinical setting. For this reason, developing methods for the automated segmentation of tumours in medical imaging is of the utmost importance and has driven significant efforts in recent years. However, challenges regarding the impracticality of 3D scans, given the large amount of voxels to be analysed, usually requires the downsampling of such images or using patches thereof when applying traditional convolutional neural networks. To overcome this problem, in this paper we propose a new methodology that uses, divided into two stages, voxel sparsification and submanifold sparse convolutional networks. This method allows segmentations to be performed with high-resolution inputs and a native 3D model architecture, obtaining state-of-the-art accuracies while significantly reducing the computational resources needed in terms of GPU memory and time. We studied the deployment of this methodology in the context of Computed Tomography images of renal cancer patients from the KiTS23 challenge, and our method achieved results competitive with the challenge winners, with Dice similarity coefficients of 95.8% for kidneys + masses, 85.7% for tumours + cysts, and 80.3% for tumours alone. Crucially, our method also offers significant computational improvements, achieving up to a 60% reduction in inference time and up to a 75\% reduction in VRAM usage compared to an equivalent dense architecture, across both CPU and various GPU cards tested.
