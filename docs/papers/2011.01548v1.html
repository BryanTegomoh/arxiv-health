Antibiotic treatment, duration of infectiousness, and disease transmission

arXiv ID: 2011.01548v1
Published: 2020-11-02
Authors: Thomas Caraco
Categories: q-bio.PE
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper challenges the conventional view that antibiotic treatment always reduces disease transmission, proposing that by suppressing within-host pathogen density, antibiotics can relax the overall rate of host removal from infectious status (e.g., recovery, isolation, mortality). Through a deterministic modeling approach, the study identifies conditions where this mechanism can paradoxically increase the duration of a host's infectiousness and, consequently, elevate the expected number of new infections.

Medical Relevance

This research is critically important for medical and public health communities as it challenges fundamental assumptions about antibiotic utility, suggesting that individual patient benefits might, under specific circumstances, contribute to increased population-level disease transmission. It necessitates a nuanced understanding of antibiotic effects beyond individual cure, directly impacting antibiotic stewardship and infectious disease control strategies.

AI Health Application

The paper itself does not utilize AI. However, the deterministic within-host and between-host dynamic models and the insights derived about antibiotic treatment's effect on disease transmission could serve as foundational components or data for AI-driven predictive models in public health, outbreak forecasting, personalized antibiotic treatment optimization, or AI-assisted decision support systems for antibiotic stewardship programs.

Key Points

  • Antibiotic therapy's impact on disease transmission is not always solely reductive; it can be complex, potentially increasing transmission under certain conditions.
  • The central hypothesis posits that if host removal rates (recovery via treatment, isolation, mortality) are dependent on within-host pathogen density, antibiotic-induced suppression of this density can prolong the infectious period by reducing other density-dependent removal events.
  • The methodology involves a deterministic within-host dynamics model that determines the infectious host's time-dependent probability of disease transmission and the duration of infectiousness.
  • The model explores within-host variables such as inoculum size, bacterial self-regulation, time between infection and therapy initiation, and antibiotic efficacy.
  • Between-host factors, specifically the size and susceptibility of groups randomly encountered by an infectious host, are also integrated into the model.
  • Key finding: The study identifies specific conditions where antibiotic treatment leads to an increased duration of host infectiousness and a higher expected number of new infections.
  • A notable implication is that lower antibiotic efficacy might convert a rare, serious bacterial disease into a more common, albeit treatable, infection.

Methodology

The study utilizes a deterministic mathematical model of within-host pathogen dynamics. This model is designed to drive the time-dependent probability of disease transmission by an infectious host, as well as the probabilistic duration of their infectious period. It systematically varies parameters at both the within-host scale (e.g., inoculum size, bacterial self-regulation, therapy initiation time, antibiotic efficacy) and the between-host scale (e.g., size/susceptibility of encountered groups) to explore their impact.

Key Findings

The primary discovery is the identification of specific conditions where antibiotic treatment can paradoxically increase the duration of a host's infectiousness, consequently leading to an elevated expected number of new infections in the population. Furthermore, the model suggests that a lower antibiotic efficacy might shift the epidemiological profile of a bacterial disease from a rare and serious infection to a more common but generally treatable one.

Clinical Impact

This work has significant clinical impact by highlighting the need to consider the broader epidemiological consequences of antibiotic prescribing beyond individual patient recovery. It suggests that clinical guidelines and antibiotic stewardship programs should account for the potential for prolonged infectiousness and increased transmission, particularly in contexts where host removal rates are density-dependent. This could lead to refined treatment protocols aiming to optimize both individual patient outcomes and population-level disease control, potentially influencing strategies for managing chronic infections or those with high community transmission potential.

Limitations

Based on the abstract, the primary limitation is that the findings are derived from a deterministic mathematical model, not empirical data. The identification of 'conditions' implies a theoretical framework rather than observed phenomena, requiring future empirical validation to confirm its applicability across diverse pathogen-host-antibiotic systems in real-world clinical and epidemiological settings.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated, the paper's identification of specific 'conditions' strongly implies a need for future research to empirically validate these theoretical predictions. This would involve designing studies to investigate how various antibiotics and bacterial pathogens influence within-host pathogen density and host removal rates, and how these factors translate to population-level transmission dynamics, particularly for diseases where such density-dependent removal mechanisms might be at play.

Abstract

Humans, domestic animals, orchard crops, and ornamental plants are commonly treated with antibiotics in response to bacterial infection. By curing infectious individuals, antibiotic therapy might limit the spread of contagious disease among hosts. But an antibiotic`s suppression of within-host pathogen density might also reduce the probability that the host is otherwise removed from infectious status prior to therapeutic recovery. When rates of both recovery via treatment and other removal events (e.g., isolation or mortality) depend directly on within-host pathogen density, antibiotic treatment can relax the overall removal rate sufficiently to increase between-host disease transmission. To explore this dependence, a deterministic within-host dynamics drives the infectious host's time-dependent probability of disease transmission, as well as the probabilistic duration of the infectious period. At the within-host scale, the model varies (1) inoculum size, (2) bacterial self-regulation, (3) the time between infection and initiation of therapy, and (4) antibiotic efficacy. At the between-host scale the model varies (5) the size/susceptibility of groups randomly encountered by an infectious host. Results identify conditions where antibiotic treatment can increase duration of a host`s infectiousness, and consequently increase the expected number of new infections. At lower antibiotic efficacy, treatment might convert a rare, serious bacterial disease into a common, but treatable infection.
