Antibiotic treatment, duration of infectiousness, and disease transmission

arXiv ID: 2011.01548v1
Published: 2020-11-02
Authors: Thomas Caraco
Categories: q-bio.PE
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper investigates the counterintuitive possibility that antibiotic treatment, while curing individuals, could paradoxically increase disease transmission by prolonging the duration of infectiousness. It proposes that if within-host pathogen density influences both recovery (via treatment) and other removal events (like isolation or mortality), antibiotics might relax the overall removal rate, thereby extending the period an individual is capable of spreading the disease. The study uses a deterministic within-host model coupled with between-host dynamics to identify conditions under which antibiotic therapy can lead to an increased expected number of new infections.

Medical Relevance

This research challenges the fundamental assumption that antibiotics invariably reduce disease transmission, highlighting a crucial epidemiological consideration for antimicrobial stewardship. It suggests that treatment strategies need to account for potential population-level impacts beyond individual patient cure, particularly concerning pathogen density-dependent host removal mechanisms.

While the paper itself employs deterministic mathematical modeling rather than AI, its findings are highly applicable to informing medical AI applications. Key Points

  • Antibiotic therapy, by suppressing within-host pathogen density, might reduce the probability of host removal from infectious status via non-treatment events (e.g., isolation, mortality) if these events depend on pathogen density.
  • When the overall host removal rate is sufficiently relaxed by antibiotic treatment, it can lead to an increase in between-host disease transmission despite individual cure.
  • A deterministic within-host dynamics model is employed to predict the infectious host's time-dependent probability of disease transmission and the probabilistic duration of the infectious period.
  • The model explores within-host variables including inoculum size, bacterial self-regulation, time between infection and therapy initiation, and antibiotic efficacy.
  • Between-host dynamics are modeled by varying the size and susceptibility of groups encountered by an infectious host.
  • Results indicate that specific conditions exist where antibiotic treatment can extend the duration of a host's infectiousness, consequently increasing the total number of new infections.
  • The paper suggests that antibiotic treatment at lower efficacy levels might transform a severe, rare bacterial disease into a more common, but treatable, infection.

Methodology

The study utilizes a deterministic mathematical model that integrates within-host bacterial dynamics with between-host disease transmission. The within-host component models the time-dependent pathogen density, which in turn drives the probability of disease transmission and the probabilistic duration of the infectious period. This model varies parameters such as initial inoculum, bacterial growth and regulation, timing of therapy, and antibiotic efficacy. The between-host scale considers the characteristics of host encounters (size and susceptibility).

Key Findings

The central finding is the identification of conditions under which antibiotic treatment can extend a host's infectious period, leading to an increased number of new infections rather than a reduction. Specifically, if antibiotic-induced suppression of pathogen density reduces the likelihood of other removal events (like isolation or mortality) that are also pathogen-density dependent, the overall rate of host removal from infectious status can decrease, thereby prolonging infectiousness. The research also suggests that low antibiotic efficacy might shift the epidemiological profile of a disease from rare and severe to common and treatable.

Clinical Impact

This work has significant potential clinical and public health impact. It suggests that indiscriminate or sub-optimally efficacious antibiotic use might inadvertently contribute to broader disease spread, necessitating a re-evaluation of treatment guidelines beyond individual patient outcomes. Clinicians and public health officials may need to consider the epidemiological consequences of varying antibiotic efficacies and the potential for density-dependent host removal mechanisms when formulating treatment protocols and antimicrobial stewardship policies, especially for diseases where host isolation or mortality is linked to pathogen burden.

Abstract

Humans, domestic animals, orchard crops, and ornamental plants are commonly treated with antibiotics in response to bacterial infection. By curing infectious individuals, antibiotic therapy might limit the spread of contagious disease among hosts. But an antibiotic`s suppression of within-host pathogen density might also reduce the probability that the host is otherwise removed from infectious status prior to therapeutic recovery. When rates of both recovery via treatment and other removal events (e.g., isolation or mortality) depend directly on within-host pathogen density, antibiotic treatment can relax the overall removal rate sufficiently to increase between-host disease transmission. To explore this dependence, a deterministic within-host dynamics drives the infectious host's time-dependent probability of disease transmission, as well as the probabilistic duration of the infectious period. At the within-host scale, the model varies (1) inoculum size, (2) bacterial self-regulation, (3) the time between infection and initiation of therapy, and (4) antibiotic efficacy. At the between-host scale the model varies (5) the size/susceptibility of groups randomly encountered by an infectious host. Results identify conditions where antibiotic treatment can increase duration of a host`s infectiousness, and consequently increase the expected number of new infections. At lower antibiotic efficacy, treatment might convert a rare, serious bacterial disease into a common, but treatable infection.
