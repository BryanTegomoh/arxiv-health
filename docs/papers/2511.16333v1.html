Beyond Generative AI: World Models for Clinical Prediction, Counterfactuals, and Planning

Summary

This paper reviews the application of "World Models" in healthcare AI, advocating for their superior ability over traditional generative AI to provide predictive, reliable, and data-efficient clinical decision support. It highlights how world models learn multimodal, temporally coherent, and action-conditioned representations crucial for clinical prediction, counterfactual evaluation, and planning in complex medical scenarios. The review outlines current capabilities, limitations, and a future research agenda for developing clinically robust prediction-first world models.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medicine as it addresses critical gaps in current AI for healthcare, offering a path towards more reliable, explainable, and actionable AI systems that can directly aid clinicians in diagnosis, prognosis, treatment planning, and surgical execution.

AI Health Application

The paper analyzes the application of 'World Models' AI to healthcare. This involves developing AI systems that learn predictive dynamics for multistep rollouts, counterfactual evaluation, and planning in clinical settings. Specific applications include longitudinal tumor simulation from medical imaging, generative event forecasting from electronic health records for disease progression, and action-conditioned guidance and control for robotic surgery. The ultimate goal is to enhance clinical decision support and ensure safe and robust AI integration in healthcare.

Key Points

  • Traditional generative AI models often lack the physical foundation and temporal/causal reasoning essential for grounded clinical decision support, showing diminishing returns for complex medical applications.
  • World Models are proposed as a superior alternative, learning predictive dynamics and multimodal, temporally coherent, action-conditioned representations that reflect the physical and causal structure of healthcare.
  • The paper surveys recent applications of World Models across three key domains: medical imaging and diagnostics (e.g., tumor simulation, JEPA-style learning), disease progression modeling from electronic health records, and robotic surgery/surgical planning.
  • A capability rubric is introduced: L1 (temporal prediction), L2 (action-conditioned prediction), L3 (counterfactual rollouts for decision support), and L4 (planning/control). Most current systems achieve L1-L2, with L3 being less common and L4 rare.
  • Identified limitations include under-specified action spaces and safety constraints, weak interventional validation, incomplete multimodal state construction, and limited trajectory-level uncertainty calibration.
  • World models enable advanced functionalities such as multistep rollouts for forecasting, counterfactual evaluation to assess alternative treatment paths, and planning/control for interventions.
  • The proposed research agenda emphasizes integrating generative backbones (transformers, diffusion, VAE) with causal/mechanical foundations to build clinically robust prediction-first world models for safe decision support.

Methodology

The paper is a comprehensive review surveying recent work on World Models applied to various healthcare systems. It analyzes how these models learn predictive dynamics and representations, evaluates their capabilities against a newly introduced rubric (L1-L4), and identifies cross-cutting gaps based on existing literature across specific medical domains.

Key Findings

The review found that World Models show promise by learning multimodal, temporally coherent, and action-conditioned representations reflecting clinical causality. While many systems achieve basic temporal (L1) and action-conditioned prediction (L2), higher-level capabilities like counterfactual rollouts (L3) and planning/control (L4) are less common. Significant limitations include issues with action space definition, safety validation, comprehensive multimodal state construction, and uncertainty calibration.

Clinical Impact

World Models have the potential to significantly impact clinical practice by enabling more accurate long-term disease progression forecasting, simulating effects of different interventions (counterfactuals) to optimize treatment strategies, and providing real-time, intelligent guidance and control for robotic surgery, ultimately leading to safer and more personalized patient care.

Limitations

['Under-specified action spaces and safety constraints in current models.', 'Weak interventional validation of model predictions in clinical settings.', 'Incomplete multimodal state construction, limiting the richness of representations.', 'Limited trajectory-level uncertainty calibration, crucial for reliability in critical applications.']

Future Directions

Future research should focus on developing clinically robust prediction-first world models that integrate powerful generative backbones (like transformers, diffusion models, VAEs) with strong causal and mechanical foundations. This aims to overcome current limitations and enhance safety and reliability for complex decision support in healthcare.

Abstract

Healthcare requires AI that is predictive, reliable, and data-efficient. However, recent generative models lack physical foundation and temporal reasoning required for clinical decision support. As scaling language models show diminishing returns for grounded clinical reasoning, world models are gaining traction because they learn multimodal, temporally coherent, and action-conditioned representations that reflect the physical and causal structure of care. This paper reviews World Models for healthcare systems that learn predictive dynamics to enable multistep rollouts, counterfactual evaluation and planning. We survey recent work across three domains: (i) medical imaging and diagnostics (e.g., longitudinal tumor simulation, projection-transition modeling, and Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture i.e., JEPA-style predictive representation learning), (ii) disease progression modeling from electronic health records (generative event forecasting at scale), and (iii) robotic surgery and surgical planning (action-conditioned guidance and control). We also introduce a capability rubric: L1 temporal prediction, L2 action-conditioned prediction, L3 counterfactual rollouts for decision support, and L4 planning/control. Most reviewed systems achieve L1--L2, with fewer instances of L3 and rare L4. We identify cross-cutting gaps that limit clinical reliability; under-specified action spaces and safety constraints, weak interventional validation, incomplete multimodal state construction, and limited trajectory-level uncertainty calibration. This review outlines a research agenda for clinically robust prediction-first world models that integrate generative backbones (transformers, diffusion, VAE) with causal/mechanical foundation for safe decision support in healthcare.
