Beyond Generative AI: World Models for Clinical Prediction, Counterfactuals, and Planning

Summary

This paper reviews World Models as a promising AI paradigm for healthcare, advocating their superiority over traditional generative models due to their capacity for learning multimodal, temporally coherent, and action-conditioned representations reflecting the physical and causal structure of care. It surveys applications across medical imaging, disease progression, and robotic surgery, while also introducing a capability rubric and identifying critical gaps for robust clinical deployment.

Medical Relevance

World Models address the critical need for AI in healthcare that is not only highly predictive but also reliable, data-efficient, and capable of understanding temporal causality, which is essential for robust clinical decision support, personalized treatment planning, and advanced surgical guidance.

AI Health Application

The paper describes the application of World Models, a type of AI, to various health contexts, including predictive AI for clinical decision support, counterfactual analysis for treatment planning, forecasting disease progression, analyzing medical images for diagnostics, and guiding robotic surgery. It aims to integrate generative AI with causal/mechanical foundations for robust and safe decision support in healthcare.

Key Points

  • World Models are highlighted as a crucial advancement over current generative AI for clinical decision support, emphasizing their ability to incorporate physical foundation, temporal reasoning, and causal structure.
  • The paper reviews World Models that learn predictive dynamics to enable multistep rollouts, counterfactual evaluation, and planning in healthcare systems.
  • Applications are surveyed across three domains: (i) medical imaging and diagnostics (e.g., longitudinal tumor simulation, JEPA-style predictive learning), (ii) disease progression modeling from EHRs (generative event forecasting), and (iii) robotic surgery and surgical planning (action-conditioned guidance).
  • A four-level capability rubric is introduced: L1 (temporal prediction), L2 (action-conditioned prediction), L3 (counterfactual rollouts for decision support), and L4 (planning/control).
  • Most current World Model systems in healthcare achieve L1-L2 capabilities, with fewer instances of L3 and rare demonstration of L4 planning/control.
  • Key limitations hindering clinical reliability include under-specified action spaces and safety constraints, weak interventional validation, incomplete multimodal state construction, and limited trajectory-level uncertainty calibration.
  • The proposed research agenda focuses on developing clinically robust prediction-first World Models by integrating generative backbones (transformers, diffusion, VAE) with causal/mechanical foundations for safe decision support.

Methodology

This is a comprehensive review paper that synthesizes existing research on World Models applied to healthcare. It surveys recent work across specific medical domains and introduces a novel four-level capability rubric (L1-L4) to categorize and assess the current functional maturity of these systems.

Key Findings

The review highlights that World Models are actively being developed for longitudinal medical simulations, generative event forecasting from EHRs, and action-conditioned surgical guidance. However, most current implementations primarily offer basic temporal and action-conditioned prediction (L1-L2). Advanced capabilities like counterfactual evaluation (L3) and planning/control (L4) remain largely underdeveloped. Significant cross-cutting gaps identified include inadequate specification of action spaces and safety constraints, insufficient interventional validation, incomplete multimodal state representations, and limited uncertainty calibration at the trajectory level.

Clinical Impact

World Models have the potential to significantly enhance clinical decision support by providing more accurate predictions of disease trajectories, simulating the outcomes of different interventions to guide treatment choices, and offering advanced guidance and control for complex procedures like robotic surgery. This could lead to more precise, effective, and safer personalized patient care.

Limitations

The paper identifies several crucial limitations of current World Model implementations in healthcare: under-specified action spaces and safety constraints, weak interventional validation methodologies, incomplete multimodal state construction, and limited trajectory-level uncertainty calibration, all of which hinder clinical reliability.

Future Directions

The authors propose a research agenda focused on developing clinically robust prediction-first World Models. This agenda emphasizes integrating modern generative backbones (such as transformers, diffusion models, and VAEs) with strong causal and mechanical foundations to achieve safe and reliable decision support in real-world healthcare settings.

Abstract

Healthcare requires AI that is predictive, reliable, and data-efficient. However, recent generative models lack physical foundation and temporal reasoning required for clinical decision support. As scaling language models show diminishing returns for grounded clinical reasoning, world models are gaining traction because they learn multimodal, temporally coherent, and action-conditioned representations that reflect the physical and causal structure of care. This paper reviews World Models for healthcare systems that learn predictive dynamics to enable multistep rollouts, counterfactual evaluation and planning. We survey recent work across three domains: (i) medical imaging and diagnostics (e.g., longitudinal tumor simulation, projection-transition modeling, and Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture i.e., JEPA-style predictive representation learning), (ii) disease progression modeling from electronic health records (generative event forecasting at scale), and (iii) robotic surgery and surgical planning (action-conditioned guidance and control). We also introduce a capability rubric: L1 temporal prediction, L2 action-conditioned prediction, L3 counterfactual rollouts for decision support, and L4 planning/control. Most reviewed systems achieve L1--L2, with fewer instances of L3 and rare L4. We identify cross-cutting gaps that limit clinical reliability; under-specified action spaces and safety constraints, weak interventional validation, incomplete multimodal state construction, and limited trajectory-level uncertainty calibration. This review outlines a research agenda for clinically robust prediction-first world models that integrate generative backbones (transformers, diffusion, VAE) with causal/mechanical foundation for safe decision support in healthcare.
