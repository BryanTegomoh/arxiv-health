Beyond Generative AI: World Models for Clinical Prediction, Counterfactuals, and Planning

Summary

This paper reviews World Models as an advanced AI paradigm for healthcare, advocating their superiority over traditional generative AI due to their ability to learn multimodal, temporally coherent, and action-conditioned representations that reflect the physical and causal structure of clinical care. It surveys their application across medical imaging, disease progression modeling, and robotic surgery, highlighting their potential for predictive dynamics, counterfactual evaluation, and planning.

Medical Relevance

This paper proposes World Models as a critical advancement for healthcare AI, addressing the need for predictive, reliable, and data-efficient systems that can provide robust clinical decision support, simulate disease progression, guide surgical procedures, and evaluate counterfactual treatment scenarios.

AI Health Application

The paper focuses on leveraging 'World Models' to provide predictive dynamics, multistep rollouts, counterfactual evaluation, and planning capabilities for AI in healthcare. This includes applications such as longitudinal tumor simulation for diagnostics, generative event forecasting for disease progression from EHRs, and action-conditioned guidance and control for robotic surgery, all aiming to enhance clinical decision support and ensure safety and reliability in healthcare.

Key Points

  • Traditional generative AI models lack the physical foundation and temporal/causal reasoning essential for reliable clinical decision support, showing diminishing returns for grounded clinical reasoning.
  • World Models learn predictive dynamics through multimodal, temporally coherent, and action-conditioned representations, enabling multistep rollouts, counterfactual evaluation, and planning in healthcare.
  • The review surveys World Model applications in medical imaging and diagnostics (e.g., tumor simulation, JEPA-style learning), disease progression modeling from EHRs (generative event forecasting), and robotic surgery/surgical planning (action-conditioned guidance).
  • A capability rubric is introduced: L1 (temporal prediction), L2 (action-conditioned prediction), L3 (counterfactual rollouts for decision support), and L4 (planning/control), revealing that most current systems only achieve L1-L2, with L3 and L4 being rare.
  • Identified gaps limiting clinical reliability include under-specified action spaces and safety constraints, weak interventional validation, incomplete multimodal state construction, and limited trajectory-level uncertainty calibration.
  • The paper outlines a research agenda for clinically robust, prediction-first World Models that integrate generative backbones (transformers, diffusion, VAE) with causal/mechanical foundations.
  • World Models offer an approach to developing AI that is predictive, reliable, data-efficient, and grounded in the physical and causal realities of healthcare, critical for safe decision support.

Methodology

This is a review paper that surveys recent work on World Models applied to healthcare systems. It categorizes and analyzes existing research across three major medical domains, introduces a novel capability rubric to evaluate system maturity, and identifies cross-cutting gaps in current implementations.

Key Findings

The review found that World Models show promise by learning predictive dynamics reflecting the physical and causal structure of care. While most current World Model systems in healthcare achieve basic temporal (L1) and action-conditioned (L2) prediction, capabilities for complex counterfactual rollouts (L3) and full planning/control (L4) are significantly less common. Critical limitations in clinical reliability include inadequately defined action spaces, weak validation of interventions, incomplete multimodal state representations, and insufficient uncertainty calibration.

Clinical Impact

World Models have the potential to significantly enhance clinical decision support by enabling accurate multistep predictions of disease progression, evaluating the outcomes of different treatment paths (counterfactuals), and providing action-conditioned guidance for interventions like robotic surgery. This can lead to more personalized, effective, and safer patient care by leveraging AI that understands the underlying causal dynamics of health.

Limitations

['Current World Models in healthcare exhibit under-specified action spaces and safety constraints, hindering their applicability in complex clinical environments.', 'There is weak interventional validation for many existing systems, making it difficult to assess their true impact on patient outcomes.', "Incomplete multimodal state construction limits the models' ability to fully capture the rich, diverse data relevant to a patient's condition.", 'Limited trajectory-level uncertainty calibration means models may not reliably quantify their confidence in predictions, which is crucial for high-stakes medical decisions.']

Future Directions

Future research should focus on developing clinically robust, prediction-first World Models that integrate advanced generative backbones (e.g., transformers, diffusion models, VAEs) with strong causal and mechanical foundations. A key agenda involves addressing the identified gaps, particularly in specifying action spaces, improving interventional validation, enhancing multimodal state construction, and developing robust uncertainty calibration mechanisms for safe and reliable decision support.

Abstract

Healthcare requires AI that is predictive, reliable, and data-efficient. However, recent generative models lack physical foundation and temporal reasoning required for clinical decision support. As scaling language models show diminishing returns for grounded clinical reasoning, world models are gaining traction because they learn multimodal, temporally coherent, and action-conditioned representations that reflect the physical and causal structure of care. This paper reviews World Models for healthcare systems that learn predictive dynamics to enable multistep rollouts, counterfactual evaluation and planning. We survey recent work across three domains: (i) medical imaging and diagnostics (e.g., longitudinal tumor simulation, projection-transition modeling, and Joint Embedding Predictive Architecture i.e., JEPA-style predictive representation learning), (ii) disease progression modeling from electronic health records (generative event forecasting at scale), and (iii) robotic surgery and surgical planning (action-conditioned guidance and control). We also introduce a capability rubric: L1 temporal prediction, L2 action-conditioned prediction, L3 counterfactual rollouts for decision support, and L4 planning/control. Most reviewed systems achieve L1--L2, with fewer instances of L3 and rare L4. We identify cross-cutting gaps that limit clinical reliability; under-specified action spaces and safety constraints, weak interventional validation, incomplete multimodal state construction, and limited trajectory-level uncertainty calibration. This review outlines a research agenda for clinically robust prediction-first world models that integrate generative backbones (transformers, diffusion, VAE) with causal/mechanical foundation for safe decision support in healthcare.
