H-Infinity Filter Enhanced CNN-LSTM for Arrhythmia Detection from Heart Sound Recordings

Summary

This paper introduces a novel CNN-H-Infinity-LSTM architecture for robust and generalized arrhythmia detection from heart sound recordings. By integrating trainable parameters inspired by the H-Infinity filter from control theory, the model significantly enhances its ability to handle noisy and small datasets, a common challenge in biomedical applications. The proposed model achieves superior performance on the PhysioNet CinC Challenge 2016 dataset, demonstrating 99.42% test accuracy and an F1 score of 98.85%, outperforming existing benchmarks.

Medical Relevance

Early and accurate detection of heart arrhythmia is crucial for preventing severe cardiac complications and improving patient outcomes. This automated, robust, and highly accurate method offers a significant advancement over subjective manual diagnoses, enabling more consistent and efficient cardiac screening.

AI Health Application

This research proposes a novel deep learning architecture (CNN-H-Infinity-LSTM) to automate the detection of heart arrhythmia from heart sound recordings. This AI application aims to improve the accuracy, consistency, and efficiency of arrhythmia diagnosis, potentially leading to earlier intervention and better patient outcomes.

Methodology

The methodology involves developing a novel deep learning architecture, CNN-H-Infinity-LSTM, which combines Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for spatial feature extraction and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) networks for capturing temporal dependencies in heart sound recordings. The core innovation is the integration of trainable parameters inspired by the H-Infinity filter from control theory. These parameters are designed to make the model more robust to noise and improve its generalization ability. The model was trained and evaluated on the PhysioNet CinC Challenge 2016 dataset.

Key Findings

The proposed CNN-H-Infinity-LSTM model achieved a test accuracy of 99.42% and an F1 score of 98.85% on the PhysioNet CinC Challenge 2016 dataset. These results indicate stable convergence and a significant outperformance of existing benchmark models for arrhythmia detection from heart sound recordings.

Clinical Impact

This research has the potential to significantly impact clinical practice by providing a highly accurate, consistent, and automated tool for arrhythmia detection. It could reduce diagnostic errors due to human subjectivity, improve efficiency in cardiac screening, and facilitate earlier intervention for cardiac patients, especially in settings with limited access to expert cardiologists or high patient volumes. The enhanced robustness against noise makes it more suitable for real-world clinical data.

Limitations

The extent of its robustness and generalization across highly diverse, real-world clinical datasets with varying noise profiles and recording qualities, or its performance on extremely small datasets, is not fully elaborated in the abstract.

Abstract

Early detection of heart arrhythmia can prevent severe future complications in cardiac patients. While manual diagnosis still remains the clinical standard, it relies heavily on visual interpretation and is inherently subjective. In recent years, deep learning has emerged as a powerful tool to automate arrhythmia detection, offering improved accuracy, consistency, and efficiency. Several variants of convolutional and recurrent neural network architectures have been widely explored to capture spatial and temporal patterns in physiological signals. However, despite these advancements, current models often struggle to generalize well in real-world scenarios, especially when dealing with small or noisy datasets, which are common challenges in biomedical applications. In this paper, a novel CNN-H-Infinity-LSTM architecture is proposed to identify arrhythmic heart signals from heart sound recordings. This architecture introduces trainable parameters inspired by the H-Infinity filter from control theory, enhancing robustness and generalization. Extensive experimentation on the PhysioNet CinC Challenge 2016 dataset, a public benchmark of heart audio recordings, demonstrates that the proposed model achieves stable convergence and outperforms existing benchmarks, with a test accuracy of 99.42% and an F1 score of 98.85%.
