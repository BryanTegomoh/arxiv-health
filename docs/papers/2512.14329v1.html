A data-physics hybrid generative model for patient-specific post-stroke motor rehabilitation using wearable sensor data

Summary

This paper introduces a data-physics hybrid generative model designed to reconstruct patient-specific neuromuscular control from a single level-ground walking trial, enabling dynamic prediction of task-conditioned locomotion for stroke survivors. By integrating wearable sensor data with a physics controller and deep reinforcement learning, the framework generates physically plausible gait simulations for challenging scenarios like slopes and stairs. In a pilot study, clinicians using these predictions to guide rehabilitation achieved significantly larger gains in Fugl-Meyer lower-extremity scores compared to control clinicians.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to post-stroke rehabilitation as it provides a personalized, dynamic, and predictive tool that can guide clinicians in tailoring task-specific gait training, potentially leading to safer, more efficient, and more effective recovery outcomes for stroke survivors.

The AI application is a data-physics hybrid generative model that reconstructs individual stroke survivors' neuromuscular control from wearable sensor data to predict task-conditioned locomotion across various rehabilitation scenarios (e.g., slopes, stairs). This model, leveraging deep reinforcement learning and generative adversarial imitation learning, aims to augment clinical decision-making by providing personalized gait simulations, guiding task selection and difficulty in post-stroke motor rehabilitation programs to improve patient outcomes.

Methodology

The study employs a data-physics hybrid generative framework. It takes input from a single 20m level-ground walking trial captured by wearable sensors. This data is used to inform a system comprising a proportional-derivative (PD) physics controller, a population-based Healthy Motion Atlas, and a goal-conditioned deep reinforcement learning (DRL) agent. The DRL agent is trained using behavior cloning (BC) and generative adversarial imitation learning (GAIL) to reconstruct patient-specific neuromuscular control and generate physically plausible, task-conditioned gait simulations for scenarios like slopes and stairs.

Key Findings

The personalized controllers preserved idiosyncratic gait patterns while significantly improving simulation fidelity: 4.73% for joint-angle fidelity and 12.10% for endpoint fidelity relative to a physics-only baseline. Training time was substantially reduced to 25.56% of the physics-only baseline. In a clinical pilot, patients whose rehabilitation was guided by the model's predictions achieved a mean increase of 6.0 points in Fugl-Meyer lower-extremity scores over 28 days, compared to 3.7 points in the control group.

Clinical Impact

This framework offers a significant clinical impact by enabling personalized and dynamic guidance for post-stroke gait rehabilitation. Clinicians can utilize the predicted task-conditioned locomotion to make informed decisions about appropriate task selection and difficulty levels, reducing the risk of unsafe activities and optimizing rehabilitation efficacy. This leads to more tailored and effective recovery strategies, as evidenced by improved Fugl-Meyer scores, ultimately enhancing functional outcomes for stroke survivors.

Abstract

Dynamic prediction of locomotor capacity after stroke is crucial for tailoring rehabilitation, yet current assessments provide only static impairment scores and do not indicate whether patients can safely perform specific tasks such as slope walking or stair climbing. Here, we develop a data-physics hybrid generative framework that reconstructs an individual stroke survivor's neuromuscular control from a single 20 m level-ground walking trial and predicts task-conditioned locomotion across rehabilitation scenarios. The system combines wearable-sensor kinematics, a proportional-derivative physics controller, a population Healthy Motion Atlas, and goal-conditioned deep reinforcement learning with behaviour cloning and generative adversarial imitation learning to generate physically plausible, patient-specific gait simulations for slopes and stairs. In 11 stroke survivors, the personalized controllers preserved idiosyncratic gait patterns while improving joint-angle and endpoint fidelity by 4.73% and 12.10%, respectively, and reducing training time to 25.56% relative to a physics-only baseline. In a multicentre pilot involving 21 inpatients, clinicians who used our locomotion predictions to guide task selection and difficulty obtained larger gains in Fugl-Meyer lower-extremity scores over 28 days of standard rehabilitation than control clinicians (mean change 6.0 versus 3.7 points). These findings indicate that our generative, task-predictive framework can augment clinical decision-making in post-stroke gait rehabilitation and provide a template for dynamically personalized motor recovery strategies.
