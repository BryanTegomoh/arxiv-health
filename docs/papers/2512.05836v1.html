Using Large Language Models to Create Personalized Networks From Therapy Sessions

Published: 2025-12-05
Authors: Clarissa W. Ong, Hiba Arnaout, Kate Sheehan, Estella Fox, Eugen Owtscharow, Iryna Gurevych

Summary

This paper presents an end-to-end pipeline leveraging Large Language Models (LLMs) to automatically generate personalized psychological networks from therapy session transcripts. The multi-step approach, which includes in-context learning for process identification and a two-step clustering method for network formation, was significantly preferred by experts for its clinical utility and interpretability over direct LLM prompting.

Medical Relevance

This research offers a scalable and automated solution for generating personalized psychological networks, critical for advancing precision psychotherapy by enabling data-driven case conceptualization and treatment planning, especially where intensive longitudinal data collection is impractical.

AI Health Application

The AI application involves using LLMs to process unstructured clinical data (therapy transcripts) to generate structured, personalized patient networks. These networks serve as a tool for clinicians to enhance case conceptualization, guide treatment planning, and potentially personalize interventions in psychotherapy, thereby functioning as an AI-driven clinical decision support system for mental health.

Key Points

  • Developed an LLM-based end-to-end pipeline to create personalized client networks from 77 therapy transcripts for case conceptualization and treatment planning.
  • Annotated 3364 psychological processes and their corresponding dimensions in therapy transcripts to facilitate model training and evaluation.
  • Applied in-context learning within the LLM to jointly identify psychological processes and their dimensions, achieving high performance even with limited training examples.
  • Introduced a novel two-step method to group identified processes into clinically meaningful clusters and generate explanation-augmented relationships between these clusters.
  • Expert evaluation demonstrated up to 90% preference for the multi-step network generation approach compared to direct prompting, citing superior clinical utility and interpretability.
  • The LLM-generated networks received favorable expert ratings, with scores for clinical relevance, novelty, and usefulness ranging from 72-75%.
  • The approach supports bottom-up case conceptualization from client utterances and facilitates the identification of latent psychological themes, offering a scalable solution for personalized treatment.

Methodology

The study involved annotating 3364 psychological processes and their dimensions from 77 therapy transcripts. Using these annotated data, in-context learning was applied to an LLM to jointly identify psychological processes and their dimensions. A subsequent two-step method was introduced to cluster these identified processes into clinically meaningful groups and generate explanation-augmented relationships between these clusters, forming the personalized networks. Expert evaluation compared these networks against those produced by direct LLM prompting for clinical utility and interpretability.

Key Findings

The LLM pipeline successfully identified psychological processes and dimensions with high performance, even with minimal training data. Experts strongly preferred the multi-step network generation approach (up to 90%) over direct prompting, rating it higher for clinical utility and interpretability. The resulting personalized networks were favorably rated by experts for their clinical relevance, novelty, and usefulness, achieving scores between 72-75%.

Clinical Impact

The pipeline provides a method for data-driven, bottom-up case conceptualization and identification of latent psychological themes directly from client therapy sessions. These personalized networks can be directly applied in clinical settings for treatment planning, supervision, and training, potentially enhancing the scalability and effectiveness of personalized psychotherapy by providing clinicians with richer insights into client dynamics.

Limitations

The abstract primarily highlights a limitation of existing network estimation methods (intensive longitudinal data) which this work aims to overcome, rather than specific limitations of the developed LLM approach. However, it implicitly suggests the need for empirical validation of treatment outcomes.

Future Directions

Future research should focus on empirically investigating whether these LLM-generated personalized networks demonstrably improve treatment outcomes when compared to other existing methods of treatment personalization, including statistically estimated networks.

Abstract

Recent advances in psychotherapy have focused on treatment personalization, such as by selecting treatment modules based on personalized networks. However, estimating personalized networks typically requires intensive longitudinal data, which is not always feasible. A solution to facilitate scalability of network-driven treatment personalization is leveraging LLMs. In this study, we present an end-to-end pipeline for automatically generating client networks from 77 therapy transcripts to support case conceptualization and treatment planning. We annotated 3364 psychological processes and their corresponding dimensions in therapy transcripts. Using these data, we applied in-context learning to jointly identify psychological processes and their dimensions. The method achieved high performance even with a few training examples. To organize the processes into networks, we introduced a two-step method that grouped them into clinically meaningful clusters. We then generated explanation-augmented relationships between clusters. Experts found that networks produced by our multi-step approach outperformed those built with direct prompting for clinical utility and interpretability, with up to 90% preferring our approach. In addition, the networks were rated favorably by experts, with scores for clinical relevance, novelty, and usefulness ranging from 72-75%. Our findings provide a proof of concept for using LLMs to create clinically relevant networks from therapy transcripts. Advantages of our approach include bottom-up case conceptualization from client utterances in therapy sessions and identification of latent themes. Networks generated from our pipeline may be used in clinical settings and supervision and training. Future research should examine whether these networks improve treatment outcomes relative to other methods of treatment personalization, including statistically estimated networks.
