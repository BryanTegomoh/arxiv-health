Summary

GRASP addresses mode collapse in text-to-image diffusion models for long-tail medical imaging by resolving gradient conflicts between frequent and rare classes. It achieves this by statically partitioning samples into clusters using external priors and then fine-tuning models with cluster-specific residual adapters. The method significantly improves generation quality and diversity for rare medical pathologies, boosting downstream classification performance for underrepresented conditions.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medical imaging by enabling robust synthetic data generation for rare and underrepresented pathologies, which can alleviate data scarcity and improve the training of diagnostic AI models. It addresses a critical bottleneck in deploying AI for conditions that lack extensive datasets.

AI Health Application

The AI application is to enhance the generation of synthetic medical images, particularly for rare diseases or conditions, using diffusion models. This synthetic data can then be used to train more robust and accurate AI models for medical diagnosis, screening, and analysis in clinical settings, thereby improving the performance of medical AI systems in long-tail scenarios where real data is scarce.

Key Points

  • Text-to-image diffusion models struggle with long-tail settings like medical imaging, leading to mode collapse and poor quality/diversity for rare pathologies.
  • The core problem is identified as gradient conflicts between frequent (head) and rare (tail) classes, which existing sampling/conditioning methods fail to resolve by only steering inference.
  • GRASP (Guided Residual Adapters with Sample-wise Partitioning) proposes to use external priors to statically partition samples into clusters, minimizing intra-group gradient clashes.
  • The method fine-tunes pre-trained models by injecting cluster-specific residual adapters directly into transformer feedforward layers, bypassing learned gating for stability and efficiency.

Methodology

GRASP utilizes external priors to statically partition samples into distinct clusters, designed to minimize intra-group gradient clashes. It then fine-unes pre-trained diffusion models by strategically injecting cluster-specific residual adapters into the transformer feedforward layers. This injection method specifically bypasses learned gating mechanisms to enhance stability and efficiency when adapting models to long-tail data distributions.

Key Findings

GRASP achieved superior FID and diversity metrics on the MIMIC-CXR-LT dataset, particularly for rare classes, significantly outperforming traditional fine-tuning and Mixture of Experts baselines. Critically, it led to considerable improvements in downstream classification performance for tail labels on the NIH-CXR-LT dataset. The method's generalizability was also confirmed through its successful application to ImageNet-LT.

Clinical Impact

GRASP has the potential to significantly impact clinical practice by enabling the generation of high-quality synthetic data for rare medical conditions, which are often severely underrepresented in real-world datasets. This can lead to the development of more robust, accurate, and equitable AI models for the diagnosis and prognosis of rare diseases, ultimately improving patient outcomes through earlier detection and better-informed treatment strategies.

Abstract

Recent advances in text-to-image diffusion models enable high-fidelity generation across diverse prompts. However, these models falter in long-tail settings, such as medical imaging, where rare pathologies comprise a small fraction of data. This results in mode collapse: tail-class outputs lack quality and diversity, undermining the goal of synthetic data augmentation for underrepresented conditions. We pinpoint gradient conflicts between frequent head and rare tail classes as the primary culprit, a factor unaddressed by existing sampling or conditioning methods that mainly steer inference without altering the learned distribution. To resolve this, we propose GRASP: Guided Residual Adapters with Sample-wise Partitioning. GRASP uses external priors to statically partition samples into clusters that minimize intra-group gradient clashes. It then fine-tunes pre-trained models by injecting cluster-specific residual adapters into transformer feedforward layers, bypassing learned gating for stability and efficiency. On the long-tail MIMIC-CXR-LT dataset, GRASP yields superior FID and diversity metrics, especially for rare classes, outperforming baselines like vanilla fine-tuning and Mixture of Experts variants. Downstream classification on NIH-CXR-LT improves considerably for tail labels. Generalization to ImageNet-LT confirms broad applicability. Our method is lightweight, scalable, and readily integrates with diffusion pipelines.
