Coupling the circadian rhythms of population movement and the immune system in infectious disease modeling

Published: 2019-10-12
Authors: Zhanwei Du, Petter Holme

Summary

This paper investigates how the coupled circadian rhythms of human population movement and the immune system influence infectious disease dynamics. Using a stochastic metapopulation model informed by hourly mobility data, the study found that these circadian rhythms generally constrain the pace and extent of disease emergence. The research suggests that strategically altering commuting times could be a public health intervention to mitigate outbreaks.

Medical Relevance

This research reveals a crucial, often overlooked, epidemiological factor: the temporal synchronization of host immune susceptibility and human interaction patterns. Understanding this coupling is vital for predicting disease spread more accurately and for designing chronologically-informed public health interventions to reduce infection rates and manage outbreaks effectively.

Methodology

The study employed a stochastic metapopulation model, a computational framework that simulates disease spread across interconnected subpopulations. This model was empirically informed by granular, hourly human mobility data collected from two medium-sized cities in China. The core investigation involved assessing how variations in the synchronization or desynchronization between the circadian clocks governing population movement and immune system function influence epidemic risk.

Key Findings

The primary findings indicate that the coupled circadian rhythms of human mobility and the immune system generally act to constrain both the pace and the overall extent of infectious disease emergence. Specific epidemiological measures, including 'strength' (a proxy for disease intensity), 'outward transmission risk', and 'introduction risk', were found to be highly intercorrelated. For instance, in Yushu City simulations (with $R_0=1.5$ and no circadian effect), outward transmission risk correlated with introduction risk at a Pearson's coefficient of 0.83, and both negatively correlated with 'strength' at -0.85 and -0.75 respectively (all with $p<0.05$).

Clinical Impact

This research suggests a novel avenue for public health intervention: leveraging chronobiology to mitigate infectious disease spread. By understanding how the timing of human interactions aligns with periods of varying immune susceptibility, public health officials could design non-pharmaceutical interventions. Specifically, strategically shifting population-wide activities, such as daily commuting times, could potentially disrupt transmission chains and reduce overall exposure during peak susceptibility periods, thereby mitigating outbreak emergence and severity.

Abstract

The dynamics of infectious diseases propagating in populations depends both on human interaction patterns, the contagion process and the pathogenesis within hosts. The immune system follows a circadian rhythm and, consequently, the chance of getting infected varies with the time of day an individual is exposed to the pathogen. The movement and interaction of people also follow 24-hour cycles, which couples these two phenomena. We use a stochastic metapopulation model informed by hourly mobility data for two medium-sized Chinese cities. By this setup, we investigate how the epidemic risk depends on the difference of the clocks governing the population movement and the immune systems. In most of the scenarios we test, we observe circadian rhythms would constrain the pace and extent of disease emergence. The three measures (strength, outward transmission risk and introduction risk) are highly correlated with each other. For example of the Yushu City, outward transmission risk and introduction risk are correlated with a Pearson's correlation coefficient of 0.83, and the risks correlate to strength with coefficients of $-0.85$ and $-0.75$, respectively (all have $p<0.05$), in simulations with no circadian effect and $R_0=1.5$. The relation between the circadian rhythms of the immune system and daily routines in human mobility can affect the pace and extent of infectious disease spreading. Shifting commuting times could mitigate the emergence of outbreaks.
