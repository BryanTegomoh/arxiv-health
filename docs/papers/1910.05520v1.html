Coupling the circadian rhythms of population movement and the immune system in infectious disease modeling

Summary

This research introduces a stochastic metapopulation model to investigate how the coupled circadian rhythms of human mobility and the immune system influence infectious disease dynamics. The study finds that these synchronized circadian patterns generally constrain the pace and extent of disease emergence, suggesting that strategic adjustments to daily routines, such as shifting commuting times, could mitigate outbreaks.

Medical Relevance

This research reveals a crucial interaction between human biological clocks and behavioral patterns in disease transmission, offering a novel perspective for epidemic control. Understanding these circadian couplings can lead to more precise forecasting models and innovative public health strategies, potentially reducing infection rates and improving population health outcomes.

  • The immune system's circadian rhythm affects an individual's susceptibility to infection based on the time of day of exposure.
  • Human movement and interaction patterns also follow 24-hour cycles, creating a coupled system with the immune response.
  • A stochastic metapopulation model, informed by hourly mobility data from two medium-sized Chinese cities, was used to simulate disease spread.
  • The study specifically examined how epidemic risk varies with the phase difference between the circadian clocks governing population movement and immune function.

Methodology

The study utilized a stochastic metapopulation model to simulate infectious disease propagation. This model was informed by real-world hourly human mobility data collected from two medium-sized Chinese cities. The core analysis involved investigating how epidemic risk changes depending on the phase difference between the assumed circadian rhythms of the immune system (modulating susceptibility) and observed population movement (modulating exposure).

Key Findings

The central finding is that the integrated circadian rhythms of human mobility and the immune system generally constrain the pace and extent of infectious disease emergence. The study observed strong correlations between epidemic risk measures (outward transmission risk and introduction risk) and an overarching 'strength' measure (likely representing the robustness of circadian effects). Specifically, stronger circadian effects were negatively correlated with both outward transmission risk and introduction risk, with Pearson's correlation coefficients of -0.85 and -0.75 respectively (p<0.05), in simulations for Yushu City with a baseline R0 of 1.5.

Clinical Impact

The findings suggest a promising avenue for public health interventions: by strategically altering population-level daily routines, such as implementing staggered or shifted commuting times, it may be possible to mitigate the risk and scope of infectious disease outbreaks. This offers a novel, non-pharmacological approach to epidemic control, potentially by desynchronizing peak human interaction times from periods of heightened immune susceptibility.

Abstract

The dynamics of infectious diseases propagating in populations depends both on human interaction patterns, the contagion process and the pathogenesis within hosts. The immune system follows a circadian rhythm and, consequently, the chance of getting infected varies with the time of day an individual is exposed to the pathogen. The movement and interaction of people also follow 24-hour cycles, which couples these two phenomena. We use a stochastic metapopulation model informed by hourly mobility data for two medium-sized Chinese cities. By this setup, we investigate how the epidemic risk depends on the difference of the clocks governing the population movement and the immune systems. In most of the scenarios we test, we observe circadian rhythms would constrain the pace and extent of disease emergence. The three measures (strength, outward transmission risk and introduction risk) are highly correlated with each other. For example of the Yushu City, outward transmission risk and introduction risk are correlated with a Pearson's correlation coefficient of 0.83, and the risks correlate to strength with coefficients of $-0.85$ and $-0.75$, respectively (all have $p<0.05$), in simulations with no circadian effect and $R_0=1.5$. The relation between the circadian rhythms of the immune system and daily routines in human mobility can affect the pace and extent of infectious disease spreading. Shifting commuting times could mitigate the emergence of outbreaks.
