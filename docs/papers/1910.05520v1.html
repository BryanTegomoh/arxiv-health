Coupling the circadian rhythms of population movement and the immune system in infectious disease modeling

arXiv ID: 1910.05520v1
Published: 2019-10-12
Authors: Zhanwei Du, Petter Holme
Categories: q-bio.PE
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a novel approach to infectious disease modeling by integrating the circadian rhythms of both the human immune system and daily population movement patterns. It demonstrates that these coupled biological and behavioral cycles significantly influence epidemic risk, generally constraining disease emergence, and suggests that manipulating these temporal dynamics could be a strategy for outbreak mitigation.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a critical new dimension to infectious disease epidemiology, revealing that the temporal alignment of an individual's physiological state (immune rhythm) with their behavioral patterns (mobility) profoundly impacts disease transmission. This understanding offers novel avenues for public health interventions, moving beyond static risk assessments to dynamic, chronobiologically informed strategies.

AI Health Application

Not explicitly an AI application in the modern machine learning sense. The paper uses a 'stochastic metapopulation model' which is a computational simulation technique for disease dynamics, rather than AI methodologies like machine learning or deep learning applied to health data.

Key Points

  • The model couples the circadian rhythm of the human immune system (affecting infection susceptibility) with 24-hour cycles of human movement and interaction.
  • A stochastic metapopulation model was developed, informed by hourly mobility data from two medium-sized Chinese cities.
  • The study investigates how epidemic risk depends on the phase difference between the clocks governing population movement and immune system function.
  • Circadian rhythms were found to constrain the pace and extent of disease emergence in most simulated scenarios.
  • Measures such as 'strength' (overall epidemic potential), 'outward transmission risk,' and 'introduction risk' were highly correlated, for example, Pearson's correlation coefficients of 0.83 for outward and introduction risk, and -0.85/-0.75 for correlating these risks to 'strength' in baseline simulations ($R_0=1.5$, no circadian effect), all with $p<0.05$.
  • The precise relationship between immune system circadian rhythms and daily human mobility routines is a critical determinant of infectious disease spread dynamics.
  • A practical implication is that shifting commuting times or other synchronized daily routines could serve as a non-pharmaceutical intervention to mitigate the emergence of infectious disease outbreaks.

Methodology

The authors utilized a stochastic metapopulation model, specifically designed to couple two circadian phenomena: the rhythm of the human immune system (influencing susceptibility to infection) and the 24-hour patterns of human movement and interaction. The model was informed by real-world hourly mobility data collected from two medium-sized cities in China. Simulations investigated the impact of varying the phase difference between these two internal and external circadian clocks on measures of epidemic risk.

Key Findings

The study found that the integrated circadian rhythms of population movement and the immune system significantly influence the dynamics of infectious disease spread. In the majority of simulated scenarios, these coupled rhythms constrained both the pace and the ultimate extent of disease emergence. Furthermore, key epidemic metrics, including 'strength' (likely referring to the overall epidemic potential), 'outward transmission risk,' and 'introduction risk,' demonstrated high correlations. For instance, in baseline simulations (no circadian effect, $R_0=1.5$), outward and introduction risks showed a Pearson's correlation of 0.83, while both correlated with 'strength' at -0.85 and -0.75, respectively (all $p<0.05$). This indicates that the specific timing relationship between an individual's immune cycle and their daily mobility routines is crucial for determining the overall spread of infectious diseases.

Clinical Impact

This research suggests a potential paradigm shift in public health interventions, moving towards 'chrono-epidemiological' strategies. By understanding how the timing of daily routines interacts with biological rhythms, interventions could be designed to strategically modify human behavior. Specifically, the findings imply that adjusting population-level activity timings, such as shifting commuting hours, could serve as an effective non-pharmaceutical intervention to mitigate the emergence of infectious disease outbreaks or slow their progression, thereby reducing overall morbidity and mortality.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stating future research directions, the paper implies that further investigation into the practical implementation and optimization of 'shifting commuting times' or other human activity schedules could be a valuable area. This would involve studying the real-world feasibility, public acceptance, and precise epidemiological impact of such chronobiologically informed interventions.

Abstract

The dynamics of infectious diseases propagating in populations depends both on human interaction patterns, the contagion process and the pathogenesis within hosts. The immune system follows a circadian rhythm and, consequently, the chance of getting infected varies with the time of day an individual is exposed to the pathogen. The movement and interaction of people also follow 24-hour cycles, which couples these two phenomena. We use a stochastic metapopulation model informed by hourly mobility data for two medium-sized Chinese cities. By this setup, we investigate how the epidemic risk depends on the difference of the clocks governing the population movement and the immune systems. In most of the scenarios we test, we observe circadian rhythms would constrain the pace and extent of disease emergence. The three measures (strength, outward transmission risk and introduction risk) are highly correlated with each other. For example of the Yushu City, outward transmission risk and introduction risk are correlated with a Pearson's correlation coefficient of 0.83, and the risks correlate to strength with coefficients of $-0.85$ and $-0.75$, respectively (all have $p<0.05$), in simulations with no circadian effect and $R_0=1.5$. The relation between the circadian rhythms of the immune system and daily routines in human mobility can affect the pace and extent of infectious disease spreading. Shifting commuting times could mitigate the emergence of outbreaks.
