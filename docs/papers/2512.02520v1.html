On the Problem of Consistent Anomalies in Zero-Shot Anomaly Detection

arXiv ID: 2512.02520v1
Published: 2025-12-02
Authors: Tai Le-Gia
Categories: cs.CV, stat.ML
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00

Summary

This dissertation addresses the 'consistent anomalies' problem in zero-shot anomaly classification and segmentation (AC/AS), a failure mode where recurring similar anomalies bias detection methods. It introduces CoDeGraph, a graph-based framework leveraging novel phenomena (similarity scaling, neighbor-burnout) to filter these anomalies. The work extends this capability to 3D medical imaging via a training-free volumetric tokenization strategy and bridges batch-based with text-based zero-shot methods, providing both theoretical understanding and practical solutions.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medical imaging because it offers a truly zero-shot approach for detecting and segmenting anomalies in 3D MRI data, critical for diagnostics where annotated anomaly datasets are often scarce or non-existent.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the development of zero-shot anomaly detection and segmentation tools for medical images (especially 3D MRI data). This enables automated identification of abnormalities (e.g., tumors, lesions, anatomical variations) without requiring large, pre-labeled datasets for training. This can significantly aid radiologists and clinicians in diagnosis, improve efficiency, and potentially allow for earlier detection of diseases, particularly in scenarios with rare conditions or limited annotated data.

Key Points

  • Formalizes the 'consistent anomalies' problem, identifying it as a systematic bias introduced by recurring similar anomalies in distance-based zero-shot detection.
  • Discovers and describes two key phenomena, 'similarity scaling' and 'neighbor-burnout', explaining how relationships among normal Vision Transformer (ViT) patch representations are altered by consistent anomalies.
  • Introduces CoDeGraph, a novel graph-based framework that employs multi-stage graph construction, community detection, and structured refinement to effectively filter the influence of consistent anomalies.
  • Proposes a training-free and computationally efficient volumetric tokenization strategy for 3D MRI data, enabling genuinely zero-shot 3D anomaly detection and segmentation without any 3D training samples.
  • Demonstrates the capability to bridge batch-based and text-based zero-shot methods by utilizing CoDeGraph-derived pseudo-masks to supervise prompt-driven vision-language models.
  • Provides a comprehensive solution for zero-shot AC/AS, encompassing theoretical understanding of bias and practical algorithmic designs for robust anomaly detection.

Methodology

The methodology involves analyzing statistical and geometric behaviors of ViT patch representations to identify anomaly-induced biases. It then develops CoDeGraph, a graph-based framework for anomaly filtering using multi-stage graph construction, community detection, and structured refinement. This framework is extended to 3D medical imaging through a training-free, computationally efficient volumetric tokenization strategy for MRI data. Finally, CoDeGraph-derived pseudo-masks are used to supervise prompt-driven vision-language models.

Key Findings

The study found that 'consistent anomalies' systematically bias distance-based anomaly detection methods. It identified 'similarity scaling' and 'neighbor-burnout' as key phenomena explaining this bias in ViT features. CoDeGraph was shown to effectively suppress these consistent anomalies. Furthermore, genuinely zero-shot 3D volumetric anomaly segmentation was achieved in MRI without any 3D training data, and CoDeGraph can bridge batch-based and text-based zero-shot methods.

Clinical Impact

This research has the potential to significantly improve clinical diagnostics by enabling earlier and more accurate detection of novel or rare anomalies in medical scans, especially in 3D MRI, without requiring labor-intensive expert annotations. It could reduce diagnostic inconsistencies by mitigating biases from recurring subtle anomalies, ultimately enhancing the efficiency and reliability of automated anomaly screening and assisting radiologists.

Abstract

Zero-shot anomaly classification and segmentation (AC/AS) aim to detect anomalous samples and regions without any training data, a capability increasingly crucial in industrial inspection and medical imaging. This dissertation aims to investigate the core challenges of zero-shot AC/AS and presents principled solutions rooted in theory and algorithmic design. We first formalize the problem of consistent anomalies, a failure mode in which recurring similar anomalies systematically bias distance-based methods. By analyzing the statistical and geometric behavior of patch representations from pre-trained Vision Transformers, we identify two key phenomena - similarity scaling and neighbor-burnout - that describe how relationships among normal patches change with and without consistent anomalies in settings characterized by highly similar objects. We then introduce CoDeGraph, a graph-based framework for filtering consistent anomalies built on the similarity scaling and neighbor-burnout phenomena. Through multi-stage graph construction, community detection, and structured refinement, CoDeGraph effectively suppresses the influence of consistent anomalies. Next, we extend this framework to 3D medical imaging by proposing a training-free, computationally efficient volumetric tokenization strategy for MRI data. This enables a genuinely zero-shot 3D anomaly detection pipeline and shows that volumetric anomaly segmentation is achievable without any 3D training samples. Finally, we bridge batch-based and text-based zero-shot methods by demonstrating that CoDeGraph-derived pseudo-masks can supervise prompt-driven vision-language models. Together, this dissertation provides theoretical understanding and practical solutions for the zero-shot AC/AS problem.
