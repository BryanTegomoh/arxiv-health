Patient-Centered Summarization Framework for AI Clinical Summarization: A Mixed-Methods Design

Summary

This paper introduces Patient-Centered Summaries (PCS) as a new standard for AI clinical summarization, aiming to capture patient values beyond biological data. Through a mixed-methods approach involving patient and clinician input, a framework was developed and used to evaluate open-source Large Language Models (LLMs), revealing that while LLMs show promise in fluency, they currently fall short of human experts in correctness and patient-centeredness.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing patient-centered care by ensuring AI-generated clinical summaries capture the holistic context of a patient, thereby facilitating more empathetic, personalized, and shared decision-making in clinical practice.

AI Health Application

The primary AI health application is the development and evaluation of an AI-powered framework (using Large Language Models) for generating patient-centered clinical summaries from patient-clinician conversations. This aims to enhance medical record-keeping, improve communication, and support patient-centered care by ensuring patient preferences and values are accurately captured in clinical documentation.

Key Points

  • Proposes Patient-Centered Summaries (PCS) as a novel standard for AI clinical summarization to include patient preferences, values, wishes, and concerns, moving beyond biology-focused summaries.
  • Utilized a mixed-methods design, including Patient and Public Involvement (PPI) groups (10 patients, 8 clinicians) in the UK through semi-structured interviews to define PCS content and structure.
  • Annotation guidelines derived from PPI findings were used by eight clinicians to create gold-standard PCS from 88 atrial fibrillation consultations.
  • Evaluated five open-source LLMs (Llama-3.2-3B, Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-8B, Gemma-3-4B, Qwen3-8B) on 72 consultations using both zero-shot and few-shot prompting.
  • Patients emphasized inclusion of lifestyle routines, social support, recent stressors, and care values, while clinicians prioritized concise functional, psychosocial, and emotional context.
  • LLM performance, measured by ROUGE-L and BERTScore, showed Llama-3.1-8B achieving the best few-shot performance (ROUGE-L 0.206, BERTScore 0.683) but still low compared to human expectations.
  • Qualitative evaluation indicated that LLMs achieved similar completeness and fluency to human experts, but significantly lacked in correctness and patient-centeredness when compared to human-generated PCS.

Methodology

The study employed a mixed-methods design. Initially, qualitative data was gathered via semi-structured interviews with two Patient and Public Involvement groups (10 patients, 8 clinicians) to define the scope and structure of Patient-Centered Summaries (PCS). These findings informed the creation of detailed annotation guidelines. Subsequently, eight clinicians used these guidelines to generate gold-standard PCS from 88 atrial fibrillation consultation transcripts. Sixteen consultations were used for prompt refinement. Finally, five open-source LLMs were evaluated (Llama-3.2-3B, Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-8B, Gemma-3-4B, Qwen3-8B) on 72 consultations using zero-shot and few-shot prompting. Performance was assessed quantitatively using ROUGE-L and BERTScore, and qualitatively for completeness, fluency, correctness, and patient-centeredness.

Key Findings

Patient and Public Involvement groups identified crucial elements for PCS, with patients highlighting lifestyle routines, social support, recent stressors, and care values, and clinicians emphasizing concise functional, psychosocial, and emotional context. In LLM evaluation, Mistral-8B (ROUGE-L 0.189) and Llama-3.1-8B (BERTScore 0.673) showed the best zero-shot performance, while Llama-3.1-8B achieved the highest few-shot results (ROUGE-L 0.206, BERTScore 0.683). However, a significant gap remains: while LLMs matched human experts in completeness and fluency, their summaries were inferior in correctness and patient-centeredness, with human-generated PCS consistently favored in these critical aspects.

Clinical Impact

Developing AI systems capable of generating PCS has the potential to transform clinical documentation by shifting focus from purely biomedical data to a more holistic patient narrative. This can enhance shared decision-making, improve care coordination, foster stronger patient-clinician relationships, and ultimately lead to care plans better aligned with individual patient needs and values, thereby contributing to better health outcomes.

Limitations

The primary limitation identified is that current open-source LLMs, despite showing promise in fluency and completeness, consistently underperform human experts in the crucial areas of correctness and patient-centeredness when generating Patient-Centered Summaries.

Future Directions

Future research should focus on improving LLM performance in accurately capturing and synthesizing patient-centered information, particularly enhancing their correctness and patient-centeredness. This may involve exploring advanced prompt engineering techniques, fine-tuning LLMs on larger and more diverse PCS datasets, developing novel architectures, or incorporating explicit reasoning capabilities to better interpret and integrate nuanced patient values and context.

Abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly demonstrating the potential to reach human-level performance in generating clinical summaries from patient-clinician conversations. However, these summaries often focus on patients' biology rather than their preferences, values, wishes, and concerns. To achieve patient-centered care, we propose a new standard for Artificial Intelligence (AI) clinical summarization tasks: Patient-Centered Summaries (PCS). Our objective was to develop a framework to generate PCS that capture patient values and ensure clinical utility and to assess whether current open-source LLMs can achieve human-level performance in this task. We used a mixed-methods process. Two Patient and Public Involvement groups (10 patients and 8 clinicians) in the United Kingdom participated in semi-structured interviews exploring what personal and contextual information should be included in clinical summaries and how it should be structured for clinical use. Findings informed annotation guidelines used by eight clinicians to create gold-standard PCS from 88 atrial fibrillation consultations. Sixteen consultations were used to refine a prompt aligned with the guidelines. Five open-source LLMs (Llama-3.2-3B, Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-8B, Gemma-3-4B, and Qwen3-8B) generated summaries for 72 consultations using zero-shot and few-shot prompting, evaluated with ROUGE-L, BERTScore, and qualitative metrics. Patients emphasized lifestyle routines, social support, recent stressors, and care values. Clinicians sought concise functional, psychosocial, and emotional context. The best zero-shot performance was achieved by Mistral-8B (ROUGE-L 0.189) and Llama-3.1-8B (BERTScore 0.673); the best few-shot by Llama-3.1-8B (ROUGE-L 0.206, BERTScore 0.683). Completeness and fluency were similar between experts and models, while correctness and patient-centeredness favored human PCS.
