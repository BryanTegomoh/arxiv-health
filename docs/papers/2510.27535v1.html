Patient-Centered Summarization Framework for AI Clinical Summarization: A Mixed-Methods Design

Summary

This study introduces a novel framework for Patient-Centered Summaries (PCS) generated by AI from patient-clinician conversations, aiming to capture patient preferences, values, and concerns beyond mere biology. Using a mixed-methods approach involving patient and clinician input, the research developed annotation guidelines and evaluated five open-source Large Language Models. While LLMs showed promise in completeness and fluency, human-generated PCS significantly outperformed them in correctness and true patient-centeredness.

Medical Relevance

This research is critical for advancing AI in healthcare by shifting the focus of clinical summarization from purely biomedical facts to holistic patient narratives, thereby fostering truly patient-centered care, improving shared decision-making, and enhancing the therapeutic relationship.

AI Health Application

The AI application described is the development and evaluation of Large Language Models (LLMs) to automatically generate Patient-Centered Summaries (PCS) from patient-clinician conversations. These summaries are designed to capture not only biological information but also patient preferences, values, wishes, and concerns, thereby enhancing medical record-keeping, improving clinical utility for healthcare professionals, and promoting patient-centered care.

Key Points

  • A new standard, Patient-Centered Summaries (PCS), is proposed for AI clinical summarization tasks, focusing on patient values, preferences, wishes, and concerns, rather than solely biological information.
  • A mixed-methods design involved semi-structured interviews with 18 Patient and Public Involvement (PPI) participants (10 patients, 8 clinicians) in the UK to inform the PCS framework and annotation guidelines.
  • Patient input emphasized the inclusion of lifestyle routines, social support, recent stressors, and care values; clinicians sought concise functional, psychosocial, and emotional context in summaries.
  • Eight expert clinicians created gold-standard PCS from 88 atrial fibrillation consultations, which were used to refine prompts and evaluate LLM performance.
  • Five open-source LLMs (Llama-3.2-3B, Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-8B, Gemma-3-4B, Qwen3-8B) were evaluated using zero-shot and few-shot prompting on 72 consultations.
  • Evaluation metrics included ROUGE-L and BERTScore for automated assessment, alongside qualitative measures covering completeness, fluency, correctness, and patient-centeredness.
  • LLM performance: Best zero-shot was Mistral-8B (ROUGE-L 0.189) and Llama-3.1-8B (BERTScore 0.673); best few-shot was Llama-3.1-8B (ROUGE-L 0.206, BERTScore 0.683).
  • Qualitative analysis revealed that while LLMs achieved similar completeness and fluency to human experts, they lagged significantly in correctness and, critically, in capturing true patient-centeredness.

Methodology

A mixed-methods design commenced with semi-structured interviews of Patient and Public Involvement (PPI) groups (10 patients, 8 clinicians) to define the content and structure of Patient-Centered Summaries (PCS). These insights informed annotation guidelines, which were then used by eight expert clinicians to create gold-standard PCS from 88 atrial fibrillation consultations. Subsequently, five open-source LLMs were tested on 72 consultations using zero-shot and few-shot prompting, with generated summaries evaluated against gold standards using ROUGE-L, BERTScore, and qualitative metrics for completeness, fluency, correctness, and patient-centeredness.

Key Findings

Patient and clinician input highlighted distinct requirements for PCS content and structure, emphasizing psychosocial and emotional context crucial for patient-centered care. While LLMs demonstrated reasonable completeness and fluency (best few-shot ROUGE-L 0.206, BERTScore 0.683 by Llama-3.1-8B), they did not achieve human-level performance in correctness and, critically, in capturing the nuanced patient-centered information.

Clinical Impact

The proposed PCS framework offers a crucial blueprint for developing AI tools that genuinely support patient-centered care in clinical settings. It highlights that current open-source LLMs, while promising, require significant advancement to accurately and consistently extract and synthesize complex patient values, preferences, and contextual information essential for comprehensive clinical decision-making and shared care planning. This research guides future AI development towards more empathetic and holistically beneficial clinical support systems.

Limitations

The abstract implicitly indicates that current open-source LLMs do not yet achieve human-level performance in correctness and patient-centeredness for this specific task. The study's focus on atrial fibrillation and a limited set of open-source LLMs suggests potential generalizability limitations to other medical conditions or more advanced proprietary models.

Future Directions

Implicitly, future research should focus on enhancing LLM architectures and fine-tuning strategies to better capture nuanced patient values and context, exploring larger and more diverse clinical datasets across various medical domains, and developing more robust and validated evaluation metrics specifically for patient-centeredness in AI-generated summaries.

Abstract

Large Language Models (LLMs) are increasingly demonstrating the potential to reach human-level performance in generating clinical summaries from patient-clinician conversations. However, these summaries often focus on patients' biology rather than their preferences, values, wishes, and concerns. To achieve patient-centered care, we propose a new standard for Artificial Intelligence (AI) clinical summarization tasks: Patient-Centered Summaries (PCS). Our objective was to develop a framework to generate PCS that capture patient values and ensure clinical utility and to assess whether current open-source LLMs can achieve human-level performance in this task. We used a mixed-methods process. Two Patient and Public Involvement groups (10 patients and 8 clinicians) in the United Kingdom participated in semi-structured interviews exploring what personal and contextual information should be included in clinical summaries and how it should be structured for clinical use. Findings informed annotation guidelines used by eight clinicians to create gold-standard PCS from 88 atrial fibrillation consultations. Sixteen consultations were used to refine a prompt aligned with the guidelines. Five open-source LLMs (Llama-3.2-3B, Llama-3.1-8B, Mistral-8B, Gemma-3-4B, and Qwen3-8B) generated summaries for 72 consultations using zero-shot and few-shot prompting, evaluated with ROUGE-L, BERTScore, and qualitative metrics. Patients emphasized lifestyle routines, social support, recent stressors, and care values. Clinicians sought concise functional, psychosocial, and emotional context. The best zero-shot performance was achieved by Mistral-8B (ROUGE-L 0.189) and Llama-3.1-8B (BERTScore 0.673); the best few-shot by Llama-3.1-8B (ROUGE-L 0.206, BERTScore 0.683). Completeness and fluency were similar between experts and models, while correctness and patient-centeredness favored human PCS.
