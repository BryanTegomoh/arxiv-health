Molecular Infectious Disease Epidemiology: Survival Analysis and Algorithms Linking Phylogenies to Transmission Trees

Summary

This paper introduces a robust statistical framework for molecular infectious disease epidemiology, integrating pathogen phylogenies with traditional epidemiologic data using a survival analysis approach. It establishes a one-to-one correspondence between phylogenetic host assignments and transmission trees, enabling more efficient and precise estimation of key transmission parameters. The methodology underscores the critical value of data from individuals who escape infection for understanding disease transmission.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a more statistically rigorous and precise method for understanding infectious disease transmission dynamics, crucial for informing public health interventions, resource allocation, and developing effective disease control and prevention strategies during outbreaks. It improves the evidence base for epidemiological decision-making.

They could be incorporated into or enhanced by AI systems for real-time outbreak detection, prediction of transmission dynamics, and optimization of public health interventions. Methodology

The authors integrate a survival analysis framework with phylogenetic data and conventional epidemiologic data (person, place, time). They mathematically prove a one-to-one correspondence between host assignments to interior nodes of a pathogen phylogeny and epidemiologically consistent transmission trees. Algorithms are developed to enumerate these trees and compute likelihoods that combine both data types. The methodology's efficiency is validated through simulation studies, and it is applied to real-world Foot-and-Mouth Disease virus outbreak data.

Key Findings

A fundamental one-to-one correspondence exists between possible assignments of hosts to interior phylogenetic nodes and transmission trees consistent with combined genomic and epidemiologic data. The developed methods allow for calculating likelihoods that rigorously integrate these data, leading to more efficient and precise estimates of infectiousness hazard ratios and baseline hazards. The study also conclusively demonstrates the critical importance of including data on individuals who escape infection for robust parameter estimation.

Abstract

Recent work has attempted to use whole-genome sequence data from pathogens to reconstruct the transmission trees linking infectors and infectees in outbreaks. However, transmission trees from one outbreak do not generalize to future outbreaks. Reconstruction of transmission trees is most useful to public health if it leads to generalizable scientific insights about disease transmission. In a survival analysis framework, estimation of transmission parameters is based on sums or averages over the possible transmission trees. A phylogeny can increase the precision of these estimates by providing partial information about who infected whom. The leaves of the phylogeny represent sampled pathogens, which have known hosts. The interior nodes represent common ancestors of sampled pathogens, which have unknown hosts. Starting from assumptions about disease biology and epidemiologic study design, we prove that there is a one-to-one correspondence between the possible assignments of interior node hosts and the transmission trees simultaneously consistent with the phylogeny and the epidemiologic data on person, place, and time. We develop algorithms to enumerate these transmission trees and show these can be used to calculate likelihoods that incorporate both epidemiologic data and a phylogeny. A simulation study confirms that this leads to more efficient estimates of hazard ratios for infectiousness and baseline hazards of infectious contact, and we use these methods to analyze data from a foot-and-mouth disease virus outbreak in the United Kingdom in 2001. These results demonstrate the importance of data on individuals who escape infection, which is often overlooked. The combination of survival analysis and algorithms linking phylogenies to transmission trees is a rigorous but flexible statistical foundation for molecular infectious disease epidemiology.
