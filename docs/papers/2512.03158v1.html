Contrastive Deep Learning for Variant Detection in Wastewater Genomic Sequencing

arXiv ID: 2512.03158v1
Published: 2025-12-02
Authors: Adele Chinda, Richmond Azumah, Hemanth Demakethepalli Venkateswara
Categories: cs.LG, q-bio.GN

Summary

This paper introduces an unsupervised deep learning framework for detecting viral variants from wastewater genomic sequencing data, leveraging a Vector-Quantized Variational Autoencoder (VQ-VAE) with masked reconstruction pretraining and contrastive learning. The approach effectively addresses challenges like high sequencing noise and lack of labeled data by learning discrete genomic patterns from k-mer tokenized sequences without requiring reference genomes. Evaluated on SARS-CoV-2 data, the framework achieves high token-level accuracy and significantly improves variant discrimination through contrastive fine-tuning, providing a scalable solution for public health genomic surveillance.

Medical Relevance

This research significantly enhances population-level viral monitoring by providing a robust, scalable, and unsupervised method for detecting viral variants in wastewater. This is crucial for early warning systems for emerging pathogens and informed public health responses, enabling rapid identification of new variants without relying on traditional, resource-intensive methods or existing genomic references.

AI Health Application

The paper applies advanced deep learning techniques (Vector-Quantized Variational Autoencoders with masked reconstruction and contrastive learning) for unsupervised detection and characterization of viral variants from complex wastewater genomic data. This AI application directly supports public health by enabling rapid, reference-free, and scalable monitoring of pathogens like SARS-CoV-2, informing public health responses, resource allocation, and biosecurity measures.

Methodology

The study employs an unsupervised deep learning framework centered on a Vector-Quantized Variational Autoencoder (VQ-VAE). Genomic sequences are first tokenized using k-mers. The VQ-VAE is extended with masked reconstruction pretraining to enhance robustness to missing or noisy data. Furthermore, contrastive learning is applied during fine-tuning to generate highly discriminative embeddings, which are then evaluated for their ability to cluster variants. The framework was evaluated on approximately 100,000 SARS-CoV-2 wastewater sequencing reads.

Key Findings

The VQ-VAE achieved high performance metrics, including 99.52% mean token-level accuracy and a 56.33% exact sequence match rate. The model demonstrated efficient learning of discrete representations, utilizing 19.73% of its codebook (101 out of 512 codes active). Critically, contrastive fine-tuning led to substantial improvements in variant discrimination, increasing the Silhouette score from 0.31 to 0.42 (+35%) with 64-dimensional embeddings and to 0.44 (+42%) with 128-dimensional embeddings, confirming the utility of discriminative embedding spaces.

Abstract

Wastewater-based genomic surveillance has emerged as a powerful tool for population-level viral monitoring, offering comprehensive insights into circulating viral variants across entire communities. However, this approach faces significant computational challenges stemming from high sequencing noise, low viral coverage, fragmented reads, and the complete absence of labeled variant annotations. Traditional reference-based variant calling pipelines struggle with novel mutations and require extensive computational resources. We present a comprehensive framework for unsupervised viral variant detection using Vector-Quantized Variational Autoencoders (VQ-VAE) that learns discrete codebooks of genomic patterns from k-mer tokenized sequences without requiring reference genomes or variant labels. Our approach extends the base VQ-VAE architecture with masked reconstruction pretraining for robustness to missing data and contrastive learning for highly discriminative embeddings. Evaluated on SARS-CoV-2 wastewater sequencing data comprising approximately 100,000 reads, our VQ-VAE achieves 99.52% mean token-level accuracy and 56.33% exact sequence match rate while maintaining 19.73% codebook utilization (101 of 512 codes active), demonstrating efficient discrete representation learning. Contrastive fine-tuning with different projection dimensions yields substantial clustering improvements: 64-dimensional embeddings achieve +35% Silhouette score improvement (0.31 to 0.42), while 128-dimensional embeddings achieve +42% improvement (0.31 to 0.44), clearly demonstrating the impact of embedding dimensionality on variant discrimination capability. Our reference-free framework provides a scalable, interpretable approach to genomic surveillance with direct applications to public health monitoring.
