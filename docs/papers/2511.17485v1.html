Summary

This paper introduces a novel computer-vision-based deep learning framework to estimate human spine age using over 18,000 MRI series, specifically curating data for age-related degeneration via UMAP and HDBSCAN. The model's clinical utility is demonstrated by the Spine Age Gap (SAG), the difference between actual and predicted age, which shows significant associations with common degenerative spine conditions and lifestyle factors. This positions SAG as a potentially valuable biomarker for overall spine health.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a quantitative, AI-driven biomarker (Spine Age Gap) that can objectively assess spine health beyond chronological age. It offers a tool to identify individuals at higher risk for degenerative spine conditions, potentially enabling earlier interventions and personalized management strategies.

AI Health Application

The AI model analyzes MRI images to estimate 'spine age' and calculate a 'spine age gap (SAG)'. This SAG can serve as a novel biomarker for assessing the severity of age-related spine degeneration, identifying patients at higher risk for specific spinal conditions, and potentially guiding lifestyle modifications for improved spine health. It provides an objective, automated tool for evaluating spine health beyond chronological age.

Methodology

A novel computer-vision-based deep learning method was applied to over 18,000 MRI series to estimate spine age. Data pre-processing involved using UMAP and HDBSCAN to cluster and identify age-based degenerative spine conditions, ensuring the dataset was restricted to subjects exhibiting only age-related degeneration. Model architecture and training were optimized through an ablation study evaluating data size, loss functions, and the impact of different spine regions. Clinical utility was subsequently assessed by calculating the Spine Age Gap (actual age - predicted age) and examining its statistical association with various degenerative conditions and lifestyle factors.

Key Findings

The Spine Age Gap (SAG) serves as a clinically meaningful biomarker. It demonstrates significant associations with key degenerative spine conditions such as disc bulges, disc osteophytes, spinal stenosis, and fractures. Additionally, SAG was found to be linked to important lifestyle factors, including smoking and physically demanding work, underscoring its potential as a comprehensive indicator of overall spine health.

Clinical Impact

The Spine Age Gap (SAG) offers a quantitative and objective biomarker for assessing an individual's spine health, providing a measure that can indicate accelerated or decelerated spine aging. This could enable clinicians to proactively identify patients at higher risk for age-related spine pathologies, facilitate personalized risk assessments, and guide early interventions or lifestyle modifications to prevent or slow the progression of degenerative spine diseases.

Abstract

The human spine is a complex structure composed of 33 vertebrae. It holds the body and is important for leading a healthy life. The spine is vulnerable to age-related degenerations that can be identified through magnetic resonance imaging (MRI). In this paper we propose a novel computer-vison-based deep learning method to estimate spine age using images from over 18,000 MRI series. Data are restricted to subjects with only age-related spine degeneration. Eligibility criteria are created by identifying common age-based clusters of degenerative spine conditions using uniform manifold approximation and projection (UMAP) and hierarchical density-based spatial clustering of applications with noise (HDBSCAN). Model selection is determined using a detailed ablation study on data size, loss, and the effect of different spine regions. We evaluate the clinical utility of our model by calculating the difference between actual spine age and model-predicted age, the spine age gap (SAG), and examining the association between these differences and spine degenerative conditions and lifestyle factors. We find that SAG is associated with conditions including disc bulges, disc osteophytes, spinal stenosis, and fractures, as well as lifestyle factors like smoking and physically demanding work, and thus may be a useful biomarker for measuring overall spine health.
