Breast Cell Segmentation Under Extreme Data Constraints: Quantum Enhancement Meets Adaptive Loss Stabilization

arXiv ID: 2512.02302v1
Published: 2025-12-02
Authors: Varun Kumar Dasoju, Qingsu Cheng, Zeyun Yu
Categories: cs.AI
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper presents a novel framework for breast cell segmentation that achieves a Dice score of 95.5% using only 599 training images, addressing significant challenges like extreme data scarcity, severe class imbalance, and high prevalence of negative samples. The approach integrates quantum-inspired edge enhancement, a stabilized multi-component loss function, and complexity-based weighted sampling to improve boundary detection and small lesion identification, thereby drastically reducing the expert annotation time required for medical AI development.

Medical Relevance

This research is critically important for accelerating the development of AI tools in medical pathology by drastically reducing the time and expertise required from highly specialized pathologists for image annotation. This breakthrough enables the creation of high-performing diagnostic AI models for breast cancer screening and research even with limited labeled data.

AI Health Application

The AI application is an automated system for high-accuracy segmentation of breast cells/nuclei in medical images (likely pathology slides), especially under extreme data constraints. This system is designed to assist pathologists by reducing manual annotation time, improving efficiency, and enhancing the detection of critical features like small lesions, which is crucial for early diagnosis and treatment of conditions such as breast cancer. It addresses the practical challenge of limited annotated medical data by leveraging advanced AI techniques.

Key Points

  • Achieved a high Dice score of 95.5% +/- 0.3% and an IoU of 91.2% +/- 0.4% for breast cell segmentation with only 599 training images, successfully navigating severe data constraints (4% breast tissue pixels, 60% negative images).
  • Introduced quantum-inspired edge enhancement via multi-scale Gabor filters as a fourth input channel, contributing a 2.1% improvement in boundary accuracy and robustly handling inter-annotator variations of +/- 3 pixels.
  • Developed a stabilized multi-component loss function, combining adaptive Dice loss with boundary-aware terms and automatic positive weighting, specifically designed to address severe class imbalance where mammary epithelial cells constitute only 0.1%-20% of the image area.
  • Implemented a complexity-based weighted sampling strategy to prioritize challenging mammary epithelial cell regions during training, leading to a 3.8% increase in small lesion detection.
  • Utilized an EfficientNet-B7/UNet++ architecture with a 4-to-3 channel projection, enabling the effective use of pretrained weights despite the addition of a quantum-enhanced input channel and limited medical imaging data.
  • Ensured robust validation on a small set of 129 images through exponential moving averaging (EMA) and statistical outlier detection, guaranteeing reliable performance estimates.
  • The framework significantly reduces the hundreds of hours typically required from pathologists for dataset annotation, directly addressing a fundamental bottleneck in clinical perception AI development.

Methodology

The framework employs an EfficientNet-B7/UNet++ architecture. It introduces a quantum-inspired edge enhancement using multi-scale Gabor filters to create a fourth input channel, which is then projected from 4-to-3 channels to enable the use of pretrained weights. Training utilizes a stabilized multi-component loss function comprising adaptive Dice loss, boundary-aware terms, and automatic positive weighting to manage severe class imbalance. A complexity-based weighted sampling strategy prioritizes challenging regions. Robust validation is achieved through exponential moving averaging and statistical outlier detection on a small validation set.

Key Findings

The framework achieved a Dice score of 95.5% +/- 0.3% and an IoU of 91.2% +/- 0.4% for breast cell segmentation using only 599 training images. Key contributions include a 2.1% improvement in boundary accuracy attributable to quantum-based enhancement and a 3.8% increase in small lesion detection from weighted sampling, demonstrating effective performance despite extreme data scarcity and class imbalance.

Clinical Impact

By achieving groundbreaking segmentation performance with extremely limited annotations, this approach dramatically reduces the medical expert time (hundreds of hours) currently required for creating high-quality datasets. This directly addresses a critical bottleneck in the development and deployment of clinical AI for breast pathology, enabling faster and more cost-effective advancements in diagnostic AI tools for breast cancer and related research.

Limitations

The abstract highlights the extreme constraints overcome by the method rather than inherent limitations of the proposed framework. These challenges include the small training dataset (599 images), severe class imbalance (0.1%-20% tissue), high proportion of negative images (60%), and a small validation set (129 images) requiring robust validation techniques to ensure reliable performance estimates.

Abstract

Annotating medical images demands significant time and expertise, often requiring pathologists to invest hundreds of hours in labeling mammary epithelial nuclei datasets. We address this critical challenge by achieving 95.5% Dice score using just 599 training images for breast cell segmentation, where just 4% of pixels represent breast tissue and 60% of images contain no breast regions. Our framework uses quantum-inspired edge enhancement via multi-scale Gabor filters creating a fourth input channel, enhancing boundary detection where inter-annotator variations reach +/- 3 pixels. We present a stabilized multi-component loss function that integrates adaptive Dice loss with boundary-aware terms and automatic positive weighting to effectively address severe class imbalance, where mammary epithelial cell regions comprise only 0.1%-20% of the total image area. Additionally, a complexity-based weighted sampling strategy is introduced to prioritize the challenging mammary epithelial cell regions. The model employs an EfficientNet-B7/UNet++ architecture with a 4-to-3 channel projection, enabling the use of pretrained weights despite limited medical imaging data. Finally, robust validation is achieved through exponential moving averaging and statistical outlier detection, ensuring reliable performance estimates on a small validation set (129 images). Our framework achieves a Dice score of 95.5% +/- 0.3% and an IoU of 91.2% +/- 0.4%. Notably, quantum-based enhancement contributes to a 2.1% improvement in boundary accuracy, while weighted sampling increases small lesion detection by 3.8%. By achieving groundbreaking performance with limited annotations, our approach significantly reduces the medical expert time required for dataset creation, addressing a fundamental bottleneck in clinical perception AI development.
