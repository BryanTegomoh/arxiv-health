PULSE-ICU: A Pretrained Unified Long-Sequence Encoder for Multi-task Prediction in Intensive Care Units

arXiv ID: 2511.22199v1
Published: 2025-11-27
Authors: Sejeong Jang, Joo Heung Yoon, Hyo Kyung Lee
Categories: cs.LG, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

PULSE-ICU is a self-supervised foundation model designed to overcome challenges of irregular ICU data for clinical prediction. It learns event-level representations from EHR sequences using a unified embedding and a Longformer-based encoder, achieving strong performance across 18 diverse prediction tasks. The model demonstrates significant improvements and robustness during external validation, suggesting a scalable framework for adaptable ICU decision support across diverse clinical environments.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a robust and adaptable AI solution for critical care, offering more accurate and generalizable predictions for patient outcomes, interventions, and condition identification in the complex ICU environment. Its ability to effectively process and learn from diverse and often messy real-world ICU data significantly improves the practical applicability and reliability of predictive models in clinical settings.

AI Health Application

PULSE-ICU is a foundation model that provides AI-powered decision support for Intensive Care Units. It performs multi-task clinical predictions, including forecasting patient mortality, predicting the need for medical interventions, and identifying patient phenotypes, leveraging large-scale EHR data to assist clinicians in patient management and improve care outcomes.

Key Points

  • Addresses the significant challenges posed by highly irregular, heterogeneous, and temporally fragmented Intensive Care Unit (ICU) data for building generalizable clinical prediction models.
  • PULSE-ICU is a self-supervised foundation model that learns event-level ICU representations directly from large-scale Electronic Health Record (EHR) sequences, eliminating the need for data resampling or extensive manual feature engineering.
  • The model's architecture includes a unified embedding module capable of encoding diverse event attributes such as identity, continuous numerical values, units of measurement, and temporal characteristics.
  • A Longformer-based encoder is incorporated to efficiently model and process long patient trajectories within the ICU data, capturing long-range dependencies.
  • PULSE-ICU was fine-tuned and evaluated across 18 distinct prediction tasks, encompassing critical areas like mortality prediction, intervention forecasting, and phenotype identification, consistently achieving strong performance across all task types.
  • Demonstrated substantial improvements and robustness during external validation on independent datasets (eICU, HiRID, P12), showing resilience to domain shift and variable constraints with minimal fine-tuning.
  • The findings suggest that this foundation-style modeling approach enhances data efficiency and adaptability for predictive analytics in critical care settings.

Methodology

PULSE-ICU employs a self-supervised learning strategy to derive event-level representations from large-scale EHR sequences. It integrates a unified embedding module to encode event identity, continuous values, units, and temporal attributes. A Longformer-based encoder then processes these embeddings to efficiently model long patient trajectories. The pretrained model is subsequently fine-tuned for specific downstream clinical prediction tasks.

Key Findings

PULSE-ICU achieved strong performance across 18 diverse clinical prediction tasks, including mortality and intervention forecasting. It demonstrated substantial improvements and robustness during external validation on eICU, HiRID, and P12 datasets, requiring minimal fine-tuning and proving resilient to domain shift and variable constraints. This approach significantly enhances data efficiency and adaptability for ICU prediction models.

Clinical Impact

PULSE-ICU has the potential to significantly improve clinical decision support in ICUs by providing more accurate and timely predictions for critical events (e.g., mortality, need for interventions) and patient conditions. Its robustness and adaptability to various clinical environments could lead to the deployment of more generalizable and reliable AI tools in critical care, reducing the need for extensive manual data preparation and feature engineering.

Abstract

Intensive care unit (ICU) data are highly irregular, heterogeneous, and temporally fragmented, posing challenges for generalizable clinical prediction. We present PULSE-ICU, a self-supervised foundation model that learns event-level ICU representations from large-scale EHR sequences without resampling or manual feature engineering. A unified embedding module encodes event identity, continuous values, units, and temporal attributes, while a Longformer-based encoder enables efficient modeling of long trajectories. PULSE-ICU was fine-tuned across 18 prediction tasks, including mortality, intervention forecasting, and phenotype identification, achieving strong performance across task types. External validation on eICU, HiRID, and P12 showed substantial improvements with minimal fine-tuning, demonstrating robustness to domain shift and variable constraints. These findings suggest that foundation-style modeling can improve data efficiency and adaptability, providing a scalable framework for ICU decision support across diverse clinical environments.
