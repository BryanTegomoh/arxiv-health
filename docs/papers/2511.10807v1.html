Invading activity fronts stabilize excitable systems against stochastic extinction

Summary

This paper demonstrates a generic mechanism to prevent stochastic extinction in finite excitable systems. It shows that in inhomogeneous settings, a vulnerable subsystem diffusively coupled to an adjacent stable patch can be reanimated and stabilized through continuous immigration flux from the interface, provided the absorbing region sustains spreading activity fronts. This process effectively eliminates finite-size extinction instabilities, ensuring population persistence.

Medical Relevance

This research provides fundamental insights into how biological populations, such as pathogens, immune cells, or beneficial microbes, can persist within a host despite intrinsic stochastic fluctuations. Understanding this mechanism is crucial for developing strategies to manage chronic infections, bolster immune system resilience, or maintain the stability of critical microbiomes.

Key Points

  • Stochastic processes often lead to extinction (absorbing states) in finite dynamical systems.
  • While spatially extended systems delay extinction, they do not entirely prevent it, and certain parameter tunings can increase vulnerability.
  • The proposed solution involves an inhomogeneous system architecture: a vulnerable subsystem coupled to a stable 'patch'.
  • Continuous immigration flux from the stable patch, facilitated by 'spreading activity fronts' within the vulnerable region, reanimates the declining population.
  • This mechanism generically eliminates finite-size extinction instabilities, stabilizing excitable systems against stochastic extinction events.
  • The concept's efficacy is demonstrated across a range of ecological and epidemiological models, including predator-prey, epidemic spreading, and cyclic competition.
  • The presence of 'invading activity fronts' is crucial for sustaining the immigration flux and thereby system stability.

Methodology

The study employs theoretical and computational modeling to analyze various dynamical systems. It investigates inhomogeneous settings where a vulnerable subsystem is diffusively coupled to a stable region, focusing on how 'spreading activity fronts' generate continuous 'immigration flux' to prevent extinction. This is applied to established models like predator-prey, epidemic spreading, and cyclic competition to demonstrate the generic nature of the stabilization mechanism.

Key Findings

The primary finding is that invading activity fronts, originating from a stable subsystem and propagating into an adjacent vulnerable region, can generate a continuous immigration flux. This flux acts as a 'reanimation' mechanism, effectively stabilizing the vulnerable subsystem and generically eliminating the finite-size extinction instabilities that would otherwise lead to the demise of the population due to stochastic fluctuations.

Abstract

Stochastic chemical reaction or population dynamics in finite systems often terminates in an absorbing state. Yet in large spatially extended systems, the time to reach species extinction (or fixation) becomes exceedingly long. Tuning control parameters may diminish the survival probability, rendering species coexistence susceptible to stochastic extinction events. In inhomogeneous settings, where a vulnerable subsystem is diffusively coupled to an adjacent stable patch, the former is reanimated through continuous influx from the interfaces, provided the absorbing region sustains spreading activity fronts. We demonstrate this generic elimination of finite-size extinction instabilities via immigration flux in predator-prey, epidemic spreading, and cyclic competition models.
