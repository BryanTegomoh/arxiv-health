Improving the Performance of Radiology Report De-identification with Large-Scale Training and Benchmarking Against Cloud Vendor Methods

arXiv ID: 2511.04079v1
Published: 2025-11-06
Authors: Eva Prakash, Maayane Attias, Pierre Chambon, Justin Xu, Steven Truong, Jean-Benoit Delbrouck, Tessa Cook, Curtis Langlotz
Categories: cs.CL
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper significantly improves automated de-identification of radiology reports using a transformer-based model fine-tuned on large, diverse datasets, achieving superior performance over previous state-of-the-art academic models and commercial cloud vendor systems. The approach demonstrates robust cross-institutional generalization and establishes a new benchmark for secure processing of sensitive clinical text. It ensures patient privacy while preserving data utility for research.

Medical Relevance

This research is critically important for enabling the safe and ethical secondary use of vast amounts of clinical data, particularly radiology reports, for research, AI development, and quality improvement by ensuring robust patient privacy through highly accurate de-identification.

AI Health Application

The AI application is an automated de-identification system for clinical text (specifically radiology reports). This system leverages transformer models to detect and remove PHI, thereby facilitating secure clinical text processing. It serves as a foundational AI tool that enables other medical AI applications by providing de-identified, usable clinical data for research, model training, and analytical purposes.

Key Points

  • A state-of-the-art transformer-based model was fine-tuned for Protected Health Information (PHI) de-identification in radiology reports.
  • Training involved large-scale, multimodal annotated radiology corpora from Stanford University, covering chest X-ray, chest CT, abdomen/pelvis CT, and brain MR reports.
  • An additional PHI category, AGE, was introduced into the model architecture to enhance detection granularity.
  • The model achieved high overall F1 scores of 0.973 on the Penn dataset and 0.996 on the Stanford dataset, outperforming or maintaining the previous academic state-of-the-art.
  • Performance evaluation using synthetic PHI generation ('hide-in-plain-sight' method) showed consistent detectability (overall F1: 0.959) across 50 datasets, indicating robustness.
  • The model significantly outperformed all commercial cloud vendor de-identification systems on synthetic Penn reports (overall F1: 0.960 vs. 0.632-0.754).
  • Large-scale and diverse (multimodal) training improved the model's cross-institutional generalization and robustness.

Methodology

The study utilized a transformer-based PHI de-identification pipeline, fine-tuning it on two extensive annotated radiology corpora from Stanford University, encompassing diverse report types like chest X-ray, chest CT, abdomen/pelvis CT, and brain MR. An 'AGE' PHI category was integrated. Model performance was assessed using token-level PHI detection on test sets from Stanford and the University of Pennsylvania, with precision, recall, and F1 scores. Furthermore, the stability of synthetic PHI generation via a 'hide-in-plain-sight' method was evaluated, and the model's performance was benchmarked against leading commercial cloud vendor systems.

Key Findings

The developed model achieved an overall F1 score of 0.973 on the Penn dataset and 0.996 on the Stanford dataset, matching or exceeding prior academic benchmarks. It demonstrated highly consistent detectability of synthetic PHI (F1: 0.959). Crucially, the model significantly outperformed all evaluated commercial cloud vendor systems, achieving an F1 score of 0.960 compared to the vendors' range of 0.632-0.754 on synthetic Penn reports.

Clinical Impact

This advanced de-identification model offers a highly accurate and robust solution for anonymizing sensitive radiology reports, which is vital for HIPAA compliance and facilitating the secure sharing and secondary use of clinical data for AI model development, epidemiological studies, and clinical research. Its superior performance over commercial alternatives provides healthcare institutions with a more trustworthy and efficient tool to accelerate medical innovation while rigorously safeguarding patient privacy.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state any limitations. The evaluation was conducted on specific radiology report types from two institutions (Stanford and University of Pennsylvania), which may limit generalizability to other report types or institutions without further validation.

Future Directions

The abstract establishes a new benchmark for secure clinical text processing but does not explicitly suggest future research directions beyond this achievement.

Abstract

Objective: To enhance automated de-identification of radiology reports by scaling transformer-based models through extensive training datasets and benchmarking performance against commercial cloud vendor systems for protected health information (PHI) detection. Materials and Methods: In this retrospective study, we built upon a state-of-the-art, transformer-based, PHI de-identification pipeline by fine-tuning on two large annotated radiology corpora from Stanford University, encompassing chest X-ray, chest CT, abdomen/pelvis CT, and brain MR reports and introducing an additional PHI category (AGE) into the architecture. Model performance was evaluated on test sets from Stanford and the University of Pennsylvania (Penn) for token-level PHI detection. We further assessed (1) the stability of synthetic PHI generation using a "hide-in-plain-sight" method and (2) performance against commercial systems. Precision, recall, and F1 scores were computed across all PHI categories. Results: Our model achieved overall F1 scores of 0.973 on the Penn dataset and 0.996 on the Stanford dataset, outperforming or maintaining the previous state-of-the-art model performance. Synthetic PHI evaluation showed consistent detectability (overall F1: 0.959 [0.958-0.960]) across 50 independently de-identified Penn datasets. Our model outperformed all vendor systems on synthetic Penn reports (overall F1: 0.960 vs. 0.632-0.754). Discussion: Large-scale, multimodal training improved cross-institutional generalization and robustness. Synthetic PHI generation preserved data utility while ensuring privacy. Conclusion: A transformer-based de-identification model trained on diverse radiology datasets outperforms prior academic and commercial systems in PHI detection and establishes a new benchmark for secure clinical text processing.
