A Diffusion Model to Shrink Proteins While Maintaining Their Function

arXiv ID: 2511.07390v1
Published: 2025-11-10
Authors: Ethan Baron, Alan N. Amin, Ruben Weitzman, Debora Marks, Andrew Gordon Wilson
Categories: cs.LG, q-bio.QM

Summary

SCISOR introduces a novel discrete diffusion model designed to computationally shrink long protein sequences while preserving their function. By training a de-noiser to reverse random insertions, SCISOR generates realistic, shorter proteins that competitively fit evolutionary data and achieve state-of-the-art predictions for deletion effects. This advancement offers a more efficient and less costly alternative to traditional experimental methods for protein optimization.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medicine as it provides an efficient computational method to design shorter, more manageable, and functionally optimized proteins. This can significantly accelerate the development and improve the characteristics of protein-based therapeutics, diagnostics, and biotechnological tools, enhancing their manufacturability, delivery, and efficacy in biological systems.

AI Health Application

SCISOR is a novel discrete diffusion model that applies AI to computationally design and shorten protein sequences. By predicting effective deletions that preserve functional motifs, it enables the creation of more manageable and deliverable therapeutic proteins, accelerating drug discovery, biopharmaceutical development, and potentially gene therapy applications by optimizing protein vectors.

Key Points

  • Addresses the challenge of long protein sequences in bioengineering and medicine, which are difficult to synthesize, fuse, or deliver, typically requiring expensive experimental shortening.
  • Proposes SCISOR, a novel discrete diffusion model that learns to delete letters (amino acids) from protein sequences.
  • SCISOR trains a de-noiser to reverse a forward noising process where random insertions are added to natural protein sequences, thereby learning an inductive bias for deletion.
  • As a generative model, SCISOR competitively fits evolutionary sequence data when compared to previous large models.
  • Achieves state-of-the-art predictions regarding the functional effects of deletions on the ProteinGym benchmark, indicating its ability to understand sequence-function relationships.
  • Successfully shrinks long protein sequences, resulting in generated proteins that are significantly more realistic and more often preserve critical functional motifs than suggestions from previous evolutionary sequence models.
  • Offers a computational approach to replace costly, time-consuming experimental campaigns for protein shortening and optimization.

Methodology

SCISOR is a novel discrete diffusion model. Its core methodology involves a forward noising process where random insertions are applied to natural protein sequences. A neural network, termed a de-noiser, is then trained to reverse this process, effectively learning to perform deletions. This training enables SCISOR to generate protein samples that resemble natural sequences, even after significant shortening, by inferring which residues can be removed while maintaining sequence integrity and function. Its performance was evaluated on evolutionary sequence data fitting and functional prediction using the ProteinGym benchmark.

Key Findings

SCISOR demonstrates competitive performance in fitting evolutionary sequence data. It achieves state-of-the-art predictions for the functional effects of deletions on the ProteinGym benchmark. Critically, when applied to shrink long proteins, SCISOR's suggested deletions result in significantly more realistic proteins and more frequently preserve functional motifs compared to previous models of evolutionary sequences.

Clinical Impact

The ability to computationally shrink proteins while preserving function has substantial clinical impact. It could lead to the design of improved protein therapeutics (e.g., antibodies, enzymes, hormones) that are smaller, more stable, easier to manufacture at scale, and more amenable to various drug delivery routes (e.g., oral, inhaled, better tissue penetration). This could reduce development costs, shorten timelines for drug discovery, and enable novel protein-based interventions for a wider range of diseases.

Abstract

Many proteins useful in modern medicine or bioengineering are challenging to make in the lab, fuse with other proteins in cells, or deliver to tissues in the body, because their sequences are too long. Shortening these sequences typically involves costly, time-consuming experimental campaigns. Ideally, we could instead use modern models of massive databases of sequences from nature to learn how to propose shrunken proteins that resemble sequences found in nature. Unfortunately, these models struggle to efficiently search the combinatorial space of all deletions, and are not trained with inductive biases to learn how to delete. To address this gap, we propose SCISOR, a novel discrete diffusion model that deletes letters from sequences to generate protein samples that resemble those found in nature. To do so, SCISOR trains a de-noiser to reverse a forward noising process that adds random insertions to natural sequences. As a generative model, SCISOR fits evolutionary sequence data competitively with previous large models. In evaluation, SCISOR achieves state-of-the-art predictions of the functional effects of deletions on ProteinGym. Finally, we use the SCISOR de-noiser to shrink long protein sequences, and show that its suggested deletions result in significantly more realistic proteins and more often preserve functional motifs than previous models of evolutionary sequences.
