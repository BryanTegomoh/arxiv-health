The First In Vitro and In Vivo Validation of the Hessian-Free Ray-Born Inversion for Quantitative Ultrasound Tomography

Published: 2025-11-20
Authors: Ashkan Javaherian
Categories: physics.med-ph

Summary

This study presents the first experimental validation of a Hessian-free ray-Born inversion technique for quantitatively reconstructing sound speed from transmission ultrasound data. The method, which combines single-scattering theory with high-frequency approximations and a unique Green's function approach, demonstrated strong potential as a computationally efficient and accurate tool suitable for clinical translation, as validated using both in-vitro and in-vivo datasets. It offers an advanced solution for quantitative ultrasound tomography.

Medical Relevance

Quantitative Ultrasound Tomography (QUS) provides non-invasive, radiation-free tissue characterization by mapping sound speed, which is a biomarker for various pathologies. This paper's method improves QUS by making it more computationally efficient and accurate, thereby enhancing its potential for routine clinical use in disease detection, diagnosis, and treatment monitoring.

Key Points

  • First experimental (in-vitro and in-vivo) validation of the Hessian-free ray-Born inversion technique for quantitative sound speed reconstruction.
  • The method uniquely employs Green's functions approximated along ray trajectories, distinguishing it from previous singly-scattered inversion approaches that only considered scattering potential heterogeneities.
  • It integrates single-scattering theory with high-frequency approximations, making it well-suited for the frequency ranges typically used in clinical ultrasound applications.
  • The inversion process involves sequential linearization and minimization of the objective function across increasing frequency bands.
  • A key innovation is a weighting scheme applied to both the solution and data spaces, which diagonalizes the Hessian, allowing for its inversion in a single, computationally efficient step.
  • Reconstructed images were rigorously evaluated by comparison with results obtained from a full-wave inversion approach based on a frequency-domain Helmholtz solver.
  • The results affirm the technique's strong potential as a computationally efficient and accurate method, highlighting its suitability for clinical translation. The method is also available as an open-source package.

Methodology

The study implemented a Hessian-free ray-Born inversion technique, which combines single-scattering theory with high-frequency approximations to reconstruct sound speed from transmission ultrasound data. Unlike prior methods, it approximates Green's functions along ray trajectories. The associated objective function is linearized and minimized sequentially across increasing frequency bands. A novel weighting scheme is applied to the solution and data spaces within each frequency subproblem, which diagonalizes the Hessian, enabling its inversion in a single step. The method was validated using experimental in-vitro and in-vivo datasets from the University of Rochester Medical Center, with reconstructed images compared to those from a full-wave inversion approach utilizing a frequency-domain Helmholtz solver.

Key Findings

The experimental validation demonstrated that the Hessian-free ray-Born inversion technique is highly effective, proving to be both computationally efficient and accurate for quantitative reconstruction of sound speed. These findings establish the method's strong potential and suitability for clinical translation in diagnostic imaging.

Its computational efficiency can facilitate faster image reconstruction, potentially enabling near real-time imaging for clinical decision-making, while enhanced accuracy provides more reliable tissue characterization. This could lead to earlier and more precise diagnoses of conditions where sound speed is a critical biomarker, such as cancer detection, assessment of liver fibrosis, or characterization of breast lesions, making QUS more accessible and practical in routine clinical workflows.

Abstract

This study presents the first experimental validation of a Hessian-free ray-Born inversion technique for quantitative reconstruction of sound speed from transmission ultrasound data. The method combines single-scattering theory with high-frequency approximations, yielding an inversion framework well suited to the frequency ranges used in clinical ultrasound applications. Unlike previous singly-scattered inversion approaches that account for medium heterogeneities only in the scattering potential, the proposed ray-Born method employs Green's functions approximated along ray trajectories determined by high-frequency assumptions. The associated objective function is linearized and minimized sequentially across increasing frequency bands. At each frequency set, the linearized subproblem is solved using a weighting scheme applied to both the solution and data spaces, which diagonalizes the Hessian and enables its inversion in a single step. The method, previously reported and released as an open-source package, was applied to in-vitro and in-vivo datasets provided by the University of Rochester Medical Center. The reconstructed images were evaluated by comparison with those obtained using a full-wave inversion approach based on a frequency-domain Helmholtz solver. The results demonstrate the strong potential of the Hessian-free ray-Born inversion as a computationally efficient and accurate method suitable for clinical translation.
