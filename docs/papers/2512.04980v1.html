Learning Causality for Longitudinal Data

arXiv ID: 2512.04980v1
Published: 2025-12-04
Authors: Mouad EL Bouchattaoui
Categories: stat.ML, cs.IT, cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00

Summary

This thesis presents advanced machine learning methods for causal inference and causal representation learning in complex, high-dimensional, time-varying medical/health data. It introduces a Causal Dynamic Variational Autoencoder (CDVAE) for robust Individual Treatment Effect (ITE) estimation and an efficient RNN-based framework with Contrastive Predictive Coding (CPC) for long-term counterfactual prediction. Furthermore, it enhances interpretability by mapping latent causes to observed variables using a novel Jacobian-based approach, providing theoretical guarantees and superior performance across tasks.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing precision medicine by enabling more accurate personalized treatment effect estimation, predicting long-term patient outcomes under various interventions, and enhancing the interpretability of complex AI models used in healthcare, ultimately facilitating better clinical decision-making and understanding of disease mechanisms.

AI Health Application

The methods developed here are foundational for AI applications in health such as: developing intelligent systems to recommend personalized treatment plans based on predicted individual response; modeling disease trajectories to anticipate future health states and intervene proactively; identifying novel latent disease subtypes and their observable manifestations; creating more robust clinical decision support systems that account for causal relationships and unobserved confounders; and accelerating drug discovery by understanding causal mechanisms of drug action and patient response.

Key Points

  • Introduces the Causal Dynamic Variational Autoencoder (CDVAE) for estimating Individual Treatment Effects (ITEs) by modeling unobserved heterogeneity driven by latent risk factors affecting only outcomes.
  • CDVAE comes with theoretical guarantees on valid latent adjustment and generalization bounds for ITE error, demonstrating superior performance over baselines and significantly improving state-of-the-art models when augmented with its latent representations.
  • Proposes an efficient framework for long-term counterfactual regression based on RNNs enhanced with Contrastive Predictive Coding (CPC) and InfoMax to capture long-range dependencies under time-varying confounding.
  • This RNN-CPC-InfoMax framework achieves state-of-the-art results for long-term counterfactual prediction while avoiding the high computational cost typically associated with transformer-based models.
  • Advances Causal Representation Learning (CRL) by developing a model-agnostic interpretability layer that uses the geometry of the decoder Jacobian to understand how latent causes manifest in observed variables.
  • Employs a sparse self-expression prior to induce modular, potentially overlapping groups of observed features aligned with shared latent influences, offering recovery guarantees without restrictive anchor feature or single-parent assumptions.
  • Develops scalable Jacobian-based regularization techniques to further improve the recovery of meaningful latent-to-observed causal structures.

Methodology

The paper employs a blend of deep learning and causal inference techniques: 1) A Causal Dynamic Variational Autoencoder (CDVAE) for modeling latent variables and estimating ITEs, incorporating theoretical guarantees. 2) Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) augmented with Contrastive Predictive Coding (CPC) and InfoMax for efficient long-term counterfactual prediction in time-series data. 3) A novel model-agnostic interpretability layer utilizing the geometry of the decoder Jacobian and sparse self-expression priors to uncover latent-to-observed causal mappings, supported by recovery guarantees and Jacobian-based regularization.

Key Findings

The CDVAE model effectively estimates ITEs, outperforming baselines and significantly improving existing SOTA methods through robust latent adjustment. The RNN-CPC-InfoMax framework achieves state-of-the-art results in efficient long-term counterfactual regression for time-varying data. Crucially, meaningful latent-to-observed causal structures can be reliably recovered using the Jacobian-based interpretability layer, even in complex overlapping scenarios and without strong prior assumptions.

Clinical Impact

This research can revolutionize clinical practice by enabling more precise personalized treatment recommendations based on individual patient characteristics and unobserved risk factors. It offers a powerful tool for predicting the long-term efficacy and safety of different therapeutic strategies, aiding clinicians in dynamically managing chronic diseases. Furthermore, the enhanced interpretability of these models can build trust in AI-driven healthcare solutions by clearly articulating how underlying biological or lifestyle factors influence observable health outcomes, potentially accelerating drug discovery and repurposing by identifying novel disease pathways.

Abstract

This thesis develops methods for causal inference and causal representation learning (CRL) in high-dimensional, time-varying data. The first contribution introduces the Causal Dynamic Variational Autoencoder (CDVAE), a model for estimating Individual Treatment Effects (ITEs) by capturing unobserved heterogeneity in treatment response driven by latent risk factors that affect only outcomes. CDVAE comes with theoretical guarantees on valid latent adjustment and generalization bounds for ITE error. Experiments on synthetic and real datasets show that CDVAE outperforms baselines, and that state-of-the-art models greatly improve when augmented with its latent substitutes, approaching oracle performance without access to true adjustment variables. The second contribution proposes an efficient framework for long-term counterfactual regression based on RNNs enhanced with Contrastive Predictive Coding (CPC) and InfoMax. It captures long-range dependencies under time-varying confounding while avoiding the computational cost of transformers, achieving state-of-the-art results and introducing CPC into causal inference. The third contribution advances CRL by addressing how latent causes manifest in observed variables. We introduce a model-agnostic interpretability layer based on the geometry of the decoder Jacobian. A sparse self-expression prior induces modular, possibly overlapping groups of observed features aligned with shared latent influences. We provide recovery guarantees in both disjoint and overlapping settings and show that meaningful latent-to-observed structure can be recovered without anchor features or single-parent assumptions. Scalable Jacobian-based regularization techniques are also developed.
