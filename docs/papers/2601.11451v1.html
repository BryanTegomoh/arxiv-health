PRISM-CAFO: Prior-conditioned Remote-sensing Infrastructure Segmentation and Mapping for CAFOs

Summary

PRISM-CAFO introduces an infrastructure-first, explainable AI pipeline for accurately identifying and characterizing Concentrated Animal Feeding Operations (CAFOs) from aerial and satellite imagery. The method leverages advanced computer vision techniques to detect key infrastructure components and achieves state-of-the-art performance, significantly improving mapping capabilities for these critical sites.

Medical Relevance

This research is directly relevant to medicine and public health by providing a robust, scalable, and explainable tool for monitoring CAFOs, which are significant sources of environmental pollution, antibiotic resistance, and potential zoonotic disease outbreaks impacting human populations.

AI Health Application

The AI method uses remote sensing to identify and characterize Concentrated Animal Feeding Operations (CAFOs). This mapping data is critical for public health surveillance, allowing health authorities to identify potential sources of human health risks (e.g., zoonotic disease outbreaks, antimicrobial resistance, environmental pollution impacting health) and biosecurity threats posed by CAFOs. The AI system provides a scalable tool for monitoring these operations to mitigate their negative impacts on human health.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical need for accurate and scalable mapping of CAFOs due to their significant risks to human health, the environment, and vulnerability to threats like infectious diseases.
  • Proposes PRISM-CAFO, an "infrastructure-first" explainable AI pipeline for automated CAFO identification and characterization using remote sensing data.
  • The methodology involves a multi-stage process: domain-tuned YOLOv8 for infrastructure detection (e.g., barns, lagoons), SAM2 for mask generation, extraction of structured descriptors (counts, areas, spatial relations), and classification via a lightweight spatial cross-attention model.
  • The pipeline outputs both CAFO type predictions and mask-level attributions, providing transparency and linking model decisions directly to specific visible infrastructure components for explainability.
  • Achieves state-of-the-art performance, with the Swin-B+PRISM-CAFO configuration surpassing the best performing baseline by up to 15% in predictive accuracy.
  • Demonstrates strong predictive capabilities across diverse U.S. regions and utilizes systematic gradient-activation analyses to quantify the impact of domain priors and enhance decision explainability.
  • The explained results confirm the practical applicability of the approach for public health and environmental monitoring of large-scale livestock operations.

Methodology

The PRISM-CAFO pipeline is an infrastructure-first approach comprising three main stages: (1) detection of candidate infrastructure (e.g., barns, feedlots, manure lagoons, silos) using a domain-tuned YOLOv8 detector, followed by SAM2 mask generation and filtering based on component-specific criteria; (2) extraction of structured descriptors (e.g., counts, areas, orientations, spatial relations) which are then fused with deep visual features using a lightweight spatial cross-attention classifier; and (3) output generation of CAFO type predictions alongside mask-level attributions for explainability, complemented by systematic gradient-activation analyses to understand the impact of domain priors.

Key Findings

The PRISM-CAFO approach achieves state-of-the-art performance in identifying and characterizing CAFOs from aerial and satellite imagery, demonstrating a significant improvement of up to 15% over existing baselines. It exhibits strong predictive capabilities across diverse U.S. regions and provides critical explainability by linking model decisions directly to visible infrastructure components, thereby enhancing trust and interpretability in its outputs.

Clinical Impact

The ability to accurately and scalably map CAFOs enables health authorities and researchers to better assess and mitigate public health risks associated with these operations, such as air and water pollution, antimicrobial resistance dissemination, and the spread of zoonotic diseases. This improved monitoring capability can inform targeted public health interventions, enhance disease surveillance efforts, and support policy-making aimed at protecting nearby communities and the wider ecosystem.

Abstract

Large-scale livestock operations pose significant risks to human health and the environment, while also being vulnerable to threats such as infectious diseases and extreme weather events. As the number of such operations continues to grow, accurate and scalable mapping has become increasingly important. In this work, we present an infrastructure-first, explainable pipeline for identifying and characterizing Concentrated Animal Feeding Operations (CAFOs) from aerial and satellite imagery. Our method (1) detects candidate infrastructure (e.g., barns, feedlots, manure lagoons, silos) with a domain-tuned YOLOv8 detector, then derives SAM2 masks from these boxes and filters component-specific criteria, (2) extracts structured descriptors (e.g., counts, areas, orientations, and spatial relations) and fuses them with deep visual features using a lightweight spatial cross-attention classifier, and (3) outputs both CAFO type predictions and mask-level attributions that link decisions to visible infrastructure. Through comprehensive evaluation, we show that our approach achieves state-of-the-art performance, with Swin-B+PRISM-CAFO surpassing the best performing baseline by up to 15\%.
