Final state sensitivity and fractal basin boundaries from coupled Chialvo neurons

Published: 2025-11-05
Authors: Bennett Lamb, Brandon B. Le
Categories: nlin.CD, math.DS, physics.bio-ph, q-bio.NC

Summary

This paper investigates the basin geometry and final state uncertainty in a system of two identical electrically asymmetrically coupled Chialvo neurons. It identifies that despite individual neurons being nonchaotic, the coupled system exhibits multistability with two qualitatively different attractors, predominantly chaotic, separated by a fractal basin boundary. This fractal boundary leads to extreme final state sensitivity, having significant implications for understanding neuronal synchronization, human behavior, and neurological disease.

Medical Relevance

This research illuminates how fundamental properties of neuronal coupling, even from individually nonchaotic units, can give rise to complex, unpredictable system behaviors like chaos and extreme sensitivity. This has profound implications for understanding the stability and variability of brain function, crucial for diagnosing and treating neurological disorders characterized by aberrant synchronization.

Key Points

  • The study models and quantifies the basin geometry and extreme final state uncertainty of two identical, electrically asymmetrically coupled Chialvo neurons.
  • Individually, Chialvo neurons are nonchaotic, but their coupling leads to complex dynamics, including chaos.
  • The system demonstrates multistability with two distinct types of attractors: a chaotic attractor and a nonchaotic attractor.
  • The chaotic basin occupies the vast majority of the coupled system's state space, while the nonchaotic basin extends to infinity due to chance synchronization.
  • A key finding is the fractal nature of the boundary between the chaotic and nonchaotic basins, resulting in extreme final state sensitivity where tiny perturbations can lead to vastly different long-term behaviors.
  • The observed uncertainty and its effect on biological neuron synchronization are suggested to be critically important for understanding both normal human behavior and the etiology of neurological diseases.
  • The mathematical structure of the coupled equations is highlighted as the determinant of the system's chaotic and nonchaotic dynamics.

Methodology

The methodology involves computational modeling and mathematical analysis of two electrically asymmetrically coupled Chialvo neurons. The authors investigate and quantify the system's basin geometry, analyzing the types of attractors (chaotic vs. nonchaotic) and the structure of the boundaries between their basins of attraction. This includes determining the mathematical reasoning for the emergence of chaotic and nonchaotic dynamics from the coupled equations.

Key Findings

The coupled Chialvo neuron system exhibits multistability with distinct chaotic and nonchaotic attractors, despite individual neurons being nonchaotic. The chaotic basin dominates the state space, but a nonchaotic basin exists due to chance synchronization and stretches infinitely. Crucially, the boundary between these basins is fractal, leading to extreme final state sensitivity, implying that very small initial condition differences can lead to vastly different long-term neuronal states.

Clinical Impact

The findings suggest that the brain's synchronization patterns, vital for cognitive functions, may be exquisitely sensitive to microscopic perturbations, potentially explaining sudden shifts in brain states or the onset of paroxysmal events in neurological disorders like epilepsy or movement disorders. Understanding these fractal boundaries and final state sensitivity could inform strategies for neuromodulation or deep brain stimulation, aiming to steer pathological brain states towards more stable or desired nonchaotic synchronizations, or to understand the inherent unpredictability in brain responses to interventions.

Abstract

We investigate and quantify the basin geometry and extreme final state uncertainty of two identical electrically asymmetrically coupled Chialvo neurons. The system's diverse behaviors are presented, along with the mathematical reasoning behind its chaotic and nonchaotic dynamics as determined by the structure of the coupled equations. The system is found to be multistable with two qualitatively different attractors. Although each neuron is individually nonchaotic, the chaotic basin takes up the vast majority of the coupled system's state space, but the nonchaotic basin stretches to infinity due to chance synchronization. The boundary between the basins is found to be fractal, leading to extreme final state sensitivity. This uncertainty and its potential effect on the synchronization of biological neurons may have significant implications for understanding human behavior and neurological disease.
