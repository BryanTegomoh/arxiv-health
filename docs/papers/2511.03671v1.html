Final state sensitivity and fractal basin boundaries from coupled Chialvo neurons

Summary

This paper investigates the complex dynamics of two asymmetrically coupled Chialvo neurons, revealing multistability with predominantly chaotic behavior despite individual non-chaotic neurons. A critical finding is the fractal nature of the basin boundaries, leading to extreme final state sensitivity that could profoundly impact synchronization patterns in biological neural networks.

Medical Relevance

The discovery of extreme final state sensitivity and fractal basin boundaries in coupled neuronal models offers a computational framework for understanding how subtle initial perturbations or inherent biological noise might lead to profound and unpredictable changes in brain function, behavior, and the manifestation or progression of neurological disorders characterized by abnormal neuronal synchronization.

Key Points

  • The study focuses on the basin geometry and extreme final state uncertainty of two identical electrically asymmetrically coupled Chialvo neurons.
  • The coupled system is found to be multistable, exhibiting two qualitatively different attractors (chaotic and nonchaotic).
  • Despite individual Chialvo neurons being nonchaotic, the coupled system's state space is largely dominated by chaotic dynamics.
  • A nonchaotic basin also exists, stretching to infinity due to instances of 'chance synchronization' between the neurons.
  • The boundary separating these chaotic and nonchaotic basins is fractal, which is the direct cause of extreme final state sensitivity.
  • Extreme final state sensitivity implies that minuscule differences in initial conditions can lead to vastly different long-term behaviors (e.g., chaotic firing vs. synchronized nonchaotic activity).
  • These findings are hypothesized to have significant implications for understanding the synchronization of biological neurons, the mechanistic basis of human behavior, and the pathogenesis of neurological diseases.

Abstract

We investigate and quantify the basin geometry and extreme final state uncertainty of two identical electrically asymmetrically coupled Chialvo neurons. The system's diverse behaviors are presented, along with the mathematical reasoning behind its chaotic and nonchaotic dynamics as determined by the structure of the coupled equations. The system is found to be multistable with two qualitatively different attractors. Although each neuron is individually nonchaotic, the chaotic basin takes up the vast majority of the coupled system's state space, but the nonchaotic basin stretches to infinity due to chance synchronization. The boundary between the basins is found to be fractal, leading to extreme final state sensitivity. This uncertainty and its potential effect on the synchronization of biological neurons may have significant implications for understanding human behavior and neurological disease.
