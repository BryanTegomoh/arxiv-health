A Cognitive Process-Inspired Architecture for Subject-Agnostic Brain Visual Decoding

arXiv ID: 2511.02565v1
Published: 2025-11-04
Authors: Jingyu Lu, Haonan Wang, Qixiang Zhang, Xiaomeng Li
Categories: cs.CV, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces the Visual Cortex Flow Architecture (VCFlow), a novel hierarchical decoding framework designed for subject-agnostic brain visual decoding from fMRI data. VCFlow models the human visual system's ventral-dorsal architecture and employs a contrastive learning strategy to reconstruct continuous visual experiences from fMRI without subject-specific training. This approach offers a fast (10 seconds per video), clinically scalable solution, sacrificing only 7% accuracy on average compared to conventional, computationally intensive methods.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for clinical applications as it enables the reconstruction of visual experiences from brain activity in a subject-agnostic manner, removing the impractical need for extensive, personalized training data. This makes brain-computer interfaces and diagnostic tools based on fMRI more accessible and feasible for a wide range of patients, particularly those with communication impairments.

AI Health Application

The application is to reconstruct visual experiences from brain activity, which could be used clinically for diagnosis, monitoring, or even potential brain-computer interfaces related to visual perception, especially in scenarios where individual-specific training data is scarce or impractical. The focus on 'clinically scalable solution' highlights its potential for real-world medical adoption.

Key Points

  • Addresses the challenge of subject-agnostic brain decoding for visual reconstruction from fMRI, which traditionally suffers from poor cross-subject generalization and requires extensive per-subject training.
  • Proposes VCFlow, a hierarchical decoding framework that explicitly models the ventral-dorsal architecture of the human visual system to learn multi-dimensional neural representations.
  • VCFlow disentangles and leverages features from the early visual cortex, ventral, and dorsal streams to capture diverse and complementary cognitive information.
  • Introduces a feature-level contrastive learning strategy to enhance the extraction of subject-invariant semantic representations, improving applicability to previously unseen subjects.
  • Achieves significantly faster reconstruction (10 seconds per video) without any retraining, compared to conventional pipelines requiring over 12 hours of per-subject data and heavy computation.
  • Demonstrates high efficiency and scalability, sacrificing only 7% accuracy on average while providing substantial benefits in speed and generalizability.
  • Holds great potential for clinical applications by enabling rapid and accessible brain visual decoding without the need for extensive individual patient data.

Methodology

The Visual Cortex Flow Architecture (VCFlow) is a hierarchical deep learning framework. It's designed to mimic the human visual system by explicitly modeling the ventral-dorsal streams. VCFlow disentangles and utilizes features from the early visual cortex, ventral stream, and dorsal stream. To enhance cross-subject generalization, it incorporates a feature-level contrastive learning strategy, which helps extract subject-invariant semantic representations from fMRI data.

Key Findings

VCFlow successfully reconstructs continuous visual experiences from fMRI for previously unseen subjects without requiring subject-specific training. It generates each reconstructed video in approximately 10 seconds, a substantial improvement over conventional methods. This efficiency comes with only a 7% average accuracy sacrifice compared to traditional pipelines that demand over 12 hours of per-subject data and heavy computation.

Clinical Impact

VCFlow offers a rapid and clinically scalable solution for brain visual decoding, significantly reducing the data collection burden and computational resources typically associated with fMRI-based reconstructions. Its subject-agnostic nature makes it directly applicable to diverse patient populations, potentially enabling faster diagnosis, enhancing communication for individuals with severe motor or speech impairments through novel brain-computer interfaces, and facilitating rehabilitation strategies where individualized, extensive training data is impractical.

Limitations

The abstract indicates a trade-off where VCFlow sacrifices only 7% accuracy on average compared to conventional, computationally intensive and subject-specific pipelines. While framed as a minor cost for significant gains in speed and generalizability, it implies a slight reduction in reconstruction fidelity compared to highly individualized methods.

Abstract

Subject-agnostic brain decoding, which aims to reconstruct continuous visual experiences from fMRI without subject-specific training, holds great potential for clinical applications. However, this direction remains underexplored due to challenges in cross-subject generalization and the complex nature of brain signals. In this work, we propose Visual Cortex Flow Architecture (VCFlow), a novel hierarchical decoding framework that explicitly models the ventral-dorsal architecture of the human visual system to learn multi-dimensional representations. By disentangling and leveraging features from early visual cortex, ventral, and dorsal streams, VCFlow captures diverse and complementary cognitive information essential for visual reconstruction. Furthermore, we introduce a feature-level contrastive learning strategy to enhance the extraction of subject-invariant semantic representations, thereby enhancing subject-agnostic applicability to previously unseen subjects. Unlike conventional pipelines that need more than 12 hours of per-subject data and heavy computation, VCFlow sacrifices only 7\% accuracy on average yet generates each reconstructed video in 10 seconds without any retraining, offering a fast and clinically scalable solution. The source code will be released upon acceptance of the paper.
