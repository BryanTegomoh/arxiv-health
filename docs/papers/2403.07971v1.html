Evaluating the Impact of Vaccine Hesitancy on the Allocation of Vital Resources During COVID-19 Pandemic

Published: 2024-03-12
Authors: Hieu Bui, Sandra Eksioglu, Ruben Proano

Summary

This paper proposes an enhanced SEIR compartmental model for COVID-19 that integrates the impact of vaccine hesitancy (VH), vaccination, and healthcare resource availability on disease dynamics. The authors use publicly available data to simulate various VH scenarios, demonstrating that reducing VH, ensuring adequate critical resources, and implementing timely vaccination programs significantly improve health outcomes and minimize fatalities.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant as it quantifies the impact of behavioral factors like vaccine hesitancy and systemic factors like resource availability on public health outcomes during a pandemic. It provides an evidence-based framework for informing public health interventions, resource allocation strategies, and vaccination campaigns to mitigate disease burden and mortality.

Key Points

  • Existing epidemiological models for COVID-19 overlooked the critical influence of vaccine hesitancy (VH) on disease spread, hospitalization rates, and fatalities.
  • An improved SEIR compartmental model is introduced, explicitly incorporating vaccination rates, vaccine hesitancy dynamics, and the availability of healthcare resources into its framework.
  • Publicly available data was leveraged to analyze dynamic changes in VH over time, which then informed the development of specific VH scenario paths for model simulations.
  • The proposed model was simulated under various VH scenarios to evaluate how public attitudes toward vaccination, resource levels, and vaccination rollout timing affect epidemic progression and severity.
  • The analysis demonstrates a direct correlation between reduced vaccine hesitancy and improved health outcomes, underscoring the importance of public trust in vaccination efforts.

  • The study reinforces the life-saving efficacy of timely and well-executed vaccination programs in controlling infectious disease outbreaks.

Methodology

The authors developed an improved SEIR (Susceptible, Exposed, Infectious, Recovered) compartmental model specifically for COVID-19. This model was enhanced by incorporating explicit parameters for vaccination, vaccine hesitancy (VH), and critical healthcare resource availability. Publicly available data was collected and analyzed to model the dynamic changes in VH over time, which was then used to generate various VH scenario paths. The proposed compartmental model was simulated for each of these VH paths to assess their impact on disease progression, hospitalizations, fatalities, and the overall severity of the epidemic.

Abstract

The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted significant challenges in the allocation of vital healthcare resources. Existing epidemiological models, specifically compartmental models, aimed to predict the spread of the COVID-19 virus and its impact on the population, but they overlooked the influence of \ac{VH} on disease dynamics, including the expected number of hospitalizations and fatalities. We propose improvements to the \ac{SEIR} model for COVID-19 by incorporating the influence of vaccination, \ac{VH}, and resource availability on the disease dynamics. We collect publicly available data and perform data analysis to capture \ac{VH} dynamic changes over time and develop scenario paths for \ac{VH}. We simulate the proposed compartmental model for each \ac{VH} path to explain the impacts of public attitudes toward vaccination, the impacts of healthcare resources on patient outcomes, and the timing of vaccination rollout on the progression and severity of the epidemic. Our analysis demonstrates that reducing \ac{VH} improves health outcomes, reinforcing the importance of addressing \ac{VH} to curb the spread of infectious diseases. Our results show that adequate levels of critical healthcare resources are crucial for minimizing fatalities and also highlight the life-saving impact of timely and effective vaccination programs.
