Topological Conditioning for Mammography Models via a Stable Wavelet-Persistence Vectorization

arXiv ID: 2512.10151v1
Published: 2025-12-10
Authors: Charles Fanning, Mehmet Emin Aktas
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a novel conditioning signal, derived from wavelet-based vectorization of persistent homology, aimed at improving the external performance and generalizability of mammography models for breast cancer detection. By integrating these stable, multi-scale topological maps as input channels, the method significantly enhanced patient-level AUC from 0.55 to 0.75 on an independent digital mammography cohort (INbreast) when augmenting a ConvNeXt Tiny model, demonstrating increased robustness across diverse data sources.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant as it directly addresses current limitations in AI-assisted breast cancer screening by improving model generalizability and reducing false negatives/positives. Enhancing model performance across diverse real-world clinical data sources is crucial for the successful and equitable deployment of AI in medical diagnosis.

AI Health Application

The AI application focuses on developing a computational model to enhance the performance and robustness of breast cancer detection in mammography. By integrating 'wavelet persistence channels' through topological data analysis, the model aims to provide more stable and accurate interpretations of mammograms, thereby assisting radiologists and improving diagnostic outcomes for breast cancer screening.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical issue of deep learning model accuracy degradation when deployed across different mammography scanners, modalities (film vs. digital), and patient populations.
  • Proposes a 'conditioning signal' generated via Topological Data Analysis (TDA), specifically using a wavelet-based vectorization of persistent homology.
  • The signal summarizes image structure that persists across intensity thresholds, converting it into spatial, multi-scale maps that are provably stable to small intensity perturbations.
  • These topological maps are integrated into a two-stage detection pipeline by concatenating them as additional input channels to a convolutional neural network (e.g., ConvNeXt Tiny).
  • The model was trained on the CBIS DDSM (digitized film mammography, US) and rigorously evaluated on two independent full-field digital mammography cohorts from different regions: INbreast (Portugal) and CMMD (China), emphasizing external validation.
  • A key finding is a substantial increase in patient-level AUC on the INbreast cohort, from 0.55 to 0.75, when augmenting ConvNeXt Tiny with these wavelet persistence channels, achieved even under a limited training budget.
  • The approach offers a promising direction for enhancing the robustness and clinical applicability of AI models in medical imaging by leveraging topological features.

Methodology

The methodology involves generating a 'conditioning signal' using a wavelet-based vectorization of persistent homology, a technique from Topological Data Analysis (TDA). This process extracts stable, multi-scale representations of image structure that persist across various intensity thresholds. These topological maps are then concatenated as additional input channels to a deep learning model (e.g., ConvNeXt Tiny) within a two-stage detection pipeline. The model is trained on a US digitized film mammography dataset (CBIS DDSM) and evaluated for external performance on independent full-field digital mammography cohorts from Portugal (INbreast) and China (CMMD), with patient-level AUC as the primary metric.

Key Findings

The primary finding is that augmenting a ConvNeXt Tiny model with the proposed wavelet-persistence conditioning channels significantly improves its external performance. Specifically, patient-level AUC increased from 0.55 to 0.75 on the independent INbreast digital mammography cohort, demonstrating enhanced generalizability and robustness under a limited training budget.

Clinical Impact

This research has the potential to significantly improve the accuracy and reliability of AI tools for breast cancer screening, leading to earlier detection, fewer missed cancers (reduced false negatives), and potentially fewer unnecessary recalls or biopsies (reduced false positives). By making AI models more robust to variations in imaging equipment and patient populations, it facilitates broader and more trustworthy integration of AI into global clinical practice, particularly beneficial for diverse healthcare settings.

Limitations

The abstract mentions the evaluation was conducted 'under a limited training budget,' which might imply potential for further improvement with more extensive training, but also suggests the method's efficiency. As an abstract, it does not explicitly detail other inherent limitations of the proposed method or the study design, such as specific computational overhead, generalizability beyond mammography, or detailed analysis of different types of topological features.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated in the abstract, logical future directions would include: evaluating the method on larger and more diverse multi-centric datasets; exploring the integration with other advanced deep learning architectures and tasks (e.g., segmentation, risk prediction); investigating the optimal parameters for wavelet-persistence vectorization; and conducting prospective clinical studies to validate the real-world impact on patient outcomes and radiologist workflow.

Abstract

Breast cancer is the most commonly diagnosed cancer in women and a leading cause of cancer death worldwide. Screening mammography reduces mortality, yet interpretation still suffers from substantial false negatives and false positives, and model accuracy often degrades when deployed across scanners, modalities, and patient populations. We propose a simple conditioning signal aimed at improving external performance based on a wavelet based vectorization of persistent homology. Using topological data analysis, we summarize image structure that persists across intensity thresholds and convert this information into spatial, multi scale maps that are provably stable to small intensity perturbations. These maps are integrated into a two stage detection pipeline through input level channel concatenation. The model is trained and validated on the CBIS DDSM digitized film mammography cohort from the United States and evaluated on two independent full field digital mammography cohorts from Portugal (INbreast) and China (CMMD), with performance reported at the patient level. On INbreast, augmenting ConvNeXt Tiny with wavelet persistence channels increases patient level AUC from 0.55 to 0.75 under a limited training budget.
