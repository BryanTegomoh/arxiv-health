Towards Automated Diagnosis of Inherited Arrhythmias: Combined Arrhythmia Classification Using Lead-Aware Spatial Attention Networks

Published: 2026-01-12
Authors: Sophie Sigfstead, River Jiang, Brianna Davies, Zachary W. M. Laksman, Julia Cadrin-Tourigny, Rafik Tadros, Habib Khan, Joseph Atallah, Christian Steinberg, Shubhayan Sanatani, Mario Talajic, Rahul Krishnan, Andrew D. Krahn, Christopher C. Cheung

Summary

This paper developed and validated a novel lead-aware deep learning framework, Lead-Aware Spatial Attention Networks (LASAN), for the automated multi-class classification of inherited arrhythmia syndromes (Arrhythmogenic Right Ventricular Cardiomyopathy (ARVC), Long QT Syndrome (LQTS), and control) from ECGs. By optimally integrating LASAN with ECG foundation models via fine-tuning, the framework achieved state-of-the-art diagnostic performance (macro-AUROC 0.990) and demonstrated clinically plausible lead dependence, supporting its potential for automated ECG screening. It outperforms all existing published models for both binary and multi-class inherited arrhythmia classification.

Medical Relevance

Inherited arrhythmias like ARVC and LQTS are life-threatening conditions associated with sudden cardiac death, making early and accurate diagnosis paramount. This research provides a highly accurate, automated, and clinically interpretable ECG-based screening tool that could significantly improve early identification, enabling timely interventions and potentially reducing morbidity and mortality.

AI Health Application

The AI application is an automated deep learning framework designed for multi-class and binary classification of inherited arrhythmias (ARVC vs LQTS vs control) from ECGs. This serves as an automated diagnostic and screening tool to identify patients at risk, potentially preventing sudden cardiac death.

Key Points

  • The study addressed the challenge of multi-class inherited arrhythmia classification (ARVC vs LQTS vs control) from ECGs with a focus on clinically grounded interpretability.
  • A 13-center Canadian cohort comprising 645 patients and 1,344 ECGs was assembled for model development and validation.
  • The methodology involved evaluating four existing ECG foundation models and three transfer learning approaches (linear probing, fine-tuning, combined), alongside the development of novel Lead-Aware Spatial Attention Networks (LASAN).
  • Fine-tuning was identified as the optimal transfer learning strategy, outperforming linear probing and combined approaches across all foundation models (mean macro-AUROC 0.904 vs 0.825).
  • The best lead-aware integration, specifically with the HuBERT-ECG hybrid model, achieved near-ceiling performance: macro-AUROC 0.990 for multi-class, 0.999 for ARVC vs control, and 0.994 for LQTS vs control.
  • Lead-group masking provided critical interpretability, showing V1-V3 leads were most critical for ARVC detection (4.54% AUROC reduction upon masking), while lateral leads were preferentially important for LQTS (2.60% AUROC drop), aligning with known disease pathophysiology.
  • The developed lead-aware architectures set a new state-of-the-art benchmark, surpassing all previously published models for both binary and multi-class inherited arrhythmia classification tasks.

Methodology

The study employed deep learning, evaluating four pre-trained ECG foundation models with three transfer learning strategies: linear probing, fine-tuning, and combined approaches. A novel Lead-Aware Spatial Attention Network (LASAN) was developed and integrated with these foundation models. Performance was benchmarked using macro-AUROC for multi-class tasks and AUROC for binary tasks. Model interpretability was assessed through 'lead-group masking,' quantifying the impact of specific ECG lead groups on diagnostic accuracy.

Key Findings

Fine-tuning was found to be the superior transfer learning strategy (mean macro-AUROC 0.904). The best lead-aware integration, particularly combining LASAN with the HuBERT-ECG foundation model, achieved exceptional performance: macro-AUROC 0.990 for multi-class classification, with binary AUROCs of 0.999 for ARVC vs control and 0.994 for LQTS vs control. Lead masking confirmed physiological plausibility by demonstrating V1-V3 leads are crucial for ARVC detection (4.54% AUROC reduction upon masking), and lateral leads are preferentially important for LQTS (2.60% AUROC drop).

Clinical Impact

This research provides a powerful, highly accurate, and interpretable automated ECG screening tool for inherited arrhythmias. Its potential to detect ARVC and LQTS earlier and more reliably could revolutionize preventative care, facilitating prompt genetic testing, risk stratification, and the implementation of life-saving therapies (e.g., ICD implantation, lifestyle modifications) before critical events occur, ultimately reducing sudden cardiac death rates.

Limitations

The abstract explicitly states that the findings support potential utility 'pending validation,' indicating that while the model performed excellently on the assembled cohort, its generalizability and robustness still need to be confirmed on independent, external datasets.

Future Directions

The primary future direction highlighted is the need for external validation of the automated ECG screening tool on independent cohorts to confirm its generalizability and robustness in diverse real-world clinical settings.

Abstract

Arrhythmogenic right ventricular cardiomyopathy (ARVC) and long QT syndrome (LQTS) are inherited arrhythmia syndromes associated with sudden cardiac death. Deep learning shows promise for ECG interpretation, but multi-class inherited arrhythmia classification with clinically grounded interpretability remains underdeveloped. Our objective was to develop and validate a lead-aware deep learning framework for multi-class (ARVC vs LQTS vs control) and binary inherited arrhythmia classification, and to determine optimal strategies for integrating ECG foundation models within arrhythmia screening tools. We assembled a 13-center Canadian cohort (645 patients; 1,344 ECGs). We evaluated four ECG foundation models using three transfer learning approaches: linear probing, fine-tuning, and combined strategies. We developed lead-aware spatial attention networks (LASAN) and assessed integration strategies combining LASAN with foundation models. Performance was compared against the established foundation model baselines. Lead-group masking quantified disease-specific lead dependence. Fine-tuning outperformed linear probing and combined strategies across all foundation models (mean macro-AUROC 0.904 vs 0.825). The best lead-aware integrations achieved near-ceiling performance (HuBERT-ECG hybrid: macro-AUROC 0.990; ARVC vs control AUROC 0.999; LQTS vs control AUROC 0.994). Lead masking demonstrated physiologic plausibility: V1-V3 were most critical for ARVC detection (4.54% AUROC reduction), while lateral leads were preferentially important for LQTS (2.60% drop). Lead-aware architectures achieved state-of-the-art performance for inherited arrhythmia classification, outperforming all existing published models on both binary and multi-class tasks while demonstrating clinically aligned lead dependence. These findings support potential utility for automated ECG screening pending validation.
