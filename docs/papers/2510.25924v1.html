Summary

This paper addresses the problem of estimating causal effects in multi-domain settings where an unobserved confounder exists and the effect may vary across domains. It proposes a novel methodology that leverages an observable proxy for the hidden confounder, demonstrating identifiability of the causal effect and introducing two consistent estimation techniques with derived confidence intervals.

Medical Relevance

This methodology is highly relevant to medicine as unobserved confounders (e.g., genetic factors, unmeasured environmental exposures, socio-economic status) are common in clinical and epidemiological studies, making direct causal inference challenging. It offers a robust framework to estimate treatment effects or intervention impacts across different patient populations or healthcare settings (domains) by utilizing readily available proxy measures (e.g., biomarkers, administrative data indicators), improving the generalizability and reliability of research findings.

AI Health Application

This research can be applied to develop more sophisticated and reliable medical AI systems that can: 1) Estimate the causal effect of treatments or interventions on patient outcomes, even when key patient characteristics (confounders) are unobserved but can be proxied by other available data (e.g., biomarkers, clinical scores). 2) Transfer causal findings or treatment guidelines learned from one healthcare domain (e.g., a specific hospital's EHR data) to another, accounting for potential differences in patient populations or clinical practices. 3) Build AI models that provide personalized treatment recommendations by understanding how causal effects vary across different patient subgroups and handling latent confounding factors. 4) Improve the rigor of epidemiological studies by providing tools to infer causality from observational data, addressing common challenges like selection bias and measurement error through proxy variables.

Abstract

We consider the problem of estimating a causal effect in a multi-domain setting. The causal effect of interest is confounded by an unobserved confounder and can change between the different domains. We assume that we have access to a proxy of the hidden confounder and that all variables are discrete or categorical. We propose methodology to estimate the causal effect in the target domain, where we assume to observe only the proxy variable. Under these conditions, we prove identifiability (even when treatment and response variables are continuous). We introduce two estimation techniques, prove consistency, and derive confidence intervals. The theoretical results are supported by simulation studies and a real-world example studying the causal effect of website rankings on consumer choices.
