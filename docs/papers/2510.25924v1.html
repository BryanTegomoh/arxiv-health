Summary

This paper addresses the challenging problem of estimating a causal effect in multi-domain settings where the true confounder is unobserved and the causal effect may vary across domains. It proposes a novel methodology that leverages a proxy variable of the hidden confounder to achieve identifiability and consistent estimation of the causal effect in a target domain, even when only the proxy is observable there.

Medical Relevance

This methodology is highly relevant for medicine and health research by enabling robust causal inference in complex real-world settings where key confounders are often unmeasured, and studies span diverse patient populations or clinical environments, potentially leading to varied treatment effects.

AI Health Application

This methodology can be applied to develop more sophisticated and reliable AI systems in medicine. For instance, it could be used to: 1) Estimate the causal effect of a new drug or treatment on patient outcomes, even when relevant patient characteristics are unobserved and trial results need to be generalized to different patient populations. 2) Evaluate the true impact of public health interventions or policies across various regions or demographics by accounting for unmeasured confounding factors. 3) Build AI models that provide more accurate and generalizable predictions of disease risk or treatment response by properly adjusting for unobserved confounders and adapting to different healthcare data sources.

Key Points

  • Addresses causal effect estimation in multi-domain scenarios, accounting for unobserved confounding and potential shifts in causal effects between domains.
  • Introduces the use of a proxy variable for the unobserved confounder as a core mechanism to enable causal inference.
  • Proves identifiability of the causal effect under these conditions, a crucial theoretical contribution, even when treatment and response variables are continuous.
  • Proposes two specific estimation techniques tailored for scenarios where variables are discrete or categorical, providing practical methods for application.
  • Demonstrates the robustness of the proposed methods by proving their consistency and deriving confidence intervals for the estimated causal effects.

The paper also establishes two consistent estimation techniques with confidence intervals, which are validated through simulations and a real-world application, demonstrating the practical feasibility of causal inference under these challenging conditions.

Abstract

We consider the problem of estimating a causal effect in a multi-domain setting. The causal effect of interest is confounded by an unobserved confounder and can change between the different domains. We assume that we have access to a proxy of the hidden confounder and that all variables are discrete or categorical. We propose methodology to estimate the causal effect in the target domain, where we assume to observe only the proxy variable. Under these conditions, we prove identifiability (even when treatment and response variables are continuous). We introduce two estimation techniques, prove consistency, and derive confidence intervals. The theoretical results are supported by simulation studies and a real-world example studying the causal effect of website rankings on consumer choices.
