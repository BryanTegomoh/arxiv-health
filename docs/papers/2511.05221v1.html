Summary

ActiTect is an open-source, automated machine learning pipeline designed for screening REM sleep behavior disorder (RBD) using standardized actigraphy data from wearable devices. The tool ensures generalizability through robust preprocessing and feature extraction from multi-device data, demonstrating strong discriminatory power (AUROC 0.95) and consistent generalization (AUROC 0.84-0.94) across internal and external cohorts. This advancement facilitates early, non-invasive detection of a major prodromal marker for alpha-synucleinopathies.

Medical Relevance

Early and accurate detection of isolated REM sleep behavior disorder (iRBD) is critical because it serves as a major prodromal marker for severe alpha-synucleinopathies such as Parkinson's disease, dementia with Lewy bodies, and multiple system atrophy, enabling earlier diagnosis, monitoring, and potential therapeutic interventions.

Methodology

The study developed ActiTect, an automated machine learning pipeline. It involved robust preprocessing to harmonize multi-device actigraphy data, automated sleep-wake detection, and extraction of physiologically interpretable nocturnal motion features. Model development utilized nested cross-validation on an internal cohort, followed by validation on a blinded local test set and two independent external cohorts. Real-world robustness was assessed via leave-one-dataset-out cross-validation, and stability analysis confirmed feature reproducibility.

Key Findings

ActiTect demonstrated strong discriminative performance for RBD detection, achieving an AUROC of 0.95 during development. The model exhibited excellent generalization across diverse settings, with AUROC values ranging from 0.84 to 0.94 on independent local and external validation cohorts. Crucially, leave-one-dataset-out cross-validation confirmed consistent real-world robustness (AUROC range 0.84-0.89), and key predictive features were found to be reproducible across datasets, supporting the reliability of the pooled multi-center model.

Clinical Impact

ActiTect offers a highly valuable, scalable, and non-invasive screening tool for iRBD using readily available wrist-worn actimeters. Its demonstrated generalizability and open-source availability can significantly accelerate early identification of individuals at high risk for developing debilitating neurodegenerative diseases, facilitating timely clinical assessment, enrollment in disease-modifying trials, and personalized disease management strategies.

Abstract

Isolated rapid eye movement sleep behavior disorder (iRBD) is a major prodromal marker of $\alpha$-synucleinopathies, often preceding the clinical onset of Parkinson's disease, dementia with Lewy bodies, or multiple system atrophy. While wrist-worn actimeters hold significant potential for detecting RBD in large-scale screening efforts by capturing abnormal nocturnal movements, they become inoperable without a reliable and efficient analysis pipeline. This study presents ActiTect, a fully automated, open-source machine learning tool to identify RBD from actigraphy recordings. To ensure generalizability across heterogeneous acquisition settings, our pipeline includes robust preprocessing and automated sleep-wake detection to harmonize multi-device data and extract physiologically interpretable motion features characterizing activity patterns. Model development was conducted on a cohort of 78 individuals, yielding strong discrimination under nested cross-validation (AUROC = 0.95). Generalization was confirmed on a blinded local test set (n = 31, AUROC = 0.86) and on two independent external cohorts (n = 113, AUROC = 0.84; n = 57, AUROC = 0.94). To assess real-world robustness, leave-one-dataset-out cross-validation across the internal and external cohorts demonstrated consistent performance (AUROC range = 0.84-0.89). A complementary stability analysis showed that key predictive features remained reproducible across datasets, supporting the final pooled multi-center model as a robust pre-trained resource for broader deployment. By being open-source and easy to use, our tool promotes widespread adoption and facilitates independent validation and collaborative improvements, thereby advancing the field toward a unified and generalizable RBD detection model using wearable devices.
