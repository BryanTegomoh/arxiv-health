Blood Pressure Prediction for Coronary Artery Disease Diagnosis using Coronary Computed Tomography Angiography

arXiv ID: 2512.10765v1
Published: 2025-12-11
Authors: Rene Lisasi, Michele Esposito, Chen Zhao
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00

Summary

This paper introduces an end-to-end pipeline that automates coronary geometry extraction from CCTA and streamlines the generation of simulated hemodynamic data. It then develops a diffusion-based regression model to predict coronary blood pressure directly from CCTA-derived features, bypassing computationally expensive CFD simulations. The model achieved state-of-the-art performance on a simulated dataset, offering a rapid and non-invasive approach for coronary artery disease diagnosis.

Medical Relevance

Coronary artery disease (CAD) diagnosis often relies on hemodynamic markers like pressure gradients, which are typically obtained through invasive procedures or computationally intensive simulations. This research offers a non-invasive, rapid, and scalable AI-driven method to derive crucial hemodynamic information from standard CCTA scans, potentially improving diagnostic accuracy, patient safety, and accessibility to advanced physiological assessment.

AI Health Application

The research develops an AI-driven, diffusion-based regression model to predict coronary blood pressure directly from CCTA-derived features. This AI application aims to rapidly and non-invasively assess coronary hemodynamics, supporting the diagnosis of Coronary Artery Disease and bypassing the need for computationally expensive and time-consuming traditional computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations in clinical settings.

Key Points

  • Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) for Coronary Artery Disease (CAD) diagnosis is computationally expensive and time-consuming, limiting its clinical integration and the availability of data for AI model training.
  • An automated end-to-end pipeline is developed to efficiently extract coronary geometry from Coronary Computed Tomography Angiography (CCTA) and generate consistent simulated hemodynamic training data.
  • The pipeline significantly reduces the manual burden associated with traditional CFD workflows, thereby enabling more consistent and scalable data generation.
  • A novel diffusion-based regression model is introduced to predict coronary blood pressure directly from CCTA-derived features, circumventing the need for slow CFD computations during diagnostic inference.
  • Evaluated on a dataset of simulated coronary hemodynamics, the proposed model achieved state-of-the-art performance with an R2 of 64.42%, a root mean squared error (RMSE) of 0.0974, and a normalized RMSE of 0.154.
  • The model outperformed several baseline approaches, demonstrating superior predictive accuracy for coronary blood pressure distributions.
  • This work provides a scalable and accessible framework designed for rapid, non-invasive blood pressure prediction to support CAD diagnosis.

Methodology

The methodology involves an end-to-end pipeline that first automates the extraction of coronary artery geometry from Coronary Computed Tomography Angiography (CCTA) scans. This geometry is then used to streamline the generation of a consistent dataset of simulated coronary hemodynamics, which serves as training data. Finally, a diffusion-based regression model is trained to predict coronary blood pressure distributions directly from the CCTA-derived features, aiming to bypass the need for traditional, slow CFD computations during clinical inference.

Key Findings

The proposed diffusion-based regression model demonstrated state-of-the-art performance in predicting coronary blood pressure, achieving an R2 of 64.42%, a root mean squared error (RMSE) of 0.0974, and a normalized RMSE of 0.154. These results indicate high predictive accuracy and superior performance compared to several baseline approaches when evaluated on a dataset of simulated coronary hemodynamics.

Clinical Impact

This framework has the potential to significantly enhance and streamline the diagnosis of Coronary Artery Disease by enabling rapid, non-invasive, and physiology-based assessment of coronary blood pressure. It could reduce the reliance on invasive procedures like cardiac catheterization or time-consuming CFD simulations, making advanced hemodynamic analysis more accessible, improving patient risk stratification, and integrating more seamlessly into routine clinical workflows.

Limitations

The primary limitation noted is that the model's performance was evaluated on a dataset of *simulated* coronary hemodynamics. This means its accuracy and generalizability have not yet been validated against real patient-specific hemodynamic measurements or clinical outcomes, which may differ from simulation data.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated as future directions, the implication of providing a "scalable and accessible framework" suggests that future work would likely involve rigorous clinical validation against real patient data, including comparison with invasive physiological measurements (e.g., fractional flow reserve, FFR) and correlation with patient outcomes. Further development could focus on integrating the system into clinical Picture Archiving and Communication Systems (PACS) and conducting large-scale, multi-center trials to confirm its diagnostic utility and impact.

Abstract

Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) based simulation of coronary blood flow provides valuable hemodynamic markers, such as pressure gradients, for diagnosing coronary artery disease (CAD). However, CFD is computationally expensive, time-consuming, and difficult to integrate into large-scale clinical workflows. These limitations restrict the availability of labeled hemodynamic data for training AI models and hinder broad adoption of non-invasive, physiology based CAD assessment. To address these challenges, we develop an end to end pipeline that automates coronary geometry extraction from coronary computed tomography angiography (CCTA), streamlines simulation data generation, and enables efficient learning of coronary blood pressure distributions. The pipeline reduces the manual burden associated with traditional CFD workflows while producing consistent training data. We further introduce a diffusion-based regression model designed to predict coronary blood pressure directly from CCTA derived features, bypassing the need for slow CFD computation during inference. Evaluated on a dataset of simulated coronary hemodynamics, the proposed model achieves state of the art performance, with an R2 of 64.42%, a root mean squared error of 0.0974, and a normalized RMSE of 0.154, outperforming several baseline approaches. This work provides a scalable and accessible framework for rapid, non-invasive blood pressure prediction to support CAD diagnosis.
