Blood Pressure Prediction for Coronary Artery Disease Diagnosis using Coronary Computed Tomography Angiography

arXiv ID: 2512.10765v1
Published: 2025-12-11
Authors: Rene Lisasi, Michele Esposito, Chen Zhao
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00

Summary

This paper introduces an end-to-end pipeline for automated coronary geometry extraction and simulation data generation, coupled with a novel diffusion-based regression model, to predict coronary blood pressure directly from CCTA-derived features. This approach aims to overcome the computational and time-intensive limitations of traditional CFD for diagnosing Coronary Artery Disease (CAD). The model achieved state-of-the-art performance on simulated hemodynamic data, offering a rapid, non-invasive method for CAD assessment.

Medical Relevance

This research offers a critical advancement for diagnosing Coronary Artery Disease by providing a fast, non-invasive, and scalable method to assess coronary blood pressure, which is a key hemodynamic marker, without the prohibitive costs and time of traditional CFD simulations. This could lead to earlier and more accurate patient stratification and treatment decisions.

AI Health Application

The paper proposes a diffusion-based regression AI model that takes CCTA-derived features as input to predict coronary blood pressure distributions. This AI application aims to provide rapid, non-invasive blood pressure predictions, circumventing computationally expensive CFD simulations, thereby supporting and accelerating the diagnosis of Coronary Artery Disease in clinical settings.

Key Points

  • Traditional Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) for coronary blood flow analysis, valuable for CAD diagnosis, is computationally expensive, time-consuming, and difficult to integrate into clinical workflows, limiting AI training data.
  • An end-to-end pipeline was developed to automate coronary geometry extraction from Coronary Computed Tomography Angiography (CCTA) and streamline simulation data generation, reducing manual effort and ensuring consistent training data.
  • A novel diffusion-based regression model is introduced to predict coronary blood pressure distributions directly from CCTA-derived features, thereby bypassing the need for slow CFD computations during clinical inference.
  • The model was evaluated on a dataset comprising simulated coronary hemodynamics.
  • It achieved state-of-the-art performance metrics: an R2 of 64.42%, a root mean squared error (RMSE) of 0.0974, and a normalized RMSE of 0.154.
  • The proposed diffusion-based model significantly outperformed several baseline approaches in predicting coronary blood pressure.
  • This work establishes a scalable and accessible framework for rapid, non-invasive blood pressure prediction to support Coronary Artery Disease diagnosis.

Methodology

The methodology involves an end-to-end pipeline that first automates the extraction of coronary geometry from Coronary Computed Tomography Angiography (CCTA) scans. This geometry is then used to streamline the generation of simulation data, likely through existing CFD tools, to create a consistent dataset for training. A key component is the introduction of a diffusion-based regression model, an AI technique, specifically designed to learn and predict coronary blood pressure distributions directly from the CCTA-derived geometric features, thus bypassing the need for computationally intensive CFD simulations during clinical inference. The model's performance was evaluated against simulated coronary hemodynamics.

Key Findings

The proposed diffusion-based regression model demonstrated state-of-the-art performance in predicting coronary blood pressure, achieving an R2 of 64.42%, a root mean squared error (RMSE) of 0.0974, and a normalized RMSE of 0.154. These metrics indicate a high degree of accuracy and predictive power, significantly outperforming several baseline approaches tested on a dataset of simulated coronary hemodynamics.

Clinical Impact

This framework has the potential to significantly improve the clinical workflow for Coronary Artery Disease (CAD) diagnosis by offering a rapid, scalable, and non-invasive means to assess coronary hemodynamics. By providing accurate blood pressure predictions without the time and cost barriers of traditional CFD, it can facilitate broader adoption of physiology-based CAD assessment, potentially leading to earlier and more precise diagnostic decisions, better patient risk stratification, and optimized treatment planning.

Limitations

While addressing the limitations of traditional CFD, the proposed model's training and evaluation relied on a dataset of *simulated* coronary hemodynamics, rather than direct in-vivo patient measurements. This suggests a need for robust clinical validation with real-world patient data.

Future Directions

The abstract implicitly points towards future efforts in clinical validation, integration into broader clinical workflows for routine CAD diagnosis, and potentially expanding the framework to predict other critical hemodynamic markers to support broader adoption of non-invasive, physiology-based CAD assessment.

Abstract

Computational fluid dynamics (CFD) based simulation of coronary blood flow provides valuable hemodynamic markers, such as pressure gradients, for diagnosing coronary artery disease (CAD). However, CFD is computationally expensive, time-consuming, and difficult to integrate into large-scale clinical workflows. These limitations restrict the availability of labeled hemodynamic data for training AI models and hinder broad adoption of non-invasive, physiology based CAD assessment. To address these challenges, we develop an end to end pipeline that automates coronary geometry extraction from coronary computed tomography angiography (CCTA), streamlines simulation data generation, and enables efficient learning of coronary blood pressure distributions. The pipeline reduces the manual burden associated with traditional CFD workflows while producing consistent training data. We further introduce a diffusion-based regression model designed to predict coronary blood pressure directly from CCTA derived features, bypassing the need for slow CFD computation during inference. Evaluated on a dataset of simulated coronary hemodynamics, the proposed model achieves state of the art performance, with an R2 of 64.42%, a root mean squared error of 0.0974, and a normalized RMSE of 0.154, outperforming several baseline approaches. This work provides a scalable and accessible framework for rapid, non-invasive blood pressure prediction to support CAD diagnosis.
