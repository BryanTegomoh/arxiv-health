Wide Area Surface Dosimetry with Conformal Scintillator Array for External Beam Radiotherapy

Summary

This paper presents a novel wide-area conformable scintillator array imaging system designed for in vivo dosimetry in external beam radiotherapy. The system demonstrates the capability to generate spatially resolved, dynamic surface dose maps across complex anatomical surfaces with high linearity, accuracy, and robust reproducibility, addressing key limitations of existing dosimetry techniques.

Medical Relevance

Accurate in vivo dosimetry is critical for verifying treatment delivery in modern radiotherapy, especially for non-uniform anatomies where current techniques often lack sufficient spatiotemporal resolution. This conformable scintillator array system offers a significant advancement by providing real-time, high-resolution surface dose mapping, thereby enhancing patient safety, treatment efficacy, and quality assurance in radiation oncology.

Key Points

  • A modular scintillator array is combined with a dual-camera stereovision system for 3D localization and a clinical Cherenkov imaging system for optical scintillation detection.
  • The system exhibits high dose linearity across the tested range, minimal beam perturbation, and consistent response (within 5%) across all available LINAC repetition rates.
  • Dose-normalized scintillation varied within 5% over clinically relevant gantry angles, with camera angle corrections successfully compensating for non-Lambertian emission.
  • Field edge detection agreed closely with radiochromic film, achieving a high gamma pass rate of 99.98% (3%/3 mm).
  • Inter-delivery reproducibility of +/-1 cGy was demonstrated, showcasing robust system performance during simulations of contralateral breast monitoring.
  • The system provides spatially resolved, dynamic surface dosimetry with minimal workflow impact, capable of generating continuous dose maps across complex anatomical surfaces while maintaining angular correction.

Methodology

The study utilized a modular scintillator array integrated with a dual-camera stereovision system for 3D localization and a clinical Cherenkov imaging system for optical scintillation detection. System performance was characterized by evaluating water equivalent thickness, dose linearity, repetition rate stability, and angular dependence. Field edge detection accuracy was compared against radiochromic film using gamma analysis, and inter-delivery reproducibility was assessed through consecutive radiation deliveries simulating contralateral breast monitoring during whole-breast tangential radiotherapy.

Key Findings

The scintillator array demonstrated high dose linearity and caused minimal beam perturbation. A consistent dose response within 5% was observed across all tested LINAC repetition rates. Dose-normalized scintillation varied within 5% over clinically relevant gantry angles, effectively corrected for non-Lambertian emission. Field edge detection showed excellent agreement with film (99.98% gamma pass rate at 3%/3 mm), and inter-delivery reproducibility was robust at +/-1 cGy.

Clinical Impact

This conformable scintillator array imaging system has strong potential for clinical integration in external beam radiotherapy. It can enable spatially resolved, dynamic surface dosimetry across complex anatomical regions, improving treatment verification, particularly for challenging sites like the breast, head and neck, or extremities. Its minimal workflow impact and ability to maintain angular correction could lead to enhanced patient safety and better quality assurance in routine clinical practice where wide-area surface dosimetry is crucial.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state specific technical limitations of the developed system. However, the conclusion's phrasing of "shows strong potential for clinical integration" implies that the system is currently in a research or pre-clinical validation stage and has not yet undergone extensive clinical trials or widespread implementation for routine use.

Future Directions

The paper indicates a strong potential for clinical integration. Therefore, future directions would likely involve further pre-clinical and clinical validation, larger-scale studies, potential refinement of the system for practical workflow, and efforts towards commercialization and widespread adoption in radiotherapy departments requiring advanced wide-area surface dosimetry.

Abstract

Background: In vivo dosimetry is essential for treatment verification in modern radiotherapy, but existing techniques are limited by spatiotemporal resolution and performance on non-uniform anatomy. Scintillation imaging dosimetry shows potential to address several of these limitations. Here, translation to conventional photon external beam radiotherapy was examined using a novel wide-area imaging and sensing technique. Methods: A modular scintillator array was combined with a dual-camera stereovision system for 3D localization and a clinical Cherenkov imaging system for optical scintillation detection. Water equivalent thickness, dose linearity, repetition rate stability, and angular dependence were characterized. Field edge detection was evaluated against radiochromic film using gamma analysis. System reproducibility was tested with consecutive deliveries simulating contralateral breast monitoring during whole-breast tangential radiotherapy. Results: The array showed high dose linearity across the tested range, minimal beam perturbation, and consistent response within 5% across all available LINAC repetition rates. Dose-normalized scintillation varied within 5% over clinically relevant gantry angles, and camera angle corrections compensated for non-Lambertian emission. Field edge detection agreed closely with film, with a 99.98% gamma pass rate (3%/3 mm). Inter-delivery reproducibility of +/-1 cGy demonstrated robust system performance. Conclusion: The conformable scintillator array imaging system provides spatially resolved, dynamic surface dosimetry with minimal workflow impact. Its ability to generate continuous dose maps across complex anatomical surfaces, while maintaining angular correction, addresses key limitations of current in vivo dosimetry and shows strong potential for clinical integration where wide-area surface dosimetry is required.
