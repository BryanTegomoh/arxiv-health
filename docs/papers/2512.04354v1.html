Summary

SmartAlert is an ML-driven clinical decision support system integrated into the EHR designed to predict stable lab results and reduce unnecessary repeat inpatient testing. A randomized controlled pilot focusing on CBC utilization demonstrated a significant 15% relative reduction in repetitive testing (1.54 vs 1.82 CBCs within 52 hours, p <0.01) without adverse safety outcomes. The study also provided critical implementation lessons for deploying complex ML models in clinical environments.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a concrete, data-driven solution to a significant healthcare challenge: over-utilization of laboratory tests. Reducing unnecessary testing can lead to substantial cost savings, minimize patient discomfort from repeated phlebotomy, decrease the risk of iatrogenic anemia, and improve the efficiency of clinical workflows without compromising patient safety.

AI Health Application

The AI application is 'SmartAlert', a machine learning (ML)-driven clinical decision support (CDS) system that predicts stable laboratory results (specifically CBCs) to provide precision guidance to clinicians and reduce unnecessary repetitive inpatient testing within a hospital setting.

Methodology

The study employed a randomized controlled pilot design to evaluate SmartAlert. The intervention was an ML-driven CDS system integrated into the EHR that generated alerts predicting stable laboratory results. It specifically targeted complete blood count (CBC) utilization in inpatient settings. The pilot involved 9270 admissions across eight acute care units in two hospitals over a seven-month period (August 15, 2024, to March 15, 2025). The primary outcome measured was the number of CBC results within 52 hours of SmartAlert display, with secondary safety outcomes also rigorously monitored. The implementation process, challenges, and lessons learned were comprehensively documented as a case study component.

Key Findings

SmartAlert resulted in a statistically significant 15% relative reduction in the number of CBC results performed within 52 hours of its display (mean 1.54 in the intervention group compared to 1.82 in the control group, p <0.01). This reduction in testing was achieved safely, with no adverse effects reported on secondary safety outcomes. Furthermore, the implementation process yielded valuable insights concerning the challenges of deploying complex ML models in clinical environments, emphasizing the importance of factors like model interpretability, robust governance, comprehensive stakeholder engagement, and user-centered design.

Clinical Impact

SmartAlert offers a scalable and precise method to optimize inpatient laboratory utilization, directly translating to reduced healthcare costs and decreased patient burden from unnecessary phlebotomy, all while maintaining patient safety. The detailed implementation lessons provide a crucial blueprint for other institutions seeking to develop and deploy similar ML-powered CDS tools, facilitating a more efficient and patient-centered healthcare system by providing precision guidance at the point of care.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state specific limitations. However, as described as a "pilot," the study's scope may be limited to specific inpatient units, hospitals, or the single targeted lab test (CBC). Generalizability to other lab tests, different hospital systems, or diverse patient populations would require further investigation. The stated future dates (August 2024 - March 2025) suggest the paper describes a planned or ongoing pilot, meaning long-term sustainability, cost-effectiveness, and broader impacts beyond the pilot's duration are not yet evaluated.

These include refining methods for interpreting and communicating probabilistic ML predictions to clinicians, enhancing stakeholder engagement strategies for broader model acceptance, establishing scalable governance processes for complex ML deployments, continually optimizing user interface design for improved clinical integration, and expanding the SmartAlert approach to encompass other commonly utilized laboratory tests. Further research would also involve evaluating long-term effectiveness, cost-benefit analysis, and generalizability across diverse clinical settings and patient demographics.

Abstract

Repetitive laboratory testing unlikely to yield clinically useful information is a common practice that burdens patients and increases healthcare costs. Education and feedback interventions have limited success, while general test ordering restrictions and electronic alerts impede appropriate clinical care. We introduce and evaluate SmartAlert, a machine learning (ML)-driven clinical decision support (CDS) system integrated into the electronic health record that predicts stable laboratory results to reduce unnecessary repeat testing. This case study describes the implementation process, challenges, and lessons learned from deploying SmartAlert targeting complete blood count (CBC) utilization in a randomized controlled pilot across 9270 admissions in eight acute care units across two hospitals between August 15, 2024, and March 15, 2025. Results show significant decrease in number of CBC results within 52 hours of SmartAlert display (1.54 vs 1.82, p <0.01) without adverse effect on secondary safety outcomes, representing a 15% relative reduction in repetitive testing. Implementation lessons learned include interpretation of probabilistic model predictions in clinical contexts, stakeholder engagement to define acceptable model behavior, governance processes for deploying a complex model in a clinical environment, user interface design considerations, alignment with clinical operational priorities, and the value of qualitative feedback from end users. In conclusion, a machine learning-driven CDS system backed by a deliberate implementation and governance process can provide precision guidance on inpatient laboratory testing to safely reduce unnecessary repetitive testing.
