Summary

This paper introduces SmartAlert, a machine learning (ML)-driven clinical decision support (CDS) system integrated into the electronic health record to reduce unnecessary repeat laboratory testing in inpatients. A randomized controlled pilot targeting complete blood count (CBC) utilization demonstrated a significant 15% relative reduction in repetitive testing without adverse safety effects, providing valuable lessons on ML-driven CDS implementation and governance.

Medical Relevance

Unnecessary repetitive laboratory testing burdens patients, increases healthcare costs, and can lead to alarm fatigue. SmartAlert offers a data-driven solution to safely optimize lab utilization, improving patient experience and hospital efficiency.

AI Health Application

The AI application is 'SmartAlert', a machine learning-driven clinical decision support system integrated into the electronic health record (EHR). It utilizes machine learning models to predict stable laboratory results (e.g., CBCs) and provides precision guidance to clinicians to reduce unnecessary repeat testing for hospitalized patients, thereby optimizing lab utilization, reducing costs, and improving patient experience and safety.

Key Points

  • SmartAlert is an ML-driven CDS system designed to predict stable laboratory results and reduce unnecessary repeat testing.
  • The system was evaluated in a randomized controlled pilot targeting CBC utilization across 9270 admissions in eight acute care units at two hospitals.
  • The pilot ran from August 15, 2024, to March 15, 2025, demonstrating a significant decrease in CBC results within 52 hours of SmartAlert display (1.54 vs 1.82, p <0.01).
  • This reduction represents a 15% relative decrease in repetitive CBC testing without adverse effects on secondary safety outcomes.
  • Key implementation lessons include interpreting probabilistic ML predictions in clinical contexts, engaging stakeholders, establishing governance processes for complex model deployment, and user interface design considerations.
  • Qualitative feedback from end-users was highlighted as valuable for system refinement and successful deployment.
  • The study concludes that ML-driven CDS, supported by deliberate implementation and governance, can safely provide precision guidance for inpatient lab testing reduction.

Methodology

The study involved implementing SmartAlert, an ML-driven CDS system, into the EHR. It was evaluated through a randomized controlled pilot targeting CBC utilization across 9270 admissions in eight acute care units across two hospitals. The primary outcome was the number of CBC results within 52 hours of SmartAlert display, with secondary safety outcomes also monitored. Qualitative feedback on implementation and user experience was also gathered.

Key Findings

SmartAlert led to a statistically significant 15% relative reduction in repetitive CBC testing (1.54 vs 1.82 tests within 52 hours, p <0.01) without any adverse effects on patient safety. Implementation success was attributed to key lessons learned regarding model interpretation, stakeholder engagement, governance, UI design, alignment with clinical priorities, and the value of end-user feedback.

Clinical Impact

This system has the potential to significantly reduce unnecessary lab testing, leading to direct cost savings for healthcare systems and patients, decreased patient burden (less blood draws), and improved efficiency in inpatient care. It demonstrates a viable strategy for safely integrating advanced ML tools into routine clinical workflows.

Limitations

The abstract describes a "case study" and "randomized controlled pilot" targeting only CBC utilization in specific acute care units. While successful, its generalizability to other lab tests, hospital settings, or broader patient populations would require further investigation. The pilot's duration (August 2024 - March 2025) is also a fixed timeframe.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stating future research directions, the paper's conclusion implies that the deliberate implementation and governance process developed for SmartAlert could serve as a model for deploying other complex ML-driven CDS systems to provide precision guidance across various aspects of clinical care beyond laboratory testing.

Abstract

Repetitive laboratory testing unlikely to yield clinically useful information is a common practice that burdens patients and increases healthcare costs. Education and feedback interventions have limited success, while general test ordering restrictions and electronic alerts impede appropriate clinical care. We introduce and evaluate SmartAlert, a machine learning (ML)-driven clinical decision support (CDS) system integrated into the electronic health record that predicts stable laboratory results to reduce unnecessary repeat testing. This case study describes the implementation process, challenges, and lessons learned from deploying SmartAlert targeting complete blood count (CBC) utilization in a randomized controlled pilot across 9270 admissions in eight acute care units across two hospitals between August 15, 2024, and March 15, 2025. Results show significant decrease in number of CBC results within 52 hours of SmartAlert display (1.54 vs 1.82, p <0.01) without adverse effect on secondary safety outcomes, representing a 15% relative reduction in repetitive testing. Implementation lessons learned include interpretation of probabilistic model predictions in clinical contexts, stakeholder engagement to define acceptable model behavior, governance processes for deploying a complex model in a clinical environment, user interface design considerations, alignment with clinical operational priorities, and the value of qualitative feedback from end users. In conclusion, a machine learning-driven CDS system backed by a deliberate implementation and governance process can provide precision guidance on inpatient laboratory testing to safely reduce unnecessary repetitive testing.
