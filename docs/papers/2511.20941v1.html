Fusion of classical and quantum kernels enables accurate and robust two-sample tests

arXiv ID: 2511.20941v1
Published: 2025-11-26
Authors: Yu Terada, Yugo Ogio, Ken Arai, Hiroyuki Tezuka, Yu Tanaka

Summary

This paper introduces MMD-FUSE, a novel hybrid two-sample testing strategy that fuses classical and quantum kernels to improve the accuracy and robustness of statistical tests, particularly for small and high-dimensional datasets. The approach leverages Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) within the Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS) framework, enhancing test power and adaptability across diverse data characteristics. The study demonstrates MMD-FUSE's superior performance over classical counterparts, highlighting its potential for more effective statistical analysis in data-limited scenarios.

Medical Relevance

This research directly addresses a critical challenge in medical and health sciences: conducting robust statistical comparisons with limited patient data, as often encountered in early-phase clinical trials, rare disease studies, or precision medicine. By enhancing the ability to determine if two groups (e.g., treatment vs. placebo) differ significantly, it can lead to more reliable evaluations of drug effectiveness and better-informed clinical decisions.

This research contributes to medical AI by providing a more powerful and robust statistical tool for comparative analysis. It can be applied in areas such as: (1) evaluating the efficacy of new drugs or treatments (e.g., comparing patient outcomes between treatment and control groups), (2) A/B testing of different healthcare interventions or digital health solutions, (3) validating new diagnostic tools by comparing patient cohorts, and (4) analyzing complex, high-dimensional biological data (e.g., '-omics' data) in a healthcare context, especially when dealing with limited sample sizes. The 'quantum kernel' aspect positions it at the cutting edge of advanced AI methods with direct applicability to medical data analysis.

Key Points

  • Two-sample tests are critical for discriminating if two sets of samples originate from the same distribution, with applications in drug effectiveness evaluation and A/B testing.
  • Existing kernel-based two-sample tests, while powerful for high-dimensional data, lack clear guidance on kernel choice, especially for small datasets.
  • The paper proposes MMD-FUSE, an enhanced Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD) based framework that incorporates quantum kernels into a hybrid strategy with classical kernels.
  • This hybrid approach combines the domain-specific inductive biases of classical kernels with the unique expressive power of quantum kernels to create a powerful and adaptive test.
  • Evaluation on synthetic and real-world clinical datasets showed that MMD-FUSE with quantum kernels, given appropriate hyperparameter tuning, consistently improves test power compared to classical methods, particularly for small and high-dimensional data.
  • The proposed hybrid framework demonstrates significant robustness, effectively adapting to varying data characteristics and achieving high test power across a broad range of scenarios.
  • The findings underscore the potential of quantum-inspired and hybrid kernel strategies to develop more effective statistical tests, providing a versatile tool for data analysis with limited sample sizes.

Methodology

The methodology is based on kernel-based two-sample tests using Maximum Mean Discrepancy (MMD-FUSE). The core innovation involves enhancing this framework by fusing classical kernels, which capture domain-specific inductive biases, with quantum kernels, which offer unique expressive power. This hybrid kernel strategy is evaluated on both synthetic and real-world clinical datasets to assess its test power and robustness.

Key Findings

1) MMD-FUSE utilizing quantum kernels consistently improves test power over classical kernel counterparts, especially in scenarios involving small and high-dimensional data, provided appropriate hyperparameter tuning is applied. 2) The proposed hybrid classical-quantum kernel framework exhibits remarkable robustness, demonstrating adaptability to diverse data characteristics and achieving high test power across various complex scenarios.

Abstract

Two-sample tests have been extensively employed in various scientific fields and machine learning such as evaluation on the effectiveness of drugs and A/B testing on different marketing strategies to discriminate whether two sets of samples come from the same distribution or not. Kernel-based procedures for hypothetical testing have been proposed to efficiently disentangle high-dimensional complex structures in data to obtain accurate results in a model-free way by embedding the data into the reproducing kernel Hilbert space (RKHS). While the choice of kernels plays a crucial role for their performance, little is understood about how to choose kernel especially for small datasets. Here we aim to construct a hypothetical test which is effective even for small datasets, based on the theoretical foundation of kernel-based tests using maximum mean discrepancy, which is called MMD-FUSE. To address this, we enhance the MMD-FUSE framework by incorporating quantum kernels and propose a novel hybrid testing strategy that fuses classical and quantum kernels. This approach creates a powerful and adaptive test by combining the domain-specific inductive biases of classical kernels with the unique expressive power of quantum kernels. We evaluate our method on various synthetic and real-world clinical datasets, and our experiments reveal two key findings: 1) With appropriate hyperparameter tuning, MMD-FUSE with quantum kernels consistently improves test power over classical counterparts, especially for small and high-dimensional data. 2) The proposed hybrid framework demonstrates remarkable robustness, adapting to different data characteristics and achieving high test power across diverse scenarios. These results highlight the potential of quantum-inspired and hybrid kernel strategies to build more effective statistical tests, offering a versatile tool for data analysis where sample sizes are limited.
