Enhancing Phenotype Discovery in Electronic Health Records through Prior Knowledge-Guided Unsupervised Learning

arXiv ID: 2511.02102v1
Published: 2025-11-03
Authors: Melanie Mayer, Kimberly Lactaoen, Gary E. Weissman, Blanca E. Himes, Rebecca A. Hubbard
Categories: stat.AP, stat.ML
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a Bayesian latent class framework that integrates clinical domain knowledge via informative priors to enhance phenotype discovery from electronic health record (EHR) data. Applied to a large asthma cohort, it successfully identified an "uncontrolled T2-high" sub-phenotype (38.7% of patients) characterized by elevated eosinophils, allergy markers, and surprisingly high healthcare utilization/medication use. This demonstrates the model's utility for hypothesis generation and cohort identification in heterogeneous diseases lacking clear phenotype definitions.

Medical Relevance

This methodology is crucial for advancing precision medicine by enabling the discovery of clinically meaningful sub-phenotypes within complex and heterogeneous diseases like asthma. Identifying such subgroups allows for more targeted therapies, personalized patient management strategies, and improved allocation of healthcare resources, ultimately leading to better patient outcomes.

AI Health Application

The paper applies Bayesian latent class modeling, an unsupervised machine learning technique, to Electronic Health Records (EHR) data. This AI application is used to discover and characterize clinically meaningful disease sub-phenotypes (e.g., 'uncontrolled T2-high' asthma) by incorporating prior medical knowledge. This facilitates hypothesis generation, patient cohort identification, and potentially leads to more targeted and effective treatment strategies for heterogeneous diseases, thus advancing precision medicine through AI-driven insights from real-world health data.

Key Points

  • Addresses the limitation of traditional unsupervised learning in EHRs by incorporating domain-specific clinical knowledge (via informative priors) to improve phenotype interpretability and clinical meaningfulness.
  • Utilizes a Bayesian latent class model, capable of modeling missing data (including missing-not-at-random patterns) and providing patient-level phenotype assignment probabilities with uncertainty.
  • Applied the model to a large cohort of 44,642 adult asthma patients from January 2017 to February 2024, specifying informative priors for Type 2 (T2) inflammation-related features.
  • Revealed clear class separation with a bimodal posterior distribution of phenotype assignment, indicating distinct patient subgroups.
  • Identified a significant sub-phenotype (38.7% of the cohort) strongly characterized by elevated eosinophil levels and allergy markers (indicative of T2 inflammation).
  • Notably, this T2-informed class also exhibited high healthcare utilization and medication use, which emerged robustly despite only weakly informative priors on these latter variables.
  • The findings suggest an "uncontrolled T2-high" asthma sub-phenotype, demonstrating the framework's power for hypothesis generation and cohort identification in heterogeneous diseases.

Methodology

The study employs a Bayesian latent class model, a statistical framework for unsupervised clustering. It integrates clinical domain knowledge by using *informative priors* for specific features (e.g., T2 inflammation-related biomarkers like eosinophils and allergy markers) and *weakly informative priors* for other clinical variables (e.g., healthcare utilization, medication use). The model is designed to handle missing data, accounting for potential missing-not-at-random patterns, and provides patient-level probabilities for phenotype assignment along with associated uncertainty estimates. The approach was applied using reusable and flexible code to a large EHR cohort of asthma patients.

Key Findings

The analysis of 44,642 adult asthma patients revealed a bimodal posterior distribution of phenotype assignment, indicating clear separation into distinct patient classes. A prominent class, comprising 38.7% of the cohort, was characterized by elevated eosinophil levels and allergy markers, consistent with Type 2 inflammation. Crucially, this T2-informed class also demonstrated significantly higher healthcare utilization and medication use, leading to the identification of an 'uncontrolled T2-high' asthma sub-phenotype. This association of T2 inflammation markers with high resource utilization emerged robustly, even with only weakly informative priors on the utilization variables.

Clinical Impact

This framework offers a powerful tool for clinicians and researchers to move beyond 'one-size-fits-all' approaches for complex diseases. By clearly delineating an 'uncontrolled T2-high' asthma sub-phenotype, it provides actionable insights for personalized treatment decisions, potentially guiding the use of targeted biologics, improving disease management strategies, and optimizing resource allocation for specific patient groups. It facilitates precision medicine by supporting the generation of new clinical hypotheses and the precise identification of patient cohorts for interventional studies.

Abstract

Objectives: Unsupervised learning with electronic health record (EHR) data has shown promise for phenotype discovery, but approaches typically disregard existing clinical information, limiting interpretability. We operationalize a Bayesian latent class framework for phenotyping that incorporates domain-specific knowledge to improve clinical meaningfulness of EHR-derived phenotypes and illustrate its utility by identifying an asthma sub-phenotype informed by features of Type 2 (T2) inflammation. Materials and methods: We illustrate a framework for incorporating clinical knowledge into a Bayesian latent class model via informative priors to guide unsupervised clustering toward clinically relevant subgroups. This approach models missingness, accounting for potential missing-not-at-random patterns, and provides patient-level probabilities for phenotype assignment with uncertainty. Using reusable and flexible code, we applied the model to a large asthma EHR cohort, specifying informative priors for T2 inflammation-related features and weakly informative priors for other clinical variables, allowing the data to inform posterior distributions. Results and Conclusion: Using encounter data from January 2017 to February 2024 for 44,642 adult asthma patients, we found a bimodal posterior distribution of phenotype assignment, indicating clear class separation. The T2 inflammation-informed class (38.7%) was characterized by elevated eosinophil levels and allergy markers, plus high healthcare utilization and medication use, despite weakly informative priors on the latter variables. These patterns suggest an "uncontrolled T2-high" sub-phenotype. This demonstrates how our Bayesian latent class modeling approach supports hypothesis generation and cohort identification in EHR-based studies of heterogeneous diseases without well-established phenotype definitions.
