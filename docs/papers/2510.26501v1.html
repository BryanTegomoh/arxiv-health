Enhancing ECG Classification Robustness with Lightweight Unsupervised Anomaly Detection Filters

Summary

This paper addresses the critical reliability challenge of Out-of-Distribution (OOD) data in deep learning-based ECG analysis for resource-constrained wearable devices, which can lead to erroneous high-confidence predictions and compromise patient safety. The authors propose and evaluate Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) as an independent, upstream filtering mechanism to improve robustness. Their optimized Deep SVDD filter, when integrated into a diagnostic pipeline, significantly enhanced classifier accuracy and enabled safer, more reliable continuous cardiovascular monitoring.

Medical Relevance

This research significantly enhances the reliability and safety of automated ECG analysis on wearable devices, which is crucial for early detection of cardiovascular diseases. By filtering out unreliable or anomalous data, it reduces the risk of misdiagnosis and ensures that patients receive more accurate and trustworthy health monitoring.

AI Health Application

The AI application is to enhance the robustness and reliability of deep learning models used for automated ECG classification and continuous cardiovascular monitoring on resource-constrained wearable devices. Specifically, it uses Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) as an upstream filter to detect out-of-distribution medical data (e.g., unseen pathologies, noisy signals) that could lead to erroneous, high-confidence diagnoses, thereby safeguarding the diagnostic classifier and improving patient safety and accuracy in AI-driven health monitoring.

Key Points

  • Addresses the challenge of Out-of-Distribution (OOD) data (e.g., unseen pathologies, noise-corrupted signals) compromising deep learning ECG analysis on resource-constrained wearables, leading to unreliable predictions and patient safety risks.
  • Proposes Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) as an independent, upstream filtering mechanism to detect OOD inputs before they reach the main diagnostic classifier.
  • Benchmarked six diverse UAD approaches, including Deep SVDD, reconstruction-based models, Masked Anomaly Detection, normalizing flows, and diffusion models.
  • Models were optimized using Neural Architecture Search (NAS) under strict resource constraints, specifically targeting models with at most 512k parameters for wearable deployment.
  • Evaluation was conducted on PTB-XL and BUT QDB datasets, assessing the detection of OOD cardiovascular disease (CVD) classes and signals deemed unsuitable for analysis due to noise.
  • Deep SVDD consistently demonstrated the best trade-off between OOD detection performance and computational efficiency among the benchmarked methods.
  • In a simulated realistic deployment, integrating the optimized Deep SVDD filter improved the diagnostic classifier's accuracy by up to 21 percentage points over a classifier-only baseline.

Methodology

The study employed Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) as an upstream filtering mechanism. Six UAD models (Deep SVDD, reconstruction-based autoencoders, Masked Anomaly Detection, normalizing flows, and diffusion models) were benchmarked. Neural Architecture Search (NAS) was utilized to optimize these models for resource-constrained environments (maximum 512k parameters). Evaluation involved detecting OOD CVD classes and noisy signals using the PTB-XL and BUT QDB datasets. A realistic deployment simulation was performed to quantify the accuracy improvement of a diagnostic classifier when augmented with the optimized UAD filter.

Key Findings

Deep SVDD emerged as the most effective UAD approach, offering the best balance between detection performance and computational efficiency. Crucially, integrating the optimized Deep SVDD filter into a deep learning diagnostic pipeline in a deployment simulation led to a substantial accuracy improvement of up to 21 percentage points compared to a baseline classifier operating without the filter.

Clinical Impact

The findings pave the way for more reliable and safer continuous cardiovascular monitoring using wearable devices. By mitigating the risks associated with OOD data, such as misdiagnosis from unseen pathologies or noisy signals, this system can enhance patient safety, provide more dependable early CVD detection, and empower clinicians with more trustworthy data for remote patient management.

Abstract

Continuous electrocardiogram (ECG) monitoring via wearables offers significant potential for early cardiovascular disease (CVD) detection. However, deploying deep learning models for automated analysis in resource-constrained environments faces reliability challenges due to inevitable Out-of-Distribution (OOD) data. OOD inputs, such as unseen pathologies or noisecorrupted signals, often cause erroneous, high-confidence predictions by standard classifiers, compromising patient safety. Existing OOD detection methods either neglect computational constraints or address noise and unseen classes separately. This paper explores Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) as an independent, upstream filtering mechanism to improve robustness. We benchmark six UAD approaches, including Deep SVDD, reconstruction-based models, Masked Anomaly Detection, normalizing flows, and diffusion models, optimized via Neural Architecture Search (NAS) under strict resource constraints (at most 512k parameters). Evaluation on PTB-XL and BUT QDB datasets assessed detection of OOD CVD classes and signals unsuitable for analysis due to noise. Results show Deep SVDD consistently achieves the best trade-off between detection and efficiency. In a realistic deployment simulation, integrating the optimized Deep SVDD filter with a diagnostic classifier improved accuracy by up to 21 percentage points over a classifier-only baseline. This study demonstrates that optimized UAD filters can safeguard automated ECG analysis, enabling safer, more reliable continuous cardiovascular monitoring on wearables.
