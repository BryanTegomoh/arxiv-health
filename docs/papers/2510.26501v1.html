Enhancing ECG Classification Robustness with Lightweight Unsupervised Anomaly Detection Filters

arXiv ID: 2510.26501v1
Published: 2025-10-30
Authors: Mustafa Fuad Rifet Ibrahim, Maurice Meijer, Alexander Schlaefer, Peer Stelldinger
Categories: cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper proposes utilizing lightweight Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) filters as an upstream mechanism to enhance the robustness and safety of deep learning models for ECG classification on wearables. By effectively identifying Out-of-Distribution (OOD) data like unseen pathologies or noise-corrupted signals, these filters prevent erroneous high-confidence predictions. Integrating an optimized Deep SVDD filter improved diagnostic classifier accuracy by up to 21 percentage points in simulated deployments, making continuous cardiovascular monitoring more reliable.

Medical Relevance

This research directly addresses a critical patient safety issue in AI-powered cardiovascular diagnostics on wearables by preventing misleading or erroneous high-confidence predictions from OOD data. It ensures that automated ECG analysis is reliable for early and accurate cardiovascular disease detection, which is vital for timely clinical intervention.

AI Health Application

The AI application focuses on developing and optimizing unsupervised anomaly detection filters (a form of medical AI) to enhance the robustness and reliability of deep learning models used for automated ECG classification. This directly supports the safe and accurate diagnosis and continuous monitoring of cardiovascular conditions on resource-constrained wearable devices, thereby improving early CVD detection and patient safety.

Key Points

  • **Problem Addressed:** Deep learning models for ECG analysis on wearables are vulnerable to Out-of-Distribution (OOD) data (unseen pathologies, noise), leading to unsafe, high-confidence incorrect predictions.
  • **Proposed Solution:** Implementing lightweight Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) as an independent, upstream filter to detect OOD inputs before classifier processing, thereby improving robustness and patient safety.
  • **Methodology:** Benchmarking six UAD methods (Deep SVDD, reconstruction-based, Masked Anomaly Detection, normalizing flows, diffusion models), optimized via Neural Architecture Search (NAS) for strict resource constraints (at most 512k parameters).
  • **Datasets & Evaluation:** Evaluation was conducted on the PTB-XL and BUT QDB datasets to assess the detection of OOD CVD classes and signals deemed unsuitable for analysis due to noise.
  • **Key Finding (Best UAD):** Deep SVDD consistently demonstrated the optimal trade-off between detection performance and computational efficiency among the benchmarked UAD approaches.
  • **Performance Improvement:** In a realistic deployment simulation, integrating the optimized Deep SVDD filter improved the diagnostic classifier's accuracy by up to 21 percentage points compared to a classifier-only baseline.
  • **Implication:** Optimized UAD filters provide a crucial safeguard for automated ECG analysis, enabling safer and more reliable continuous cardiovascular monitoring on resource-constrained wearable devices.

Methodology

The study involved benchmarking six Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) approaches, including Deep SVDD, reconstruction-based models (e.g., autoencoders), Masked Anomaly Detection, normalizing flows, and diffusion models. These models were optimized using Neural Architecture Search (NAS) to ensure strict resource constraints (maximum 512k parameters) suitable for wearable deployment. Performance was evaluated on the PTB-XL and BUT QDB datasets, specifically focusing on their ability to detect Out-of-Distribution (OOD) cardiovascular disease classes and noise-corrupted signals.

Key Findings

The primary finding demonstrated that Deep SVDD consistently achieved the best trade-off between OOD detection performance and computational efficiency across all benchmarked UAD methods. Crucially, in a realistic deployment simulation, integrating the optimized Deep SVDD filter with a diagnostic ECG classifier led to a significant improvement in accuracy, by up to 21 percentage points, compared to the classifier operating without this upstream filter.

Clinical Impact

This research provides a practical and computationally efficient solution to a significant challenge in deploying AI for continuous ECG monitoring on wearables, directly enhancing patient safety and diagnostic reliability. By filtering out OOD data, it prevents potentially dangerous erroneous high-confidence predictions, ensuring clinicians receive more trustworthy automated analyses. This advancement can lead to earlier and more accurate interventions for cardiovascular diseases, making wearable-based health monitoring a more dependable tool in clinical practice.

Abstract

Continuous electrocardiogram (ECG) monitoring via wearables offers significant potential for early cardiovascular disease (CVD) detection. However, deploying deep learning models for automated analysis in resource-constrained environments faces reliability challenges due to inevitable Out-of-Distribution (OOD) data. OOD inputs, such as unseen pathologies or noisecorrupted signals, often cause erroneous, high-confidence predictions by standard classifiers, compromising patient safety. Existing OOD detection methods either neglect computational constraints or address noise and unseen classes separately. This paper explores Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) as an independent, upstream filtering mechanism to improve robustness. We benchmark six UAD approaches, including Deep SVDD, reconstruction-based models, Masked Anomaly Detection, normalizing flows, and diffusion models, optimized via Neural Architecture Search (NAS) under strict resource constraints (at most 512k parameters). Evaluation on PTB-XL and BUT QDB datasets assessed detection of OOD CVD classes and signals unsuitable for analysis due to noise. Results show Deep SVDD consistently achieves the best trade-off between detection and efficiency. In a realistic deployment simulation, integrating the optimized Deep SVDD filter with a diagnostic classifier improved accuracy by up to 21 percentage points over a classifier-only baseline. This study demonstrates that optimized UAD filters can safeguard automated ECG analysis, enabling safer, more reliable continuous cardiovascular monitoring on wearables.
