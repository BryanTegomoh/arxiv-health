Enhancing ECG Classification Robustness with Lightweight Unsupervised Anomaly Detection Filters

arXiv ID: 2510.26501v1
Published: 2025-10-30
Authors: Mustafa Fuad Rifet Ibrahim, Maurice Meijer, Alexander Schlaefer, Peer Stelldinger
Categories: cs.LG

Summary

This paper addresses the critical reliability challenges of deep learning-based ECG analysis on resource-constrained wearables, particularly concerning Out-of-Distribution (OOD) data such as unseen pathologies or noise. It proposes and evaluates lightweight Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) filters as an independent, upstream mechanism to robustly detect and mitigate OOD inputs. The study demonstrates that an optimized Deep SVDD filter, when integrated with a diagnostic classifier, significantly enhances classification accuracy by filtering OOD inputs, thus enabling safer and more reliable continuous cardiovascular monitoring.

Medical Relevance

This research directly enhances the safety and reliability of continuous cardiovascular disease monitoring through wearable ECG devices, which is crucial for early detection and patient management. By filtering out unreliable data, it ensures that automated diagnoses are based on valid inputs, preventing misdiagnosis or delayed intervention and improving patient outcomes.

AI Health Application

This research applies AI (deep learning, unsupervised anomaly detection) to enhance the robustness and safety of automated ECG analysis systems deployed on wearable devices. The AI system acts as an upstream filter to detect and flag out-of-distribution data (including novel pathologies or noise) before it can lead to erroneous diagnoses by a primary diagnostic classifier, thereby enabling more reliable continuous cardiovascular monitoring and early disease detection.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical problem of Out-of-Distribution (OOD) data in deep learning-based ECG analysis for wearables, which causes erroneous, high-confidence predictions and compromises patient safety.
  • Proposes Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) as an independent, upstream filtering mechanism to detect OOD inputs like unseen pathologies or noisy signals.
  • Benchmarked six UAD approaches (Deep SVDD, reconstruction-based, Masked Anomaly Detection, normalizing flows, diffusion models) optimized via Neural Architecture Search (NAS) under strict resource constraints (at most 512k parameters).
  • Evaluated UAD performance on PTB-XL and BUT QDB datasets for detecting OOD CVD classes and noise-corrupted signals.
  • Deep SVDD consistently demonstrated the best trade-off between OOD detection performance and computational efficiency among the tested UAD methods.
  • In a realistic deployment simulation, integrating the optimized Deep SVDD filter improved diagnostic classifier accuracy by up to 21 percentage points compared to a classifier-only baseline.
  • The research demonstrates the feasibility of safeguarding automated ECG analysis on wearables, leading to more reliable continuous cardiovascular monitoring.

Methodology

The study benchmarked six Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) techniques, including Deep SVDD, reconstruction-based models, Masked Anomaly Detection, normalizing flows, and diffusion models. These models were optimized using Neural Architecture Search (NAS) to adhere to strict resource constraints (maximum 512k parameters). Evaluation was performed on the PTB-XL and BUT QDB datasets, assessing the UAD filters' ability to detect both Out-of-Distribution (OOD) cardiovascular disease classes and noise-corrupted ECG signals. A realistic deployment simulation was conducted by integrating the best-performing UAD filter (optimized Deep SVDD) upstream of a standard diagnostic classifier.

Key Findings

Deep SVDD emerged as the most effective UAD approach, consistently achieving the optimal balance between detection capability for OOD CVD classes and noisy signals, and computational efficiency within the specified resource constraints. The integration of the optimized Deep SVDD filter demonstrably improved the accuracy of a subsequent diagnostic classifier by up to 21 percentage points in a simulated deployment scenario, compared to using the classifier alone.

Clinical Impact

This technology can significantly improve the safety and trustworthiness of automated ECG analysis on wearable devices, reducing the risk of erroneous high-confidence predictions caused by unseen pathologies or noisy data. This enables more reliable early detection of cardiovascular diseases, potentially leading to timely interventions and better patient outcomes in continuous remote monitoring settings, enhancing the utility of wearable technology in healthcare.

Abstract

Continuous electrocardiogram (ECG) monitoring via wearables offers significant potential for early cardiovascular disease (CVD) detection. However, deploying deep learning models for automated analysis in resource-constrained environments faces reliability challenges due to inevitable Out-of-Distribution (OOD) data. OOD inputs, such as unseen pathologies or noisecorrupted signals, often cause erroneous, high-confidence predictions by standard classifiers, compromising patient safety. Existing OOD detection methods either neglect computational constraints or address noise and unseen classes separately. This paper explores Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) as an independent, upstream filtering mechanism to improve robustness. We benchmark six UAD approaches, including Deep SVDD, reconstruction-based models, Masked Anomaly Detection, normalizing flows, and diffusion models, optimized via Neural Architecture Search (NAS) under strict resource constraints (at most 512k parameters). Evaluation on PTB-XL and BUT QDB datasets assessed detection of OOD CVD classes and signals unsuitable for analysis due to noise. Results show Deep SVDD consistently achieves the best trade-off between detection and efficiency. In a realistic deployment simulation, integrating the optimized Deep SVDD filter with a diagnostic classifier improved accuracy by up to 21 percentage points over a classifier-only baseline. This study demonstrates that optimized UAD filters can safeguard automated ECG analysis, enabling safer, more reliable continuous cardiovascular monitoring on wearables.
