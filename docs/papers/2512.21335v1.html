Autonomous Uncertainty Quantification for Computational Point-of-care Sensors

Published: 2025-12-24
Authors: Artem Goncharov, Rajesh Ghosh, Hyou-Arm Joung, Dino Di Carlo, Aydogan Ozcan

Summary

This paper presents an autonomous uncertainty quantification technique for neural network-based computational point-of-care (POC) sensors to mitigate misdiagnosis risks from erroneous predictions. Using a paper-based Lyme disease diagnostic platform (xVFA) as a testbed, the Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO)-based approach successfully identified and excluded uncertain predictions, leading to a significant increase in diagnostic sensitivity from 88.2% to 95.7% during blinded testing, all without requiring ground truth information. This enhances the reliability of rapid, accessible diagnostics.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for improving patient safety and trust in rapid, accessible point-of-care diagnostics by minimizing the occurrence of misdiagnosis due to AI model errors, especially in emergency, remote, or resource-limited settings. It enhances the reliability of critical diagnostic information directly impacting clinical decision-making.

AI Health Application

The paper applies neural network-based algorithms for rapid point-of-care diagnosis of Lyme disease from sensor signals. Its key AI application is the development and implementation of an autonomous uncertainty quantification technique (Monte Carlo dropout) to identify and exclude erroneous predictions, thereby enhancing the robustness, sensitivity, and reliability of AI-driven diagnostic systems in a medical context.

Key Points

  • Neural network-based algorithms in computational POC sensors are prone to 'hallucinations' and erroneous predictions, posing a risk of misdiagnosis.
  • An autonomous uncertainty quantification (UQ) technique, utilizing Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO), was developed to address this challenge in POC diagnostics.
  • The UQ method was integrated into a paper-based computational vertical flow assay (xVFA) platform designed for rapid (<20 min), low-volume (20 uL serum), and cost-effective Lyme disease diagnosis.
  • MCDO autonomously identified and excluded diagnostic predictions with high uncertainty, thereby improving the reliability of the diagnostic output.
  • Blinded testing with new patient samples demonstrated a significant improvement in diagnostic sensitivity, increasing from 88.2% to 95.7%.
  • The technique operates autonomously, meaning it enhances diagnostic robustness without requiring real-time access to patients' ground truth diagnostic information during its application.

Methodology

The study incorporated a Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO)-based uncertainty quantification approach into a neural network-driven computational point-of-care system. The testbed was a paper-based vertical flow assay (xVFA) platform for Lyme disease, comprising a disposable paper assay, a handheld optical reader, and a neural network for inference from sensor signals. MCDO involves performing multiple forward passes through the neural network during inference with dropout layers active to obtain a distribution of predictions, from which uncertainty can be quantified. High-uncertainty predictions were then autonomously identified and excluded from the final diagnostic output.

Key Findings

The primary finding is that the integration of Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO)-based uncertainty quantification significantly enhanced the diagnostic performance of the computational point-of-care system. Specifically, in blinded testing with new patient samples for Lyme disease, the diagnostic sensitivity of the xVFA platform increased from 88.2% to 95.7% by autonomously filtering out erroneous predictions associated with high uncertainty.

Clinical Impact

This technology has the potential to dramatically improve the trustworthiness and accuracy of AI-driven POC diagnostics, particularly for critical conditions like Lyme disease where timely and accurate diagnosis is essential for effective treatment. By autonomously reducing misdiagnosis rates, it can lead to more appropriate and timely patient management, better health outcomes, and increased confidence in decentralized healthcare solutions in various clinical settings.

Abstract

Computational point-of-care (POC) sensors enable rapid, low-cost, and accessible diagnostics in emergency, remote and resource-limited areas that lack access to centralized medical facilities. These systems can utilize neural network-based algorithms to accurately infer a diagnosis from the signals generated by rapid diagnostic tests or sensors. However, neural network-based diagnostic models are subject to hallucinations and can produce erroneous predictions, posing a risk of misdiagnosis and inaccurate clinical decisions. To address this challenge, here we present an autonomous uncertainty quantification technique developed for POC diagnostics. As our testbed, we used a paper-based, computational vertical flow assay (xVFA) platform developed for rapid POC diagnosis of Lyme disease, the most prevalent tick-borne disease globally. The xVFA platform integrates a disposable paper-based assay, a handheld optical reader and a neural network-based inference algorithm, providing rapid and cost-effective Lyme disease diagnostics in under 20 min using only 20 uL of patient serum. By incorporating a Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO)-based uncertainty quantification approach into the diagnostics pipeline, we identified and excluded erroneous predictions with high uncertainty, significantly improving the sensitivity and reliability of the xVFA in an autonomous manner, without access to the ground truth diagnostic information of patients. Blinded testing using new patient samples demonstrated an increase in diagnostic sensitivity from 88.2% to 95.7%, indicating the effectiveness of MCDO-based uncertainty quantification in enhancing the robustness of neural network-driven computational POC sensing systems.
