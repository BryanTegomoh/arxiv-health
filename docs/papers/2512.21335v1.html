Autonomous Uncertainty Quantification for Computational Point-of-care Sensors

Published: 2025-12-24

Summary

This paper introduces an autonomous uncertainty quantification technique to improve the reliability of neural network-based computational point-of-care (POC) sensors. By integrating Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO) into a diagnostic pipeline for Lyme disease, the authors significantly enhanced diagnostic sensitivity and reliability by identifying and excluding erroneous, high-uncertainty predictions without needing ground truth data.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for improving the accuracy and trustworthiness of rapid, low-cost diagnostics in critical, resource-limited, or remote medical settings, thereby reducing misdiagnosis risks and enabling more reliable clinical decisions for prevalent conditions like Lyme disease.

AI Health Application

The research applies neural network-based algorithms for inferring diagnoses from rapid diagnostic tests at the point of care. It then develops an autonomous uncertainty quantification technique (Monte Carlo Dropout) to identify and exclude erroneous predictions from these AI models, thereby significantly improving the sensitivity and reliability of neural network-driven computational POC sensing systems for medical diagnosis.

Key Points

  • Computational POC sensors using neural networks are prone to 'hallucinations' and erroneous predictions, leading to risks of misdiagnosis.
  • An autonomous uncertainty quantification (UQ) technique based on Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO) was developed to address this challenge.
  • The technique was tested on a paper-based, computational vertical flow assay (xVFA) platform for rapid Lyme disease diagnosis (<20 min, 20 uL serum).
  • The xVFA platform combines a disposable paper assay, a handheld optical reader, and a neural network-based inference algorithm.
  • MCDO was incorporated into the diagnostics pipeline to identify and autonomously exclude erroneous predictions exhibiting high uncertainty.
  • Blinded testing on new patient samples demonstrated a significant increase in diagnostic sensitivity from 88.2% to 95.7%.
  • This approach enhances the robustness and reliability of neural network-driven computational POC sensing systems, particularly in settings lacking centralized medical facilities.

Methodology

The study utilized a paper-based, computational vertical flow assay (xVFA) platform for Lyme disease diagnosis, which integrates a disposable assay, a handheld optical reader, and a neural network for inference. The core methodological advancement involved incorporating Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO) into the neural network's inference process. This allowed for the quantification of uncertainty in diagnostic predictions. Predictions associated with high uncertainty were then autonomously identified and excluded, effectively filtering out potentially erroneous results without requiring access to ground truth diagnostic information during real-time operation. The method's effectiveness was validated through blinded testing using new patient samples.

Key Findings

The autonomous uncertainty quantification technique, leveraging Monte Carlo dropout, significantly improved the diagnostic performance of the computational xVFA platform. Specifically, in blinded testing with new patient samples, the diagnostic sensitivity for Lyme disease increased from 88.2% to 95.7% after the exclusion of high-uncertainty predictions.

Clinical Impact

The enhanced diagnostic sensitivity and reliability achieved through this uncertainty quantification technique directly mitigate the risk of misdiagnosis in point-of-care settings. This translates to more accurate and timely clinical decisions, particularly beneficial in emergency, remote, or resource-limited areas where access to centralized lab facilities is scarce, ultimately leading to better patient outcomes for diseases like Lyme disease.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations of the presented study. However, implicit considerations include that the method's performance is demonstrated for a specific disease (Lyme disease) and a particular platform (xVFA), suggesting potential future work on generalizability across other diseases and computational POC sensor types. The trade-off between sensitivity improvement and the rate of exclusion of uncertain predictions (which might lead to 'no result' instances) is also not detailed.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated in the abstract, logical future directions suggested by the work include validating the MCDO-based uncertainty quantification on other diseases and computational POC platforms, exploring the generalizability of the technique across different neural network architectures, and potentially integrating this autonomous UQ for other diagnostic parameters or multi-marker assays to further enhance comprehensive diagnostic reliability.

Abstract

Computational point-of-care (POC) sensors enable rapid, low-cost, and accessible diagnostics in emergency, remote and resource-limited areas that lack access to centralized medical facilities. These systems can utilize neural network-based algorithms to accurately infer a diagnosis from the signals generated by rapid diagnostic tests or sensors. However, neural network-based diagnostic models are subject to hallucinations and can produce erroneous predictions, posing a risk of misdiagnosis and inaccurate clinical decisions. To address this challenge, here we present an autonomous uncertainty quantification technique developed for POC diagnostics. As our testbed, we used a paper-based, computational vertical flow assay (xVFA) platform developed for rapid POC diagnosis of Lyme disease, the most prevalent tick-borne disease globally. The xVFA platform integrates a disposable paper-based assay, a handheld optical reader and a neural network-based inference algorithm, providing rapid and cost-effective Lyme disease diagnostics in under 20 min using only 20 uL of patient serum. By incorporating a Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO)-based uncertainty quantification approach into the diagnostics pipeline, we identified and excluded erroneous predictions with high uncertainty, significantly improving the sensitivity and reliability of the xVFA in an autonomous manner, without access to the ground truth diagnostic information of patients. Blinded testing using new patient samples demonstrated an increase in diagnostic sensitivity from 88.2% to 95.7%, indicating the effectiveness of MCDO-based uncertainty quantification in enhancing the robustness of neural network-driven computational POC sensing systems.
