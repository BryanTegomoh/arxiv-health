Summary

This paper introduces an autonomous uncertainty quantification (UQ) technique using Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO) to enhance the reliability of neural network-based computational point-of-care (POC) sensors. Applied to a rapid Lyme disease diagnostic platform (xVFA), the method successfully identifies and excludes erroneous predictions with high uncertainty, significantly improving diagnostic sensitivity from 88.2% to 95.7% in blinded patient testing.

This research is crucial for improving the trustworthiness of AI-driven rapid diagnostic tests in point-of-care settings, reducing the risk of misdiagnosis for critical conditions like Lyme disease and enabling more accurate clinical decisions in resource-limited or emergency situations.

Methodology

The methodology involves integrating a Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO) approach into the neural network inference pipeline of a computational point-of-care sensor. As a testbed, a paper-based vertical flow assay (xVFA) platform for Lyme disease was used, which comprises a disposable assay, a handheld optical reader, and a neural network algorithm. During inference, MCDO quantifies prediction uncertainty by performing multiple forward passes with dropout active, allowing the system to identify and exclude predictions associated with high uncertainty autonomously, without needing access to ground truth labels during operation.

The MCDO-based uncertainty quantification technique effectively identified and filtered out erroneous predictions from the neural network. This autonomous exclusion of high-uncertainty results led to a substantial improvement in the diagnostic performance of the xVFA platform for Lyme disease, increasing its sensitivity from 88.2% to 95.7% during blinded testing on new patient samples, thereby enhancing the overall reliability of the system.

Abstract

Computational point-of-care (POC) sensors enable rapid, low-cost, and accessible diagnostics in emergency, remote and resource-limited areas that lack access to centralized medical facilities. These systems can utilize neural network-based algorithms to accurately infer a diagnosis from the signals generated by rapid diagnostic tests or sensors. However, neural network-based diagnostic models are subject to hallucinations and can produce erroneous predictions, posing a risk of misdiagnosis and inaccurate clinical decisions. To address this challenge, here we present an autonomous uncertainty quantification technique developed for POC diagnostics. As our testbed, we used a paper-based, computational vertical flow assay (xVFA) platform developed for rapid POC diagnosis of Lyme disease, the most prevalent tick-borne disease globally. The xVFA platform integrates a disposable paper-based assay, a handheld optical reader and a neural network-based inference algorithm, providing rapid and cost-effective Lyme disease diagnostics in under 20 min using only 20 uL of patient serum. By incorporating a Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO)-based uncertainty quantification approach into the diagnostics pipeline, we identified and excluded erroneous predictions with high uncertainty, significantly improving the sensitivity and reliability of the xVFA in an autonomous manner, without access to the ground truth diagnostic information of patients. Blinded testing using new patient samples demonstrated an increase in diagnostic sensitivity from 88.2% to 95.7%, indicating the effectiveness of MCDO-based uncertainty quantification in enhancing the robustness of neural network-driven computational POC sensing systems.
