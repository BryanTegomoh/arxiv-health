Autonomous Uncertainty Quantification for Computational Point-of-care Sensors

Summary

This paper introduces an autonomous uncertainty quantification (UQ) technique, leveraging Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO), to enhance the reliability of neural network-based computational point-of-care (POC) diagnostic sensors. Applied to a paper-based xVFA platform for rapid Lyme disease diagnosis, this method significantly improved diagnostic sensitivity from 88.2% to 95.7% by identifying and excluding erroneous, high-uncertainty predictions without requiring ground truth data during operation.

Medical Relevance

This research significantly improves the trustworthiness and accuracy of AI-driven point-of-care diagnostics, enabling more reliable rapid disease detection in emergency, remote, or resource-limited areas. By mitigating the risk of misdiagnosis from neural network errors, it enhances patient safety and facilitates timely, appropriate clinical interventions.

AI Health Application

The AI application involves using neural network-based algorithms for inferring diagnoses from rapid diagnostic tests at the point of care. Specifically, it applies Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO)-based uncertainty quantification to these neural networks to identify and exclude erroneous predictions, thereby significantly improving the sensitivity and reliability of computational POC sensing systems for diseases like Lyme disease. This enhances the safety and accuracy of AI-driven medical diagnostics.

Key Points

  • Neural network-based computational POC sensors are prone to erroneous predictions ('hallucinations'), posing risks of misdiagnosis and inaccurate clinical decisions in diagnostic settings.
  • The study develops and implements an autonomous uncertainty quantification (UQ) technique based on Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO) to address the unreliability of AI-driven diagnostics.
  • The proposed method was tested on a paper-based, computational vertical flow assay (xVFA) platform specifically designed for rapid (under 20 minutes) and cost-effective Lyme disease diagnosis using only 20 uL of patient serum.
  • MCDO is integrated into the xVFA's diagnostic pipeline, which consists of a disposable paper-based assay, a handheld optical reader, and a neural network-based inference algorithm.
  • The uncertainty quantification operates autonomously, enabling the identification and exclusion of erroneous predictions characterized by high uncertainty without real-time access to ground truth diagnostic information.
  • Blinded testing using new patient samples demonstrated a substantial improvement in diagnostic sensitivity, increasing from 88.2% to 95.7% (a 7.5 percentage point increase).
  • The autonomous MCDO-based UQ approach significantly enhances the robustness, reliability, and safety of neural network-driven computational POC sensing systems, making them more suitable for clinical deployment.

Methodology

The study incorporated a Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO)-based uncertainty quantification approach into a neural network-driven computational point-of-care (xVFA) platform. This platform uses a paper-based vertical flow assay and a handheld optical reader to analyze patient serum (20 uL) for Lyme disease diagnosis. The MCDO method was utilized to generate multiple predictions for each sample, allowing for the quantification of prediction uncertainty, which was then used to autonomously identify and exclude erroneous, high-uncertainty results. The efficacy was validated through blinded testing on a cohort of new patient samples.

Key Findings

The integration of MCDO-based uncertainty quantification into the xVFA system autonomously identified and excluded erroneous predictions with high uncertainty. This resulted in a significant improvement in diagnostic sensitivity for Lyme disease, increasing from 88.2% to 95.7% during blinded testing with new patient samples, thereby enhancing the overall reliability of the computational POC sensor.

This reduces misdiagnosis rates, improves patient trust in AI-powered tools, enables earlier and more targeted treatment, and ultimately contributes to better patient outcomes and more efficient healthcare delivery in underserved populations.

Abstract

Computational point-of-care (POC) sensors enable rapid, low-cost, and accessible diagnostics in emergency, remote and resource-limited areas that lack access to centralized medical facilities. These systems can utilize neural network-based algorithms to accurately infer a diagnosis from the signals generated by rapid diagnostic tests or sensors. However, neural network-based diagnostic models are subject to hallucinations and can produce erroneous predictions, posing a risk of misdiagnosis and inaccurate clinical decisions. To address this challenge, here we present an autonomous uncertainty quantification technique developed for POC diagnostics. As our testbed, we used a paper-based, computational vertical flow assay (xVFA) platform developed for rapid POC diagnosis of Lyme disease, the most prevalent tick-borne disease globally. The xVFA platform integrates a disposable paper-based assay, a handheld optical reader and a neural network-based inference algorithm, providing rapid and cost-effective Lyme disease diagnostics in under 20 min using only 20 uL of patient serum. By incorporating a Monte Carlo dropout (MCDO)-based uncertainty quantification approach into the diagnostics pipeline, we identified and excluded erroneous predictions with high uncertainty, significantly improving the sensitivity and reliability of the xVFA in an autonomous manner, without access to the ground truth diagnostic information of patients. Blinded testing using new patient samples demonstrated an increase in diagnostic sensitivity from 88.2% to 95.7%, indicating the effectiveness of MCDO-based uncertainty quantification in enhancing the robustness of neural network-driven computational POC sensing systems.
