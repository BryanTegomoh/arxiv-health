Analytical Model of Prompt Gamma Timing for Spatiotemporal Emission Reconstruction in Particle Therapy

arXiv ID: 2510.25328v1
Published: 2025-10-29
Authors: Julius Werner, Malte Schmidt, Francesco Pennazio, Jorge Roser, Jona Kasprzak, Veronica Ferrero, Magdalena Rafecas
Categories: physics.med-ph
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces an analytical system model for Prompt Gamma Timing (PGT) to reconstruct spatiotemporal prompt-gamma emissions in particle therapy. The model significantly reduces computational time by 1500 times compared to Monte-Carlo (MC) based models, enabling faster evaluation of new detector arrangements and beam scenarios. Despite minor differences in multi-detector configurations, the analytical model maintains accuracy in stopping power estimation, proving robust for critical clinical applications.

Medical Relevance

Accurate determination of stopping power is paramount in particle therapy to ensure the prescribed dose is delivered precisely to the tumor while sparing healthy tissue. This work provides a computationally efficient tool for range verification, which can lead to safer, more precise, and personalized cancer treatments by enabling faster optimization and real-time monitoring during therapy.

AI Health Application

The paper describes an analytical model for computational efficiency in medical physics, not an AI application itself. However, the significantly reduced calculation time (1500 times faster) for reconstructing spatiotemporal emission distributions could be a critical enabler for future AI-driven applications in particle therapy, such as real-time adaptive treatment planning, AI-optimized detector designs, or machine learning models for dose verification that require rapid feedback.

Key Points

  • Particle therapy critically relies on precise knowledge of tissue stopping power, which is measured via spatiotemporal prompt-gamma photon emissions.
  • Previous Prompt Gamma Timing (PGT) systems utilized computationally intensive Monte-Carlo (MC) based models, limiting the study of new detector setups and beam scenarios.
  • The authors propose an analytical system model for prompt-gamma transport and detection, explicitly dependent on emission and detection time, to overcome computational bottlenecks and eliminate statistical noise.
  • Evaluation using the MERLINO multi-detector-PGT prototype showed excellent agreement between the analytical and MC models for single-detector setups.
  • For multi-detector configurations with close-packed, partially obstructing detectors, intercrystal scatter led to differences of up to 10% between the analytical and MC models.
  • Despite these small inaccuracies, the analytical model proved robust, showing no significant difference in reconstructing spatiotemporal distributions or estimating stopping power compared to the MC model.
  • The analytical model achieved a 1500-fold reduction in calculation time, drastically accelerating research and development for PGT-based systems.

Methodology

The study involved developing an analytical system model that explicitly accounts for time-of-emission and time-of-detection dependencies in prompt-gamma transport. This analytical model was then quantitatively compared against a reference Monte-Carlo (MC) based system model. The comparison was performed using data for the MERLINO multi-detector-PGT prototype, evaluating agreement for single and multi-detector setups, and critically, the models' performance in reconstructing spatiotemporal emission distributions and estimating stopping power.

Key Findings

The analytical model demonstrated excellent agreement with MC for single detectors but showed up to 10% differences in multi-detector arrangements due to intercrystal scatter. Critically, these discrepancies did not significantly affect the accuracy of spatiotemporal distribution reconstruction or stopping power estimation. The most significant finding is the 1500-fold reduction in computation time, making the model highly efficient for practical applications.

Clinical Impact

This research provides a significantly faster and more practical tool for developing and optimizing Prompt Gamma Timing systems for in-vivo range verification in particle therapy. The accelerated model enables rapid testing of new detector designs and beam scanning techniques, potentially leading to more accurate dose delivery, reduced treatment uncertainties, and ultimately improved patient outcomes in cancer treatment.

Limitations

The analytical model exhibited differences of up to 10% compared to the MC-based reference in scenarios where multiple detectors were placed close together, leading to intercrystal scatter and partial obstruction. While these inaccuracies did not significantly impact the overall reconstruction performance in tested scenarios, they represent a nuance in the model's accuracy for complex detector geometries.

Future Directions

The 1500-fold reduction in computation time opens up extensive possibilities for new studies in PGT-based systems. This includes rapid exploration and optimization of novel detector arrangements, advanced beam scanning scenarios, and potentially the integration of this model into real-time feedback systems for adaptive particle therapy.

Abstract

Particle therapy relies on up-to-date knowledge of the stopping power of the patient tissues to deliver the prescribed dose distribution. The stopping power describes the average particle motion, which is encoded in the distribution of prompt-gamma photon emissions in time and space. We reconstruct the spatiotemporal emission distribution from multi-detector Prompt Gamma Timing (PGT) data. Solving this inverse problem relies on an accurate model of the prompt-gamma transport and detection including explicitly the dependencies on the time of emission and detection. Our previous work relied on Monte-Carlo (MC) based system models. The tradeoff between computational resources and statistical noise in the system model prohibits studies of new detector arrangements and beam scanning scenarios. Therefore, we propose here an analytical system model to speed up recalculations for new beam positions and to avoid statistical noise in the model. We evaluated the model for the MERLINO multi-detector-PGT prototype. Comparisons between the analytical model and a MC-based reference showed excellent agreement for single-detector setups. When several detectors were placed close together and partially obstructed each other, intercrystal scatter led to differences of up to 10 % between the analytical and MC-based model. Nevertheless, when evaluating the performance in reconstructing the spatiotemporal distribution and estimating the stopping power, no significant difference between the models was observed. Hence, the procedure proved robust against the small inaccuracies of the model for the tested scenarios. The model calculation time was reduced by 1500 times, now enabling many new studies for PGT-based systems.
