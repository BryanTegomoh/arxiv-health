Beyond Curve Fitting: Neuro-Symbolic Agents for Context-Aware Epidemic Forecasting

arXiv ID: 2511.23276v1
Published: 2025-11-28
Authors: Joongwon Chae, Runming Wang, Chen Xiong, Gong Yunhan, Lian Zhang, Ji Jiansong, Dongmei Yu, Peiwu Qin

Summary

This paper introduces a novel two-agent neuro-symbolic framework designed for context-aware Hand, Foot and Mouth Disease (HFMD) forecasting, which integrates semantic reasoning into probabilistic predictions. It utilizes a Large Language Model (LLM) for interpreting diverse contextual signals and a neuro-symbolic core for combining this interpretation with historical data. The framework demonstrates competitive point forecasting accuracy, robust 90% prediction intervals, and human-interpretable rationales on real-world HFMD datasets.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a significant advancement in infectious disease surveillance by enabling more accurate, context-sensitive, and interpretable forecasts for diseases like HFMD, which is crucial for timely public health interventions and resource allocation in response to dynamic epidemiological and environmental factors.

AI Health Application

The AI application involves using large language models (LLMs) as 'event interpreters' and a neuro-symbolic core to process diverse contextual signals (e.g., school schedules, weather, reports) along with historical disease case counts to produce calibrated probabilistic forecasts for epidemics. This provides interpretable, context-aware predictions to inform public health decision-making and improve disease control.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical need for context-aware HFMD forecasting by semantically interpreting causal interplay of drivers (e.g., school calendars, weather), a limitation in classical and recent foundation models.
  • Proposes a unique two-agent framework: an LLM 'event interpreter' and a neuro-symbolic core for probabilistic forecasting.
  • The LLM 'event interpreter' processes heterogeneous signals (school schedules, meteorological summaries, reports) into a scalar transmission-impact signal, providing contextual understanding.

  • Evaluated on real-world HFMD datasets from Hong Kong (2023-2024) and Lishui, China (2024), demonstrating practical applicability.
  • Achieves competitive point forecasting accuracy compared to traditional and foundation-model baselines (Chronos, TimesFM), while offering robust 90% prediction intervals (coverage 0.85-1.00).
  • A key advantage is the generation of human-interpretable rationales, making the forecasts actionable and transparent for public health workflows.

Methodology

The methodology centers on a two-agent neuro-symbolic framework. An LLM-based 'event interpreter' module semantically processes diverse, heterogeneous signals (e.g., school schedules, weather data, reports) to derive a scalar 'transmission-impact signal'. This signal is then fed into a 'neuro-symbolic core' that combines it with historical HFMD case counts to generate calibrated probabilistic forecasts, including 90% prediction intervals. The framework was evaluated using real-world HFMD datasets from specific geographical regions over defined periods.

Key Findings

The framework achieved competitive point forecasting accuracy against traditional and advanced foundation models. It successfully delivered robust 90% prediction intervals with high coverage (0.85-1.00), which is critical for uncertainty quantification. Notably, it also provided human-interpretable rationales, enhancing the transparency and actionable utility of its context-aware forecasts.

Clinical Impact

This approach offers a tangible clinical and public health impact by enabling more precise and understandable epidemic forecasts. Public health authorities can leverage these context-aware predictions to anticipate HFMD outbreaks more effectively, leading to proactive measures such as targeted public health campaigns, timely resource allocation, and optimized interventions (e.g., school closures, awareness programs), thereby mitigating disease spread and improving community health outcomes.

Abstract

Effective surveillance of hand, foot and mouth disease (HFMD) requires forecasts accounting for epidemiological patterns and contextual drivers like school calendars and weather. While classical models and recent foundation models (e.g., Chronos, TimesFM) incorporate covariates, they often lack the semantic reasoning to interpret the causal interplay between conflicting drivers. In this work, we propose a two-agent framework decoupling contextual interpretation from probabilistic forecasting. An LLM "event interpreter" processes heterogeneous signals-including school schedules, meteorological summaries, and reports-into a scalar transmission-impact signal. A neuro-symbolic core then combines this with historical case counts to produce calibrated probabilistic forecasts. We evaluate the framework on real-world HFMD datasets from Hong Kong (2023-2024) and Lishui, China (2024). Compared to traditional and foundation-model baselines, our approach achieves competitive point forecasting accuracy while providing robust 90% prediction intervals (coverage 0.85-1.00) and human-interpretable rationales. Our results suggest that structurally integrating domain knowledge through LLMs can match state-of-the-art performance while yielding context-aware forecasts that align with public health workflows.
