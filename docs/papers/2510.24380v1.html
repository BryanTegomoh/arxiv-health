APEX: Approximate-but-exhaustive search for ultra-large combinatorial synthesis libraries

arXiv ID: 2510.24380v1
Published: 2025-10-28
Authors: Aryan Pedawi, Jordi Silvestre-Ryan, Bradley Worley, Darren J Hsu, Kushal S Shah, Elias Stehle, Jingrong Zhang, Izhar Wallach
Categories: cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces APEX, an approximate-but-exhaustive search protocol designed to overcome the challenges of virtual screening in ultra-large make-on-demand combinatorial synthesis libraries (CSLs). APEX utilizes a neural network surrogate that exploits CSL structure to predict objectives and constraints, enabling full library enumeration on a GPU in under a minute. This approach allows for efficient and accurate retrieval of approximate top-$k$ compounds, significantly outperforming existing virtual screening methods in both accuracy and speed.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for drug discovery as it dramatically improves the efficiency and success rate of virtual screening in identifying promising drug candidates from vast chemical spaces. By enabling a more exhaustive and rapid search, APEX can accelerate the identification of molecules with optimal properties for various therapeutic targets.

AI Health Application

The paper describes an AI-driven method (neural network surrogate) called APEX to significantly accelerate and improve virtual screening for ultra-large combinatorial synthesis libraries. This application of AI directly impacts drug discovery by enabling more exhaustive and efficient identification of potential drug candidates against medically relevant targets, thereby speeding up the development of new therapeutics.

Key Points

  • The primary challenge in virtual screening for ultra-large combinatorial synthesis libraries (CSLs) is their immense size (tens of billions), which severely limits the percentage of compounds that can be evaluated (often <0.1%).
  • Traditional virtual screening algorithms struggle with amortization, offering little efficiency gain when computational objectives or constraints change during a campaign.
  • APEX (Approximate-but-Exhaustive search protocol) employs a neural network surrogate model that capitalizes on the inherent structure of CSLs to predict computational objectives (e.g., docking scores) and constraints.
  • This novel methodology enables the full enumeration of entire CSLs on a consumer GPU in less than a minute, facilitating the exact retrieval of approximate top-$k$ sets of compounds.
  • A benchmark CSL comprising over 10 million compounds was developed and annotated with ground-truth docking scores across five medically relevant targets, along with RDKit physicochemical properties, for rigorous evaluation.
  • APEX consistently demonstrated superior performance in both retrieval accuracy and runtime when compared to alternative virtual screening algorithms, validating its efficiency and effectiveness.

Methodology

APEX leverages a neural network surrogate model specifically designed to exploit the inherent combinatorial structure of CSLs. This model is trained to predict computational objectives (like docking scores) and various physicochemical constraints. Once trained, this surrogate allows for an approximate-but-exhaustive search, enabling the full enumeration of ultra-large libraries on a consumer GPU within seconds. The method was validated against a custom-built benchmark CSL containing over 10 million compounds, each pre-annotated with ground-truth docking scores for five medically relevant targets and RDKit-derived physicochemical properties, allowing for direct comparison of retrieved top-$k$ sets against the actual best compounds.

Key Findings

The key findings indicate that APEX significantly enhances the efficiency and accuracy of virtual screening. It achieves consistently strong performance in retrieval accuracy, identifying high-scoring compounds more effectively than alternative methods. Furthermore, APEX offers a substantial improvement in runtime, allowing for rapid, near-exhaustive exploration of vast CSLs within minutes, even when objectives or constraints are modified during a screening campaign.

Clinical Impact

By drastically improving the speed and precision of virtual screening, APEX has the potential to accelerate the early stages of drug discovery. This can lead to the faster identification of novel, high-quality drug candidates for various diseases, ultimately reducing the time and cost associated with bringing new therapeutics to patients and potentially enabling the discovery of previously unattainable compounds.

Limitations

The abstract highlights the significant limitations of *existing* virtual screening algorithms, such as their inability to screen more than 0.1% of compounds in ultra-large libraries and their poor amortization for changing objectives/constraints. However, it does not explicitly state specific limitations of the APEX method itself.

Future Directions

Abstract

Make-on-demand combinatorial synthesis libraries (CSLs) like Enamine REAL have significantly enabled drug discovery efforts. However, their large size presents a challenge for virtual screening, where the goal is to identify the top compounds in a library according to a computational objective (e.g., optimizing docking score) subject to computational constraints under a limited computational budget. For current library sizes -- numbering in the tens of billions of compounds -- and scoring functions of interest, a routine virtual screening campaign may be limited to scoring fewer than 0.1% of the available compounds, leaving potentially many high scoring compounds undiscovered. Furthermore, as constraints (and sometimes objectives) change during the course of a virtual screening campaign, existing virtual screening algorithms typically offer little room for amortization. We propose the approximate-but-exhaustive search protocol for CSLs, or APEX. APEX utilizes a neural network surrogate that exploits the structure of CSLs in the prediction of objectives and constraints to make full enumeration on a consumer GPU possible in under a minute, allowing for exact retrieval of approximate top-$k$ sets. To demonstrate APEX's capabilities, we develop a benchmark CSL comprised of more than 10 million compounds, all of which have been annotated with their docking scores on five medically relevant targets along with physicohemical properties measured with RDKit such that, for any objective and set of constraints, the ground truth top-$k$ compounds can be identified and compared against the retrievals from any virtual screening algorithm. We show APEX's consistently strong performance both in retrieval accuracy and runtime compared to alternative methods.
