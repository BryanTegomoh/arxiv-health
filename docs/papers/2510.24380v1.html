APEX: Approximate-but-exhaustive search for ultra-large combinatorial synthesis libraries

arXiv ID: 2510.24380v1
Published: 2025-10-28
Authors: Aryan Pedawi, Jordi Silvestre-Ryan, Bradley Worley, Darren J Hsu, Kushal S Shah, Elias Stehle, Jingrong Zhang, Izhar Wallach

Summary

This paper introduces APEX, an approximate-but-exhaustive search protocol designed to overcome the challenges of virtual screening in ultra-large make-on-demand combinatorial synthesis libraries (CSLs) for drug discovery. APEX leverages a neural network surrogate that exploits CSL structure to predict objectives and constraints, enabling full library enumeration on a consumer GPU in under a minute. This allows for the rapid and accurate identification of approximate top-$k$ compounds, outperforming existing virtual screening methods in both retrieval accuracy and runtime.

Medical Relevance

This research significantly enhances the initial phase of drug discovery by accelerating and making more comprehensive the virtual screening process for vast chemical spaces. It could lead to the faster and more efficient identification of novel drug candidates, ultimately speeding up the development of new treatments for various diseases.

AI Health Application

The research describes an AI-powered (neural network surrogate) virtual screening protocol (APEX) designed to significantly accelerate the identification of high-scoring compounds from ultra-large chemical libraries. This enables faster and more efficient discovery of potential drug candidates for various medically relevant targets, thereby directly impacting drug development and therapeutic innovation.

Key Points

  • Ultra-large combinatorial synthesis libraries (CSLs) like Enamine REAL, numbering in tens of billions, pose significant challenges for efficient virtual screening in drug discovery.
  • Traditional virtual screening methods are computationally limited, often screening less than 0.1% of compounds, leading to the potential undiscovery of many high-scoring drug candidates.
  • APEX (Approximate-but-exhaustive search) is proposed as a novel protocol utilizing a neural network (NN) surrogate that exploits the inherent structural regularities of CSLs.
  • The NN surrogate predicts computational objectives (e.g., docking scores) and physicochemical constraints, enabling full enumeration of entire CSLs on a consumer GPU in under a minute.
  • This rapid enumeration allows for the exact retrieval of approximate top-$k$ sets, significantly improving the ability to identify the highest-scoring compounds efficiently.
  • A benchmark CSL comprising over 10 million compounds was developed, annotated with docking scores for five medically relevant targets and RDKit physicochemical properties, to provide ground truth for evaluation.
  • APEX consistently demonstrated strong performance in both retrieval accuracy and computational runtime when compared against alternative virtual screening algorithms.

Methodology

APEX employs a neural network (NN) surrogate model specifically designed to exploit the underlying structural regularities of combinatorial synthesis libraries (CSLs). This NN is trained to rapidly predict computational objectives, such as docking scores against specific protein targets, and physicochemical properties/constraints (measured with RDKit). By leveraging these NN predictions, APEX enables an approximate-but-exhaustive search across the entire CSL. The search is optimized for full enumeration on a consumer-grade GPU, achieving results in under a minute. A large benchmark CSL of over 10 million compounds was created, annotated with ground-truth docking scores for five medically relevant targets and physicochemical properties, to rigorously validate APEX's performance against existing virtual screening methods.

Key Findings

APEX consistently exhibits superior performance in both the accuracy of compound retrieval and computational runtime when compared to existing virtual screening methodologies. It effectively addresses the scalability issues of ultra-large CSLs, enabling the identification of high-scoring compounds with unprecedented speed and comprehensiveness, by allowing an exact search over the neural network's approximate predictions.

Clinical Impact

By drastically improving the efficiency and accuracy of virtual screening, APEX has the potential to accelerate the early stages of drug development. This could lead to a significant reduction in the time and cost associated with identifying promising lead compounds, thereby speeding up the pipeline for novel therapeutics and potentially bringing more effective and targeted treatments to patients sooner across a broad spectrum of diseases.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations of the APEX method itself, but rather highlights the limitations of prior virtual screening approaches that APEX aims to overcome. A potential implicit limitation, given the 'approximate-but-exhaustive' nature, is the fidelity and generalization capability of the neural network surrogate model; the accuracy of its predictions directly influences the quality of the 'approximate top-$k$ sets' retrieved. Its performance might be sensitive to CSL structures or biological targets not well-represented in its training data.

Abstract

Make-on-demand combinatorial synthesis libraries (CSLs) like Enamine REAL have significantly enabled drug discovery efforts. However, their large size presents a challenge for virtual screening, where the goal is to identify the top compounds in a library according to a computational objective (e.g., optimizing docking score) subject to computational constraints under a limited computational budget. For current library sizes -- numbering in the tens of billions of compounds -- and scoring functions of interest, a routine virtual screening campaign may be limited to scoring fewer than 0.1% of the available compounds, leaving potentially many high scoring compounds undiscovered. Furthermore, as constraints (and sometimes objectives) change during the course of a virtual screening campaign, existing virtual screening algorithms typically offer little room for amortization. We propose the approximate-but-exhaustive search protocol for CSLs, or APEX. APEX utilizes a neural network surrogate that exploits the structure of CSLs in the prediction of objectives and constraints to make full enumeration on a consumer GPU possible in under a minute, allowing for exact retrieval of approximate top-$k$ sets. To demonstrate APEX's capabilities, we develop a benchmark CSL comprised of more than 10 million compounds, all of which have been annotated with their docking scores on five medically relevant targets along with physicohemical properties measured with RDKit such that, for any objective and set of constraints, the ground truth top-$k$ compounds can be identified and compared against the retrievals from any virtual screening algorithm. We show APEX's consistently strong performance both in retrieval accuracy and runtime compared to alternative methods.
