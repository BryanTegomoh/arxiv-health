Reproduction Numbers R_0, R_t for COVID-19 Infections with Gaussian Distribution of Generation Times, and of Serial Intervals including Presymptomatic Transmission

arXiv ID: 2511.04574v1
Published: 2025-11-06
Authors: Derek Marsh
Categories: q-bio.PE
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper addresses a methodological discrepancy in calculating COVID-19 reproduction numbers (R_0, R_t) using the Lotka-Euler equation when considering Gaussian-distributed serial intervals (SIs) that account for presymptomatic transmission. It highlights that allowing SIs to extend to minus infinity in Lotka-Euler leads to lower R values than predicted by the discretized renewal equation. The main contribution is a reformulated Lotka-Euler equation that incorporates an explicit lower cut-off for Gaussian SI distributions, enabling more consistent and accurate estimation of reproduction numbers in the context of presymptomatic transmission.

Medical Relevance

Accurate calculation of reproduction numbers is fundamental for public health decision-making, enabling effective deployment of preventative measures, accurate assessment of epidemic control, and precise planning for vaccination campaigns to achieve herd immunity.

Key Points

  • Reproduction numbers (R_0, R_t) are critical metrics for assessing epidemic progression, intervention effectiveness, and required vaccination coverage.
  • R values are linked to daily case counts via the renewal equation and to exponential growth/decay rates via the Lotka-Euler equation.
  • While generation time (GT) is the theoretical ideal, directly observable serial interval (SI) is used in practice.
  • COVID-19's presymptomatic transmission results in negative serial intervals, which can be appropriately described by a Gaussian distribution.
  • A common analytical flaw is allowing Gaussian-distributed SIs to extend to minus infinity within the Lotka-Euler equation, which leads to an underestimation of R values compared to the discretized renewal equation.
  • The paper formulates a revised Lotka-Euler equation for Gaussian SI distributions that includes an explicit lower cut-off, rectifying the aforementioned discrepancy.
  • This new formulation is utilized to explore the specific consequences of presymptomatic transmission on calculated COVID-19 reproduction numbers.

Methodology

The paper uses mathematical modeling and theoretical formulation. Specifically, it re-derives and formulates the Lotka-Euler equation for Gaussian distributions of serial intervals, incorporating an explicit lower cut-off. This reformulated equation is then used to analytically explore the implications of presymptomatic transmission on reproduction number calculations, comparing its outputs with those from the discretized renewal equation and common practices of allowing serial intervals to extend to minus infinity.

Key Findings

The primary finding is that the common practice of applying Gaussian-distributed serial intervals (accounting for presymptomatic transmission) to the Lotka-Euler equation by allowing them to extend to minus infinity results in systematically lower reproduction number estimates than those derived from the discretized renewal equation. The paper's novel formulation, which explicitly imposes a lower cut-off on the Gaussian serial interval distribution within the Lotka-Euler equation, provides a more consistent and theoretically sound method for estimating R values, thereby resolving this discrepancy.

Clinical Impact

This work directly impacts the accuracy of epidemiological modeling and the subsequent public health strategies for infectious diseases like COVID-19. More accurate R_0 and R_t estimations mean better-informed decisions on intervention intensity, resource allocation (e.g., hospital beds, testing capacity), and vaccination targets. This helps prevent both over-reaction (unnecessary economic/social disruption) and under-reaction (uncontrolled outbreaks).

Limitations

The abstract points to a significant limitation of previous methodologies: the inconsistency arising from extending Gaussian serial intervals to minus infinity within the Lotka-Euler equation, leading to underestimated reproduction numbers. The paper's contribution is to address and overcome this specific methodological limitation.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated, the refined methodology provides a more robust framework for real-time epidemiological analyses. Future research could involve applying this reformulated Lotka-Euler equation to diverse real-world COVID-19 datasets, comparing its performance against other models, and potentially extending the approach to other infectious diseases characterized by presymptomatic transmission and complex serial interval distributions.

Abstract

Basic and instantaneous reproduction numbers, "R" _"0" and "R" _"t" , are important metrics to assess progress of an epidemic and effectiveness of preventative interventions undertaken, and also to estimate coverage needed for vaccination. Reproduction numbers are related to the daily number of positive cases recorded by the national public health authorities, via the renewal equation. During periods of exponential growth or decay they are linked also to the rate constants by the Lotka-Euler equation. For either application, we need the distribution of generation times between primary and secondary infections. In practice, we use instead the directly observable serial interval between symptoms onset of infector and infectee. Pre-symptomatic transmission that occurs in COVID infection causes serial intervals to extend to negative values, which can be described with a Gaussian distribution. Consistent application of the two approaches requires careful attention to lower limits imposed on the distribution. Allowing Gaussian-distributed serial intervals to extend to minus infinity with the Lotka-Euler equation, as commonly is done, results in lower reproduction numbers than predicted from the discretized renewal equation. Here, we formulate the Lotka-Euler equation for Gaussian distributions including an explicit lower cut-off, and use this to explore the consequences of presymptomatic transmission for COVID-19 infections.
