Summary

This paper announces the public release of a novel whole-slide image dataset of prostate core needle biopsies from an underrepresented Middle Eastern population (Erbil, Iraq). The dataset aims to address the critical lack of diversity in existing digital pathology AI datasets, thereby supporting the development and validation of AI models that are generalizable across globally diverse populations.

Medical Relevance

This dataset is crucial for improving the clinical utility and equity of AI in pathology by enabling the development of models that perform reliably across diverse patient populations, preventing diagnostic biases for underrepresented groups, particularly in prostate cancer diagnosis.

AI Health Application

The dataset is designed to enable the development and validation of artificial intelligence models for automated diagnosis and grading of prostate cancer from histopathology images. This includes improving the robustness and generalizability of these AI models across diverse patient populations, which is critical for their real-world clinical application in diagnostic pathology.

Key Points

  • Dataset comprises 339 whole-slide images (WSIs) of prostate core needle biopsies from 185 consecutive patients from Erbil, Iraq.
  • Slides are associated with independently assigned Gleason scores and International Society of Urological Pathology (ISUP) grades by three pathologists.
  • Images were acquired using a mix of high-throughput (Leica, Hamamatsu) and compact (Grundium) scanners, allowing for cross-scanner robustness evaluations.
  • All data is de-identified and provided in native formats, facilitating various research applications.
  • The dataset specifically enables grading concordance analyses, color normalization research, and cross-scanner robustness evaluations for AI models.
  • It addresses the scarcity and Western-centric bias of current public histopathology datasets, promoting AI generalizability.
  • Data will be publicly accessible via the Bioimage Archive (BIA) under a CC BY 4.0 license.

Methodology

The study involved collecting 339 prostate core needle biopsy WSIs from 185 consecutive patients in Erbil, Iraq. Three independent pathologists assigned Gleason scores and ISUP grades to the slides. Scanning was performed using three different scanner types (Leica Aperio, Hamamatsu NanoZoomer, Grundium Ocus), and all images were de-identified before being prepared for public release in their native formats.

Key Findings

The primary 'finding' is the creation and characterization of a unique public dataset: 339 de-identified prostate biopsy WSIs from a Middle Eastern population, complete with expert-assigned Gleason scores and ISUP grades, and captured across multiple scanner platforms. This dataset directly addresses a significant gap in global AI pathology resources.

Clinical Impact

The availability of this diverse dataset will facilitate the training and validation of more robust and generalizable AI models for prostate cancer diagnosis and grading. This can lead to more accurate, consistent, and equitable AI-assisted pathology services globally, ultimately improving patient outcomes, especially in regions currently underrepresented in AI model development.

Limitations

The abstract highlights a critical limitation in the field: publicly available histopathology datasets are scarce and predominantly represent Western populations, hindering the generalizability of AI models. This paper's dataset directly seeks to mitigate this existing limitation. No explicit limitations of the presented dataset itself are detailed in the abstract.

Future Directions

The dataset is specifically designed to enable future research into grading concordance analyses among pathologists, color normalization techniques for digital pathology, and crucial evaluations of cross-scanner robustness for pathology AI models. It also directly supports the development and validation of AI models tailored for diverse global populations.

Abstract

Artificial intelligence (AI) is increasingly used in digital pathology. Publicly available histopathology datasets remain scarce, and those that do exist predominantly represent Western populations. Consequently, the generalizability of AI models to populations from less digitized regions, such as the Middle East, is largely unknown. This motivates the public release of our dataset to support the development and validation of pathology AI models across globally diverse populations. We present 339 whole-slide images of prostate core needle biopsies from a consecutive series of 185 patients collected in Erbil, Iraq. The slides are associated with Gleason scores and International Society of Urological Pathology grades assigned independently by three pathologists. Scanning was performed using two high-throughput scanners (Leica and Hamamatsu) and one compact scanner (Grundium). All slides were de-identified and are provided in their native formats without further conversion. The dataset enables grading concordance analyses, color normalization, and cross-scanner robustness evaluations. Data will be deposited in the Bioimage Archive (BIA) under accession code: to be announced (TBA), and released under a CC BY 4.0 license.
