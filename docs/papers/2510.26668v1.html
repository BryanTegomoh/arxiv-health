Zoeppritz equations: from seismology to medical exploration

Summary

This paper investigates extending the application of Zoeppritz equations, traditionally used in seismology, to medical ultrasound for detailed tissue characterization. It demonstrates how analyzing angle-dependent reflection coefficients, critical angles, and waveform distortions can separate density from speed of sound mismatches and determine the thickness of intermediate layers, even at subwavelength resolution, which has significant implications for medical diagnosis.

Medical Relevance

This research significantly advances medical ultrasound by providing quantitative methods to assess tissue elastic properties and micro-structural details. This capability is crucial for early, non-invasive detection and characterization of pathological conditions like arterial plaque, which directly impacts patient outcomes in preventing serious events such as strokes.

AI Health Application

The research aims to develop methods for extracting richer information from ultrasound data (e.g., separating density from speed of sound mismatch, detecting subwavelength layers). This enhanced information is proposed for integration into medical imaging systems and, critically, for training artificial intelligence systems that assist in medical diagnosis, particularly for conditions like arterial plaque detection.

Key Points

  • The study applies Zoeppritz equations, which determine reflected and transmitted coefficients at planar interfaces, from seismology to the medical field, specifically for ultrasound imaging.
  • It investigates methods to extract rich information about media properties from angle-dependent reflection coefficients (Amplitudes Vs. Angle - AVA).
  • A key finding is the ability to separate the effect of density mismatch from speed of sound mismatch at an interface by measuring reflection amplitudes as a function of the angle of incidence.
  • Analysis of critical angles and the waveform distortion of reflected waves can reveal the thickness of an intermediate layer between two media, even achieving subwavelength resolution.
  • These advanced analytical techniques are proposed for integration into medical imaging systems to enhance diagnostic capabilities.
  • The methodology is also suggested as valuable input for training artificial intelligence systems that assist in medical diagnosis.
  • A specific clinical application highlighted is the potential for early detection and characterization of plaque formation and hardening in arteries, particularly those irrigating the brain, to help prevent cerebrovascular accidents.

Methodology

The study employs theoretical investigation into the behavior of angle-dependent reflection coefficients, critical angles, and wave distortion at interfaces between two and three media. It focuses on how these parameters can be measured and interpreted to deduce elastic properties (density, speed of sound) and structural characteristics (layer thickness) of the media.

Key Findings

It was shown that density can be separated from speed of sound mismatch by analyzing reflection amplitudes as a function of incidence angle (AVA). Additionally, examining critical angles and waveform distortion of reflected waves provides a means to determine the thickness of an intermediate layer with subwavelength resolution.

Clinical Impact

The potential clinical impact includes improved early diagnosis of diseases by providing more detailed and quantitative tissue characterization via ultrasound. This is particularly relevant for the prevention of cerebrovascular accidents through early and precise detection of arterial plaque, and for enhancing the accuracy of AI-assisted diagnostic tools.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations of the conducted investigation. However, it implies that prior approximations of Zoeppritz equations in geophysical exploration may not have fully utilized their potential, suggesting this work aims to overcome such limitations by a more complete application.

Future Directions

The authors suggest integrating these findings into medical imaging workflows and utilizing the derived information to train artificial intelligence systems for more accurate and assisted medical diagnosis.

Abstract

More than a century ago, Karl Bernhard Zoeppritz derived the equations that determine the reflected and transmitted coefficients at a planar interface for an incident seismic wave. The coefficients so obtained are a function of the elastic parameters of the media on each side of the interface and the angle of incidence. Approximations of the equations have been proposed and used in geophysical exploration, however, full use of the equations and their generalization to multiple layers could offer richer information about the properties of the media and be helpful in medical diagnosis via ultrasound. In this work, we investigate how to extract information from the angle-dependent reflection coefficients, including critical angles and the wave distortion at the interface between two and three media. It is shown that it is possible to separate the effect of density from speed of sound mismatch by measuring amplitudes as a function of angle of incidence (AVA). And examining the critical angle and waveform distortion of the reflected waves can reveal the thickness of an intermediate layer, even with subwavelength resolution. These studies could be integrated into medical imaging and also into the training of artificial intelligence systems that assist in diagnosis. In particular, they could help prevent cerebrovascular accidents by early detection of the formation and hardening of plaque in the arteries that irrigate the brain.
