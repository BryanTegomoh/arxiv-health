Zoeppritz equations: from seismology to medical exploration

Published: 2025-10-30
Authors: Harry G. Saavedra, Ramiro Moro

Summary

This paper investigates the application of Zoeppritz equations, traditionally used in seismology, to medical ultrasound for extracting richer information about tissue properties. It demonstrates the potential to differentiate between density and speed of sound mismatches using angle-dependent reflection coefficients and to detect subwavelength layer thicknesses by analyzing critical angles and waveform distortion at interfaces.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a theoretical framework to enhance medical ultrasound diagnosis by extracting more precise and quantitative tissue properties, which could lead to earlier and more accurate detection of pathologies like arterial plaque, thereby preventing severe health events such as strokes.

AI Health Application

The research aims to extract richer information from angle-dependent reflection coefficients (e.g., density, speed of sound mismatch, subwavelength layer thickness) which could be integrated into the training of artificial intelligence systems to improve diagnostic accuracy and detail in medical ultrasound. Specifically, this could aid AI in the early detection and characterization of plaque formation and hardening in arteries.

Key Points

  • The Zoeppritz equations, which determine reflection and transmission coefficients at planar interfaces for incident seismic waves, are proposed for medical ultrasound applications.
  • The research explores extracting detailed information from angle-dependent reflection coefficients (AVA) as a function of incidence angle.
  • It is shown that measuring reflected wave amplitudes as a function of angle of incidence (AVA) can separate the effects of density mismatch from speed of sound mismatch between media.
  • Analysis of critical angles and the distortion of reflected waveforms can reveal the thickness of an intermediate layer, even achieving subwavelength resolution.
  • The methodology is investigated for interfaces involving both two and three distinct media, indicating its potential for complex biological structures.
  • These advanced analytical techniques are suggested for integration into medical imaging systems and for training artificial intelligence systems designed to assist in diagnosis.
  • A specific and high-impact medical application highlighted is the early detection of plaque formation and hardening in cerebral arteries to prevent cerebrovascular accidents.

Methodology

The methodology involves a theoretical investigation of how to extract information from the angle-dependent reflection coefficients derived from Zoeppritz equations. This includes analyzing critical angles and observing the wave distortion at interfaces between two and three media, with a focus on relating these observations to the elastic parameters (density and speed of sound) and structural properties (layer thickness).

Key Findings

The primary findings indicate that it is possible to separate the individual contributions of density and speed of sound mismatch from angle-dependent reflection measurements. Furthermore, by examining critical angles and waveform distortion, the thickness of an intermediate layer can be determined, even when its dimensions are below the wavelength of the ultrasound.

Clinical Impact

The potential clinical impact is significant, offering a pathway for enhanced resolution and specificity in medical ultrasound diagnostics. This could enable the early and precise detection of critical conditions like atherosclerotic plaque in arteries, particularly those supplying the brain, which is crucial for preventing cerebrovascular accidents (strokes). It also proposes a novel data source for AI-assisted medical diagnosis.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state any limitations. The language used, such as "could offer richer information" and "possible to separate," suggests the work is theoretical or in an early investigatory phase, rather than reporting on a fully validated or implemented clinical system.

Future Directions

The authors suggest that these studies should be integrated into medical imaging technologies. Additionally, the derived analytical methods and data could be crucial for training artificial intelligence systems aimed at assisting in medical diagnosis.

Abstract

More than a century ago, Karl Bernhard Zoeppritz derived the equations that determine the reflected and transmitted coefficients at a planar interface for an incident seismic wave. The coefficients so obtained are a function of the elastic parameters of the media on each side of the interface and the angle of incidence. Approximations of the equations have been proposed and used in geophysical exploration, however, full use of the equations and their generalization to multiple layers could offer richer information about the properties of the media and be helpful in medical diagnosis via ultrasound. In this work, we investigate how to extract information from the angle-dependent reflection coefficients, including critical angles and the wave distortion at the interface between two and three media. It is shown that it is possible to separate the effect of density from speed of sound mismatch by measuring amplitudes as a function of angle of incidence (AVA). And examining the critical angle and waveform distortion of the reflected waves can reveal the thickness of an intermediate layer, even with subwavelength resolution. These studies could be integrated into medical imaging and also into the training of artificial intelligence systems that assist in diagnosis. In particular, they could help prevent cerebrovascular accidents by early detection of the formation and hardening of plaque in the arteries that irrigate the brain.
