Summary

This paper introduces Chain-of-Anomaly-Thoughts (CoAT), a multi-agent reasoning framework that enhances anomaly detection in automated video surveillance by mitigating Large Vision-Language Models' (LVLMs) inherent bias towards normality. CoAT incorporates an inductive criminal bias and an anomaly-focused classification layer, significantly boosting F1-score for Anomaly Detection by 11.8 percentage points in low-resolution footage and Anomaly Classification by 3.78 percentage points in high-resolution videos.

Methodology

Chain-of-Anomaly-Thoughts (CoAT) is proposed as a multi-agent reasoning framework. It specifically addresses the inherent bias of Large Vision-Language Models (LVLMs) towards normality by integrating an 'inductive criminal bias' into the reasoning process. A key component is a final, anomaly-focused classification layer that guides the model to prioritize and identify anomalous events over normal interpretations, diverging from standard Chain-of-Thought approaches.

Key Findings

The CoAT framework significantly improves performance in anomaly detection tasks: An 11.8 percentage point (p.p.) increase in F1-score for Anomaly Detection on challenging low-resolution video footage, and a 3.78 percentage point (p.p.) improvement in Anomaly Classification in high-resolution videos.

Abstract

Automated video surveillance with Large Vision-Language Models is limited by their inherent bias towards normality, often failing to detect crimes. While Chain-of-Thought reasoning strategies show significant potential for improving performance in language tasks, the lack of inductive anomaly biases in their reasoning further steers the models towards normal interpretations. To address this, we propose Chain-of-Anomaly-Thoughts (CoAT), a multi-agent reasoning framework that introduces inductive criminal bias in the reasoning process through a final, anomaly-focused classification layer. Our method significantly improves Anomaly Detection, boosting F1-score by 11.8 p.p. on challenging low-resolution footage and Anomaly Classification by 3.78 p.p. in high-resolution videos.
