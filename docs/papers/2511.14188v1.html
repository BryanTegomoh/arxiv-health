A region-specific brain dysfunction underlies cognitive impairment in long COVID brain fog

Summary

This research identifies a specific regional brain dysfunction in the right inferior insula that underlies cognitive impairment in Long COVID "brain fog," characterized by aberrant perceptual processing and increased impulsive responses. Crucially, the study demonstrates that non-invasive neuromodulation targeting this region can rescue the observed perceptual deficit, establishing it as a novel therapeutic target.

Medical Relevance

This study provides a neurobiological explanation for a debilitating symptom of Long COVID and offers a specific, non-invasive neuromodulation strategy as a potential treatment, addressing a significant unmet medical need for affected patients.

Key Points

  • Long COVID brain fog patients exhibit an aberrant perceptual processing pattern, specifically committing significantly more 'false alarms' (impulsive responses to non-signals) despite preserved overall accuracy.
  • High-density EEG and structural MRI provided converging evidence of a specific deficit in the right inferior insula, characterized by a blunted neural monitoring signal and localized cortical atrophy.
  • This right inferior insula dysfunction and associated perceptual impairment were independently confirmed in a larger 796-participant UK Biobank longitudinal COVID re-imaging cohort.
  • The UK Biobank cohort also demonstrated longitudinal atrophy of the right inferior insula in COVID-19 survivors compared to healthy controls.
  • A proof-of-principle randomized, sham-controlled trial (n=40) showed that non-invasive, excitatory theta-burst ultrasound stimulation (TBS) targeting the right inferior insula rescued the perceptual deficit by reducing false alarms.
  • The findings provide strong evidence for a causal role of right inferior insula dysfunction in Long COVID-related perceptual impairment.
  • Modulation of the right inferior insula demonstrates a therapeutic potential, establishing it as a novel and specific target for Long COVID cognitive impairment.

Methodology

The study utilized a multi-modal approach: behavioral perceptual processing tasks to assess cognitive function, high-density (128-channel) EEG to measure neural signals, structural MRI for brain anatomy, and analysis of a large longitudinal UK Biobank cohort for validation. Finally, a randomized, sham-controlled proof-of-principle trial (n=40) employed non-invasive excitatory theta-burst ultrasound stimulation (TBS) for targeted neuromodulation.

Key Findings

Long COVID brain fog is linked to an aberrant perceptual processing pattern characterized by increased false alarms. This behavioral deficit is mediated by dysfunction and cortical atrophy in the right inferior insula, evidenced by blunted neural monitoring signals and confirmed longitudinally. Crucially, targeted excitatory theta-burst ultrasound stimulation to this region successfully rescued the perceptual deficit by reducing false alarms, demonstrating a causal role for the right inferior insula and its potential as a therapeutic target.

Clinical Impact

The identification of a specific brain region (right inferior insula) and a non-invasive neuromodulation technique (theta-burst ultrasound stimulation) provides a direct, actionable therapeutic pathway for addressing the debilitating cognitive impairment associated with Long COVID brain fog. This could lead to the development of targeted, evidence-based interventions to improve quality of life for Long COVID patients.

Abstract

Long COVID "brain fog" is a common and debilitating subjective syndrome often associated with persistent cognitive impairment after COVID-19 infection. Here we identify a specific regional brain dysfunction that mediates this cognitive impairment and provide evidence that targeted neuromodulation improves this deficit. In 120 patients with long COVID brain fog, we found an aberrant perceptual processing pattern. Patients with more severe brain fog committed significantly more false alarms (impulsive responses to non-signals) despite preserved overall accuracy. Both high-density (128-channel) EEG and structural MRI analyses provided converging evidence of a right inferior insula deficit, characterized by a blunted neural monitoring signal and cortical atrophy. We confirmed this deficit in a separate 796-participant UK Biobank longitudinal COVID re-imaging cohort, where COVID-19 survivors also showed selective impairment on a perceptual processing task and corresponding longitudinal atrophy of the right inferior insula compared with healthy controls. Finally, in a proof-of-principle randomized, sham-controlled trial (n = 40), a non-invasive, excitatory theta-burst ultrasound stimulation protocol targeting the right inferior insula rescued the perceptual deficit by reducing false alarms. These findings provide evidence of a causal role for right inferior insula dysfunction in long COVID-related perceptual impairment and show that modulation of this region can rescue the deficit, establishing it as a novel therapeutic target for long COVID cognitive impairment.
