Summary

This paper introduces ProstNFound+, a novel adaptation of medical foundation models (FMs) for prostate cancer (PCa) detection using micro-ultrasound (ŒºUS). It presents the first prospective validation of such a system, demonstrating strong generalization and consistent high performance five years later in a new clinical setting, aligning closely with expert clinical protocols.

Medical Relevance

This research significantly advances prostate cancer diagnosis by leveraging cutting-edge AI (foundation models) on micro-ultrasound, potentially leading to more accurate, consistent, and scalable detection of clinically significant PCa, thus improving patient outcomes and reducing reliance on highly specialized expert interpretation.

AI Health Application

The research develops and prospectively evaluates a medical foundation model (ProstNFound+) designed to detect clinically significant prostate cancer from micro-ultrasound images. This AI application aims to provide a cancer heatmap and risk score, offering an automated, scalable, and interpretable tool to assist clinicians in diagnosing prostate cancer, thereby enhancing diagnostic systems in healthcare.

Key Points

  • ProstNFound+ is the first medical foundation model (FM) adapted for prostate cancer detection from micro-ultrasound (ŒºUS) to undergo prospective clinical validation.
  • The model incorporates a medical FM, adapter tuning, and a custom prompt encoder designed to embed PCa-specific clinical biomarkers.
  • ProstNFound+ generates a cancer heatmap and a risk score specifically for clinically significant prostate cancer.
  • Crucial prospective evaluation was performed on data acquired five years after initial training, from a new, previously unseen clinical site, demonstrating robust generalization.
  • Results show strong generalization to the prospective data with no performance degradation compared to retrospective evaluation.
  • Model predictions align closely with standard clinical scoring protocols (PRI-MUS and PI-RADS) and produce interpretable heatmaps consistent with biopsy-confirmed lesions.
  • The study highlights ProstNFound+'s potential for clinical deployment as a scalable, interpretable alternative to current expert-driven diagnostic protocols.

Methodology

ProstNFound+ is built upon a medical foundation model, enhanced with adapter tuning and a custom prompt encoder that integrates PCa-specific clinical biomarkers. The model was trained on multi-center retrospective micro-ultrasound data and subsequently prospectively validated on data acquired five years later from a new, independent clinical site. Model predictions, comprising a cancer heatmap and a risk score for clinically significant PCa, were benchmarked against established clinical scoring protocols (PRI-MUS and PI-RADS).

Key Findings

The study found that ProstNFound+ demonstrated strong generalization to new prospective data, exhibiting no performance degradation compared to its retrospective evaluation. Its predictions showed close alignment with established clinical scores, and the generated interpretable heatmaps consistently corresponded with biopsy-confirmed cancerous lesions.

Clinical Impact

ProstNFound+ offers a promising, scalable, and interpretable automated diagnostic tool for prostate cancer detection from micro-ultrasound. Its demonstrated robust prospective generalization suggests it could significantly enhance clinical workflows, potentially improving diagnostic accuracy and consistency, reducing inter-reader variability, and providing a high-performance alternative to expert-driven protocols.

Abstract

Purpose: Medical foundation models (FMs) offer a path to build high-performance diagnostic systems. However, their application to prostate cancer (PCa) detection from micro-ultrasound ({\mu}US) remains untested in clinical settings. We present ProstNFound+, an adaptation of FMs for PCa detection from {\mu}US, along with its first prospective validation. Methods: ProstNFound+ incorporates a medical FM, adapter tuning, and a custom prompt encoder that embeds PCa-specific clinical biomarkers. The model generates a cancer heatmap and a risk score for clinically significant PCa. Following training on multi-center retrospective data, the model is prospectively evaluated on data acquired five years later from a new clinical site. Model predictions are benchmarked against standard clinical scoring protocols (PRI-MUS and PI-RADS). Results: ProstNFound+ shows strong generalization to the prospective data, with no performance degradation compared to retrospective evaluation. It aligns closely with clinical scores and produces interpretable heatmaps consistent with biopsy-confirmed lesions. Conclusion: The results highlight its potential for clinical deployment, offering a scalable and interpretable alternative to expert-driven protocols.
