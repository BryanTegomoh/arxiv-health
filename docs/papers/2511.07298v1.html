LMM-IQA: Image Quality Assessment for Low-Dose CT Imaging

Published: 2025-11-10

Summary

This paper introduces an LLM-based image quality assessment (IQA) system for low-dose computed tomography (CT) imaging, addressing the inherent trade-off between reduced radiation exposure and potential diagnostic quality degradation. The system uniquely generates both quantitative numerical quality scores and qualitative textual descriptions of degradations, with performance enhanced by systematically integrated inference strategies.

Medical Relevance

Low-dose CT is vital for patient safety by reducing radiation exposure, but ensuring diagnostic quality is paramount. This research provides a robust and interpretable tool to consistently assess LDCT image quality, thereby supporting accurate diagnoses and confident clinical decision-making while maximizing the benefits of reduced radiation.

AI Health Application

An LLM-based AI system that automatically assesses the quality of low-dose CT images, providing both numerical scores and interpretable textual descriptions of image degradations (e.g., noise, blur, contrast loss). This application aims to enhance diagnostic reliability, improve patient safety by validating low-dose protocols, and streamline clinical workflows in radiology.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical challenge of maintaining diagnostic quality in low-dose CT imaging, where noise, blur, and contrast loss are often increased.
  • Proposes a novel LLM-based system specifically for Image Quality Assessment (IQA) in medical imaging, particularly for low-dose CT.
  • The system provides a dual output: objective numerical quality scores and subjective, interpretable textual descriptions of specific image degradations (e.g., noise, blur, contrast loss).
  • Various inference strategies, including zero-shot learning, metadata integration, and error feedback, were systematically explored and shown to progressively improve the system's overall assessment performance.
  • Resultant assessments yield numerical scores that are highly correlated with true image quality, indicating robustness and accuracy.
  • The generated textual descriptions offer clinically interpretable insights into image degradations, thereby enhancing the utility for medical professionals.
  • The comprehensive and interpretable outputs are designed to add significant value to existing clinical workflows by providing clear and consistent image quality evaluations.

Methodology

The study proposes an LLM-based quality assessment system that generates both numerical scores and textual descriptions of degradations. Its performance was evaluated by systematically examining various inference strategies, ranging from a zero-shot approach to integrating metadata and incorporating error feedback mechanisms, demonstrating the progressive contribution of each method.

Key Findings

The LLM-based IQA system produced highly correlated numerical quality scores and interpretable textual descriptions of specific image degradations. The systematic investigation of inference strategies confirmed their cumulative and progressive contribution to enhancing the overall performance and robustness of the quality assessment.

Clinical Impact

This system has the potential to significantly improve clinical workflows by providing radiologists and clinicians with consistent, robust, and interpretable assessments of low-dose CT image quality. This can aid in confident diagnosis, reduce inter-observer variability in image interpretation, and ensure that images, despite lower radiation doses, meet the necessary diagnostic standards, ultimately enhancing patient care and safety.

Abstract

Low-dose computed tomography (CT) represents a significant improvement in patient safety through lower radiation doses, but increased noise, blur, and contrast loss can diminish diagnostic quality. Therefore, consistency and robustness in image quality assessment become essential for clinical applications. In this study, we propose an LLM-based quality assessment system that generates both numerical scores and textual descriptions of degradations such as noise, blur, and contrast loss. Furthermore, various inference strategies - from the zero-shot approach to metadata integration and error feedback - are systematically examined, demonstrating the progressive contribution of each method to overall performance. The resultant assessments yield not only highly correlated scores but also interpretable output, thereby adding value to clinical workflows. The source codes of our study are available at https://github.com/itu-biai/lmms_ldct_iqa.
