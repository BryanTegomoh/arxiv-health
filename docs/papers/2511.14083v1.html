Automated glenoid bone loss measurement and segmentation in CT scans for pre-operative planning in shoulder instability

arXiv ID: 2511.14083v1
Published: 2025-11-18

Summary

A fully automated deep learning pipeline was developed and validated for measuring glenoid bone loss on 3D CT scans using a multi-stage approach. This method demonstrated strong agreement with consensus readings and superior consistency compared to manual surgeon measurements, making it a reliable tool for pre-operative planning in shoulder instability.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and consistent measurement of glenoid bone loss is critical for effective surgical planning in shoulder instability, influencing treatment choice and patient outcomes. This automated pipeline addresses the current challenges of manual methods, such as inter-reader variability and time consumption, by providing a standardized and highly reliable diagnostic tool.

AI Health Application

The AI application is an automated deep learning pipeline that performs segmentation of anatomical structures (glenoid, humerus) and detects landmarks on 3D CT scans to precisely measure glenoid bone loss. This AI tool is designed to provide time-efficient and more consistent measurements than current manual methods, aiding surgeons in pre-operative planning for shoulder instability and in screening patients, thereby improving surgical outcomes and patient management.

Key Points

  • Developed a fully automated deep learning pipeline for precise glenoid bone loss measurement on 3D Computed Tomography (CT) scans.
  • The pipeline comprises three main stages: U-Net-based segmentation of the glenoid and humerus, a second neural network for anatomical glenoid rim landmark detection, and geometric fitting (PCA, projection, circle fitting) for bone loss calculation.
  • Validated on a retrospective dataset of 91 patient CT scans with manually labeled segmentations, landmarks, and bone loss measurements serving as ground truth.
  • Automated measurements exhibited strong agreement with consensus readings and significantly exceeded surgeon-to-surgeon consistency (Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) 0.84 vs 0.78).
  • Demonstrated superior consistency in both low (ICC 0.71 vs 0.63) and high (ICC 0.83 vs 0.21) bone loss subgroups compared to manual readings (P < 0.001).
  • Achieved high recall for classifying patients into low (0.714) and high (0.857) bone loss severity categories, with no misclassification between these critical groups.
  • The method is presented as a time-efficient and clinically reliable tool suitable for preoperative planning and screening in patients with shoulder instability.

Methodology

The study employed a multi-stage deep learning pipeline. Stage 1 utilized a U-Net convolutional neural network to automatically segment the glenoid and humerus from 3D CT images. Stage 2 involved a second neural network designed to detect specific anatomical glenoid rim points. In Stage 3, geometric fitting techniques, including Principal Component Analysis (PCA), projection onto an en-face view, and best-circle fitting, were applied to these detected points to precisely compute the percentage of glenoid bone loss. The pipeline was trained and validated using 91 retrospectively collected patient CT scans, accompanied by expert manual labels for segmentation, landmarks, and bone loss measurements.

Key Findings

The automated bone loss measurements showed strong agreement with consensus readings and significantly surpassed surgeon-to-surgeon consistency, with an ICC of 0.84 compared to 0.78 for human readers. This superior consistency was particularly evident in both low-bone-loss (ICC 0.71 vs 0.63) and high-bone-loss (ICC 0.83 vs 0.21) subgroups (P < 0.001). For classifying patients into low, medium, and high bone loss categories, the pipeline achieved a recall of 0.714 for low severity and 0.857 for high severity, crucially without any cases being misclassified between the low and high severity groups.

Clinical Impact

This automated deep learning pipeline offers a significant clinical advancement by providing a time-efficient and highly reliable method for measuring glenoid bone loss. It can standardize pre-operative planning for shoulder instability, reduce inter-reader variability among orthopedic surgeons and radiologists, and facilitate more accurate and consistent surgical decision-making. The ability to precisely classify bone loss severity can improve patient screening, identify candidates for specific surgical techniques (e.g., bone transfer procedures), and ultimately lead to better surgical outcomes and reduced recurrence rates in shoulder instability patients.

Abstract

Reliable measurement of glenoid bone loss is essential for operative planning in shoulder instability, but current manual and semi-automated methods are time-consuming and often subject to interreader variability. We developed and validated a fully automated deep learning pipeline for measuring glenoid bone loss on three-dimensional computed tomography (CT) scans using a linear-based, en-face view, best-circle method. Shoulder CT images of 91 patients (average age, 40 years; range, 14-89 years; 65 men) were retrospectively collected along with manual labels including glenoid segmentation, landmarks, and bone loss measurements. The multi-stage algorithm has three main stages: (1) segmentation, where we developed a U-Net to automatically segment the glenoid and humerus; (2) anatomical landmark detection, where a second network predicts glenoid rim points; and (3) geometric fitting, where we applied principal component analysis (PCA), projection, and circle fitting to compute the percentage of bone loss. The automated measurements showed strong agreement with consensus readings and exceeded surgeon-to-surgeon consistency (intraclass correlation coefficient (ICC) 0.84 vs 0.78), including in low- and high-bone-loss subgroups (ICC 0.71 vs 0.63 and 0.83 vs 0.21, respectively; P < 0.001). For classifying patients into low, medium, and high bone-loss categories, the pipeline achieved a recall of 0.714 for low and 0.857 for high severity, with no low cases misclassified as high or vice versa. These results suggest that our method is a time-efficient and clinically reliable tool for preoperative planning in shoulder instability and for screening patients with substantial glenoid bone loss. Code and dataset are available at https://github.com/Edenliu1/Auto-Glenoid-Measurement-DL-Pipeline.
