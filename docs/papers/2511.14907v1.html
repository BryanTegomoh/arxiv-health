nnMIL: A generalizable multiple instance learning framework for computational pathology

arXiv ID: 2511.14907v1
Published: 2025-11-18
Authors: Xiangde Luo, Jinxi Xiang, Yuanfeng Ji, Ruijiang Li
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00

Summary

nnMIL is a novel multiple-instance learning (MIL) framework designed to overcome generalizability and reliability limitations of existing methods in computational pathology. It effectively aggregates rich patch-level features from foundation models into robust, slide-level clinical predictions. This framework demonstrates superior performance and strong cross-model generalization across 35 diverse clinical tasks, enhancing the practical deployment of AI in real-world settings.

Medical Relevance

This framework significantly advances computational pathology by providing a more robust, generalizable, and reliable method for AI-driven diagnosis, prognosis, and molecular profiling from whole-slide images, directly impacting patient care and precision medicine. Its ability to quantify uncertainty makes AI predictions more trustworthy for clinical decision-making.

AI Health Application

The paper introduces nnMIL, an AI framework utilizing multiple instance learning, for analyzing whole-slide images in computational pathology. Its application is to provide robust, generalized, and reliable AI assistance for tasks such as disease diagnosis, histologic subtyping, molecular biomarker detection, and cancer prognosis prediction, thereby improving clinical inference and supporting treatment decisions.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical challenge of limited generalizability and reliability in existing multiple-instance learning (MIL) methods for aggregating patch-level features from pathology foundation models into slide-level predictions.
  • Introduces random sampling at both the patch and feature levels, enabling large-batch optimization, task-aware sampling strategies, and efficient, scalable training across diverse datasets and model architectures.
  • Employs a lightweight aggregator that performs sliding-window inference to generate ensemble slide-level predictions, coupled with principled uncertainty estimation for enhanced clinical trustworthiness.
  • Consistently outperformed existing MIL methods across a large-scale evaluation involving 40,000 Whole-Slide Images (WSIs), 35 distinct clinical tasks, and four different pathology foundation models.
  • Demonstrated significant improvements in diverse clinical applications including disease diagnosis, histologic subtyping, molecular biomarker detection, and pan-cancer prognosis prediction.
  • Showcased strong cross-model generalization capabilities and robust survival stratification in multiple independent external cohorts, underscoring its real-world applicability and reliability.
  • Offers a practical and generalizable solution for translating advanced pathology foundation models into clinically meaningful and deployable AI systems.

Methodology

nnMIL is a multiple-instance learning framework that connects patch-level foundation models to slide-level clinical inference. It introduces random sampling at both the patch and feature levels to facilitate large-batch optimization and task-aware sampling strategies. A lightweight aggregator performs sliding-window inference to create ensemble slide-level predictions, and the framework also supports principled uncertainty estimation for its outputs. This design promotes efficient, scalable training and robust inference.

Key Findings

nnMIL consistently outperformed existing MIL methods across 35 clinical tasks, including disease diagnosis, histologic subtyping, molecular biomarker detection, and pan-cancer prognosis, on a dataset of 40,000 WSIs and utilizing four different pathology foundation models. It demonstrated strong cross-model generalization, reliable uncertainty quantification, and robust survival stratification when validated on multiple external cohorts.

Clinical Impact

This framework offers a practical and reliable solution for integrating advanced AI models into clinical pathology workflows, enabling more accurate and generalizable predictions for diagnosis, prognosis, and personalized treatment. Its robust performance and reliable uncertainty estimates can foster greater trust in AI-driven pathology tools, accelerating their adoption and improving patient outcomes in real-world healthcare settings.

Limitations

The abstract primarily highlights the limitations of *existing* MIL methods that nnMIL aims to overcome (design constraints hindering generalizability and reliability). It does not explicitly state specific limitations or caveats of the nnMIL framework itself within this summary.

Future Directions

The paper implies future efforts in advancing the development and deployment of reliable AI systems in real-world clinical settings, particularly in translating the powerful capabilities of pathology foundation models into clinically meaningful and actionable predictions.

Abstract

Computational pathology holds substantial promise for improving diagnosis and guiding treatment decisions. Recent pathology foundation models enable the extraction of rich patch-level representations from large-scale whole-slide images (WSIs), but current approaches for aggregating these features into slide-level predictions remain constrained by design limitations that hinder generalizability and reliability. Here, we developed nnMIL, a simple yet broadly applicable multiple-instance learning framework that connects patch-level foundation models to robust slide-level clinical inference. nnMIL introduces random sampling at both the patch and feature levels, enabling large-batch optimization, task-aware sampling strategies, and efficient and scalable training across datasets and model architectures. A lightweight aggregator performs sliding-window inference to generate ensemble slide-level predictions and supports principled uncertainty estimation. Across 40,000 WSIs encompassing 35 clinical tasks and four pathology foundation models, nnMIL consistently outperformed existing MIL methods for disease diagnosis, histologic subtyping, molecular biomarker detection, and pan- cancer prognosis prediction. It further demonstrated strong cross-model generalization, reliable uncertainty quantification, and robust survival stratification in multiple external cohorts. In conclusion, nnMIL offers a practical and generalizable solution for translating pathology foundation models into clinically meaningful predictions, advancing the development and deployment of reliable AI systems in real-world settings.
