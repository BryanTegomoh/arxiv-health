A Review of Wearable Sweat Monitoring Platforms: From Biomarker Detection to Signal Processing Systems

arXiv ID: 2512.01320v1
Published: 2025-12-01
Authors: Yuhan Zheng
Categories: q-bio.BM
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00

Summary

This review provides a comprehensive analysis of wearable sweat monitoring platforms, detailing advancements in biomarker detection and signal processing systems. It highlights sweat's potential for personalized health and precision medicine due to its noninvasive nature, while also addressing critical challenges such as the lack of clinically validated standards and the need to establish reliable correlations between sweat and blood analytes.

Medical Relevance

This research is fundamentally important for advancing noninvasive continuous health monitoring, thereby enabling more effective personalized health management, precise disease diagnostics, and optimized interventions in medicine and sports through real-time molecular-level analysis.

AI Health Application

While the paper itself reviews the foundational technology (biomarkers, sensors, signal processing hardware) for wearable sweat monitoring platforms, the data generated by such platforms is a prime candidate for subsequent analysis using medical AI. AI/machine learning algorithms could be applied to interpret complex biomarker patterns from continuous sweat monitoring, predict health outcomes, personalize interventions, detect early signs of disease, or optimize athletic performance. However, this specific review focuses on the *platform development* and *signal processing systems*, not the AI applications themselves.

Key Points

  • Wearable electronics are transformative for continuous, real-time health monitoring, enabling personalized health management, precision medicine, and athletic performance optimization.
  • Sweat is an ideal, noninvasive biofluid for next-generation wearable sensors due to its ease of acquisition and richness in molecular analytes.
  • A significant barrier to large-scale commercialization is the absence of clinically validated standards for sweat analysis and the necessity of establishing clear relationships between sweat and blood analyte concentrations.
  • The review offers an in-depth examination of sweat biomarkers, with a specific focus on their *partitioning mechanisms* to reveal underlying connections to systemic metabolic pathways.
  • It provides a novel, mechanistic-level analysis of biosensors employed in sweat sensing, filling a recognized gap in previous review literature.
  • The paper analyzes the electronic architectures crucial for sensor signal processing, giving particular emphasis to the *analog circuitry* responsible for interfacing with electrochemical sweat sensors from an interdisciplinary perspective.

Methodology

This paper employs a systematic literature review methodology, providing an overview of sweat biomarkers and their partitioning, offering a mechanistic-level examination of biosensors, and conducting an interdisciplinary analysis of electronic architectures, particularly analog circuitry, relevant to wearable sweat monitoring platforms.

Key Findings

The review's key insights include a detailed understanding of sweat biomarker partitioning mechanisms linking them to systemic metabolic pathways, a comprehensive mechanistic analysis of biosensors specific to sweat sensing, and an in-depth exploration of the electronic architectures, especially analog circuitry, vital for processing electrochemical sweat sensor signals.

Clinical Impact

By systematically addressing key technical and validation challenges, this review significantly contributes to accelerating the development and clinical translation of robust wearable sweat monitoring platforms, potentially leading to widespread adoption of noninvasive, real-time diagnostic and health management tools, which could transform patient care and athletic optimization.

Limitations

The review implicitly highlights the primary limitations within the field: the current lack of clinically validated standards for sweat analysis and the insufficient establishment of quantitative relationships between sweat and blood analyte concentrations, which are prerequisites for widespread clinical utility and commercialization.

Future Directions

Future research must prioritize establishing robust, clinically validated standards for sweat analysis and rigorously defining the quantitative correlations between sweat and blood analyte concentrations. This will be crucial for overcoming existing barriers to commercialization and enabling the reliable clinical application of wearable sweat monitoring platforms.

Abstract

Wearable electronics hold great potential in defining new paradigms of modern healthcare, including personalized health management, precision medicine, and athletic performance optimization. This stems from their ability in enabling continuous, real-time health monitoring. To enable molecular-level analysis, biofluids rich in molecular analytes have become one of the most important target samples for wearable sensors. Among them, sweat stands out as an ideal candidate for next-generation wearable health monitoring platforms due to its completely noninvasive nature and ease of acquisition. In recent years, several studies have demonstrated feasible prototype designs for sweat-based wearable sensors. However, one of the major gaps toward large-scale commercialization is the development of clinically validated standards for sweat analysis. One key requirement is to establish the relationship between sweat analytes and those of blood, the latter serving as the gold standard in modern diagnostics. This review provides an overview of sweat biomarkers, with a particular focus on their partitioning mechanisms, which reveal the underlying connections between sweat analytes and their counterparts within systemic metabolic pathways. In addition, this review offers a mechanistic-level examination of biosensors employed in sweat sensing, addressing a gap that has not been adequately covered in prior reviews. Given the critical role of electronic systems in constructing highly integrated wearable sweat-monitoring platforms, this review also analyzes the electronic architectures used for sensor signal processing from an interdisciplinary perspective, with particular emphasis on the analog circuitry that interfaces with electrochemical sensors.
