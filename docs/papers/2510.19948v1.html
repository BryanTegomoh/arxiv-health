Drug-disease networks and drug repurposing

Summary

This paper presents a novel drug-disease network, meticulously constructed from diverse data sources, to facilitate drug repurposing. It demonstrates that advanced network-based link prediction methods, especially graph embedding and network model fitting, achieve superior performance in predicting new drug-disease associations, significantly outperforming previous approaches and thus substantially reducing the search space for viable repurposing candidates.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a powerful computational tool to accelerate drug discovery by identifying new therapeutic uses for existing drugs, which can significantly reduce development costs and timelines, bringing new treatments to patients faster.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the use of natural language processing, graph embedding, and network model fitting to analyze drug-disease networks. This enables in silico prediction of novel drug-disease associations, which significantly reduces the search space for drug repurposing efforts, thereby accelerating the identification of existing drugs for new therapeutic uses.

Key Points

  • Addresses the high cost and time of de novo drug development by focusing on efficient drug repurposing.
  • Introduces a novel drug-disease network compiled through a hybrid approach: combining existing databases (textual/machine-readable), natural language processing (NLP) tools, and expert hand curation.
  • Applies network-based link prediction methods to infer potential new drug-disease associations within this network.
  • Identifies graph embedding and network model fitting as particularly effective computational methods for this prediction task.
  • Achieves impressive prediction performance, with an Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC) exceeding 0.95 and average precision almost 1000 times better than random chance.
  • Demonstrates a significant improvement in prediction efficacy compared to prior computational approaches, enhancing the reliability of in silico drug repurposing predictions.
  • The primary goal is to drastically reduce the millions of potential drug-disease combinations to a manageable and highly probable set, making drug repurposing more feasible and cost-effective.

Methodology

The authors constructed a novel drug-disease network by integrating data from existing textual and machine-readable databases, employing natural language processing (NLP) tools, and performing hand curation to refine the associations. This network was then subjected to analysis using various network-based link prediction methods, with a focus on graph embedding techniques and network model fitting. The predictive efficacy of these methods was rigorously assessed via cross-validation tests to quantify performance.

Key Findings

The study found that several network-based link prediction methods, notably those based on graph embedding and network model fitting, achieved exceptionally high prediction performance for drug-disease associations. These methods yielded an Area Under the ROC Curve (AUC) above 0.95 and an average precision nearly a thousand times greater than chance. This performance represents a significant advancement over previously established computational approaches for predicting drug-disease relationships.

Clinical Impact

This work has the potential to significantly accelerate the identification of new therapeutic indications for existing drugs, leading to faster and more cost-effective development of treatments. By providing highly accurate computational predictions, it can guide preclinical and clinical trials more efficiently, ultimately expanding the arsenal of available therapies for a wide range of diseases and improving patient outcomes.

Abstract

Repurposing existing drugs to treat new diseases is a cost-effective alternative to de novo drug development, but there are millions of potential drug-disease combinations to be considered with only a small fraction being viable. In silico predictions of drug-disease associations can be invaluable for reducing the size of the search space. In this work we present a novel network of drugs and the diseases they treat, compiled using a combination of existing textual and machine-readable databases, natural-language processing tools, and hand curation, and analyze it using network-based link prediction methods to identify potential drug-disease combinations. We measure the efficacy of these methods using cross-validation tests and find that several methods, particularly those based on graph embedding and network model fitting, achieve impressive prediction performance, significantly better than previous approaches, with area under the ROC curve above 0.95 and average precision almost a thousand times better than chance.
