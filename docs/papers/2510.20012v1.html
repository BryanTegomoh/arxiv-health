AI Pose Analysis and Kinematic Profiling of Range-of-Motion Variations in Resistance Training

Published: 2025-10-22
Authors: Adam Diamant
Categories: stat.AP, cs.CV

Summary

This study develops an AI-based pose estimation pipeline to precisely quantify movement kinematics in resistance training, analyzing range-of-motion (ROM) variations in lengthened partial (pROM) versus full ROM (fROM) exercises. It found that pROM involved smaller ROM and shorter durations, particularly during the eccentric phase, with participant-level differences being the primary source of variability. The research introduces a novel, consistent metric, %ROM, demonstrating AI's potential to enhance research and personalize resistance training prescription.

Medical Relevance

This research provides objective and precise methods for analyzing exercise mechanics, crucial for evidence-based resistance training prescription, rehabilitation protocols, and injury prevention in clinical settings by enabling personalized adjustments based on individual movement patterns.

AI Health Application

The AI pose analysis system can be used to objectively measure and track human movement during resistance exercises. This enables clinicians and health professionals to more accurately prescribe exercises, monitor patient/client progress, identify incorrect forms that could lead to injury, and tailor rehabilitation or training programs based on precise kinematic data. It offers an objective tool for evidence-based exercise prescription in clinical and health settings.

Methodology

The study utilized an AI-based pose estimation pipeline to process video data. From 280 recordings of 26 participants performing 8 upper-body exercises, frame-level joint-angle trajectories were extracted. These trajectories underwent filtering and smoothing, after which per-set metrics (range of motion, tempo, concentric/eccentric phase durations) were derived. A random-effects meta-analytic model was applied to statistically analyze the data, accounting for within-participant and between-exercise variability.

Key Findings

Lengthened partial ROM repetitions were performed with a smaller range of motion and shorter overall durations, particularly in the eccentric phase, compared to full ROM. Participant-level differences, rather than exercise type, were the primary source of movement variation, with heterogeneous treatment effects observed. A novel metric, %ROM (proportion of full ROM achieved during pROM), was introduced and demonstrated consistency across exercises.

Clinical Impact

The AI-based kinematic profiling can lead to highly personalized resistance training prescriptions, optimizing exercise programs for individual athletes or patients. It provides objective data for physical therapists to tailor rehabilitation exercises, monitor progress, and reduce injury risk by identifying precise movement deviations. The %ROM metric offers a standardized way to prescribe partial ranges of motion, enhancing clarity and consistency in training protocols.

Abstract

This study develops an AI-based pose estimation pipeline to enable precise quantification of movement kinematics in resistance training. Using video data from Wolf et al. (2025), which compared lengthened partial (pROM) and full range-of-motion (fROM) training across eight upper-body exercises in 26 participants, 280 recordings were processed to extract frame-level joint-angle trajectories. After filtering and smoothing, per-set metrics were derived, including range of motion (ROM), tempo, and concentric/eccentric phase durations. A random-effects meta-analytic model was applied to account for within-participant and between-exercise variability. Results show that pROM repetitions were performed with a smaller ROM and shorter overall durations, particularly during the eccentric phase of movement. Variance analyses revealed that participant-level differences, rather than exercise-specific factors, were the primary driver of variation, although there is substantial evidence of heterogeneous treatment effects. We then introduce a novel metric, \%ROM, which is the proportion of full ROM achieved during pROM, and demonstrate that this definition of lengthened partials remains relatively consistent across exercises. Overall, these findings suggest that lengthened partials differ from full ROM training not only in ROM, but also in execution dynamics and consistency, highlighting the potential of AI-based methods for advancing research and improving resistance training prescription.
