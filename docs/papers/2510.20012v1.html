Summary

This study introduces an AI-based pose estimation pipeline for precise kinematic quantification in resistance training, comparing lengthened partial (pROM) and full (fROM) ranges of motion. It found that pROM repetitions had smaller ROM and shorter durations, especially during the eccentric phase, and identified participant-level variations as the primary driver of differences. The research proposes a novel, consistent metric, "%ROM", highlighting the utility of AI for advancing resistance training research and prescription.

Medical Relevance

This research offers objective, data-driven insights into the biomechanics of different resistance training techniques, which is crucial for evidence-based exercise prescription in rehabilitation, injury prevention, and athletic performance optimization, leading to more effective and potentially safer training outcomes.

The AI pose analysis system can be used to provide personalized and optimized resistance training prescriptions, monitor patient progress in rehabilitation settings, ensure correct exercise form to prevent injuries, and support remote monitoring of physical activity in various health contexts.

Methodology

An AI-based pose estimation pipeline was developed to process video data from 280 recordings comparing lengthened partial and full range-of-motion training across eight upper-body exercises. Frame-level joint-angle trajectories were extracted, filtered, and smoothed. Per-set metrics, including ROM, tempo, and concentric/eccentric phase durations, were derived. A random-effects meta-analytic model was then applied to statistically account for within-participant and between-exercise variability.

Key Findings

Lengthened partial ROM repetitions consistently showed a smaller range of motion and shorter overall durations, predominantly due to a reduction in the eccentric phase of movement. Participant-level differences were the primary determinant of kinematic variation, rather than exercise-specific factors, despite the presence of heterogeneous treatment effects. A novel metric, "%ROM" (proportion of full ROM achieved), was introduced and demonstrated consistency across exercises, indicating a stable definition of lengthened partials.

Clinical Impact

This AI-driven methodology provides clinicians, physical therapists, and strength coaches with a precise, objective tool for analyzing and quantifying resistance exercise execution. This can lead to more individualized and optimized training prescriptions for rehabilitation, injury prevention, and performance enhancement, by allowing for precise control and monitoring of ROM, tempo, and phase durations based on objective data.

Abstract

This study develops an AI-based pose estimation pipeline to enable precise quantification of movement kinematics in resistance training. Using video data from Wolf et al. (2025), which compared lengthened partial (pROM) and full range-of-motion (fROM) training across eight upper-body exercises in 26 participants, 280 recordings were processed to extract frame-level joint-angle trajectories. After filtering and smoothing, per-set metrics were derived, including range of motion (ROM), tempo, and concentric/eccentric phase durations. A random-effects meta-analytic model was applied to account for within-participant and between-exercise variability. Results show that pROM repetitions were performed with a smaller ROM and shorter overall durations, particularly during the eccentric phase of movement. Variance analyses revealed that participant-level differences, rather than exercise-specific factors, were the primary driver of variation, although there is substantial evidence of heterogeneous treatment effects. We then introduce a novel metric, \%ROM, which is the proportion of full ROM achieved during pROM, and demonstrate that this definition of lengthened partials remains relatively consistent across exercises. Overall, these findings suggest that lengthened partials differ from full ROM training not only in ROM, but also in execution dynamics and consistency, highlighting the potential of AI-based methods for advancing research and improving resistance training prescription.
