AI Pose Analysis and Kinematic Profiling of Range-of-Motion Variations in Resistance Training

arXiv ID: 2510.20012v1
Published: 2025-10-22
Authors: Adam Diamant
Categories: stat.AP, cs.CV
Relevance Score: 0.85 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This study introduces an AI-based pose estimation pipeline for precise kinematic quantification of resistance training, analyzing lengthened partial (pROM) and full range-of-motion (fROM) exercises. It reveals that pROM repetitions are characterized by smaller ROM and shorter durations, especially in the eccentric phase, with participant-level differences being the primary driver of variation. The research underscores the potential of AI-driven methods to advance exercise science and optimize resistance training prescription by providing objective insights into execution dynamics and consistency.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a highly precise and objective method for analyzing exercise form and range of motion, critical for injury prevention, rehabilitation efficacy, and optimizing training outcomes across various medical and health contexts. It offers data-driven insights to inform evidence-based exercise prescription for clinicians, physical therapists, and sports medicine professionals.

AI Health Application

AI-driven pose analysis for optimizing exercise prescription, monitoring rehabilitation progress, ensuring proper form to prevent injury, and personalizing fitness regimes based on precise kinematic profiling.

Key Points

  • Development of an AI-based pose estimation pipeline to precisely quantify movement kinematics (e.g., joint-angle trajectories) in resistance training.
  • Analysis of 280 video recordings from 26 participants performing eight upper-body exercises, comparing lengthened partial (pROM) and full range-of-motion (fROM) training.
  • Derivation of per-set metrics including range of motion (ROM), tempo, and durations of concentric and eccentric movement phases after filtering and smoothing joint-angle data.
  • Results indicate pROM repetitions are performed with a smaller ROM and shorter overall durations, with a particularly notable reduction in the eccentric phase, compared to fROM.
  • Variance analyses revealed that participant-level differences, rather than exercise-specific factors, were the primary drivers of kinematic variation, alongside evidence of heterogeneous treatment effects.
  • Introduction of a novel metric, "%ROM", defined as the proportion of full ROM achieved during pROM, which was demonstrated to be relatively consistent across different exercises.
  • The findings highlight that lengthened partials differ from full ROM training not only in the extent of movement but also in their execution dynamics and consistency, emphasizing the utility of AI in exercise science and prescription.

Methodology

An AI-based pose estimation pipeline was developed to extract frame-level joint-angle trajectories from video data of resistance training. These raw trajectories underwent filtering and smoothing, followed by the derivation of per-set metrics such as total range of motion (ROM), movement tempo, and specific durations for the concentric and eccentric phases. A random-effects meta-analytic model was then applied to statistically account for within-participant and between-exercise variability when comparing lengthened partial and full range-of-motion training. A new metric, %ROM, was also defined and analyzed for consistency.

Clinical Impact

This research offers a powerful tool for individualized exercise prescription and monitoring in clinical settings. Physical therapists and sports medicine practitioners can leverage AI-driven kinematic analysis to: 1) precisely tailor rehabilitation protocols by specifying exact ROM and tempo, ensuring optimal recovery and injury prevention; 2) objectively evaluate patient progress and adherence to prescribed movement patterns; 3) provide quantitative feedback to athletes and patients to improve technique, enhance performance, and mitigate injury risk; and 4) standardize research on exercise interventions, leading to more robust and applicable clinical guidelines. This enables a more evidence-based and precise approach to resistance training in both therapeutic and performance-enhancement contexts.

Abstract

This study develops an AI-based pose estimation pipeline to enable precise quantification of movement kinematics in resistance training. Using video data from Wolf et al. (2025), which compared lengthened partial (pROM) and full range-of-motion (fROM) training across eight upper-body exercises in 26 participants, 280 recordings were processed to extract frame-level joint-angle trajectories. After filtering and smoothing, per-set metrics were derived, including range of motion (ROM), tempo, and concentric/eccentric phase durations. A random-effects meta-analytic model was applied to account for within-participant and between-exercise variability. Results show that pROM repetitions were performed with a smaller ROM and shorter overall durations, particularly during the eccentric phase of movement. Variance analyses revealed that participant-level differences, rather than exercise-specific factors, were the primary driver of variation, although there is substantial evidence of heterogeneous treatment effects. We then introduce a novel metric, \%ROM, which is the proportion of full ROM achieved during pROM, and demonstrate that this definition of lengthened partials remains relatively consistent across exercises. Overall, these findings suggest that lengthened partials differ from full ROM training not only in ROM, but also in execution dynamics and consistency, highlighting the potential of AI-based methods for advancing research and improving resistance training prescription.
