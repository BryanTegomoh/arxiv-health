Design and Construction of a Dedicated Radiolucent 8-element Flexible Radiofrequency (RF) Torso Coil for the 1.0T Australian MRI-Linac System

Summary

This paper details the design, construction, and validation of a dedicated, flexible 8-element receive-only radiofrequency (RF) torso coil for the 1.0T Australian MRI-Linac system. The new coil significantly improves signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) compared to the existing whole-body coil, enabling faster, higher-quality imaging essential for real-time tumor tracking and adaptive radiotherapy, while ensuring full radiolucency compatible with radiation beam delivery.

Medical Relevance

This innovation is crucial for advancing MRI-guided radiotherapy, providing superior soft-tissue visualization and real-time tumor tracking during cancer treatment. It enables more precise and adaptive radiotherapy, which can lead to improved treatment efficacy and reduced collateral damage to healthy tissues.

Not a direct AI application. However, the improved image quality, enhanced anatomical detail, and increased imaging speed provided by this hardware development are critical enabling factors for future AI applications in areas such as real-time tumor tracking, automated image segmentation for treatment planning, and adaptive radiotherapy planning where fast, high-quality imaging is essential.

Key Points

  • The current 1.0T Australian MRI-Linac relies on a whole-body RF coil, whose large coil-patient separation limits SNR and fast imaging required for real-time tumour tracking.
  • A dedicated, flexible 8-element receive-only RF torso array was designed, tailored to the specific geometry and beam access requirements of the MRI-Linac system.
  • The new coil demonstrated substantial SNR improvement: approximately three-fold at the imaging center and eight-fold at superficial regions compared to the whole-body coil.
  • In vivo abdominal imaging confirmed enhanced anatomical detail, sharper tissue boundaries, and enabled parallel imaging with GRAPPA acceleration factors up to three without severe artefacts.
  • The coil array's radiolucency was rigorously validated through dosimetry measurements and dynamic beam-on noise analysis, showing dose differences below 1% and no measurable degradation in image noise during radiation delivery.
  • Validation methods included electromagnetic simulations, bench measurements, phantom imaging, in vivo abdominal imaging, dosimetry measurements, and dynamic beam-on noise analysis.
  • The work provides a practical RF solution compatible with patient rotation and beam delivery on the Australian MRI-Linac, crucial for advanced online adaptive radiotherapy.

Methodology

The methodology encompassed electromagnetic simulations for coil design, followed by bench measurements for electrical characterization. Performance was validated through phantom imaging to quantify SNR, and in vivo abdominal imaging to assess anatomical detail and parallel imaging capabilities. Radiodensity was confirmed via dosimetry measurements, and compatibility during radiation delivery was assessed through dynamic beam-on noise analysis.

Key Findings

The key findings include an approximate three-fold SNR improvement at the center and eight-fold at superficial regions, significant enhancement in anatomical detail and tissue boundary sharpness, and successful parallel imaging with GRAPPA acceleration up to factor three. Crucially, the coil was confirmed to be radiolucent with dose differences below 1% and no measurable image noise degradation during beam-on operation.

Clinical Impact

This dedicated RF coil will enable clinical implementation of high-SNR, fast imaging for real-time tumor tracking and online adaptive radiotherapy on the Australian MRI-Linac. This will allow clinicians to more accurately target tumors, dynamically adjust treatment delivery to account for organ motion, and potentially improve treatment outcomes by maximizing tumor dose while minimizing exposure to surrounding healthy tissues.

Abstract

Magnetic resonance imaging-guided linear accelerators (MRI-Linacs) are an emerging treatment technology that enable online soft-tissue visualisation and adaptive radiotherapy. The Australian 1.0 T MRI-Linac employs a fixed, inline beamline, with treatment angles achieved via patient couch rotation, and currently relies on a radiolucent whole-body radiofrequency (RF) coil. However, the large coil-patient separation limits signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and constrains the fast imaging required for real-time tumour tracking. Here, we show the design and validation of a dedicated, flexible 8-element receive-only RF torso array tailored to the geometry and beam access requirements of the Australian MRI-Linac. Electromagnetic simulations, bench measurements, and phantom imaging demonstrate approximately three-fold SNR improvement at the centre and eight-fold improvement at superficial regions compared with the whole-body coil. In vivo abdominal imaging confirms enhanced anatomical detail and sharper tissue boundaries, and the increased SNR enables parallel imaging with GRAPPA acceleration factors up to three without severe artefacts. Dosimetry measurements and dynamic beam-on noise analysis confirm that the coil array is effectively radiolucent, with dose differences below 1% and no measurable degradation in image noise. This work demonstrates a practical RF solution for accelerated, high-SNR imaging compatible with patient rotation and beam delivery on the Australian MRI-Linac.
