Summary

This paper addresses the challenging problem of segmenting white matter hyperintensities (WMH) and ischaemic stroke lesions (ISL) from FLAIR MRI, which are difficult to differentiate due to visual confounding and co-occurrence. The research evaluates six deep learning training strategies using large, partially labelled datasets, demonstrating that leveraging such data, particularly through pseudolabels, significantly improves segmentation model performance for these confounded lesions.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and automated segmentation of WMH and ISL is crucial for quantifying cerebral small vessel disease progression, assessing stroke severity, and guiding treatment strategies, ultimately leading to improved diagnostic precision and patient management in neurology.

AI Health Application

The paper describes the development and comparative evaluation of deep learning models for the automated segmentation of white matter hyperintensities and ischemic stroke lesions from FLAIR MRI scans. This is a direct medical AI application aimed at improving the accuracy and efficiency of diagnosing and quantifying these features, which are important for clinical assessment, research, and understanding neurological diseases like stroke and cerebral small vessel disease.

Key Points

  • WMH and ISL are key imaging markers of cerebral small vessel disease (SVD) and are visually ambiguous on FLAIR MRI, complicating accurate segmentation.
  • The study investigates six deep learning training strategies designed to effectively utilize partially labelled datasets for combined WMH and ISL segmentation.
  • A substantial dataset was compiled by combining privately held (fully and partially labelled) and publicly available (partially labelled) MRI volumes, totaling 2052 scans (1341 with WMH, 1152 with ISL ground truth).
  • The core challenge lies in developing and validating robust deep learning models for these features given their visual similarities and frequent co-occurrence within subjects.
  • Results show that several methods successfully leveraged partially labelled data to enhance model performance, overcoming traditional data annotation bottlenecks.
  • The strategy employing 'pseudolabels' was identified as the most effective among the evaluated methods, yielding the best overall segmentation results.
  • Improved automated segmentation of WMH and ISL is critical for advancing SVD research, quantifying disease burden, and supporting clinical decision-making.

Methodology

The study utilized deep learning models to perform image segmentation, comparing six distinct training strategies optimized for datasets with partial ground truth annotations. A large combined dataset of 2052 FLAIR MRI volumes, sourced from private fully/partially labelled datasets and publicly available partially labelled datasets, was used for training and evaluating these strategies.

Key Findings

The primary finding is that several deep learning training strategies can effectively leverage partially labelled datasets to significantly improve the performance of WMH and ISL segmentation models. Specifically, the strategy incorporating the use of pseudolabels demonstrated the superior performance among all methods evaluated.

Clinical Impact

This research provides a validated approach for developing more accurate and robust automated tools for segmenting WMH and ISL, even with limited fully annotated data. This will enable better quantification of disease burden in cerebral small vessel disease, enhance diagnostic capabilities for stroke, facilitate more precise monitoring of disease progression, and support the development of personalized treatment plans in clinical neurology and stroke care.

Abstract

White matter hyperintensities (WMH) and ischaemic stroke lesions (ISL) are imaging features associated with cerebral small vessel disease (SVD) that are visible on brain magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scans. The development and validation of deep learning models to segment and differentiate these features is difficult because they visually confound each other in the fluid-attenuated inversion recovery (FLAIR) sequence and often appear in the same subject. We investigated six strategies for training a combined WMH and ISL segmentation model using partially labelled data. We combined privately held fully and partially labelled datasets with publicly available partially labelled datasets to yield a total of 2052 MRI volumes, with 1341 and 1152 containing ground truth annotations for WMH and ISL respectively. We found that several methods were able to effectively leverage the partially labelled data to improve model performance, with the use of pseudolabels yielding the best result.
