NeuroLex: A Lightweight Domain Language Model for EEG Report Understanding and Generation

arXiv ID: 2511.12851v1
Published: 2025-11-17
Authors: Kang Yin, Hye-Bin Shin
Categories: cs.CL, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

NeuroLex is a novel, lightweight domain-adaptive language model specifically developed for understanding and generating clinical electroencephalogram (EEG) reports, addressing the limitations of general-purpose LMs in capturing specialized linguistic conventions. Trained purely on EEG report text, it achieves superior performance in EEG-specific tasks such as summarization, information extraction, and robustness to negation compared to general models of the same scale. This model serves as a foundation for interpretable and language-driven neural decoding in brain-computer interface applications.

Medical Relevance

Clinical EEG reports are crucial for diagnosing and managing neurological conditions. NeuroLex's ability to accurately interpret and generate these complex, domain-specific texts can significantly enhance the efficiency and precision of clinical neurophysiology, ultimately aiding clinicians and improving patient care.

AI Health Application

NeuroLex serves as a specialized AI tool for processing clinical EEG reports. Its applications include automated summarization of EEG reports, assisting clinicians with report generation and polishing, providing accurate answers to terminology questions regarding EEG interpretations, and serving as a linguistic backbone for multimodal systems that integrate EEG signals with language for neurological diagnosis, brain-computer interfaces, and neural decoding. It aims to enhance the efficiency, accuracy, and interpretability of EEG analysis within healthcare settings.

Key Points

  • NeuroLex is a lightweight, domain-adaptive language model uniquely tailored to the linguistic and diagnostic conventions of clinical EEG reports.
  • It was trained exclusively on EEG report text from the Harvard Electroencephalography Database, ensuring high specificity to the domain.
  • The model employs span-corruption for pretraining and instruction-style fine-tuning for tasks like report polishing, paragraph summarization, and terminology question answering.
  • NeuroLex can function as an independent textual model or as a decoder backbone for multimodal systems integrating EEG signals with language.
  • Comprehensive evaluations demonstrate NeuroLex's superior performance, including lower perplexity, higher extraction and summarization accuracy, and better label efficiency, compared to general LMs of equivalent scale.
  • It exhibits improved robustness against critical issues in clinical text, such as negation and factual hallucination, which are vital for diagnostic accuracy.
  • The model aims to bridge biomedical text modeling with brain-computer interface applications, offering a pathway towards interpretable and language-driven neural decoding.

Methodology

NeuroLex is a lightweight domain-adaptive language model pretrained using span-corruption on a dataset consisting purely of EEG report text from the Harvard Electroencephalography Database. It was then fine-tuned using an instruction-style approach on specific EEG-related tasks, including report polishing, paragraph summarization, and terminology question answering, to learn the inherent syntax and reasoning patterns of EEG interpretation.

Key Findings

NeuroLex significantly outperforms general-purpose language models of the same scale, achieving lower perplexity, higher accuracy in information extraction and summarization tasks, and superior label efficiency. A critical finding is its improved robustness to negation and factual hallucination, which are common challenges in clinical text analysis and essential for reliable medical applications.

Clinical Impact

NeuroLex has the potential to streamline clinical workflows by automating or assisting in the understanding and generation of EEG reports, thereby reducing cognitive load on neurologists and improving diagnostic efficiency. Furthermore, its role as a linguistic backbone for multimodal systems could lead to more interpretable brain-computer interfaces that translate complex neural activity directly into clinically meaningful language.

Limitations

While effective, NeuroLex is described as a 'lightweight' model trained 'purely on EEG report text from the Harvard Electroencephalography Database,' which may imply limitations in its generalizability to diverse EEG reporting styles across different institutions or broader biomedical textual domains beyond EEG. The abstract does not detail its performance within a full multimodal EEG-language system.

Future Directions

The paper explicitly states that NeuroLex offers a 'foundation for interpretable and language-driven neural decoding.' This indicates future research will likely focus on integrating NeuroLex as a decoder backbone into multimodal EEG-language systems, aiming to directly translate real-time brain signals into clinically relevant linguistic outputs.

Abstract

Clinical electroencephalogram (EEG) reports encode domain-specific linguistic conventions that general-purpose language models (LMs) fail to capture. We introduce NeuroLex, a lightweight domain-adaptive language model trained purely on EEG report text from the Harvard Electroencephalography Database. Unlike existing biomedical LMs, NeuroLex is tailored to the linguistic and diagnostic characteristics of EEG reporting, enabling it to serve as both an independent textual model and a decoder backbone for multimodal EEG-language systems. Using span-corruption pretraining and instruction-style fine-tuning on report polishing, paragraph summarization, and terminology question answering, NeuroLex learns the syntax and reasoning patterns characteristic of EEG interpretation. Comprehensive evaluations show that it achieves lower perplexity, higher extraction and summarization accuracy, better label efficiency, and improved robustness to negation and factual hallucination compared with general models of the same scale. With an EEG-aware linguistic backbone, NeuroLex bridges biomedical text modeling and brain-computer interface applications, offering a foundation for interpretable and language-driven neural decoding.
