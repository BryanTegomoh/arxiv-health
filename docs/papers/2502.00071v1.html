Including frameworks of public health ethics in computational modelling of infectious disease interventions

Summary

This paper addresses the critical gap where computational models for infectious disease interventions often lack integrated ethical frameworks. It presents a proof-of-concept demonstrating how public health values like outcome equity and aggregate benefit can be incorporated into a simple vaccination model. The study reveals significant value trade-offs, showing that optimal intervention strategies can differ based on the prioritized ethical framework.

Medical Relevance

This work is crucial for developing more ethically robust and equitable public health strategies, moving beyond purely epidemiological or economic optimizations to explicitly consider the moral dimensions of interventions, especially during infectious disease crises.

AI Health Application

The research applies computational modeling to evaluate and inform public health intervention strategies, integrating ethical considerations into the decision-making process. This constitutes a form of computational decision support for health and public health policy, which is a significant area of application for medical AI and advanced analytics.

Key Points

  • Computational models used for public health intervention decisions often do not explicitly integrate ethical frameworks or key public health values.
  • The authors propose a proof-of-concept to embed multiple public health values into the evaluation of computational model outcomes.
  • A simple computational model simulating vaccination against a pathogen (e.g., SARS-CoV-2) was used for demonstration.
  • The methodology involved examining a bounded space of alternative prioritizations for specific values: outcome equity and aggregate benefit.
  • The study identified significant value trade-offs, indicating that the 'optimal' intervention strategy varies depending on which ethical value is prioritized.
  • This work provides a foundational approach for incorporating diverse ethical values directly into the decision criteria used to evaluate public health interventions.
  • The integration of ethics into modelling helps bridge the gap between technical predictions and morally sound public health policy.

Methodology

The authors developed a proof-of-concept by integrating specific public health values (outcome equity and aggregate benefit) as explicit evaluation criteria within a simple computational model of vaccination. They explored a bounded parameter space of different value prioritizations to observe how these frameworks influenced the identification of optimal intervention strategies and to pinpoint potential value trade-offs.

Key Findings

The primary finding is the clear identification of value trade-offs, where the optimal strategies for infectious disease interventions (e.g., vaccination) differ significantly based on the ethical framework applied. Prioritizing outcome equity led to different optimal strategies compared to prioritizing aggregate benefit, demonstrating that ethical considerations fundamentally alter optimal decision pathways.

Clinical Impact

This research has the potential to enhance the ethical rigor of public health decision-making by providing a structured method for health authorities and policymakers to explicitly weigh and visualize the consequences of prioritizing different ethical values (e.g., equitable resource distribution vs. maximizing overall health outcomes) when designing and implementing interventions like vaccine campaigns during pandemics.

Limitations

The study is presented as a 'proof-of-concept' utilizing a 'simple computational model,' which implies it serves as an initial demonstration and may not yet encompass the full complexity of real-world epidemiological dynamics, the exhaustive range of public health interventions, or a comprehensive suite of ethical values and their intricate interdependencies.

Future Directions

Future work could involve expanding this approach to incorporate a wider array of public health ethical values into more complex and realistic computational models of infectious disease spread and intervention. This would enable more nuanced and robust ethical analyses to inform public health policy, potentially leading to the development of decision-support tools that explicitly integrate ethical considerations alongside epidemiological forecasts.

Abstract

Decisions on public health interventions to control infectious disease are often informed by computational models. Interpreting the predicted outcomes of a public health decision requires not only high-quality modelling, but also an ethical framework for assessing the benefits and harms associated with different options. The design and specification of ethical frameworks matured independently of computational modelling, so many values recognised as important for ethical decision-making are missing from computational models. We demonstrate a proof-of-concept approach to incorporate multiple public health values into the evaluation of a simple computational model for vaccination against a pathogen such as SARS-CoV-2. By examining a bounded space of alternative prioritisations of values (outcome equity and aggregate benefit) we identify value trade-offs, where the outcomes of optimal strategies differ depending on the ethical framework. This work demonstrates an approach to incorporating diverse values into decision criteria used to evaluate outcomes of models of infectious disease interventions.
