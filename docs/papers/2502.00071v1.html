Including frameworks of public health ethics in computational modelling of infectious disease interventions

Published: 2025-01-31
Authors: Alexander E. Zarebski, Nefel Tellioglu, Jessica E. Stockdale, Julie A. Spencer, Wasiur R. KhudaBukhsh, Joel C. Miller, Cameron Zachreson

Summary

This paper introduces a proof-of-concept for integrating public health ethics frameworks directly into computational models used to inform infectious disease interventions. It demonstrates how explicitly incorporating diverse values, such as outcome equity and aggregate benefit, reveals significant value trade-offs, leading to different optimal intervention strategies depending on the prioritized ethical framework. This approach aims to enhance the ethical robustness of public health decision-making by moving beyond purely epidemiological outcomes.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing ethical decision-making in public health, especially during infectious disease crises. By explicitly incorporating ethical values into predictive models, it ensures that interventions are not solely based on epidemiological efficiency but also consider broader societal impacts like equity, leading to more just and socially acceptable health policies.

AI Health Application

The paper describes the integration of ethical frameworks into 'computational modelling' for evaluating and optimizing public health interventions. While not explicitly using the term 'AI', this approach aligns with the application of computational decision support systems in health. Such systems, particularly those that handle complex multi-objective optimization ('optimal strategies'), scenario analysis, and the incorporation of qualitative values ('outcome equity', 'aggregate benefit', 'ethical frameworks') into quantitative models, often leverage or can be enhanced by AI/machine learning techniques for predictive modeling, policy recommendation, and ethical AI development in a health context.

Key Points

  • Traditional computational models for infectious disease interventions often lack integrated ethical frameworks and recognized public health values.
  • The study proposes a proof-of-concept method to incorporate multiple public health values (e.g., outcome equity, aggregate benefit) into the evaluation of model-predicted outcomes.
  • A simple computational model simulating vaccination against a pathogen like SARS-CoV-2 serves as the demonstration case.
  • The methodology involves exploring a bounded space of alternative prioritizations for these values to assess their impact on intervention choices.
  • Key finding: The research identifies clear value trade-offs, where the 'optimal' intervention strategies differ based on the specific ethical framework or value prioritization applied.
  • This work provides a structured approach for integrating diverse ethical values directly into the decision criteria used to evaluate outcomes from infectious disease intervention models.
  • The integration facilitates a more comprehensive assessment of both benefits and harms associated with various public health policy options.

Methodology

The study utilizes a proof-of-concept approach, embedding specific public health values (e.g., outcome equity, aggregate benefit) into the evaluation metrics of a simplified computational model. This model simulates vaccination interventions for a hypothetical pathogen akin to SARS-CoV-2. The core method involves systematically exploring a bounded parameter space of different prioritizations of these values to observe how such ethical considerations influence the identification of optimal intervention strategies.

Key Findings

The main discovery is the presence of significant value trade-offs: the identification of an 'optimal' public health intervention strategy is highly dependent on the particular ethical framework or the relative prioritization of values chosen (e.g., prioritizing equitable outcomes versus maximizing aggregate population benefit). This demonstrates that ethical considerations can fundamentally alter the recommended policy, underscoring the need for their explicit inclusion.

Clinical Impact

This work holds the potential to significantly improve the ethical quality and societal acceptance of public health policies. It provides a structured framework for public health officials and policymakers to explicitly analyze and articulate the ethical implications of different intervention strategies, fostering transparency and facilitating more equitable resource allocation and intervention design during outbreaks and beyond. This could lead to better-informed and more defensible public health decisions.

Limitations

The authors explicitly state that this work represents a "proof-of-concept approach" and utilizes a "simple computational model." Furthermore, it examines a "bounded space of alternative prioritisations of values," suggesting a limited scope in terms of model complexity, the range of ethical values considered, and the breadth of value prioritization exploration in this initial demonstration.

Future Directions

While not explicitly detailed, the paper's contribution as a "proof-of-concept" inherently suggests future work involving the expansion to more complex epidemiological models, incorporation of a wider array of public health values and ethical considerations, and application to real-world infectious disease scenarios to validate and refine the proposed methodology.

Abstract

Decisions on public health interventions to control infectious disease are often informed by computational models. Interpreting the predicted outcomes of a public health decision requires not only high-quality modelling, but also an ethical framework for assessing the benefits and harms associated with different options. The design and specification of ethical frameworks matured independently of computational modelling, so many values recognised as important for ethical decision-making are missing from computational models. We demonstrate a proof-of-concept approach to incorporate multiple public health values into the evaluation of a simple computational model for vaccination against a pathogen such as SARS-CoV-2. By examining a bounded space of alternative prioritisations of values (outcome equity and aggregate benefit) we identify value trade-offs, where the outcomes of optimal strategies differ depending on the ethical framework. This work demonstrates an approach to incorporating diverse values into decision criteria used to evaluate outcomes of models of infectious disease interventions.
