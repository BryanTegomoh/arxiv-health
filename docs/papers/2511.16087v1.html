AssayMatch: Learning to Select Data for Molecular Activity Models

arXiv ID: 2511.16087v1
Published: 2025-11-20
Authors: Vincent Fan, Regina Barzilay
Categories: cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

AssayMatch is a novel framework that enhances the performance of machine learning models in drug discovery by intelligently selecting training data. It addresses the issue of noise and variability in aggregated bioactivity datasets by curating smaller, more homogenous training sets specifically attuned to a test set, even when test molecule labels are unknown. This is achieved by leveraging data attribution to finetune language embeddings of assay descriptions, capturing compatibility beyond mere semantic similarity.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to drug discovery and development as it directly tackles a fundamental challenge in leveraging large-scale, heterogeneous bioactivity data for predictive modeling. By enabling the curation of higher-quality, more relevant training datasets, AssayMatch can significantly accelerate the identification and optimization of novel drug candidates.

AI Health Application

AssayMatch is an AI framework that leverages machine learning and natural language processing (through language embeddings) to improve the quality and relevance of training data for molecular activity models. Its application is to enhance the accuracy and efficiency of predictive models used in drug discovery, helping to identify potential drug candidates more effectively and filter out noisy or incompatible experimental data from public bioactivity databases (like ChEMBL).

Key Points

  • **Problem Addressed**: Machine learning models in drug discovery often suffer from performance degradation due to noise and heterogeneity in aggregated bioactivity data from diverse sources like ChEMBL.
  • **AssayMatch Framework**: Introduces a data selection framework designed to construct smaller, more homogenous training datasets specifically tailored to a target test set.
  • **Data Attribution for Compatibility**: Employs data attribution methods to quantitatively assess the contribution and compatibility of each training assay with respect to model performance.
  • **Finetuned Language Embeddings**: These attribution scores are crucial for finetuning language embeddings of text-based assay descriptions, enabling them to capture the functional compatibility between assays, rather than just semantic similarity.
  • **Real-world Applicability**: A key feature is its ability to perform data selection for test sets with unknown labels, mirroring typical real-world drug discovery campaigns where activities of candidate molecules are not predetermined.
  • **Performance Superiority**: Models trained on data curated by AssayMatch consistently surpass the performance of models trained on the complete, uncurated dataset across two common machine learning architectures.
  • **Noise Reduction and Data Efficiency**: Demonstrates significant capability in filtering out harmful or noisy experimental data, leading to improved predictive power and enhanced data efficiency for molecular activity models, with increased prediction capability over a strong language-only baseline for 9/12 model-target pairs.

Methodology

AssayMatch's methodology involves quantifying the contribution of individual training assays to model performance using data attribution methods. These attribution scores are then used to finetune pre-trained language embeddings of text-based assay descriptions. This finetuning process teaches the embeddings to capture assay compatibility rather than just semantic similarity. At test time, these finetuned embeddings are utilized to rank and select the most relevant and compatible training data for a given test set, critically, without requiring knowledge of the test set labels.

Key Findings

The main findings indicate that machine learning models trained on data selected by AssayMatch achieve superior predictive performance compared to models trained on the complete, uncurated dataset. Specifically, AssayMatch improved prediction capability for 9 out of 12 model-target pairs over a strong language-only baseline, demonstrating its effectiveness in filtering noisy experiments and enhancing data quality and efficiency across different ML architectures.

Clinical Impact

AssayMatch has the potential to profoundly impact the early stages of drug discovery by enabling the development of more accurate and robust predictive models for molecular activity. This can lead to more efficient identification of promising drug candidates, reduced experimental costs, and a faster progression through hit-to-lead and lead optimization phases, ultimately accelerating the development of new therapeutic agents.

Abstract

The performance of machine learning models in drug discovery is highly dependent on the quality and consistency of the underlying training data. Due to limitations in dataset sizes, many models are trained by aggregating bioactivity data from diverse sources, including public databases such as ChEMBL. However, this approach often introduces significant noise due to variability in experimental protocols. We introduce AssayMatch, a framework for data selection that builds smaller, more homogenous training sets attuned to the test set of interest. AssayMatch leverages data attribution methods to quantify the contribution of each training assay to model performance. These attribution scores are used to finetune language embeddings of text-based assay descriptions to capture not just semantic similarity, but also the compatibility between assays. Unlike existing data attribution methods, our approach enables data selection for a test set with unknown labels, mirroring real-world drug discovery campaigns where the activities of candidate molecules are not known in advance. At test time, embeddings finetuned with AssayMatch are used to rank all available training data. We demonstrate that models trained on data selected by AssayMatch are able to surpass the performance of the model trained on the complete dataset, highlighting its ability to effectively filter out harmful or noisy experiments. We perform experiments on two common machine learning architectures and see increased prediction capability over a strong language-only baseline for 9/12 model-target pairs. AssayMatch provides a data-driven mechanism to curate higher-quality datasets, reducing noise from incompatible experiments and improving the predictive power and data efficiency of models for drug discovery. AssayMatch is available at https://github.com/Ozymandias314/AssayMatch.
