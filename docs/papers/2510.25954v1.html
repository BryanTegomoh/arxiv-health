Application and Validation of Geospatial Foundation Model Data for the Prediction of Health Facility Programmatic Outputs -- A Case Study in Malawi

Summary

This study evaluates the utility of Geospatial Foundation Model (GeoFM) embeddings, derived from sources like Google Population Dynamics and mobile Call Detail Records, for predicting 15 routine health programmatic outputs in Malawi. The research demonstrates that GeoFM-based XGBoost models, particularly a Multi-GeoFM approach, significantly improve predictive performance over traditional geostatistical methods for most indicators, offering a valuable tool to enhance constrained health information systems in Low and Middle-Income Countries (LMICs).

Medical Relevance

This research is critical for improving public health surveillance, resource allocation, and targeted interventions in LMICs where routine health data are often unreliable and incomplete. By providing more accurate and timely estimates of key health indicators, it directly informs policy decisions and operational planning to address pressing health challenges like HIV, child health, and population dynamics.

AI Health Application

The paper applies Geospatial Foundation Models (GeoFMs), including Google Population Dynamics Foundation Model (PDFM), Google AlphaEarth (satellite imagery), and mobile phone call detail records (CDR), along with XGBoost models, to synthesize diverse spatial, temporal, and behavioral data. This AI-driven approach is used to predict 15 routine health programmatic outputs, such as new HIV cases and child vaccinations, aiming to improve the reliability and coverage of health data for decision-making and strengthening health information systems in LMICs. This represents an application of predictive analytics and advanced machine learning for public health and healthcare resource planning.

Key Points

  • Addresses the challenge of unreliable and incomplete routine health data in Low and Middle-Income Countries (LMICs) by exploring novel geospatial data sources.
  • Evaluates three Geospatial Foundation Model (GeoFM) embedding sources: Google Population Dynamics Foundation Model (PDFM), Google AlphaEarth (satellite imagery-derived), and mobile phone Call Detail Records (CDR), alongside a combined 'Multi-GeoFM' model.
  • Utilized XGBoost models to predict 15 routine health programmatic outputs across 552 health catchment areas in Malawi, based on data from January 2021 to May 2023.

Methodology

The study employed XGBoost models to predict 15 health programmatic outputs, leveraging three Geospatial Foundation Model (GeoFM) embedding sources: Google Population Dynamics Foundation Model (PDFM), Google AlphaEarth (derived from satellite imagery), and mobile phone Call Detail Records (CDR). Data from 552 health catchment areas in Malawi, spanning January 2021 to May 2023, were utilized. Model performance was assessed using the R2 metric, with an 80/20 training/test data split and 5-fold cross-validation used during training. Performance was compared against traditional geospatial interpolation methods as a baseline.

Key Findings

Embedding-based GeoFM approaches significantly improved predictive performance over traditional geostatistical methods for 13 of 15 (87%) indicators. The Multi-GeoFM model, which integrated all three embedding sources, yielded the most robust predictions, with average 5-fold cross-validated R2 values of 0.63 (test R2 0.64) for population density, 0.57 (test R2 0.68) for new HIV cases, and 0.47 (test R2 0.55) for child vaccinations. Conversely, prediction was poor for targets with low primary data availability, specifically noted for TB and malnutrition cases.

Clinical Impact

The enhanced predictive capacity for health outcomes and population dynamics can lead to more efficient and equitable allocation of limited health resources, facilitating better planning of vaccination campaigns, more targeted HIV prevention and treatment programs, and improved public health surveillance in LMICs. This contributes to more effective public health interventions, stronger responses to disease outbreaks, and ultimately, better health outcomes for vulnerable populations.

Limitations

The predictive performance of the models was mixed across indicators. Notably, prediction was poor for health targets characterized by low primary data availability, such as tuberculosis (TB) and malnutrition cases. This suggests that the utility of GeoFM embeddings can be constrained by the inherent data scarcity for certain specific health indicators.

Future Directions

The study concludes that integrating multiple GeoFM sources is an efficient and valuable tool for supplementing and strengthening constrained routine health information systems. This implicitly suggests future research could focus on further validation, expansion of these methodologies to a wider range of health indicators and other LMIC contexts, and potentially their integration into operational health data systems to routinely inform decision-making.

Abstract

The reliability of routine health data in low and middle-income countries (LMICs) is often constrained by reporting delays and incomplete coverage, necessitating the exploration of novel data sources and analytics. Geospatial Foundation Models (GeoFMs) offer a promising avenue by synthesizing diverse spatial, temporal, and behavioral data into mathematical embeddings that can be efficiently used for downstream prediction tasks. This study evaluated the predictive performance of three GeoFM embedding sources - Google Population Dynamics Foundation Model (PDFM), Google AlphaEarth (derived from satellite imagery), and mobile phone call detail records (CDR) - for modeling 15 routine health programmatic outputs in Malawi, and compared their utility to traditional geospatial interpolation methods. We used XGBoost models on data from 552 health catchment areas (January 2021-May 2023), assessing performance with R2, and using an 80/20 training and test data split with 5-fold cross-validation used in training. While predictive performance was mixed, the embedding-based approaches improved upon baseline geostatistical methods in 13 of 15 (87%) indicators tested. A Multi-GeoFM model integrating all three embedding sources produced the most robust predictions, achieving average 5-fold cross validated R2 values for indicators like population density (0.63), new HIV cases (0.57), and child vaccinations (0.47) and test set R2 of 0.64, 0.68, and 0.55, respectively. Prediction was poor for prediction targets with low primary data availability, such as TB and malnutrition cases. These results demonstrate that GeoFM embeddings imbue a modest predictive improvement for select health and demographic outcomes in an LMIC context. We conclude that the integration of multiple GeoFM sources is an efficient and valuable tool for supplementing and strengthening constrained routine health information systems.
