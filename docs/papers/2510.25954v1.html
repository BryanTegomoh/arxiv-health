Application and Validation of Geospatial Foundation Model Data for the Prediction of Health Facility Programmatic Outputs -- A Case Study in Malawi

Summary

This study validated Geospatial Foundation Models (GeoFMs) for predicting 15 routine health programmatic outputs in Malawi, addressing the challenges of unreliable health data in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs). The research demonstrated that embedding-based GeoFM approaches, particularly a multi-source integration model, significantly improved prediction accuracy over traditional geostatistical methods for most indicators, offering a novel tool to strengthen constrained health information systems.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a critical solution for supplementing and strengthening health information systems in LMICs, where routine data is often incomplete or delayed. By enabling more accurate and timely predictions of health outcomes, it directly supports public health planning, resource allocation, and program monitoring for various disease control and health promotion initiatives.

AI Health Application

The paper uses AI models (GeoFMs, XGBoost) to leverage diverse geospatial, temporal, and behavioral data to predict key health outcomes and programmatic outputs. This application aims to overcome data scarcity and improve the reliability of health data in LMICs, thereby enhancing public health surveillance, planning, and intervention strategies by providing more robust predictions for indicators like disease incidence (HIV, TB), vaccination rates, and nutritional status.

Key Points

  • The study aimed to overcome limitations in routine health data in LMICs by exploring novel data sources from Geospatial Foundation Models (GeoFMs).
  • Three GeoFM embedding sources were evaluated: Google Population Dynamics Foundation Model (PDFM), Google AlphaEarth (satellite imagery-derived), and mobile phone Call Detail Records (CDR).
  • The predictive performance of these GeoFMs was compared against traditional geospatial interpolation methods for 15 routine health programmatic outputs in 552 health catchment areas in Malawi (Jan 2021-May 2023).
  • XGBoost models were utilized for prediction, with performance assessed using R2, an 80/20 training/test data split, and 5-fold cross-validation during training.
  • GeoFM embedding-based approaches improved predictive performance over baseline geostatistical methods in 13 out of 15 (87%) indicators tested.
  • A Multi-GeoFM model, integrating all three embedding sources, produced the most robust predictions, achieving average 5-fold cross-validated R2 values of 0.63 for population density, 0.57 for new HIV cases, and 0.47 for child vaccinations (with test set R2s of 0.64, 0.68, and 0.55 respectively).
  • Prediction was notably poor for targets with low primary data availability, such as tuberculosis (TB) and malnutrition cases, highlighting a data sparsity limitation.

Methodology

The study employed XGBoost machine learning models to predict 15 routine health programmatic outputs across 552 health catchment areas in Malawi. It evaluated three GeoFM embedding sources (Google PDFM, Google AlphaEarth, CDR) and a combined Multi-GeoFM model, comparing their predictive utility against traditional geospatial interpolation methods. Data spanned January 2021 to May 2023. Model performance was assessed using the R2 metric, utilizing an 80/20 training and test data split with 5-fold cross-validation during the training phase.

Key Findings

GeoFM embedding approaches, particularly the integrated Multi-GeoFM model, significantly enhanced the prediction of routine health indicators compared to traditional geostatistical methods for 87% of the indicators tested. The Multi-GeoFM model achieved strong predictive R2 values for population density (0.63 CV, 0.64 test), new HIV cases (0.57 CV, 0.68 test), and child vaccinations (0.47 CV, 0.55 test). However, predictive performance was poor for indicators characterized by low primary data availability, such as TB and malnutrition cases.

Clinical Impact

The enhanced predictive capabilities offered by GeoFMs can lead to more effective and equitable health service delivery in LMICs. Clinicians and public health practitioners can leverage these insights for better resource allocation (e.g., vaccine distribution, antiretroviral therapy clinics), targeted intervention planning (e.g., HIV prevention campaigns), and more efficient monitoring of programmatic outputs, ultimately improving patient outcomes and public health coverage where traditional data is scarce.

Limitations

A primary limitation noted is the poor predictive performance for health targets with low primary data availability, specifically exemplified by tuberculosis and malnutrition cases. This suggests that even advanced GeoFM embeddings may struggle where the underlying ground truth data is exceedingly sparse.

Future Directions

The abstract concludes that integrating multiple GeoFM sources is an efficient and valuable tool for supplementing and strengthening constrained routine health information systems, implying potential for broader application and integration into existing public health surveillance frameworks. No explicit future research directions were detailed.

Abstract

The reliability of routine health data in low and middle-income countries (LMICs) is often constrained by reporting delays and incomplete coverage, necessitating the exploration of novel data sources and analytics. Geospatial Foundation Models (GeoFMs) offer a promising avenue by synthesizing diverse spatial, temporal, and behavioral data into mathematical embeddings that can be efficiently used for downstream prediction tasks. This study evaluated the predictive performance of three GeoFM embedding sources - Google Population Dynamics Foundation Model (PDFM), Google AlphaEarth (derived from satellite imagery), and mobile phone call detail records (CDR) - for modeling 15 routine health programmatic outputs in Malawi, and compared their utility to traditional geospatial interpolation methods. We used XGBoost models on data from 552 health catchment areas (January 2021-May 2023), assessing performance with R2, and using an 80/20 training and test data split with 5-fold cross-validation used in training. While predictive performance was mixed, the embedding-based approaches improved upon baseline geostatistical methods in 13 of 15 (87%) indicators tested. A Multi-GeoFM model integrating all three embedding sources produced the most robust predictions, achieving average 5-fold cross validated R2 values for indicators like population density (0.63), new HIV cases (0.57), and child vaccinations (0.47) and test set R2 of 0.64, 0.68, and 0.55, respectively. Prediction was poor for prediction targets with low primary data availability, such as TB and malnutrition cases. These results demonstrate that GeoFM embeddings imbue a modest predictive improvement for select health and demographic outcomes in an LMIC context. We conclude that the integration of multiple GeoFM sources is an efficient and valuable tool for supplementing and strengthening constrained routine health information systems.
