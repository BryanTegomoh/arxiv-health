Application and Validation of Geospatial Foundation Model Data for the Prediction of Health Facility Programmatic Outputs -- A Case Study in Malawi

Summary

This study demonstrates the utility of Geospatial Foundation Models (GeoFMs) in improving the prediction of routine health programmatic outputs in low- and middle-income countries (LMICs) where traditional data are often constrained. By synthesizing diverse spatial, temporal, and behavioral data into mathematical embeddings, GeoFMs, particularly a Multi-GeoFM approach, enhanced predictive performance for key health indicators compared to baseline geostatistical methods. The research concludes that integrating multiple GeoFM sources can efficiently supplement and strengthen routine health information systems in resource-limited settings.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for public health in LMICs by offering a solution to unreliable and incomplete routine health data, which hinders effective health planning and resource allocation. Improved data predictions can lead to more targeted interventions, better monitoring of disease burden, and enhanced program evaluation in areas most in need.

AI Health Application

This paper applies Geospatial Foundation Models (GeoFMs) and XGBoost, which are AI/machine learning techniques, to predict critical health programmatic outputs and demographic indicators (e.g., new HIV cases, child vaccinations, population density) from diverse spatial, temporal, and behavioral data. The application aims to enhance the reliability and coverage of health information systems in LMICs, thereby improving data-driven decision-making for public health interventions and healthcare resource allocation.

Key Points

  • Routine health data in LMICs often suffer from reporting delays and incomplete coverage, necessitating novel data sources.
  • Geospatial Foundation Models (GeoFMs) utilize diverse spatial, temporal, and behavioral data to create mathematical embeddings for downstream prediction tasks.
  • The study evaluated three GeoFM embedding sources: Google Population Dynamics Foundation Model (PDFM), Google AlphaEarth (satellite imagery), and mobile phone Call Detail Records (CDR) for predicting 15 health programmatic outputs in Malawi.
  • XGBoost models were employed on data from 552 health catchment areas (Jan 2021-May 2023), with performance assessed using R2, an 80/20 train/test split, and 5-fold cross-validation.
  • GeoFM embedding-based approaches significantly improved predictions over traditional geostatistical methods in 13 out of 15 (87%) indicators tested.
  • A Multi-GeoFM model, integrating all three embedding sources, produced the most robust predictions, achieving strong R2 values for indicators like population density (cross-validated R2 0.63, test R2 0.64), new HIV cases (cross-validated R2 0.57, test R2 0.68), and child vaccinations (cross-validated R2 0.47, test R2 0.55).
  • Prediction performance was poor for targets with low primary data availability, such as Tuberculosis (TB) and malnutrition cases, indicating a threshold for data utility.
  • The study advocates for the integration of multiple GeoFM sources as an efficient and valuable tool for augmenting constrained routine health information systems.

Methodology

The study utilized XGBoost models to predict 15 routine health programmatic outputs across 552 health catchment areas in Malawi, using data from January 2021 to May 2023. Three GeoFM embedding sources‚ÄîGoogle Population Dynamics Foundation Model (PDFM), Google AlphaEarth (derived from satellite imagery), and mobile phone Call Detail Records (CDR)‚Äîwere evaluated individually and in a combined 'Multi-GeoFM' model. Performance was measured by R2, using an 80/20 training/test data split with 5-fold cross-validation during the training phase. Results were compared against traditional geospatial interpolation methods.

Key Findings

GeoFM embedding-based approaches surpassed baseline geostatistical methods for 13 of 15 (87%) indicators. The integrated Multi-GeoFM model demonstrated the most robust predictive power, achieving average 5-fold cross-validated R2 values of 0.63 (test R2 0.64) for population density, 0.57 (test R2 0.68) for new HIV cases, and 0.47 (test R2 0.55) for child vaccinations. However, prediction accuracy was notably poor for indicators with low primary data availability, such as TB and malnutrition cases.

Clinical Impact

This approach offers a practical and efficient method to enhance data-driven decision-making in LMICs, critical for public health initiatives. By providing more reliable and timely estimates of health indicators (e.g., HIV prevalence, vaccination coverage), health programs can be better planned, resources allocated more effectively, and interventions targeted to specific populations, potentially leading to improved health outcomes and more resilient health systems.

Limitations

The predictive performance was mixed, with improvements being described as 'modest' for select outcomes. A significant limitation was the poor prediction for targets with low primary data availability (e.g., TB and malnutrition cases), indicating that GeoFMs are most effective when a foundational level of primary data exists. The study was also confined to a single country (Malawi).

Future Directions

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract, but the findings suggest further validation and application across different LMICs and for a wider range of health indicators to strengthen global health information systems. Exploring methods to improve predictions for indicators with very low primary data availability would also be a crucial next step.

Abstract

The reliability of routine health data in low and middle-income countries (LMICs) is often constrained by reporting delays and incomplete coverage, necessitating the exploration of novel data sources and analytics. Geospatial Foundation Models (GeoFMs) offer a promising avenue by synthesizing diverse spatial, temporal, and behavioral data into mathematical embeddings that can be efficiently used for downstream prediction tasks. This study evaluated the predictive performance of three GeoFM embedding sources - Google Population Dynamics Foundation Model (PDFM), Google AlphaEarth (derived from satellite imagery), and mobile phone call detail records (CDR) - for modeling 15 routine health programmatic outputs in Malawi, and compared their utility to traditional geospatial interpolation methods. We used XGBoost models on data from 552 health catchment areas (January 2021-May 2023), assessing performance with R2, and using an 80/20 training and test data split with 5-fold cross-validation used in training. While predictive performance was mixed, the embedding-based approaches improved upon baseline geostatistical methods in 13 of 15 (87%) indicators tested. A Multi-GeoFM model integrating all three embedding sources produced the most robust predictions, achieving average 5-fold cross validated R2 values for indicators like population density (0.63), new HIV cases (0.57), and child vaccinations (0.47) and test set R2 of 0.64, 0.68, and 0.55, respectively. Prediction was poor for prediction targets with low primary data availability, such as TB and malnutrition cases. These results demonstrate that GeoFM embeddings imbue a modest predictive improvement for select health and demographic outcomes in an LMIC context. We conclude that the integration of multiple GeoFM sources is an efficient and valuable tool for supplementing and strengthening constrained routine health information systems.
