DispatchMAS: Fusing taxonomy and artificial intelligence agents for emergency medical services

Summary

This paper introduces DispatchMAS, a novel taxonomy-grounded, Large Language Model (LLM)-powered multi-agent system (MAS) designed to simulate realistic Emergency Medical Dispatch (EMD) scenarios. The system, comprising Caller and Dispatcher Agents, was rigorously evaluated by physicians and through automated linguistic analysis, demonstrating high clinical plausibility, effectiveness in dispatch, and efficacy in providing guidance. This research offers a robust framework for improving EMD processes through AI-driven simulation and potential future real-time decision support.

Medical Relevance

This system directly addresses critical issues in emergency medical services by enhancing the quality and consistency of emergency medical dispatch. It offers a safe, scalable environment for training dispatchers and evaluating protocols, ultimately aiming to improve patient outcomes by ensuring timely and appropriate emergency responses.

AI Health Application

Developing an AI-powered multi-agent system (LLM-based) to simulate emergency medical dispatch (EMD) scenarios for training emergency dispatchers, evaluating call protocols, and serving as a foundation for real-time decision support to augment human dispatchers in high-stakes medical emergencies.

Key Points

  • The study addresses challenges in Emergency Medical Dispatch (EMD) such as caller distress, ambiguity, and dispatcher cognitive load, proposing AI augmentation.
  • A comprehensive clinical taxonomy was developed, encompassing 32 chief complaints and 6 caller identities derived from MIMIC-III, alongside a six-phase call protocol.
  • DispatchMAS utilizes an AutoGen-based Multi-Agent System (MAS) with distinct Caller and Dispatcher Agents, grounding interactions in a 'fact commons' to ensure clinical plausibility and mitigate misinformation.
  • Evaluation involved a hybrid approach: four physicians assessed 100 simulated cases for 'Guidance Efficacy' and 'Dispatch Effectiveness,' complemented by automated linguistic analysis (sentiment, readability, politeness).
  • Physician evaluation showed substantial inter-rater agreement (Gwe's AC1 > 0.70) and confirmed excellent Dispatch Effectiveness (94% correct agent contact) and Guidance Efficacy (91% advice provision).
  • Algorithmic metrics indicated a neutral affective profile (73.7% neutral sentiment), high readability (Flesch 80.9), and consistent politeness (60.0% polite).
  • The system's high fidelity in simulating diverse, clinically plausible dispatch scenarios positions it as a valuable tool for dispatcher training, protocol evaluation, and a foundation for real-time decision support.

Methodology

The methodology involved constructing a clinical taxonomy (32 chief complaints, 6 caller identities from MIMIC-III) and a six-phase call protocol. An AutoGen-based Multi-Agent System (MAS) was developed, featuring Caller and Dispatcher Agents, with interactions grounded in a 'fact commons' for clinical plausibility. Evaluation was hybrid: human physicians (n=4) assessed 100 simulated cases for "Guidance Efficacy" and "Dispatch Effectiveness" (with Gwe's AC1 for inter-rater agreement), and automated linguistic analysis (sentiment, readability, politeness) was performed.

Key Findings

Human evaluation showed substantial inter-rater agreement (Gwe's AC1 > 0.70). The system achieved excellent Dispatch Effectiveness (e.g., 94% contacting the correct potential other agents) and high Guidance Efficacy (advice provided in 91% of cases), both rated highly by physicians. Algorithmic metrics corroborated these findings, indicating a predominantly neutral affective profile (73.7% neutral sentiment; 90.4% neutral emotion), high readability (Flesch 80.9), and a consistently polite communication style (60.0% polite; 0% impolite).

Clinical Impact

The DispatchMAS system has significant potential clinical impact by providing a high-fidelity simulation environment for emergency medical dispatcher training, allowing them to practice handling diverse, complex scenarios safely. It can also be used for rigorous evaluation and refinement of EMD protocols. Furthermore, it lays the groundwork for developing advanced AI agents that could offer real-time decision support to dispatchers, thereby potentially reducing errors, improving response times, and optimizing resource allocation in emergency situations.

Limitations

The study's primary limitation, inherent in its design, is that the system operates within a simulated environment. While designed for high clinical plausibility and evaluated by physicians, its real-world performance or direct clinical impact in live EMD operations has not yet been assessed.

Future Directions

The authors suggest that this work outlines a pathway for safely integrating advanced AI agents into real-time emergency response workflows. Future directions include developing the system further to serve as a foundation for real-time decision support for human dispatchers and exploring its application in diverse, complex EMD scenarios beyond the current scope.

Abstract

Objective: Emergency medical dispatch (EMD) is a high-stakes process challenged by caller distress, ambiguity, and cognitive load. Large Language Models (LLMs) and Multi-Agent Systems (MAS) offer opportunities to augment dispatchers. This study aimed to develop and evaluate a taxonomy-grounded, LLM-powered multi-agent system for simulating realistic EMD scenarios. Methods: We constructed a clinical taxonomy (32 chief complaints, 6 caller identities from MIMIC-III) and a six-phase call protocol. Using this framework, we developed an AutoGen-based MAS with Caller and Dispatcher Agents. The system grounds interactions in a fact commons to ensure clinical plausibility and mitigate misinformation. We used a hybrid evaluation framework: four physicians assessed 100 simulated cases for "Guidance Efficacy" and "Dispatch Effectiveness," supplemented by automated linguistic analysis (sentiment, readability, politeness). Results: Human evaluation, with substantial inter-rater agreement (Gwe's AC1 > 0.70), confirmed the system's high performance. It demonstrated excellent Dispatch Effectiveness (e.g., 94 % contacting the correct potential other agents) and Guidance Efficacy (advice provided in 91 % of cases), both rated highly by physicians. Algorithmic metrics corroborated these findings, indicating a predominantly neutral affective profile (73.7 % neutral sentiment; 90.4 % neutral emotion), high readability (Flesch 80.9), and a consistently polite style (60.0 % polite; 0 % impolite). Conclusion: Our taxonomy-grounded MAS simulates diverse, clinically plausible dispatch scenarios with high fidelity. Findings support its use for dispatcher training, protocol evaluation, and as a foundation for real-time decision support. This work outlines a pathway for safely integrating advanced AI agents into emergency response workflows.
