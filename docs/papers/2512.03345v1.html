HalluGen: Synthesizing Realistic and Controllable Hallucinations for Evaluating Image Restoration

arXiv ID: 2512.03345v1
Published: 2025-12-03
Authors: Seunghoi Kim, Henry F. J. Tregidgo, Chen Jin, Matteo Figini, Daniel C. Alexander
Categories: cs.CV, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces HalluGen, a diffusion-based framework designed to synthesize realistic and controllable hallucinations in medical images, specifically for low-field MRI enhancement. It addresses the critical challenge of evaluating generative model hallucinations in safety-critical domains by creating the first large-scale, annotated hallucination dataset and demonstrating its utility in benchmarking image quality metrics and training robust hallucination detectors.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medical imaging, especially in resource-limited settings utilizing low-field MRI. By enabling the systematic evaluation and detection of AI-generated hallucinations in restored images, it directly addresses a major safety concern that could otherwise lead to critical misdiagnoses and undermine trust in AI-enhanced diagnostic tools.

AI Health Application

The research focuses on enhancing the safety and reliability of AI models used for medical image restoration, particularly in low-field MRI to prevent diagnostic errors caused by AI hallucinations. It introduces a framework (HalluGen) and tools (SHAFE, hallucination detectors) to synthesize, evaluate, and mitigate these errors, which is crucial for the trustworthy deployment of AI in medical diagnostics and healthcare settings, especially where access to high-quality imaging might be limited.

Key Points

  • Generative models used in image restoration for safety-critical domains (e.g., low-field MRI) are prone to 'hallucinations' ‚Äì plausible but incorrect structures ‚Äì which can lead to serious diagnostic errors.
  • Traditional evaluation of hallucinations is hindered by a circular dependency, requiring costly and subjective labeled data.
  • HalluGen is a novel diffusion-based framework that synthesizes realistic hallucinations with controllable type, location, and severity, resulting in perceptually realistic but semantically incorrect outputs (segmentation IoU drops from 0.86 to 0.36).
  • The framework was used to construct the first large-scale hallucination dataset, comprising 4,350 annotated images derived from 1,450 brain MR images, enabling systematic evaluation.
  • HalluGen facilitates benchmarking image quality metrics and the development of SHAFE (Semantic Hallucination Assessment via Feature Evaluation), a new feature-based metric with improved hallucination sensitivity over traditional metrics.
  • The synthetic data allows for training reference-free hallucination detectors that effectively generalize to real-world restoration failures, enhancing their practical applicability.
  • HalluGen and its open dataset establish a scalable foundation for robust evaluation and mitigation of hallucinations in safety-critical image restoration, particularly relevant for medical imaging.

Methodology

The core methodology involves HalluGen, a diffusion-based generative framework, used to synthesize controllable and realistic hallucinations. This framework was then leveraged to create a large-scale dataset of 4,350 annotated brain MRI images with induced hallucinations. The utility was demonstrated through two main applications: (1) benchmarking existing image quality metrics and developing a novel feature-based metric called SHAFE with soft-attention pooling, and (2) training reference-free hallucination detectors that generalize to real restoration failures.

Key Findings

HalluGen successfully synthesizes perceptually realistic but semantically incorrect hallucinations in brain MR images, significantly degrading segmentation IoU from 0.86 to 0.36. The generated dataset is the first large-scale resource for systematic hallucination evaluation. The developed SHAFE metric demonstrated improved sensitivity for detecting hallucinations. Crucially, hallucination detectors trained on HalluGen's synthetic data proved capable of generalizing to and identifying real restoration failures.

Clinical Impact

This work has significant potential clinical impact by improving the safety and reliability of AI models used for medical image restoration. It provides tools to better evaluate and detect potentially misleading AI-generated artifacts, thereby reducing the risk of diagnostic errors. This is particularly beneficial for low-field MRI, which is vital in resource-constrained environments, ensuring that AI enhancements do not inadvertently introduce harmful misinformation into clinical assessments.

Limitations

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract. However, potential inherent limitations of synthetic data could include generalizability to all possible types of real-world hallucinations or imaging modalities beyond brain MRI.

Future Directions

The paper states that HalluGen and its dataset establish the first scalable foundation for evaluating hallucinations. This implies future directions could involve expanding the application of HalluGen to other safety-critical imaging domains, developing more advanced hallucination detection and mitigation strategies using the rich dataset, and potentially exploring how to integrate hallucination awareness directly into restoration model training.

Abstract

Generative models are prone to hallucinations: plausible but incorrect structures absent in the ground truth. This issue is problematic in image restoration for safety-critical domains such as medical imaging, industrial inspection, and remote sensing, where such errors undermine reliability and trust. For example, in low-field MRI, widely used in resource-limited settings, restoration models are essential for enhancing low-quality scans, yet hallucinations can lead to serious diagnostic errors. Progress has been hindered by a circular dependency: evaluating hallucinations requires labeled data, yet such labels are costly and subjective. We introduce HalluGen, a diffusion-based framework that synthesizes realistic hallucinations with controllable type, location, and severity, producing perceptually realistic but semantically incorrect outputs (segmentation IoU drops from 0.86 to 0.36). Using HalluGen, we construct the first large-scale hallucination dataset comprising 4,350 annotated images derived from 1,450 brain MR images for low-field enhancement, enabling systematic evaluation of hallucination detection and mitigation. We demonstrate its utility in two applications: (1) benchmarking image quality metrics and developing Semantic Hallucination Assessment via Feature Evaluation (SHAFE), a feature-based metric with soft-attention pooling that improves hallucination sensitivity over traditional metrics; and (2) training reference-free hallucination detectors that generalize to real restoration failures. Together, HalluGen and its open dataset establish the first scalable foundation for evaluating hallucinations in safety-critical image restoration.
