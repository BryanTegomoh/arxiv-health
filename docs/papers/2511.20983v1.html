Privacy-Preserving Federated Vision Transformer Learning Leveraging Lightweight Homomorphic Encryption in Medical AI

arXiv ID: 2511.20983v1
Published: 2025-11-26
Authors: Al Amin, Kamrul Hasan, Liang Hong, Sharif Ullah
Categories: cs.CV, cs.CR

Summary

This paper introduces a privacy-preserving federated learning framework that combines Vision Transformers (ViT) with lightweight homomorphic encryption (HE) for secure medical AI. It addresses the vulnerability of conventional federated learning to gradient reconstruction attacks by encrypting compact ViT CLS tokens, significantly reducing communication overhead while maintaining high diagnostic accuracy for multi-institutional histopathology classification.

Medical Relevance

This framework is highly relevant for medical AI as it enables secure collaboration across healthcare institutions, facilitating the training of more robust and accurate diagnostic models on diverse, larger datasets without violating patient privacy regulations like HIPAA. This can lead to improved diagnostic accuracy and personalized medicine.

AI Health Application

The AI application is a privacy-preserving federated learning framework that uses Vision Transformers (ViT) and homomorphic encryption (HE) to securely classify medical images (specifically histopathology slides for lung cancer) across multiple healthcare institutions. This enables collaborative AI model training for improved diagnostic accuracy without directly sharing sensitive patient data, thus addressing a critical privacy concern in medical AI.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical challenge of privacy-preserving collaborative machine learning in healthcare, bypassing HIPAA restrictions and mitigating gradient vulnerabilities in conventional federated learning (FL).
  • Proposes a novel methodology leveraging Vision Transformers (ViT) by encrypting only the compact 768-dimensional CLS token using CKKS homomorphic encryption for secure aggregation, rather than full model gradients.
  • Demonstrates a significant 30-fold reduction in communication overhead compared to gradient encryption, requiring only 326 KB of encrypted data transmission per aggregation round.
  • Proves that conventional FL gradients are highly susceptible to model inversion attacks, enabling near-perfect image reconstruction (PSNR: 52.26 dB, SSIM: 0.999, NMI: 0.741).
  • The proposed CLS-protected HE approach effectively prevents these model inversion attacks and enables encrypted inference directly on ciphertexts.
  • Achieves robust diagnostic performance in a three-client federated setup for lung cancer histopathology classification, with 96.12% global accuracy in the unencrypted domain and 90.02% in the encrypted domain.
  • Enables secure multi-institutional collaboration for AI model training without direct patient data sharing, adhering to stringent privacy regulations.

Methodology

The framework integrates Federated Learning (FL) with Vision Transformers (ViT) for local client training. Instead of sending raw gradients, clients extract the compact 768-dimensional CLS token from their locally trained ViT models. These CLS tokens are then encrypted using the CKKS homomorphic encryption scheme and transmitted to a central server. The server performs secure aggregation and encrypted inference directly on these ciphertexts. The method's privacy and performance were evaluated on a three-client federated setup for lung cancer histopathology classification, comparing it against the vulnerability of conventional FL gradients to model inversion attacks.

Key Findings

Conventional FL gradients are highly vulnerable to model inversion attacks, allowing near-perfect reconstruction of sensitive input images (PSNR: 52.26 dB, SSIM: 0.999, NMI: 0.741). In contrast, the proposed CLS-protected HE approach effectively prevents such attacks while enabling encrypted inference. This method achieves a 30-fold communication reduction (only 326 KB/round) and maintains high global classification accuracy (96.12% unencrypted, 90.02% encrypted) for lung cancer histopathology.

Clinical Impact

This technology can significantly impact clinical practice by enabling the development of more accurate and robust AI models for medical diagnostics, particularly in fields like histopathology. By facilitating secure, multi-institutional collaboration, it allows for training on larger, more diverse patient datasets, leading to AI tools that can assist pathologists in more precise and efficient disease diagnosis, ultimately improving patient outcomes without compromising sensitive medical data.

Abstract

Collaborative machine learning across healthcare institutions promises improved diagnostic accuracy by leveraging diverse datasets, yet privacy regulations such as HIPAA prohibit direct patient data sharing. While federated learning (FL) enables decentralized training without raw data exchange, recent studies show that model gradients in conventional FL remain vulnerable to reconstruction attacks, potentially exposing sensitive medical information. This paper presents a privacy-preserving federated learning framework combining Vision Transformers (ViT) with homomorphic encryption (HE) for secure multi-institutional histopathology classification. The approach leverages the ViT CLS token as a compact 768-dimensional feature representation for secure aggregation, encrypting these tokens using CKKS homomorphic encryption before transmission to the server. We demonstrate that encrypting CLS tokens achieves a 30-fold communication reduction compared to gradient encryption while maintaining strong privacy guarantees. Through evaluation on a three-client federated setup for lung cancer histopathology classification, we show that gradients are highly susceptible to model inversion attacks (PSNR: 52.26 dB, SSIM: 0.999, NMI: 0.741), enabling near-perfect image reconstruction. In contrast, the proposed CLS-protected HE approach prevents such attacks while enabling encrypted inference directly on ciphertexts, requiring only 326 KB of encrypted data transmission per aggregation round. The framework achieves 96.12 percent global classification accuracy in the unencrypted domain and 90.02 percent in the encrypted domain.
