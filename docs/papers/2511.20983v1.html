Privacy-Preserving Federated Vision Transformer Learning Leveraging Lightweight Homomorphic Encryption in Medical AI

arXiv ID: 2511.20983v1
Published: 2025-11-26
Authors: Al Amin, Kamrul Hasan, Liang Hong, Sharif Ullah
Categories: cs.CV, cs.CR
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a privacy-preserving federated learning framework that integrates Vision Transformers (ViT) with lightweight homomorphic encryption (HE) to enable secure multi-institutional medical image classification for histopathology. By encrypting compact ViT CLS tokens instead of full gradients, the proposed method significantly reduces communication overhead (30-fold) while effectively thwarting gradient-based reconstruction attacks that could expose sensitive patient data. The framework achieves high classification accuracy (96.12% unencrypted, 90.02% encrypted) on lung cancer histopathology data.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing medical AI by enabling healthcare institutions to collaboratively train robust diagnostic models using diverse, sensitive patient data without violating privacy regulations like HIPAA, ultimately leading to improved diagnostic accuracy and more generalizable AI solutions in healthcare.

AI Health Application

A privacy-preserving AI system designed for secure, collaborative diagnostic image analysis across multiple healthcare institutions. Specifically, it enables federated learning for histopathology classification (e.g., lung cancer detection) using Vision Transformers and homomorphic encryption, ensuring patient data confidentiality and compliance with regulations like HIPAA.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical challenge of patient data privacy (HIPAA) in collaborative medical AI, enabling multi-institutional learning without direct data sharing.
  • Proposes a novel federated learning framework combining Vision Transformers (ViT) for feature extraction and CKKS Homomorphic Encryption (HE) for secure aggregation.
  • Utilizes the compact 768-dimensional CLS token from ViT as the secure, privacy-preserving representation for aggregation, encrypted before transmission.
  • Demonstrates a 30-fold communication reduction when encrypting CLS tokens (326 KB per round) compared to encrypting full gradients.
  • Proves the extreme vulnerability of conventional FL gradients to model inversion attacks, enabling near-perfect image reconstruction (PSNR: 52.26 dB, SSIM: 0.999, NMI: 0.741).
  • Confirms the proposed CLS-protected HE approach effectively prevents these reconstruction attacks, ensuring strong privacy guarantees.
  • Achieves high global classification accuracy for lung cancer histopathology: 96.12% in the unencrypted domain and 90.02% in the encrypted domain.

Methodology

The framework integrates federated learning with Vision Transformers (ViT) and CKKS homomorphic encryption. Local clients train ViT models on their private datasets. Instead of sending raw gradients, clients extract the compact 768-dimensional CLS token from their ViT models, encrypt these tokens using CKKS homomorphic encryption, and send the ciphertexts to a central server. The server performs secure aggregation and encrypted inference directly on these ciphertexts, preventing any reconstruction attacks on sensitive input data.

Key Findings

Conventional federated learning gradients are highly susceptible to model inversion attacks, leading to near-perfect reconstruction of sensitive medical images (PSNR: 52.26 dB, SSIM: 0.999, NMI: 0.741). The proposed CLS-protected HE approach successfully prevents these attacks while reducing communication overhead by 30-fold (to 326 KB per round) compared to gradient encryption. The framework achieves 96.12% global classification accuracy in the unencrypted domain and 90.02% in the encrypted domain for lung cancer histopathology.

Clinical Impact

This framework has the potential to accelerate the development and deployment of highly accurate AI models for medical diagnosis by allowing secure collaboration among hospitals, overcoming critical privacy barriers. It could lead to more robust and generalizable diagnostic tools for diseases like cancer from histopathology images, without compromising patient confidentiality, thereby improving diagnostic precision and potentially reducing misdiagnosis rates in clinical settings.

Limitations

While not explicitly stated as a limitation of their own work, homomorphic encryption typically introduces computational overhead, though the abstract emphasizes communication efficiency gains.

Abstract

Collaborative machine learning across healthcare institutions promises improved diagnostic accuracy by leveraging diverse datasets, yet privacy regulations such as HIPAA prohibit direct patient data sharing. While federated learning (FL) enables decentralized training without raw data exchange, recent studies show that model gradients in conventional FL remain vulnerable to reconstruction attacks, potentially exposing sensitive medical information. This paper presents a privacy-preserving federated learning framework combining Vision Transformers (ViT) with homomorphic encryption (HE) for secure multi-institutional histopathology classification. The approach leverages the ViT CLS token as a compact 768-dimensional feature representation for secure aggregation, encrypting these tokens using CKKS homomorphic encryption before transmission to the server. We demonstrate that encrypting CLS tokens achieves a 30-fold communication reduction compared to gradient encryption while maintaining strong privacy guarantees. Through evaluation on a three-client federated setup for lung cancer histopathology classification, we show that gradients are highly susceptible to model inversion attacks (PSNR: 52.26 dB, SSIM: 0.999, NMI: 0.741), enabling near-perfect image reconstruction. In contrast, the proposed CLS-protected HE approach prevents such attacks while enabling encrypted inference directly on ciphertexts, requiring only 326 KB of encrypted data transmission per aggregation round. The framework achieves 96.12 percent global classification accuracy in the unencrypted domain and 90.02 percent in the encrypted domain.
