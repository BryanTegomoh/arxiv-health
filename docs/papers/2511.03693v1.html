Summary

This paper introduces a privacy-preserving federated learning framework for automated colorectal cancer (CRC) histopathological grading, overcoming challenges like inter-observer variability and data privacy constraints. By integrating multi-scale feature learning with a dual-stream deep learning architecture, the system achieves high accuracy, particularly for aggressive Grade III tumors, and outperforms comparable centralized models.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and consistent colorectal cancer grading is a critical prognostic factor, directly influencing treatment decisions and patient outcomes. This research provides a method to develop highly performant AI models using sensitive multi-institutional histopathological data while fully preserving patient privacy, directly addressing a major bottleneck in collaborative medical AI development and improving diagnostic reliability.

AI Health Application

The AI application is a scalable, privacy-preserving federated learning framework that uses a dual-stream ResNetRS50 backbone for multi-scale feature learning. It is designed to automatically grade colorectal cancer from histopathological images, improving diagnostic accuracy and consistency while overcoming data privacy and sharing constraints across multiple hospitals. This enables the development of clinical AI tools for digital pathology.

Methodology

The study proposes a federated learning (FL) framework for distributed training of a CRC histopathological grading model. It leverages a dual-stream ResNetRS50 backbone to enable multi-scale feature learning, simultaneously capturing both fine-grained cellular details and broader tissue architecture. The FL system incorporates FedProx stabilization to counteract client drift caused by heterogeneous data distributions across participating institutions. The framework's performance was rigorously evaluated on the CRC-HGD dataset and benchmarked against a centrally trained model.

Key Findings

The proposed federated multi-scale approach achieved an overall accuracy of 83.5%, notably outperforming a comparable centralized model which scored 81.6%. A critical finding for clinical application is the high recall of 87.5% for identifying aggressive Grade III tumors, a key metric for preventing underdiagnosis of severe cases. Performance was also found to improve with higher magnification levels, reaching 88.0% accuracy at 40x, underscoring the value of multi-scale analysis.

Clinical Impact

This research provides a pathway for deploying more accurate, consistent, and privacy-aware automated histopathological grading of colorectal cancer, directly addressing the limitations of subjective human grading and data privacy. Its high recall for aggressive Grade III tumors can significantly reduce dangerous false negatives, leading to earlier and more appropriate interventions for patients. The federated learning paradigm enables collaborative AI development among hospitals without centralizing sensitive patient data, accelerating the integration of advanced AI into routine digital pathology workflows.

Abstract

Colorectal cancer (CRC) grading is a critical prognostic factor but remains hampered by inter-observer variability and the privacy constraints of multi-institutional data sharing. While deep learning offers a path to automation, centralized training models conflict with data governance regulations and neglect the diagnostic importance of multi-scale analysis. In this work, we propose a scalable, privacy-preserving federated learning (FL) framework for CRC histopathological grading that integrates multi-scale feature learning within a distributed training paradigm. Our approach employs a dual-stream ResNetRS50 backbone to concurrently capture fine-grained nuclear detail and broader tissue-level context. This architecture is integrated into a robust FL system stabilized using FedProx to mitigate client drift across heterogeneous data distributions from multiple hospitals. Extensive evaluation on the CRC-HGD dataset demonstrates that our framework achieves an overall accuracy of 83.5%, outperforming a comparable centralized model (81.6%). Crucially, the system excels in identifying the most aggressive Grade III tumors with a high recall of 87.5%, a key clinical priority to prevent dangerous false negatives. Performance further improves with higher magnification, reaching 88.0% accuracy at 40x. These results validate that our federated multi-scale approach not only preserves patient privacy but also enhances model performance and generalization. The proposed modular pipeline, with built-in preprocessing, checkpointing, and error handling, establishes a foundational step toward deployable, privacy-aware clinical AI for digital pathology.
