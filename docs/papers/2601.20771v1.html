Cross-Country Learning for National Infectious Disease Forecasting Using European Data

Summary

This paper introduces a cross-country learning approach for infectious disease forecasting, training a single machine learning model on multi-national time series data to enhance predictions for a specific country. A case study on COVID-19 forecasting in Cyprus, utilizing European surveillance data, demonstrates that integrating data from other countries consistently improves forecasting performance over models relying solely on national data. This method addresses the common limitation of scarce and less varied single-country historical data, benefiting public health planning.

Medical Relevance

Improved accuracy in infectious disease forecasting is vital for public health, enabling better planning for resource allocation, more timely implementation of control measures, and proactive policy decisions to mitigate disease spread and severity.

AI Health Application

Applying machine learning models to cross-country time series surveillance data to forecast national infectious disease incidence. This enables improved accuracy for public health planning, resource allocation, and timely medical interventions, especially in settings with limited national historical data.

Methodology

The study employed a cross-country learning methodology, training various machine learning models on time series surveillance data from multiple European countries. These models were then used for multi-step forecasting of COVID-19 cases in a target country (Cyprus). The research involved analyzing the influence of different lookback window lengths and the effect of 'data augmentation' achieved by pooling international data.

Key Findings

Incorporating infectious disease surveillance data from other countries consistently and significantly improves multi-step forecasting performance compared to models trained exclusively on national historical data.

Clinical Impact

Enhanced forecasting accuracy can directly inform clinical preparedness, allowing healthcare systems to anticipate surges in patient demand, optimize resource allocation (e.g., hospital beds, ICUs, ventilators, medical staff), and guide vaccine or therapeutic distribution strategies, ultimately leading to more effective patient management and reduced mortality.

Limitations

The empirical focus of the study was specifically on COVID-19 case forecasting in Cyprus using European data, which, while demonstrating the concept, suggests further validation across different infectious diseases and diverse geographical or epidemiological contexts is needed for broader generalization.

Future Directions

The proposed framework and its findings are noted to be broadly applicable, suggesting future research could involve applying this cross-country learning approach to forecasting other infectious diseases and in various geographical settings, especially those facing limitations in the availability of extensive national historical data.

Abstract

Accurate forecasting of infectious disease incidence is critical for public health planning and timely intervention. While most data-driven forecasting approaches rely primarily on historical data from a single country, such data are often limited in length and variability, restricting the performance of machine learning (ML) models. In this work, we investigate a cross-country learning approach for infectious disease forecasting, in which a single model is trained on time series data from multiple countries and evaluated on a country of interest. This setting enables the model to exploit shared epidemic dynamics across countries and to benefit from an enlarged training set. We examine this approach through a case study on COVID-19 case forecasting in Cyprus, using surveillance data from European countries. We evaluate multiple ML models and analyse the impact of the lookback window length and cross-country `data augmentation' on multi-step forecasting performance. Our results show that incorporating data from other countries can lead to consistent improvements over models trained solely on national data. Although the empirical focus is on Cyprus and COVID-19, the proposed framework and findings are applicable to infectious disease forecasting more broadly, particularly in settings with limited national historical data.
