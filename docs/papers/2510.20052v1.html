Summary

This paper introduces a novel Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA) approach designed for dynamic and multi-dimensional efficiency evaluation in large organizational datasets. It proposes two regularized DEA models, SBM and GP-SBM, which integrate both desirable and undesirable outputs and enhance discriminatory power through regularization. The methods were validated for computational efficiency and applied to a case study of Canadian hospitals, demonstrating superior performance in capturing input/output correlations compared to conventional benchmarking.

Methodology

The study introduces two regularized Data Envelopment Analysis (DEA) models: a Slack-Based Measure (SBM) and a linearized Goal Programming SBM (GP-SBM). SBM calculates an aggregate efficiency score first and then disaggregates it across organizational dimensions, whereas GP-SBM first computes dimension-level efficiencies before aggregating them into an overall score. Both models incorporate a regularization parameter to improve discriminatory power and are designed to integrate both desirable and undesirable outputs directly. The methodology was tested for computational efficiency and validity using multiple datasets and applied to real-world hospital data.

Key Findings

The proposed SBM and GP-SBM models demonstrate computational efficiency and validity for large datasets. Crucially, they better capture complex correlations among input/output variables compared to traditional benchmarking methods. In the Ontario hospital case study, these models provided a more accurate and integrated assessment of technical efficiency, clinical efficiency, and patient experience, outperforming conventional approaches that evaluate dimensions in isolation before aggregation.

Abstract

We propose an approach for dynamic efficiency evaluation across multiple organizational dimensions using data envelopment analysis (DEA). The method generates both dimension-specific and aggregate efficiency scores, incorporates desirable and undesirable outputs, and is suitable for large-scale problem settings. Two regularized DEA models are introduced: a slack-based measure (SBM) and a linearized version of a nonlinear goal programming model (GP-SBM). While SBM estimates an aggregate efficiency score and then distributes it across dimensions, GP-SBM first estimates dimension-level efficiencies and then derives an aggregate score. Both models utilize a regularization parameter to enhance discriminatory power while also directly integrating both desirable and undesirable outputs. We demonstrate the computational efficiency and validity of our approach on multiple datasets and apply it to a case study of twelve hospitals in Ontario, Canada, evaluating three theoretically grounded dimensions of organizational effectiveness over a 24-month period from January 2018 to December 2019: technical efficiency, clinical efficiency, and patient experience. Our numerical results show that SBM and GP-SBM better capture correlations among input/output variables and outperform conventional benchmarking methods that separately evaluate dimensions before aggregation.
