Hyperbolic Graph Embeddings Reveal the Host-Pathogen Interactome

Summary

This research introduces ApexPPI, a novel deep learning framework that utilizes hyperbolic graph embeddings and multi-task hyperbolic graph neural networks to map host-pathogen protein interactions. By integrating multimodal biological data, the model achieved significantly higher accuracy than previous methods, identifying thousands of high-confidence interactions, including many involving human GPCRs, which were partially validated by AlphaFold 3 structural modeling.

Medical Relevance

Understanding the host-pathogen interactome is crucial for identifying key molecular targets and mechanisms underlying infectious diseases. This work provides a powerful, AI-driven platform to rapidly map these interactions, accelerating the discovery of novel diagnostics and therapeutics against various pathogens.

AI Health Application

The AI application involves using hyperbolic graph embeddings and multi-task hyperbolic graph neural networks to predict host-pathogen protein interactions. This AI-driven prediction aims to identify potential drug targets and mechanisms of infection, thereby accelerating the discovery of new treatments for infectious diseases.

Key Points

  • It integrates diverse biological data sources, including protein sequences, gene perturbation experimental data, and existing interaction networks.
  • The model employs multi-task hyperbolic graph neural networks for predicting pathogen-host protein interactions (PPIs).
  • Mapping protein features into hyperbolic space resulted in markedly higher prediction accuracy compared to prior methodologies.

Methodology

ApexPPI is a deep learning framework built on multi-task hyperbolic graph neural networks. It represents protein networks in hyperbolic Riemannian space to leverage their hierarchical and scale-free properties. The model integrates multimodal biological data, specifically protein sequences, gene perturbation experiment results, and complementary protein interaction networks, to predict pathogen-host protein interactions. Predicted complexes were subsequently validated for structural plausibility using AlphaFold 3 structural modeling.

Key Findings

The study demonstrated that representing protein networks in hyperbolic space significantly enhanced the accuracy of host-pathogen interaction predictions over existing methods. From a vast search space, the model identified thousands of high-confidence host-pathogen protein interactions, notably highlighting numerous interactions with human G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs). The accuracy of these predictions was further corroborated by AlphaFold 3 structural modeling for dozens of predicted complexes.

Clinical Impact

This comprehensive map of host-pathogen protein interactions provides an invaluable resource for therapeutic development. It can guide the identification of novel drug targets, facilitate the design of host-directed or pathogen-specific interventions, and accelerate the discovery process for new treatments against a wide range of infectious diseases.

The generated comprehensive map of host-pathogen protein interactions serves as a fundamental resource for future research, particularly in discovering and developing new treatments for infectious diseases. Abstract

Infections depend on interactions between pathogen and host proteins, but comprehensively mapping these interactions is challenging and labor intensive. Many biological networks have hierarchical, scale-free structure, so we developed a deep learning framework, ApexPPI, that represents protein networks in hyperbolic Riemannian space to capture these features. Our model integrates multimodal biological data (protein sequences, gene perturbation experiments, and complementary interaction networks) to predict likely interactions between pathogen and host proteins through multi-task hyperbolic graph neural networks. Mapping protein features into hyperbolic space led to much higher accuracy than previous methods in predicting host-pathogen interactions. From tens of millions of possible protein pairs, our model identified thousands of high-confidence interactions, including many involving human G-protein-coupled receptors (GPCRs). We validated dozens of these predicted complexes using AlphaFold 3 structural modeling, supporting the accuracy of our predictions. This comprehensive map of host-pathogen protein interactions provides a resource for discovering new treatments and illustrates how advanced AI can unravel complex biological systems.
