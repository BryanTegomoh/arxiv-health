Automated Extraction of Fluoropyrimidine Treatment and Treatment-Related Toxicities from Clinical Notes Using Natural Language Processing

arXiv ID: 2510.20727v1
Published: 2025-10-23
Authors: Xizhi Wu, Madeline S. Kreider, Philip E. Empey, Chenyu Li, Yanshan Wang
Categories: cs.CL, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This research developed and evaluated various Natural Language Processing (NLP) methods to automatically extract fluoropyrimidine treatment and associated toxicity information from unstructured clinical notes. The study found that Large Language Model (LLM)-based approaches, particularly with error-analysis prompting, significantly outperformed traditional machine learning, deep learning, and rule-based methods, achieving perfect F1 scores for both treatment and toxicity extraction.

Medical Relevance

Automated, accurate extraction of fluoropyrimidine treatment and toxicity data from clinical notes is crucial for enhancing patient safety, enabling real-world evidence generation for oncology research, and improving pharmacovigilance by efficiently identifying and monitoring adverse drug reactions.

AI Health Application

The AI application involves developing and evaluating various NLP models, including deep learning (BERT, ClinicalBERT) and Large Language Models (LLMs), to automatically identify and extract specific medical entities (fluoropyrimidine treatments and related toxicities) from unstructured clinical notes. This automates data abstraction for clinical research, drug safety monitoring, and potentially decision support systems in oncology.

Key Points

  • Fluoropyrimidines, commonly used in colorectal and breast cancers, are associated with toxicities (e.g., hand-foot syndrome, cardiotoxicity) often documented within clinical notes.
  • A gold-standard dataset of 236 clinical notes from 204,165 adult oncology patients was created and expertly annotated for treatment regimens and toxicities.
  • A comprehensive comparison involved rule-based, machine learning (Random Forest, SVM, LR), deep learning (BERT, ClinicalBERT), and LLM-based (zero-shot, error-analysis prompting) NLP approaches.
  • LLM-based methods, specifically error-analysis prompting, achieved optimal F1 scores of 1.000 for both treatment and toxicity extraction.
  • Machine learning models (LR, SVM) ranked second for toxicity extraction (F1=0.937), outperforming deep learning (BERT F1=0.839, ClinicalBERT F1=0.886) and rule-based methods (F1=0.858) for this category.
  • Machine and deep learning models were noted to be limited by small training data, showing reduced generalizability, especially for rare toxicity categories.
  • The study concludes that LLM-based NLP is the most effective approach for this task, holding strong potential for oncology research and pharmacovigilance.

Methodology

The study constructed a gold-standard dataset of 236 manually annotated clinical notes from adult oncology patients. NLP methodologies evaluated included rule-based systems, machine learning (Random Forest, Support Vector Machine, Logistic Regression), deep learning (BERT, ClinicalBERT), and Large Language Model (LLM) approaches (zero-shot and error-analysis prompting). Models were trained and tested using an 80:20 split and evaluated based on precision, recall, and F1 scores for extracting treatment regimens and toxicities.

Key Findings

LLM-based error-analysis prompting achieved the highest performance, with F1 scores of 1.000 for both treatment and toxicity extraction. Zero-shot prompting also performed exceptionally well for treatment (F1=1.000) but slightly lower for toxicities (F1=0.876). Logistic Regression and SVM models were the next best for toxicities (F1=0.937). Deep learning models (BERT F1=0.873 treatment, F1=0.839 toxicities; ClinicalBERT F1=0.873 treatment, F1=0.886 toxicities) and rule-based methods (F1=0.857 treatment, F1=0.858 toxicities) generally underperformed compared to LLMs and leading ML models.

Clinical Impact

The successful application of LLM-based NLP for extracting critical treatment and toxicity data directly from clinical notes can significantly streamline the monitoring of adverse drug reactions, facilitate retrospective clinical research on treatment outcomes, and enhance proactive patient management for fluoropyrimidine-related toxicities, thereby improving patient safety and quality of care in oncology.

Limitations

The generalizability of machine and deep learning approaches was limited, primarily due to the relatively small training dataset, particularly affecting the performance on rare toxicity categories. The study's focus on fluoropyrimidines and specific toxicity types might also limit direct generalizability to other drug classes or adverse events without further validation.

Future Directions

The strong performance of LLM-based NLP suggests its significant potential for broader application in oncology research and pharmacovigilance, implying future work could focus on deploying these models in real-world clinical settings and extending their capabilities to other cancer treatments and adverse event monitoring.

Abstract

Objective: Fluoropyrimidines are widely prescribed for colorectal and breast cancers, but are associated with toxicities such as hand-foot syndrome and cardiotoxicity. Since toxicity documentation is often embedded in clinical notes, we aimed to develop and evaluate natural language processing (NLP) methods to extract treatment and toxicity information. Materials and Methods: We constructed a gold-standard dataset of 236 clinical notes from 204,165 adult oncology patients. Domain experts annotated categories related to treatment regimens and toxicities. We developed rule-based, machine learning-based (Random Forest, Support Vector Machine [SVM], Logistic Regression [LR]), deep learning-based (BERT, ClinicalBERT), and large language models (LLM)-based NLP approaches (zero-shot and error-analysis prompting). Models used an 80:20 train-test split. Results: Sufficient data existed to train and evaluate 5 annotated categories. Error-analysis prompting achieved optimal precision, recall, and F1 scores (F1=1.000) for treatment and toxicities extraction, whereas zero-shot prompting reached F1=1.000 for treatment and F1=0.876 for toxicities extraction.LR and SVM ranked second for toxicities (F1=0.937). Deep learning underperformed, with BERT (F1=0.873 treatment; F1= 0.839 toxicities) and ClinicalBERT (F1=0.873 treatment; F1 = 0.886 toxicities). Rule-based methods served as our baseline with F1 scores of 0.857 in treatment and 0.858 in toxicities. Discussion: LMM-based approaches outperformed all others, followed by machine learning methods. Machine and deep learning approaches were limited by small training data and showed limited generalizability, particularly for rare categories. Conclusion: LLM-based NLP most effectively extracted fluoropyrimidine treatment and toxicity information from clinical notes, and has strong potential to support oncology research and pharmacovigilance.
