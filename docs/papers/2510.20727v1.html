Automated Extraction of Fluoropyrimidine Treatment and Treatment-Related Toxicities from Clinical Notes Using Natural Language Processing

arXiv ID: 2510.20727v1
Published: 2025-10-23
Authors: Xizhi Wu, Madeline S. Kreider, Philip E. Empey, Chenyu Li, Yanshan Wang
Categories: cs.CL, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper developed and evaluated various Natural Language Processing (NLP) methods to automatically extract fluoropyrimidine treatment and associated toxicity information from oncology clinical notes. The study found that Large Language Model (LLM)-based approaches, particularly with error-analysis prompting, achieved superior performance (F1=1.000) compared to traditional machine learning, deep learning, and rule-based methods. This highlights the potential of LLMs to support oncology research and pharmacovigilance by efficiently extracting crucial clinical data.

Medical Relevance

Automated, accurate extraction of fluoropyrimidine treatment and toxicity data from clinical notes is crucial for enhancing patient safety, enabling timely pharmacovigilance, and accelerating large-scale oncology research into adverse drug events and treatment outcomes.

AI Health Application

Develops and evaluates AI/NLP models (including LLMs) to automatically extract specific cancer treatment details (fluoropyrimidines) and their associated toxicities from unstructured clinical notes, with the aim of supporting oncology research and pharmacovigilance (drug safety monitoring and risk management).

Key Points

  • Fluoropyrimidine treatments are widely prescribed but associated with significant toxicities (e.g., hand-foot syndrome, cardiotoxicity) often embedded in unstructured clinical notes.
  • A gold-standard dataset of 236 clinical notes from 204,165 adult oncology patients was constructed and annotated by domain experts for 5 categories related to treatment regimens and toxicities.
  • The study evaluated a range of NLP approaches: rule-based, machine learning (Random Forest, SVM, Logistic Regression), deep learning (BERT, ClinicalBERT), and LLMs (zero-shot and error-analysis prompting).
  • LLM-based approaches, especially error-analysis prompting, achieved optimal F1 scores of 1.000 for both treatment and toxicities extraction.
  • Machine learning methods (LR, SVM) ranked second for toxicities (F1=0.937), outperforming deep learning models (BERT F1=0.839-0.873; ClinicalBERT F1=0.873-0.886) and rule-based baselines (F1=0.857-0.858).
  • Machine and deep learning models were limited by small training data, which affected their generalizability, particularly for rare toxicity categories.
  • The findings demonstrate that LLM-based NLP is highly effective for extracting this complex clinical information, showing strong potential for oncology research and pharmacovigilance applications.

Methodology

The study involved creating a gold-standard dataset of 236 clinical notes from adult oncology patients, manually annotated by domain experts for 5 categories of fluoropyrimidine treatment and related toxicities. Various NLP models were developed and evaluated using an 80:20 train-test split, including rule-based methods, machine learning algorithms (Random Forest, Support Vector Machine, Logistic Regression), deep learning models (BERT, ClinicalBERT), and Large Language Models (zero-shot and error-analysis prompting). Performance was measured using precision, recall, and F1 scores.

Key Findings

Error-analysis prompting with LLMs achieved optimal performance with F1 scores of 1.000 for both treatment and toxicity extraction. LLM zero-shot prompting also excelled for treatment (F1=1.000) but was slightly lower for toxicities (F1=0.876). Logistic Regression and SVM ranked second for toxicities (F1=0.937). Deep learning models (BERT and ClinicalBERT) showed F1 scores ranging from 0.839 to 0.886, while rule-based methods served as a baseline with F1 scores around 0.857-0.858.

Clinical Impact

This research provides a highly effective and automated solution for extracting critical treatment and toxicity information from unstructured clinical notes. This capability can significantly improve real-time patient monitoring for adverse drug reactions, enhance the efficiency and accuracy of pharmacovigilance activities, and facilitate large-scale retrospective analyses for drug safety and treatment optimization in oncology.

Limitations

The study identified that machine learning and deep learning approaches were limited by the relatively small training data size, which hindered their generalizability, particularly for the extraction of rare toxicity categories.

Future Directions

The strong performance of LLM-based NLP suggests a promising future for its deployment in broader clinical settings to support comprehensive oncology research and enhance pharmacovigilance efforts by automating data extraction from vast amounts of clinical text.

Abstract

Objective: Fluoropyrimidines are widely prescribed for colorectal and breast cancers, but are associated with toxicities such as hand-foot syndrome and cardiotoxicity. Since toxicity documentation is often embedded in clinical notes, we aimed to develop and evaluate natural language processing (NLP) methods to extract treatment and toxicity information. Materials and Methods: We constructed a gold-standard dataset of 236 clinical notes from 204,165 adult oncology patients. Domain experts annotated categories related to treatment regimens and toxicities. We developed rule-based, machine learning-based (Random Forest, Support Vector Machine [SVM], Logistic Regression [LR]), deep learning-based (BERT, ClinicalBERT), and large language models (LLM)-based NLP approaches (zero-shot and error-analysis prompting). Models used an 80:20 train-test split. Results: Sufficient data existed to train and evaluate 5 annotated categories. Error-analysis prompting achieved optimal precision, recall, and F1 scores (F1=1.000) for treatment and toxicities extraction, whereas zero-shot prompting reached F1=1.000 for treatment and F1=0.876 for toxicities extraction.LR and SVM ranked second for toxicities (F1=0.937). Deep learning underperformed, with BERT (F1=0.873 treatment; F1= 0.839 toxicities) and ClinicalBERT (F1=0.873 treatment; F1 = 0.886 toxicities). Rule-based methods served as our baseline with F1 scores of 0.857 in treatment and 0.858 in toxicities. Discussion: LMM-based approaches outperformed all others, followed by machine learning methods. Machine and deep learning approaches were limited by small training data and showed limited generalizability, particularly for rare categories. Conclusion: LLM-based NLP most effectively extracted fluoropyrimidine treatment and toxicity information from clinical notes, and has strong potential to support oncology research and pharmacovigilance.
