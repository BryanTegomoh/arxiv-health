Text Rationalization for Robust Causal Effect Estimation

Summary

This paper introduces Confounding-Aware Token Rationalization (CATR), a novel framework addressing the challenge of positivity assumption violations when using high-dimensional text as confounders in causal inference. CATR selects a sparse, necessary subset of textual tokens to preserve confounding information, mitigating positivity violations and yielding more accurate, stable, and interpretable causal effect estimates. The method was validated on synthetic data and a real-world clinical dataset (MIMIC-III).

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medicine as it improves the reliability of causal effect estimation from complex textual data in electronic health records, crucial for understanding treatment efficacy, disease progression, and intervention impacts in clinical settings. The use of the MIMIC-III database directly links it to critical care and health outcomes research.

AI Health Application

This research introduces a novel AI/NLP framework (Confounding-Aware Token Rationalization) designed to improve the accuracy and robustness of causal effect estimation from clinical text data, such as electronic health records. This can be applied in healthcare to better understand the true impact of treatments or interventions by mitigating confounding factors, leading to more reliable insights for clinical decision support, drug discovery, personalized medicine, and healthcare policy formulation based on real-world observational data.

Key Points

  • High-dimensional text data, when used as confounders in causal inference, often violates the positivity assumption due to redundant or spurious features.
  • This violation leads to issues like extreme propensity scores, unstable weights, and inflated variance in treatment effect estimates.
  • The proposed solution is Confounding-Aware Token Rationalization (CATR), a framework designed to select a sparse subset of relevant tokens.
  • CATR utilizes a residual-independence diagnostic to identify tokens that sufficiently preserve confounding information necessary for unconfoundedness, while discarding irrelevant text.
  • By achieving this rationalization, CATR effectively mitigates observational-level positivity violations.
  • The framework demonstrably stabilizes downstream causal effect estimators, producing more accurate, stable, and interpretable results.
  • Empirical validation was performed on both synthetic datasets and a real-world clinical database, MIMIC-III, showing superior performance over existing baselines.

Methodology

The core methodology is Confounding-Aware Token Rationalization (CATR). It operates by selecting a sparse, necessary subset of textual tokens from high-dimensional text data. This selection is guided by a novel residual-independence diagnostic, which is specifically designed to ensure that the chosen tokens are sufficient to preserve the confounding information required for achieving unconfoundedness in causal effect estimation. This process effectively prunes irrelevant or spurious textual features that would otherwise inflate dimensionality and violate the positivity assumption.

Key Findings

The key findings indicate that CATR successfully mitigates observational-level positivity violations associated with high-dimensional text confounders. Experimental results on both synthetic data and the real-world MIMIC-III database demonstrate that CATR consistently yields causal effect estimates that are significantly more accurate, stable, and interpretable compared to current baseline methods.

Clinical Impact

CATR has the potential to significantly enhance the rigor and reliability of evidence-based medicine by enabling more robust causal inference from rich, often unstructured, textual clinical notes. This could lead to more confident and precise estimation of treatment effects, improved understanding of medical interventions, and better-informed clinical decision-making, ultimately facilitating the development of more effective clinical guidelines and personalized treatment strategies.

Abstract

Recent advances in natural language processing have enabled the increasing use of text data in causal inference, particularly for adjusting confounding factors in treatment effect estimation. Although high-dimensional text can encode rich contextual information, it also poses unique challenges for causal identification and estimation. In particular, the positivity assumption, which requires sufficient treatment overlap across confounder values, is often violated at the observational level, when massive text is represented in feature spaces. Redundant or spurious textual features inflate dimensionality, producing extreme propensity scores, unstable weights, and inflated variance in effect estimates. We address these challenges with Confounding-Aware Token Rationalization (CATR), a framework that selects a sparse necessary subset of tokens using a residual-independence diagnostic designed to preserve confounding information sufficient for unconfoundedness. By discarding irrelevant texts while retaining key signals, CATR mitigates observational-level positivity violations and stabilizes downstream causal effect estimators. Experiments on synthetic data and a real-world study using the MIMIC-III database demonstrate that CATR yields more accurate, stable, and interpretable causal effect estimates than existing baselines.
