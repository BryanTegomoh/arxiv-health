Simultaneous Image Quality Improvement and Artefacts Correction in Accelerated MRI

arXiv ID: 2511.23274v1
Published: 2025-11-28
Authors: Georgia Kanli, Daniele Perlo, Selma Boudissa, Radovan Jirik, Olivier Keunen
Categories: cs.CV, cs.AI, physics.med-ph
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces USArt (Under-Sampling and Artifact correction model), a novel deep learning approach designed to simultaneously improve image quality from under-sampled MRI data and correct for common artefacts like noise and motion. Addressing a critical gap where existing methods tackle either acceleration or artefact correction independently, USArt demonstrates significant improvements in signal-to-noise ratio and contrast, achieving up to 5x acceleration while maintaining high image quality in 2D brain anatomical images.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant as it enables faster MRI scans without sacrificing image quality or diagnostic accuracy, crucial for patients who struggle to remain still or require multiple sequences. By simultaneously correcting common artefacts, it improves the reliability of diagnoses in neurological imaging.

AI Health Application

The AI application described is a deep learning model called USArt, designed to recover high-quality MR images from under-sampled data. Its purpose is to achieve up to 5x faster MRI scans while simultaneously correcting for noise and motion artefacts. This directly benefits healthcare by improving diagnostic accuracy (by correcting artefacts and improving SNR/contrast), enhancing patient experience (through shorter scan times, especially for children or claustrophobic patients), and potentially increasing patient throughput in clinical settings. It enables the acquisition of more data or more sequences in the same time frame, providing richer diagnostic information.

Key Points

  • MRI acquisition is inherently time-consuming, necessitating strategies like k-space under-sampling to accelerate the process, which typically degrades image quality.
  • Both under-sampled and full-sampled MRI images are susceptible to artefacts (e.g., noise, motion), which negatively impact diagnostic accuracy.
  • Existing deep learning methods address either image quality restoration from under-sampled data OR artefact correction, but not both simultaneously.
  • USArt, a dual sub-model approach, was developed to tackle both under-sampling and artefact correction (noise, motion) concurrently, specifically for 2D brain anatomical images with Cartesian sampling.
  • The model significantly increased signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and contrast in restored images, showing robustness across various under-sampling strategies and degradation levels.
  • Gradient under-sampling emerged as the most effective strategy, allowing for up to 5x acceleration while simultaneously correcting artefacts without significant image degradation.
  • This integrated approach enhances MRI efficiency and diagnostic utility, making it suitable for real-world clinical settings where multiple degradation factors often co-exist.

Methodology

The proposed USArt model employs a dual sub-model deep learning architecture. It is specifically customized for recovering high-quality 2D brain anatomical images from under-sampled k-space data acquired with Cartesian sampling, simultaneously correcting for noise and motion artefacts. The approach was evaluated across various under-sampling strategies and degradation levels.

Key Findings

USArt achieved a remarkable increase in signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and contrast in restored images. It successfully enabled up to 5x acceleration of MRI acquisition while simultaneously correcting for noise and motion artefacts, without significant image degradation. The gradient under-sampling strategy was identified as yielding the best outcomes among those explored, demonstrating the model's robustness in real-world scenarios.

Clinical Impact

The clinical impact is substantial: it allows for significantly faster MRI examinations, reducing patient discomfort and motion-induced artefacts, thereby enhancing patient compliance and throughput. Improved image quality, even from accelerated acquisitions, leads to more reliable diagnoses, particularly for brain pathologies, and expands MRI accessibility for vulnerable patient populations.

Limitations

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract, but the specificity to '2D brain anatomical images acquired with Cartesian sampling' implies a current scope limitation, suggesting that its direct applicability to 3D imaging, other body parts, or alternative k-space sampling patterns might require further development and validation.

Future Directions

Future research directions are not explicitly mentioned in the abstract. However, given the identified scope, logical next steps would involve extending the USArt model to 3D MRI, adapting it for other anatomical regions, exploring different k-space sampling trajectories, and validating its performance in a broader range of clinical scenarios and with diverse patient populations.

Abstract

MR data are acquired in the frequency domain, known as k-space. Acquiring high-quality and high-resolution MR images can be time-consuming, posing a significant challenge when multiple sequences providing complementary contrast information are needed or when the patient is unable to remain in the scanner for an extended period of time. Reducing k-space measurements is a strategy to speed up acquisition, but often leads to reduced quality in reconstructed images. Additionally, in real-world MRI, both under-sampled and full-sampled images are prone to artefacts, and correcting these artefacts is crucial for maintaining diagnostic accuracy. Deep learning methods have been proposed to restore image quality from under-sampled data, while others focused on the correction of artefacts that result from the noise or motion. No approach has however been proposed so far that addresses both acceleration and artefacts correction, limiting the performance of these models when these degradation factors occur simultaneously. To address this gap, we present a method for recovering high-quality images from under-sampled data with simultaneously correction for noise and motion artefact called USArt (Under-Sampling and Artifact correction model). Customized for 2D brain anatomical images acquired with Cartesian sampling, USArt employs a dual sub-model approach. The results demonstrate remarkable increase of signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) and contrast in the images restored. Various under-sampling strategies and degradation levels were explored, with the gradient under-sampling strategy yielding the best outcomes. We achieved up to 5x acceleration and simultaneously artefacts correction without significant degradation, showcasing the model's robustness in real-world settings.
