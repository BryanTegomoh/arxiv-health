Hierarchical Semantic Learning for Multi-Class Aorta Segmentation

Published: 2025-11-18
Authors: Pengcheng Shi
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00

Summary

This paper introduces a novel deep learning framework for multi-class aorta segmentation, addressing challenges of hierarchical anatomical relationships and severe class imbalance. It leverages a curriculum learning strategy combined with a novel fractal softmax for hierarchical semantic learning, achieving significant improvements in segmentation accuracy and efficiency. The proposed method accelerates model convergence and provides a fivefold inference speed-up, making it suitable for real-time clinical applications.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and efficient 3D segmentation of the aorta is paramount for the diagnosis, risk assessment, and precise planning of interventions for life-threatening cardiovascular pathologies like dissections and aneurysms. This framework's improved accuracy and real-time capability directly enhance clinical decision-making, optimize surgical precision for minimally invasive repairs, and can ultimately lead to better patient outcomes.

AI Health Application

The AI application is an advanced deep learning framework designed for automated, multi-class 3D segmentation of the aorta and its complex branch vessels from medical imaging data. This AI model aids in the accurate diagnosis of severe aortic pathologies (e.g., aneurysms, dissections), assists in detailed pre-operative planning for minimally invasive cardiovascular interventions, and improves the efficiency and practicality of clinical workflows by providing real-time, precise anatomical analysis.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical need for detailed 3D anatomical analysis of the aorta for pathologies (dissection, aneurysm, atherosclerosis) and pre-operative planning for minimally invasive repairs.
  • Tackles two key limitations of existing segmentation methods: overlooking hierarchical anatomical relationships within vascular structures and struggling with severe class imbalance inherent in medical imaging data.
  • Proposes a curriculum learning strategy that progressively learns anatomical constraints by decomposing complex structures from simple to complex components, inspired by human cognition.
  • Introduces a novel fractal softmax function specifically designed for hierarchical semantic learning, enabling the model to better understand and segment nested anatomical structures.
  • The curriculum learning framework inherently manages class imbalance by first establishing robust feature representations for dominant classes before tackling rare but anatomically critical structures, leading to accelerated model convergence.
  • Demonstrates significant performance improvements, achieving an 11.65% Dice score improvement for nnU-Net ResEnc M (using hierarchical semantic loss) on the validation set (epoch 50) and a 5.6% higher Dice score than baselines on the test set.
  • Implements a two-stage inference strategy that provides up to a fivefold acceleration, enhancing clinical practicality and making the framework suitable for real-time clinical applications.

Methodology

The study employs a curriculum learning strategy combined with a novel fractal softmax function for hierarchical semantic learning. This approach is designed to progressively learn anatomical constraints by decomposing complex structures into simpler components. The framework utilizes a two-stage inference strategy to achieve significant acceleration in prediction time. Evaluation was performed using nnU-Net ResEnc M as a baseline architecture, comparing performance with and without the proposed hierarchical semantic loss and fractal softmax.

Key Findings

The proposed model significantly improves segmentation accuracy, demonstrating an 11.65% Dice score increase on the validation set for the nnU-Net ResEnc M baseline when incorporating the hierarchical semantic loss, and a 5.6% higher Dice score on the test set compared to other baselines. Crucially, the framework achieves up to a fivefold acceleration in inference speed and significantly accelerates model convergence, making it highly efficient.

Its real-time processing capability allows for rapid diagnostic support and pre-operative planning, potentially leading to faster and more precise minimally invasive repairs, thereby improving patient safety and surgical outcomes in urgent cardiovascular scenarios.

Abstract

The aorta, the body's largest artery, is prone to pathologies such as dissection, aneurysm, and atherosclerosis, which often require timely intervention. Minimally invasive repairs involving branch vessels necessitate detailed 3D anatomical analysis. Existing methods often overlook hierarchical anatomical relationships while struggling with severe class imbalance inherent in vascular structures. We address these challenges with a curriculum learning strategy that leverages a novel fractal softmax for hierarchical semantic learning. Inspired by human cognition, our approach progressively learns anatomical constraints by decomposing complex structures from simple to complex components. The curriculum learning framework naturally addresses class imbalance by first establishing robust feature representations for dominant classes before tackling rare but anatomically critical structures, significantly accelerating model convergence in multi-class scenarios. Our two-stage inference strategy achieves up to fivefold acceleration, enhancing clinical practicality. On the validation set at epoch 50, our hierarchical semantic loss improves the Dice score of nnU-Net ResEnc M by 11.65%. The proposed model demonstrates a 5.6% higher Dice score than baselines on the test set. Experimental results show significant improvements in segmentation accuracy and efficiency, making the framework suitable for real-time clinical applications.
