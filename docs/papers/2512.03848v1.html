PULSE: A Unified Multi-Task Architecture for Cardiac Segmentation, Diagnosis, and Few-Shot Cross-Modality Clinical Adaptation

arXiv ID: 2512.03848v1
Published: 2025-12-03
Authors: Hania Ghouse, Maryam Alsharqi, Farhad R. Nezami, Muzammil Behzad
Categories: cs.CV, cs.AI

Summary

PULSE introduces a novel multi-task vision-language framework that unifies cardiac anatomical segmentation, disease classification, and clinically grounded report generation within a single architecture. Leveraging self-supervised representations and a composite supervision strategy, it demonstrates robust generalization across diverse datasets and enables few-shot adaptation to new imaging modalities. This unified approach aims to overcome the fragmentation prevalent in current cardiac image analysis pipelines, moving towards a scalable foundation model for comprehensive clinical assessment.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medicine as it offers a unified, efficient, and robust AI solution for critical cardiac assessments, streamlining diagnostic workflows and improving the consistency and accuracy of interpretations across various imaging modalities. It has the potential to reduce the workload on clinicians and accelerate the integration of advanced AI into daily clinical practice.

AI Health Application

PULSE is an AI framework designed for automated, unified analysis of cardiac images. It can perform anatomical segmentation of the heart, classify cardiac diseases for diagnosis, and generate clinically grounded text reports. This enables AI-assisted diagnosis, streamlined medical reporting, and serves as a foundation for cardiovascular AI in clinical settings.

Key Points

  • **Unified Multi-Task Architecture:** PULSE integrates anatomical segmentation, disease classification, and clinically grounded text generation into one coherent vision-language framework, departing from traditional task-specific models.
  • **Self-Supervised Representations:** The framework is built upon self-supervised representations, which are crucial for learning robust, task-invariant cardiac priors and enhancing generalization capabilities.
  • **Composite Supervision Strategy:** Optimization is driven by a sophisticated composite supervision strategy that meticulously balances region overlap learning, pixel-wise classification fidelity, and boundary-aware Intersection over Union (IoU) refinement.
  • **Multi-Scale Decoder for Segmentation:** Anatomical segmentation is performed via a multi-scale token reconstruction decoder, allowing for fine-grained pixel-level understanding.
  • **Shared Global Representations for Diagnosis & Reporting:** Shared global representations support both disease classification and the generation of clinically grounded text, enabling a transition from raw pixels to structured anatomical understanding and ultimately clinical reasoning.
  • **Robust Generalization & Few-Shot Adaptation:** PULSE demonstrates robust generalization across various cardiac datasets and possesses the critical ability to adapt to new imaging modalities (e.g., MRI, CT, ultrasound) with minimal supervision, showcasing strong few-shot learning capabilities.
  • **Foundation Model Potential:** The architecture represents a significant step towards developing a scalable, foundation-style AI framework for comprehensive cardiac analysis, aiming to revolutionize how cardiac images are interpreted clinically.

Methodology

PULSE is a multi-task vision-language framework primarily based on self-supervised representations. It employs a composite supervision strategy balancing region overlap, pixel-wise classification, and boundary-aware IoU refinement. A multi-scale token reconstruction decoder is utilized for anatomical segmentation, while shared global representations facilitate disease classification and the generation of clinically grounded textual reports.

The key findings indicate that PULSE successfully unifies previously fragmented cardiac analysis tasks (segmentation, classification, text generation) within a single architecture. It demonstrates the ability to learn task-invariant cardiac priors, generalize robustly across diverse datasets, and adapt to new imaging modalities with minimal supervision, validating its effectiveness for few-shot cross-modality clinical adaptation.

Abstract

Cardiac image analysis remains fragmented across tasks: anatomical segmentation, disease classification, and grounded clinical report generation are typically handled by separate networks trained under different data regimes. No existing framework unifies these objectives within a single architecture while retaining generalization across imaging modalities and datasets. We introduce PULSE, a multi-task vision-language framework built on self-supervised representations and optimized through a composite supervision strategy that balances region overlap learning, pixel wise classification fidelity, and boundary aware IoU refinement. A multi-scale token reconstruction decoder enables anatomical segmentation, while shared global representations support disease classification and clinically grounded text output allowing the model to transition from pixels to structures and finally clinical reasoning within one architecture. Unlike prior task-specific pipelines, PULSE learns task-invariant cardiac priors, generalizes robustly across datasets, and can be adapted to new imaging modalities with minimal supervision. This moves the field closer to a scalable, foundation style cardiac analysis framework.
