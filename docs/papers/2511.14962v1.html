Reconstruction of three-dimensional shapes of normal and disease-related erythrocytes from partial observations using multi-fidelity neural networks

Authors: Haizhou Wen, He Li, Zhen Li

Summary

This study introduces a multi-fidelity neural network (MFNN) for reconstructing full three-dimensional (3D) erythrocyte (RBC) shapes from partial two-dimensional observations, such as microscope cross-sections. By fusing high-fidelity cross-sections with a low-fidelity reference and incorporating physical constraints, the MFNN achieves over 95% coordinate accuracy across diverse RBC morphologies. This capability enables precise quantitative analysis of RBC morphological parameters crucial for understanding aging and various blood disorders.

Medical Relevance

Accurate 3D erythrocyte morphology is a critical biomarker for diagnosing and monitoring numerous hematological diseases and understanding cellular aging. This research offers a non-invasive, quantitative method to derive precise 3D RBC shapes from readily available 2D microscopy data, potentially accelerating diagnosis and informing treatment strategies for a range of blood disorders.

AI Health Application

The paper describes a medical AI application where multi-fidelity neural networks are used for advanced medical image analysis. Specifically, AI is employed to reconstruct the full 3D shape of red blood cells from partial 2D observations (microscope images). This AI-driven reconstruction aims to enable more accurate and quantitative analysis of RBC morphology, which can assist clinicians and researchers in diagnosing and monitoring various RBC disorders and understanding the impact of aging on RBCs.

Key Points

  • A Multi-Fidelity Neural Network (MFNN) is proposed to reconstruct complete 3D erythrocyte shapes using partial 2D cross-sectional observations, such as those from microscope images.
  • The MFNN architecture combines a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for processing low-fidelity reference RBC data with a Feedforward Neural Network (FFNN) to capture complex, nonlinear morphological correlations.
  • Training data is generated via Dissipative Particle Dynamics (DPD) simulations, modeling the stomatocyte-discocyte-echinocyte transformation, and the network training is augmented with surface area and volume constraints for regularization.
  • The method achieves high reconstruction accuracy, demonstrating over 95% coordinate accuracy in recovering complex RBC morphologies when provided with at least two orthogonal cross-sections.
  • Strategic sampling, particularly utilizing informative oblique cross-sections that intersect spicule tips in echinocytes, significantly improves both local and global feature reconstruction, highlighting the value of feature-aware data acquisition.
  • The MFNN exhibits enhanced robustness against noise, varying sampling strategies, and shape dissimilarity, especially when physically constrained training is applied.
  • This approach provides a quantitative tool for analyzing RBC morphological parameters, which is essential for understanding the physiology of RBC aging and the pathology of various RBC disorders.

Methodology

The study employs a Multi-Fidelity Neural Network (MFNN) that fuses high-fidelity RBC cross-sections with a morphologically similar low-fidelity reference 3D shape. The MFNN integrates a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) trained on low-fidelity data and a Feedforward Neural Network (FFNN) to capture nonlinear correlations. Training data is generated from Dissipative Particle Dynamics (DPD) simulations modeling RBC shape transformations (stomatocyte-discocyte-echinocyte), and is regularized by incorporating surface area and volume constraints, grounded in topological homeomorphism theory.

Key Findings

The MFNN predictor successfully reconstructs complex RBC morphologies with over 95% coordinate accuracy, requiring a minimum of two orthogonal cross-sections. Informative oblique cross-sections, particularly those intersecting echinocyte spicule tips, significantly enhance both local and global feature reconstruction. The physically constrained training further bolsters the model's robustness against noise, varied sampling strategies, and shape dissimilarities.

Clinical Impact

This methodology holds significant potential for clinical applications by enabling quantitative analysis of 3D RBC morphological parameters from standard microscope images. This could facilitate more precise diagnosis, monitoring, and prognosis of conditions like sickle cell disease, thalassemias, hereditary spherocytosis, and other RBC disorders, as well as providing insights into the aging process of RBCs.

Limitations

The primary limitation, though not explicitly stated as such, is that the training data is generated through Dissipative Particle Dynamics (DPD) simulations. While sophisticated, simulation data may not perfectly replicate the complexities and variabilities of real-world experimental microscopy data from human RBCs, potentially introducing a 'sim-to-real' gap that would require further experimental validation.

Future Directions

The study's implications suggest future work would involve validating the MFNN with real-world, experimentally acquired microscope images of human RBCs, rather than just simulated data. Further integration into clinical diagnostic pipelines for automated, high-throughput quantitative analysis of RBC morphology would also be a logical next step.

Abstract

Reconstruction of 3D erythrocyte or red blood cell (RBC) morphology from partial observations, such as microscope images, is essential for understanding the physiology of RBC aging and the pathology of various RBC disorders. In this study, we propose a multi-fidelity neural network (MFNN) approach to fuse high-fidelity cross-sections of an RBC, with a morphologically similar low-fidelity reference 3D RBC shape to recover its full 3D surface. The MFNN predictor combines a convolutional neural network trained on low-fidelity reference RBC data with a feedforward neural network that captures nonlinear morphological correlations, and augments training with surface area and volume constraints for regularization in the low-fidelity branch. This approach is theoretically grounded by a topological homeomorphism between a sphere and 3D RBC surfaces, with training data generated by dissipative particle dynamics simulations of stomatocyte-discocyte-echinocyte transformation. Benchmarking across diverse RBC shapes observed in normal and aged populations, our results show that the MFNN predictor can reconstruct complex RBC morphologies with over 95% coordinate accuracy when provided with at least two orthogonal cross-sections. It is observed that informative oblique cross-sections intersecting spicule tips of echinocytes improve both local and global feature reconstruction, highlighting the value of feature-aware sampling. Our study further evaluates the influence of sampling strategies, shape dissimilarity, and noise, showing enhanced robustness under physically constrained training. Altogether, these results demonstrate the capability of MFNN to reconstruct the 3D shape of normal and aged RBCs from partial cross-sections as observed in conventional microscope images, which could facilitate the quantitative analysis of RBC morphological parameters in normal and disease-related RBC samples.
