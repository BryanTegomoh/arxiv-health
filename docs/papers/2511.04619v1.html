Dynamic causal discovery in Alzheimer's disease through latent pseudotime modelling

Summary

This paper introduces a novel framework for dynamic causal discovery in Alzheimer's disease (AD) by inferring a latent disease pseudotime, which orders patients along a data-driven disease trajectory independent of chronological age. It overcomes limitations of static causal models by demonstrating how causal relationships evolve over this pseudotime, significantly improving diagnostic prediction compared to age, and revealing dynamic interactions among both novel and established AD biomarkers.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for understanding the complex and evolving pathophysiology of Alzheimer's disease by moving beyond static causal models. It offers a new data-driven approach to characterize disease progression and identify dynamic biomarker interactions, potentially leading to more precise diagnostic strategies and targeted therapeutic interventions.

AI Health Application

This research applies machine learning (latent variable models, causal discovery algorithms) to health data (Alzheimer's disease patient data) to infer a data-driven disease trajectory (pseudotime), improve diagnostic prediction, and understand the dynamic causal relationships between disease biomarkers. This constitutes a medical AI application for disease understanding, diagnosis, and potentially prognosis.

Key Points

  • Addresses the limitation of static graph assumptions in causal discovery for diseases like AD, which have an evolving pathophysiology modulated by a latent disease pseudotime.
  • Proposes the application of an existing latent variable model to infer a 'disease pseudotime' that orders patients along a data-driven disease trajectory, independent of chronological age.
  • Demonstrates that this inferred pseudotime significantly outperforms chronological age in predicting AD diagnosis (AUC 0.82 vs. 0.59).
  • Shows that incorporating minimal, disease-agnostic background knowledge substantially improves the accuracy and orientation of the learned causal graphs.
  • Reveals dynamic causal interactions between novel AD biomarkers (e.g., Neurofilament Light Chain (NfL), Glial Fibrillary Acidic Protein (GFAP)) and established markers, indicating their evolving roles during disease progression.
  • Provides a practical framework for performing dynamic causal discovery in AD, even when traditional static causal model assumptions are violated.

Methodology

The study applies an existing latent variable model to real-world Alzheimer's disease data. This model is utilized to infer a latent 'pseudotime' variable that orders patients along a data-driven disease trajectory, distinct from their chronological age. Subsequently, the framework learns how causal relationships between various disease markers dynamically evolve across this inferred pseudotime. The methodology also incorporates minimal, disease-agnostic background knowledge to improve the accuracy and orientation of the inferred causal graphs.

Abstract

The application of causal discovery to diseases like Alzheimer's (AD) is limited by the static graph assumptions of most methods; such models cannot account for an evolving pathophysiology, modulated by a latent disease pseudotime. We propose to apply an existing latent variable model to real-world AD data, inferring a pseudotime that orders patients along a data-driven disease trajectory independent of chronological age, then learning how causal relationships evolve. Pseudotime outperformed age in predicting diagnosis (AUC 0.82 vs 0.59). Incorporating minimal, disease-agnostic background knowledge substantially improved graph accuracy and orientation. Our framework reveals dynamic interactions between novel (NfL, GFAP) and established AD markers, enabling practical causal discovery despite violated assumptions.
