Summary

This paper validates a novel stochastic differential equation (SDE)-based model for proton beam energy deposition by comparing its predictions against Geant4 in simplified phantom scenarios. The SDE model demonstrated comparable accuracy to high-fidelity Monte Carlo simulations while achieving a significant fivefold computational speed-up, highlighting its potential for accelerating dose calculations and treatment planning in proton therapy.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to proton therapy as it introduces a method to drastically speed up dose calculations without compromising accuracy. Faster calculations enable more efficient treatment planning, adaptive radiotherapy, and potentially real-time dose monitoring, which can lead to improved patient care and outcomes.

AI Health Application

The described SDE-based Monte Carlo method is a computational physics model, not inherently an AI application in the machine learning sense. However, advanced computational methods like this are foundational for modern, data-driven, and potentially AI-accelerated treatment planning systems in health. Speeding up dose calculations can enable more complex optimization algorithms, adaptive therapy planning, or real-time decision support systems that might incorporate AI components in the future.

Key Points

  • A newly proposed stochastic differential equation (SDE)-based model for proton beam energy deposition was validated.
  • The SDE model utilizes standard approximations for interaction cross sections and mean excitation energies, ensuring adaptability to new materials and configurations.
  • Validation was performed against Geant4 in both homogeneous and heterogeneous phantom scenarios to assess accuracy.
  • The SDE-based dose distributions showed excellent agreement with Geant4, with range differences consistently within 0.4 mm.
  • Accuracy was further confirmed by high 3D gamma pass rates, exceeding 98% under 3%/2 mm criteria with a 1% dose threshold.
  • The model achieved a significant computational speed-up, being approximately five times faster than Geant4, a consistency observed across different Geant4 physics lists.
  • This research demonstrates that the SDE approach can deliver accuracy comparable to high-fidelity Monte Carlo methods at a fraction of the computational cost, promising more efficient proton therapy planning.

Methodology

The study implemented a stochastic differential equation (SDE) framework for modeling proton beam energy deposition, building on previous work. This implementation incorporated standard approximations for interaction cross sections and mean excitation energies to ensure material adaptability. The SDE model's dose predictions were then benchmarked against the established Geant4 Monte Carlo simulation toolkit across homogeneous and heterogeneous phantom geometries. Validation metrics included analysis of range differences, 3D gamma pass rates (using 3%/2 mm criteria and a 1% dose threshold), and comparative assessment of computational speed.

Abstract

Objective: To validate a newly proposed stochastic differential equation (SDE)-based model for proton beam energy deposition by comparing its predictions with those from Geant4 in simplified phantom scenarios. Approach: Building on previous work in Crossley et al. (2025), where energy deposition from a proton beam was modelled using an SDE framework, we implemented the model with standard approximations to interaction cross sections and mean excitation energies, which makes simulations easily adaptable to new materials and configurations. The model was benchmarked against Geant4 in homogeneous and heterogeneous phantoms. Main results: The SDE-based dose distributions agreed well with Geant4, showing range differences within 0.4 mm and 3D gamma pass rates exceeding 98% under 3%/2 mm criteria with a 1% dose threshold. The model achieved a computational speed-up of approximately fivefold relative to Geant4, consistent across different Geant4 physics lists. Significance: These results demonstrate that the SDE approach can reproduce accuracy comparable to high-fidelity Monte Carlo for proton therapy at a fraction of the computational cost, highlighting its potential for accelerating dose calculations and treatment planning.
