Cross-Domain Validation of a Resection-Trained Self-Supervised Model on Multicentre Mesothelioma Biopsies

arXiv ID: 2512.01681v1
Published: 2025-12-01
Authors: Farzaneh Seyedshahi, Francesca Damiola, Sylvie Lantuejoul, Ke Yuan, John Le Quesne
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper demonstrates the successful cross-domain validation of a self-supervised encoder, initially trained on large mesothelioma resection specimens, for application to small biopsy material. The model effectively captures meaningful morphological patterns from biopsies, enabling accurate prediction of patient survival and classification of tumor subtypes. This innovation broadens the practical utility of AI in mesothelioma diagnostics by overcoming the common limitation of requiring large tissue samples.

Medical Relevance

Accurate subtype classification and outcome prediction in mesothelioma are critical for guiding personalized therapy and patient management. This research provides a method to derive such crucial information from commonly available small biopsies, thus making advanced computational pathology tools more accessible and clinically applicable.

AI Health Application

The AI application is a self-supervised computational pathology model designed to classify mesothelioma tumor subtypes and predict patient survival from biopsy images. This tool aims to support medical professionals in diagnosis and treatment planning, addressing current limitations in real-world clinical settings.

Key Points

  • Computational pathology models are typically trained on large resection specimens, which limits their use in real-world clinical settings where small biopsies are more common.
  • The study utilized a self-supervised encoder model, trained on images derived from mesothelioma resection tissues.
  • Crucially, this resection-trained encoder was successfully applied and validated on biopsy material, demonstrating cross-domain generalizability.
  • The model was able to extract and capture meaningful morphological patterns from the smaller biopsy samples.
  • Using these extracted patterns, the model could effectively predict patient survival outcomes.
  • The model also demonstrated the capability to classify different tumor subtypes of mesothelioma based on biopsy morphology.
  • This approach highlights the significant potential of AI-driven tools to enhance diagnosis and support treatment planning for mesothelioma patients, particularly when only biopsies are available.

Methodology

The study employed a self-supervised learning approach to train an encoder on large tissue images from mesothelioma resection specimens. This pre-trained encoder was then subjected to cross-domain validation by applying it to small biopsy material from multicentre mesothelioma cohorts to extract morphological patterns. The utility of these extracted patterns was subsequently evaluated for tasks such as patient survival prediction and tumor subtype classification.

Key Findings

A self-supervised encoder trained on resection tissue can be successfully transferred and applied to biopsy material, effectively capturing meaningful morphological patterns. Utilizing these biopsy-derived patterns, the model can accurately predict patient survival and classify tumor subtypes in mesothelioma, demonstrating robust cross-domain validation.

Clinical Impact

This research provides a pathway for integrating AI-driven diagnostic and prognostic tools directly into the workflow for mesothelioma patients, even when only small biopsy samples are available. It has the potential to significantly improve the accuracy and speed of subtype classification and outcome prediction, leading to more timely and personalized treatment decisions.

Most computational pathology models are trained on large tissue images from resection specimens, limiting their use in real-world settings where small biopsies are common. We show that a self-supervised encoder trained on resection tissue can be applied to biopsy material, capturing meaningful morphological patterns.
