Cross-Domain Validation of a Resection-Trained Self-Supervised Model on Multicentre Mesothelioma Biopsies

arXiv ID: 2512.01681v1
Published: 2025-12-01
Authors: Farzaneh Seyedshahi, Francesca Damiola, Sylvie Lantuejoul, Ke Yuan, John Le Quesne
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This research demonstrates a cross-domain validation approach where a self-supervised encoder, initially trained on large resection tissue images, effectively extracts meaningful morphological patterns from smaller mesothelioma biopsy material. This model successfully leverages these patterns to accurately predict patient survival and classify tumor subtypes, addressing a significant limitation of traditional computational pathology models in real-world clinical settings.

Medical Relevance

Accurate subtype classification and prognosis in mesothelioma are crucial for personalized therapy and patient management. This AI model directly addresses a critical clinical gap by enabling reliable predictions from commonly available small biopsies, thus facilitating earlier and more informed clinical decisions.

AI Health Application

The AI application is a self-supervised model trained on histopathological images (resection specimens) and validated on biopsy material to classify mesothelioma tumor subtypes and predict patient survival. This directly supports medical diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment planning for mesothelioma patients.

Key Points

  • Traditional computational pathology models are often limited in real-world application due to reliance on large resection specimens, whereas small biopsies are common for diagnosis.
  • The study proposes a self-supervised encoder model, initially trained on resection tissue, for application to biopsy material in mesothelioma.
  • The self-supervised learning approach allows the model to capture meaningful morphological patterns within the smaller biopsy samples.
  • Using the extracted morphological patterns, the model demonstrated the capability to predict patient survival in mesothelioma.
  • The model also proved effective in accurately classifying tumor subtypes within the mesothelioma biopsy samples.
  • This methodology addresses the critical need for AI-driven diagnostic and prognostic tools applicable to routinely collected small biopsy specimens.
  • The findings highlight the potential for AI tools to enhance diagnosis, guide therapy, and support treatment planning in mesothelioma by overcoming data domain discrepancies.

Methodology

The study utilized a self-supervised learning approach for an encoder model. This encoder was initially trained on large tissue images derived from resection specimens. Subsequently, its ability to extract meaningful morphological patterns and perform predictive tasks was validated on multicentre mesothelioma biopsy material.

Key Findings

The self-supervised model, despite being trained on resection tissue, successfully captured relevant morphological patterns from biopsy material. Leveraging these patterns, the model demonstrated robust performance in both predicting patient survival and accurately classifying tumor subtypes in mesothelioma.

Clinical Impact

This research provides a pathway for developing AI-driven tools that can be directly integrated into routine clinical practice, particularly where only small biopsy samples are available. It can significantly enhance diagnostic precision, improve prognostic accuracy for mesothelioma patients, and support clinicians in making timely and personalized treatment decisions, ultimately improving patient care.

Abstract

Accurate subtype classification and outcome prediction in mesothelioma are essential for guiding therapy and patient care. Most computational pathology models are trained on large tissue images from resection specimens, limiting their use in real-world settings where small biopsies are common. We show that a self-supervised encoder trained on resection tissue can be applied to biopsy material, capturing meaningful morphological patterns. Using these patterns, the model can predict patient survival and classify tumor subtypes. This approach demonstrates the potential of AI-driven tools to support diagnosis and treatment planning in mesothelioma.
