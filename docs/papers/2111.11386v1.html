Summary

This paper introduces a novel approach to Sensitivity Analysis (SA) by integrating within-host virus-immune system interactions with population-level epidemic models. It utilizes an age-since-infection structured vector-host model where epidemiological parameters are functions of within-host dynamics, demonstrating that immunological parameters significantly impact population-scale disease metrics like R0 and infectiousness, with a case study on Rift Valley Fever Disease.

Medical Relevance

This research bridges the gap between micro-scale immunological processes and macro-scale epidemic dynamics, providing a more comprehensive understanding of disease spread that can directly inform the development of drugs, vaccines, and public health interventions by identifying crucial within-host targets.

  • The research connects individual-level (within-host) viral and antibody dynamics, governed by an ODE system, to population-level disease spread in an age-since-infection structured vector-host model.
  • Immunological parameters are shown to significantly influence population-level quantities such as the basic reproduction number (R0), final epidemic size, and host infectiousness at different outbreak phases.

Methodology

The study employs Sensitivity Analysis (SA) on an age-since-infection structured vector-host model. This model integrates within-host virus and antibody dynamics, which are governed by an Ordinary Differential Equation (ODE) system, directly linking them to epidemiological parameters. The framework investigates the impact of immunological parameters on population-level disease quantities like R0, final epidemic size, and infectiousness. Rift Valley Fever Disease serves as a case study, utilizing existing parameter estimations.

Key Findings

These effects are significant at the population scale.

This can guide the design of more targeted drug and vaccine development efforts by identifying critical immunological pathways influencing disease spread.

Abstract

Sensitivity Analysis (SA) is a useful tool to measure the impact of changes in model parameters on the infection dynamics, particularly to quantify the expected efficacy of disease control strategies. SA has only been applied to epidemic models at the population level, ignoring the effect of within-host virus-with-immune-system interactions on the disease spread. Connecting the scales from individual to population can help inform drug and vaccine development. Thus the value of understanding the impact of immunological parameters on epidemiological quantities. Here we consider an age-since-infection structured vector-host model, in which epidemiological parameters are formulated as functions of within-host virus and antibody densities, governed by an ODE system. We then use SA for these immuno-epidemiological models to investigate the impact of immunological parameters on population-level disease dynamics such as basic reproduction number, final size of the epidemic or the infectiousness at different phases of an outbreak. As a case study, we consider Rift Valley Fever Disease (RFVD) utilizing parameter estimations from prior studies. SA indicates that 1% increase in within-host pathogen growth rate can lead up to 8% increase in R0; up to 1% increase in steady-state infected host abundance, and up to 4% increase in infectiousness of hosts when the reproduction number R0 is larger than one. These significant increases in population-scale disease quantities suggest that control strategies that reduce the within-host pathogen growth can be important in reducing disease prevalence.
