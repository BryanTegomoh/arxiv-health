UNAAGI: Atom-Level Diffusion for Generating Non-Canonical Amino Acid Substitutions

Published: 2025-12-11
Authors: Han Tang, Wouter Boomsma
Categories: q-bio.BM

Summary

UNAAGI is a novel diffusion-based generative model addressing the limitation of current inverse folding methods that are restricted to designing natural amino acid (NAA) substitutions. By modeling protein side chains at the atomic level, UNAAGI enables the exploration and generation of both NAAs and non-canonical amino acids (NCAAs), demonstrating substantially improved performance for NCAA substitutions and suggesting a unified methodological foundation for protein engineering and structure-based drug design.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing protein engineering and drug discovery by enabling the rational design of proteins incorporating diverse non-canonical amino acids, potentially leading to novel therapeutics with enhanced stability, function, or target specificity.

AI Health Application

UNAAGI is a diffusion-based generative AI model designed to predict and generate amino acid substitutions, including non-canonical ones, from atomic-level protein structures. This AI application is crucial for: 1) accelerating the design of novel proteins with desired therapeutic or diagnostic functions; 2) improving the prediction of mutational effects, which is vital for understanding genetic diseases and drug resistance; and 3) aiding structure-based drug design by enabling a more comprehensive exploration of protein-ligand interactions and protein modifications.

Methodology

UNAAGI utilizes a diffusion-based generative modeling approach within an E(3)-equivariant framework. It reconstructs residue identities directly from atomic-level protein structures. Crucially, it models side chains in full atomic detail, allowing for the generation of both natural and non-canonical amino acids within a single, unified generative paradigm.

Key Findings

UNAAGI achieves substantially improved performance on non-canonical amino acid (NCAA) substitutions compared to current state-of-the-art methods, as demonstrated on experimentally benchmarked mutation effect datasets. Furthermore, the findings suggest a shared methodological foundation between protein engineering and structure-based drug design.

It has the potential to facilitate the development of next-generation biologics and targeted drug candidates, leading to more effective treatments and diagnostics.

Abstract

Proposing beneficial amino acid substitutions, whether for mutational effect prediction or protein engineering, remains a central challenge in structural biology. Recent inverse folding models, trained to reconstruct sequences from structure, have had considerable impact in identifying functional mutations. However, current approaches are constrained to designing sequences composed exclusively of natural amino acids (NAAs). The larger set of non-canonical amino acids (NCAAs), which offer greater chemical diversity, and are frequently used in in-vivo protein engineering, remain largely inaccessible for current variant effect prediction methods. To address this gap, we introduce \textbf{UNAAGI}, a diffusion-based generative model that reconstructs residue identities from atomic-level structure using an E(3)-equivariant framework. By modeling side chains in full atomic detail rather than as discrete tokens, UNAAGI enables the exploration of both canonical and non-canonical amino acid substitutions within a unified generative paradigm. We evaluate our method on experimentally benchmarked mutation effect datasets and demonstrate that it achieves substantially improved performance on NCAA substitutions compared to the current state-of-the-art. Furthermore, our results suggest a shared methodological foundation between protein engineering and structure-based drug design, opening the door for a unified training framework across these domains.
