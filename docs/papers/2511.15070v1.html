The role of antibody-mediated immunity in shaping the seasonality of respiratory viruses

Summary

This paper investigates how antibody waning and antigenic variation interact with seasonal forcing to shape the recurrent epidemics of respiratory viruses. Using an SIS immuno-epidemiological model, the authors reveal complex dynamics including multi-year periodicity, quasiperiodicity, and chaos when seasonal forcing is introduced, significantly influencing epidemic timing, magnitude, and cumulative infection burden.

Medical Relevance

Understanding these complex dynamics is crucial for predicting the timing and severity of respiratory virus epidemics, allowing healthcare systems to better prepare for surges in infections and hospitalizations, thus reducing the substantial burden on public health.

AI Health Application

This paper is a mathematical modeling study in epidemiology, not an AI application itself. However, the insights and models developed could inform or be integrated into AI-driven forecasting systems for disease outbreaks, resource allocation in hospitals, or personalized risk assessment for respiratory infections.

  • Respiratory virus epidemics in temperate regions recur yearly, imposing significant healthcare burden, but the interaction of seasonal forcing with immunity and antigenic variation is poorly understood.
  • A susceptible-infectious-susceptible (SIS) immuno-epidemiological model was developed, stratifying the susceptible population by antibody level, which decays due to both waning immunity and antigenic drift.
  • In the absence of seasonal forcing, the model exhibits two Hopf bifurcations related to the effective antibody decay rate, leading to periodic epidemic solutions.
  • With seasonal forcing, the model shows complex dynamics, including multi-year periodicity, quasiperiodicity, and chaos, stemming from interactions between forcing strength and effective antibody decay rate.

Methodology

The study employs a susceptible-infectious-susceptible (SIS) immuno-epidemiological model. This model stratifies the susceptible population based on their antibody level against the circulating virus strain, with antibody levels declining due to both natural waning immunity and antigenic drift. The model's dynamics were analyzed both in the absence and presence of seasonal forcing, which cyclically varies the virus transmission rate.

Key Findings

Key findings include the observation of two Hopf bifurcations in the absence of seasonal forcing, indicating conditions for periodic epidemic solutions. When seasonal forcing is introduced, the model reveals complex interactions between forcing strength and effective antibody decay, leading to diverse dynamic outcomes such as multi-year periodicity, quasiperiodicity, and chaos. The timing and magnitude of seasonal epidemics are highly sensitive to these interactions. Importantly, seasonal forcing can cause resonant amplification or damping, significantly altering the cumulative infection burden compared to non-forced scenarios.

Clinical Impact

This research provides a deeper, more mechanistic understanding of the factors driving respiratory virus seasonality, which can inform more accurate epidemic forecasting. This improved predictability enables better resource allocation for healthcare systems (e.g., ICU beds, staffing, vaccine distribution) and optimization of public health interventions like vaccination campaign timing, ultimately aiming to mitigate the societal and healthcare burden of seasonal epidemics.

Abstract

In temperate regions, respiratory virus epidemics recur on a yearly basis, primarily during the winter season. This is believed to be induced by seasonal forcing, where the rate at which the virus can be transmitted varies cyclically across the course of each year. Seasonal epidemics can place substantial burden upon the healthcare system, with large numbers of infections and hospitalisations occurring across a short time period. However, the interactions between seasonal forcing and the factors necessary for epidemic resurgence - such as waning immunity, antigenic variation or demography - remain poorly understood. In this manuscript, we examine how the dynamics of antibody waning and antigenic variation can shape the seasonal recurrence of epidemics. We develop a susceptible-infectious-susceptible (SIS) immuno-epidemiological model of respiratory virus spread, where the susceptible population is stratified by their antibody level against the currently circulating strain of the virus, with this decaying as both antibody waning and antigenic drift occur. In the absence of seasonal forcing, we demonstrate the existence of two Hopf bifurcations over the effective antibody decay rate, with associated periodic model solutions. When seasonal forcing is introduced, we identify complex interactions between the strength of forcing and the effective antibody decay rate, yielding myriad dynamics including multi-year periodicity, quasiperiodicity and chaos. The timing and magnitude of seasonal epidemics is highly sensitive to this interaction, with the distribution of infection timing (by time of year) varying substantially across the parameter space. Finally, we show that seasonal forcing can produce resonant amplification (or damping) resulting in a cumulative infection burdens that is greater (or lesser) than would otherwise be observed.
