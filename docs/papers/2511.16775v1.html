A Respiratory Motion Analysis for Guiding Stereotactic Arrhythmia Radiotherapy Motion Management

arXiv ID: 2511.16775v1
Published: 2025-11-20
Authors: Yuhao Wang, Yao Hao, Hongyu An, H Michael Gach, Clifford Robinson, Phillip S Cuculich, Deshan Yang
Categories: physics.med-ph
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces gCGF, a novel groupwise deformable image registration algorithm incorporating principal component filtering and spatial smoothing, designed to accurately quantify cardiac respiratory motion in r4DCTs for Stereotactic Arrhythmia Radiotherapy (STAR) patients. It effectively removes cardiac motion artifacts to isolate respiratory motion, demonstrating high precision on digital phantoms and providing detailed heart and VT target motion magnitudes in 20 STAR patients. The findings offer crucial insights for improving motion management in STAR.

Medical Relevance

Accurate quantification and management of cardiorespiratory motion are critical for improving the precision of Stereotactic Arrhythmia Radiotherapy (STAR), enabling tighter internal target volume (ITV) margins and potentially reducing radiation dose to healthy cardiac tissue, thus enhancing treatment efficacy and safety for ventricular tachycardia patients.

Key Points

  • Stereotactic Arrhythmia Radiotherapy (STAR) for ventricular tachycardia (VT) requires internal target volume (ITV) expansions due to unmanaged cardiorespiratory motion in conventional r4DCT imaging.
  • A novel groupwise surface-to-surface deformable image registration (DIR) algorithm, gCGF, was developed to accurately quantify respiratory motion while suppressing cardiac motion artifacts.
  • gCGF incorporates a principal component filtering (PCF) mechanism and spatial smoothing to iteratively register heart contours across r4DCT phases, effectively separating cyclic respiratory motion from random cardiac motion.
  • Validation on 8 digital phantoms with simulated cardiac artifacts demonstrated gCGF's high accuracy, achieving a mean target registration error (TRE) of 0.63 ¬± 0.51 mm, along with improved phase smoothness and artifact reduction.
  • Applied to 20 STAR patients, the heart's maximum respiratory motion magnitude ranged from 3.6 to 7.9 mm (mean: 1.0-2.6 mm), with a peak-to-peak range of 6.2 to 14.7 mm.
  • For VT targets, the maximum respiratory motion magnitude ranged from 3.0 to 6.7 mm (mean: 0.8-2.9 mm), and the peak-to-peak range was 4.7 to 11.8 mm.
  • A statistically significant dominance of the first principal component of the motion direction (p = 0) was observed, indicating a highly predictable primary motion axis.

Methodology

The study developed gCGF, a groupwise surface-to-surface deformable image registration algorithm. This algorithm iteratively registers heart contours from an average respiratory-phase CT to ten r4DCT phases. It integrates a novel Principal Component Filtering (PCF) mechanism to identify and remove random cardiac motion components, and a spatial smoothing mechanism to enhance registration quality and isolate cyclic respiratory motion. The algorithm was quantitatively validated using 8 digital phantoms with simulated cardiac artifacts and compared to a state-of-the-art groupwise DIR method via an ablation study. Subsequently, gCGF was applied to the r4DCTs of 20 STAR patients to analyze the respiratory motion characteristics of the heart and VT targets.

Key Findings

gCGF achieved a mean target registration error of 0.63 ¬± 0.51 mm on digital phantoms, successfully improving phase smoothness and reducing cardiac motion artifacts. In STAR patients, the heart's maximum respiratory motion ranged from 3.6 to 7.9 mm (mean: 1.0-2.6 mm), with peak-to-peak motion from 6.2 to 14.7 mm. For VT targets, maximum motion ranged from 3.0 to 6.7 mm (mean: 0.8-2.9 mm), and peak-to-peak motion ranged from 4.7 to 11.8 mm. A significant dominance of the first principal component of motion direction (p = 0) was observed.

Clinical Impact

This research provides a more robust and accurate method for quantifying cardiac respiratory motion during STAR, which can lead to more precise motion management strategies. By effectively removing cardiac motion artifacts and isolating true respiratory motion, gCGF could facilitate the reduction of internal target volume (ITV) expansions. This, in turn, may allow for higher dose conformity, minimized radiation exposure to healthy cardiac structures, and potentially improved treatment outcomes and reduced toxicity for patients undergoing STAR for ventricular tachycardia.

Abstract

Stereotactic Arrhythmia Radiotherapy (STAR) treats ventricular tachycardia (VT) but requires internal target volume (ITV) expansions to compensate for cardiorespiratory motion. Current clinical r4DCT imaging methods are limited, and the reconstructed r4DCTs suffer from unmanaged cardiac motion artifacts that affect the quantitative assessment of respiratory motion. A groupwise surface-to-surface deformable image registration (DIR) algorithm, named gCGF, was developed. A novel principal component filtering (PCF) mechanism and a spatial smoothing mechanism were developed and incorporated into gCGF to iteratively register heart contours from an average respiratory-phase CT to ten r4DCT phases while removing random cardiac motion from the cyclic respiratory motion. The performance of the groupwise DIR was quantitatively validated using 8 digital phantoms with simulated cardiac artifacts. An ablation study was conducted to compare gCGF to another comparable state-of-the-art groupwise DIR method. gCGF was applied to r4DCTs of 20 STAR patients to analyze the respiratory motion of the heart. Validation on digital phantoms showed that gCGF achieved a mean target registration error of 0.63+-0.51 mm while successfully achieving phase smoothness and reducing cardiac motion artifacts. Among all STAR patients, the heart's maximum and mean respiratory motion magnitudes ranged from 3.6 to 7.9 mm and 1.0 mm to 2.6 mm. The peak-to-peak motion range was from 6.2 to 14.7 mm. For VT targets, the max and mean motion magnitude ranges were 3.0 to 6.7 mm and 0.8 to 2.9 mm, respectively. The peak-to-peak range was from 4.7 to 11.8 mm. Significant dominance of the first principal component of the motion direction was observed (p = 0).
