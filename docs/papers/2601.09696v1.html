Empathy Applicability Modeling for General Health Queries

Published: 2026-01-14
Authors: Shan Randhawa, Agha Ali Raza, Kentaro Toyama, Julie Hui, Mustafa Naseem
Categories: cs.CL

Summary

This paper introduces the Empathy Applicability Framework (EAF) to enable anticipatory modeling of empathy needs in patient queries, a crucial aspect often lacking in LLM-driven clinical workflows. EAF classifies queries based on clinical, contextual, and linguistic cues, validated by a dual-annotated benchmark dataset that demonstrates strong classifier performance against baselines.

Medical Relevance

This research is critical for integrating empathetic communication into AI-driven healthcare, addressing a key limitation of LLMs in clinical settings. By proactively identifying empathy needs, it can significantly enhance patient satisfaction, trust, and potentially improve health outcomes in digital and asynchronous care environments.

AI Health Application

This research is directly applicable to developing AI systems (specifically LLMs) that can better understand and respond to patient needs with appropriate empathy. It aims to enable anticipatory empathy modeling for LLMs used in clinical workflows and asynchronous healthcare, thereby enhancing the quality of AI-driven patient interactions and support.

Key Points

  • Identifies the critical gap of LLMs lacking clinical empathy and current NLP frameworks being reactive rather than anticipatory in addressing patient emotional needs.
  • Proposes the Empathy Applicability Framework (EAF), a theory-driven approach to classify patient queries for the applicability of emotional reactions and interpretations.
  • The EAF classification relies on analyzing clinical, contextual, and linguistic cues present in the patient's query.
  • Releases a benchmark dataset of real patient queries, dual-annotated by human experts and GPT-4o, observing substantial human-GPT alignment in cases of human consensus.
  • Trains classifiers on both human-labeled and GPT-only annotations to predict empathy applicability, achieving strong performance.
  • The developed classifiers significantly outperform heuristic and zero-shot LLM baselines, demonstrating the effectiveness of the EAF approach.
  • Error analysis reveals persistent challenges including implicit distress, clinical-severity ambiguity, and contextual hardship in patient queries, highlighting areas for future improvement.

Methodology

The methodology involved developing the Empathy Applicability Framework (EAF) as a theory-driven classification system. A benchmark dataset of real patient queries was created and dual-annotated by human experts and GPT-4o. Classifier models were trained on both human-labeled and GPT-only annotations to predict empathy applicability, and their performance was validated against heuristic and zero-shot LLM baselines. Error analysis was conducted to identify common failure modes.

Key Findings

The EAF successfully provides a robust framework for classifying patient queries based on empathy applicability. There is substantial alignment between human and GPT-4o annotations where human consensus exists. Classifiers trained using EAF achieve strong predictive performance for empathy needs, outperforming traditional baselines. However, significant challenges persist in accurately identifying implicit distress, resolving clinical-severity ambiguity, and recognizing contextual hardship from patient query text.

Clinical Impact

This framework enables LLMs to identify empathy needs in patient queries before generating responses, leading to more empathetic, context-aware, and clinically appropriate AI interactions. It is particularly impactful for asynchronous healthcare settings, allowing for more nuanced and caring communication even without immediate human intervention, thereby improving the quality of digital health interactions and supporting clinicians.

Limitations

['Persistent challenges in identifying implicit distress within patient queries.', 'Ambiguity in discerning clinical severity solely from query text.', 'Difficulty in recognizing and accounting for contextual hardship indicated in patient statements.', 'Implicit need for multi-annotator modeling and culturally diverse annotation suggests limitations in current annotation breadth or depth.']

Future Directions

['Implementing multi-annotator modeling to capture a broader range of human interpretations and nuances.', 'Integrating clinician-in-the-loop calibration processes to continuously refine and improve model accuracy and clinical relevance.', "Expanding annotation efforts to include culturally diverse populations to ensure the framework's applicability and fairness across different demographics and backgrounds."]

Abstract

LLMs are increasingly being integrated into clinical workflows, yet they often lack clinical empathy, an essential aspect of effective doctor-patient communication. Existing NLP frameworks focus on reactively labeling empathy in doctors' responses but offer limited support for anticipatory modeling of empathy needs, especially in general health queries. We introduce the Empathy Applicability Framework (EAF), a theory-driven approach that classifies patient queries in terms of the applicability of emotional reactions and interpretations, based on clinical, contextual, and linguistic cues. We release a benchmark of real patient queries, dual-annotated by Humans and GPT-4o. In the subset with human consensus, we also observe substantial human-GPT alignment. To validate EAF, we train classifiers on human-labeled and GPT-only annotations to predict empathy applicability, achieving strong performance and outperforming the heuristic and zero-shot LLM baselines. Error analysis highlights persistent challenges: implicit distress, clinical-severity ambiguity, and contextual hardship, underscoring the need for multi-annotator modeling, clinician-in-the-loop calibration, and culturally diverse annotation. EAF provides a framework for identifying empathy needs before response generation, establishes a benchmark for anticipatory empathy modeling, and enables supporting empathetic communication in asynchronous healthcare.
