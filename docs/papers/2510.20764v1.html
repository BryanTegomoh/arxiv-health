Summary

This paper identifies an "Acoustic Emission (AE) cascade" as a reliable predictor of impending failure in quasi-brittle materials like rock and bone. By monitoring the inverse AE energy rate, the study demonstrates that this cascade provides a crucial warning period before material fracture, with significant implications for preventing patient injury in medical settings.

Medical Relevance

This research offers a novel method to predict bone failure, specifically skull fracture, during medical procedures such as neurosurgical head immobilization. The predictive warning could enable timely intervention, significantly enhancing patient safety and preventing severe complications.

AI Health Application

The identified Acoustic Emission (AE) cascade provides a predictive warning of impending bone failure. This mechanism is highly suitable for the development of a real-time medical AI application. An AI system (e.g., using machine learning or deep learning) could be trained on AE data to continuously monitor stress on bone (e.g., during skull pinning in neurosurgery), detect the characteristic cascade pattern, and issue automated, timely alerts to surgical staff. This would enable proactive intervention to prevent catastrophic bone fracture and patient injury during surgical procedures.

Key Points

  • Quasi-brittle materials such as rock and bone fail through microcrack coalescence, which is accompanied by an increasing Acoustic Emission (AE) event rate.
  • The study investigated AE rates in granite beams under three-point bending and human cadaver skulls subjected to neurosurgical-like pinning loads.
  • A consistent "quasi-linear cascade of the inverse AE energy rate" was identified in both materials leading up to failure.
  • This AE cascade is attributed to a rate-dependent material failure law involving a power-law coupling between the response rate, a driver, and the accumulated response.
  • For granite beams, the cascade allowed for accurate prediction of failure time over the final 30% of their lifespan (ranging from 35 seconds to two days).
  • In human cadaver skulls, the commencement of the failure cascade provided a warning period of 30-70 seconds before actual fracture.
  • This warning time is deemed sufficient for basic remedial action to avoid patient injury during head immobilization in neurosurgical procedures.

Methodology

The study employed experimental setups for two quasi-brittle materials. For rock, granite beams were subjected to fixed loading under three-point bending. For bone, human cadaver skulls were loaded with pinning forces mimicking those used for head immobilization in neurosurgical procedures. In both cases, Acoustic Emission (AE) event rates were continuously monitored to track the progression to failure.

Key Findings

The primary discovery is an "Acoustic Emission (AE) cascade" – a quasi-linear increase in the inverse AE energy rate – that consistently precedes failure in both rock and bone. This cascade is linked to a specific rate-dependent material failure law and serves as a robust predictor of impending failure, offering a 30-70 second warning for skull fracture.

Clinical Impact

The ability to predict skull fracture 30-70 seconds in advance during neurosurgical head immobilization procedures is a significant clinical breakthrough. This warning period is sufficient for surgeons to take immediate remedial action, thereby preventing potential skull fractures, pin site complications, and associated patient injuries, greatly improving surgical safety.

Abstract

Quasi brittle materials such as rock and bone are understood to fail via microcrack coalescence. The accompanying Acoustic Emission (AE) event rate is known to increase as failure progresses. Here we examine the progression of the AE event rate for both rock and bone under conditions where failure progresses under fixed loading. The experiments for rock entail subjecting granite beams to a fixed loading under three point bending and monitoring the time-dependent failure. For bone, human cadaver skulls are loaded under pinning loads similar to those used for immobilization of the head for neurosurgical procedures. AE rates are shown to be consistent with a rate dependent material failure law including a quasi-linear cascade of the inverse AE energy rate in the lead up to failure that is shown here to arise because of a coupling wherein a response rate is a power law of a driver and the driver is, in turn, a power law of the accumulated response. For both materials and experimental configurations this cascade provides warning of impending failure. For granite beams there is accurate prediction of failure time over the final 30 percent of the specimens lifetimes, which ranged from 35 seconds to two days. For skull fracture, the commencement of the failure cascade provides 30-70 seconds for warning of failure, which would be sufficient for basic remedial action to avoid patient injury in a surgical application.
