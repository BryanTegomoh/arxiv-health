Detection and Localization of Subdural Hematoma Using Deep Learning on Computed Tomography

arXiv ID: 2512.09393v1
Published: 2025-12-10
Authors: Vasiliki Stoumpou, Rohan Kumar, Bernard Burman, Diego Ojeda, Tapan Mehta, Dimitris Bertsimas
Categories: cs.CV, cs.LG
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a novel multimodal deep-learning framework that integrates structured clinical data with 3D computed tomography (CT) imaging for highly accurate detection and precise localization of subdural hematoma (SDH). The framework achieved superior diagnostic performance (AUC 0.9407) and generated interpretable, anatomically meaningful localization maps, addressing the critical need for rapid and transparent tools in neurosurgical emergencies.

Medical Relevance

Subdural hematoma is a time-sensitive neurosurgical emergency whose incidence is rising in aging populations. This system's ability to provide rapid, accurate, and spatially localized SDH detection significantly improves diagnostic efficiency and supports timely clinical intervention, directly impacting patient outcomes and resource allocation in critical care settings.

AI Health Application

This research applies multimodal deep learning (convolutional neural networks, transformers) to analyze medical imaging (CT scans) and structured clinical data for automated, accurate, and interpretable detection and localization of subdural hematoma. The AI system is designed to act as a diagnostic aid, improving triage and decision-making in radiology and emergency workflows.

Key Points

  • Developed a multimodal deep-learning framework combining structured clinical variables, a 3D convolutional neural network (CNN) on CT volumes, and a transformer-enhanced 2D segmentation model for SDH detection and localization.
  • Trained and evaluated the system on a large dataset of 25,315 head CT studies from Hartford HealthCare, with 3,774 (14.9%) confirmed SDH cases.
  • Clinical variables alone provided modest discriminatory power (AUC 0.75), whereas imaging-only models (3D CNN and 2D segmentation) achieved substantially higher AUCs (0.922 and 0.926, respectively).
  • The integrated multimodal ensemble demonstrated the best overall performance, achieving an AUC of 0.9407 (95% CI, 0.930‚Äì0.951) for SDH detection.
  • Beyond detection, the framework produces anatomically meaningful, voxel-level probability maps, offering transparent and grounded localization of SDH.
  • The system is designed to integrate multimodal clinical and imaging information to support real-time decision-making in a transparent manner.
  • Potential for integration into radiology workflows to streamline triage, reduce time to intervention, and improve consistency in SDH management.

Methodology

The study employed a multimodal deep-learning framework comprising three main components: (1) tabular models trained on structured clinical variables (demographics, comorbidities, medications, laboratory results); (2) a 3D convolutional neural network (CNN) trained on complete CT volumes for initial detection; and (3) a transformer-enhanced 2D segmentation model designed to generate voxel-level probability maps and refine localization. These individual predictors were then combined using a greedy ensemble strategy to achieve optimal performance, leveraging a large dataset of over 25,000 head CT studies.

Key Findings

Clinical variables alone offered modest performance (AUC 0.75). However, imaging-based models, including a 3D CNN (AUC 0.922) and a 2D segmentation model (AUC 0.926), showed significantly higher accuracy. The multimodal ensemble, integrating all clinical and imaging components, achieved the highest detection performance with an AUC of 0.9407 (95% CI, 0.930‚Äì0.951). Crucially, the system also generated anatomically meaningful localization maps consistent with known SDH patterns, providing interpretable outputs.

Clinical Impact

This high-performing and interpretable framework offers a robust tool for rapid and accurate SDH detection and localization, which is vital for neurosurgical emergencies. Its potential integration into radiology workflows could lead to streamlined triage, reduced time to critical medical intervention, and enhanced consistency in SDH diagnosis and management, ultimately improving patient care and outcomes.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations. However, common considerations for such AI models include the generalizability of findings to external datasets from different institutions or CT scanner types, potential biases in the training data, and the real-world computational resources required for widespread real-time deployment in diverse clinical environments.

Abstract

Background. Subdural hematoma (SDH) is a common neurosurgical emergency, with increasing incidence in aging populations. Rapid and accurate identification is essential to guide timely intervention, yet existing automated tools focus primarily on detection and provide limited interpretability or spatial localization. There remains a need for transparent, high-performing systems that integrate multimodal clinical and imaging information to support real-time decision-making. Methods. We developed a multimodal deep-learning framework that integrates structured clinical variables, a 3D convolutional neural network trained on CT volumes, and a transformer-enhanced 2D segmentation model for SDH detection and localization. Using 25,315 head CT studies from Hartford HealthCare (2015--2024), of which 3,774 (14.9\%) contained clinician-confirmed SDH, tabular models were trained on demographics, comorbidities, medications, and laboratory results. Imaging models were trained to detect SDH and generate voxel-level probability maps. A greedy ensemble strategy combined complementary predictors. Findings. Clinical variables alone provided modest discriminatory power (AUC 0.75). Convolutional models trained on CT volumes and segmentation-derived maps achieved substantially higher accuracy (AUCs 0.922 and 0.926). The multimodal ensemble integrating all components achieved the best overall performance (AUC 0.9407; 95\% CI, 0.930--0.951) and produced anatomically meaningful localization maps consistent with known SDH patterns. Interpretation. This multimodal, interpretable framework provides rapid and accurate SDH detection and localization, achieving high detection performance and offering transparent, anatomically grounded outputs. Integration into radiology workflows could streamline triage, reduce time to intervention, and improve consistency in SDH management.
