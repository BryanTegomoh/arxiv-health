Geographically-dependent individual-level models for infectious diseases transmission

arXiv ID: 1908.06822v1
Published: 2019-08-15
Authors: Md Mahsin, Rob Deardon, Patrick Brown
Categories: stat.AP, physics.soc-ph, q-bio.PE
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces Geographically-Dependent Individual-Level Models (GD-ILMs), an extension of traditional Individual-Level Models (ILMs), to explicitly account for the influence of absolute spatial location and varying risk factors on infectious disease transmission. By incorporating a conditional autoregressive model, GD-ILMs can capture unobserved spatially structured latent covariates, enabling the identification of high-risk geographical regions and formulation of etiological hypotheses for disease spread. The reliability of these new models was investigated using simulated epidemic data and real-world 2009 Alberta seasonal influenza outbreak data.

Medical Relevance

This research significantly enhances the precision and utility of infectious disease modeling by addressing geographical specificities and varying risk factors, directly supporting more effective public health policy decisions, targeted interventions, and resource allocation during outbreaks of emerging infectious diseases.

AI Health Application

The paper utilizes advanced statistical modeling (Bayesian statistical framework, MCMC methods, spatio-temporal models) to achieve its goals. While these are not typically classified as 'AI' in the narrow sense of machine learning or deep learning, they represent sophisticated computational approaches for prediction and decision support in health. These models provide foundational methodologies and insights that are crucial for developing AI-driven public health surveillance systems, outbreak prediction tools, and risk assessment platforms that inform healthcare and policy decisions.

Key Points

  • Traditional discrete-time Individual-Level Models (ILMs) for infectious diseases assume transmission probability depends solely on spatial separation, neglecting the importance of actual spatial locations.
  • The study proposes a novel class of Geographically-Dependent Individual-Level Models (GD-ILMs) that generalize ILMs to incorporate the direct effect of spatial location on disease transmission.
  • GD-ILMs utilize a conditional autoregressive (CAR) model to capture effects of unobserved spatially structured latent covariates or measurement error, enhancing model flexibility to spatial heterogeneity.
  • The models are designed to evaluate the impact of spatially varying risk factors (e.g., education, environmental conditions) on disease spread.
  • A key objective of GD-ILMs is to facilitate the formulation of etiological hypotheses and identify geographical regions exhibiting unusually high transmission risk for targeted intervention.
  • Model reliability was investigated and demonstrated using both simulated epidemic data and data from the 2009 Alberta seasonal influenza outbreak.
  • GD-ILMs are implemented and fitted within a robust Bayesian statistical framework, employing Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods for parameter estimation.

Methodology

The study generalizes existing discrete-time Individual-Level Models (ILMs) to create Geographically-Dependent ILMs (GD-ILMs) by allowing the probability of disease transmission to depend on spatial location, not just separation. Specifically, a Conditional Autoregressive (CAR) model is integrated into the framework to capture the effects of unobserved spatially structured latent covariates or measurement error. The models are fitted using a Bayesian statistical framework, employing Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods for inference. Model reliability was investigated using a combination of simulated epidemic data and real-world 2009 Alberta seasonal influenza outbreak data.

Key Findings

The study successfully developed and investigated the reliability of Geographically-Dependent Individual-Level Models (GD-ILMs), demonstrating their capacity to incorporate spatial location and associated varying risk factors into infectious disease transmission models. By integrating CAR models, GD-ILMs can effectively capture unobserved spatial structure, enabling the identification of high-risk geographical regions and supporting the formulation of etiological hypotheses for disease spread, as evidenced by their application to simulated and real-world influenza data.

Clinical Impact

The GD-ILMs provide public health agencies with a powerful tool to precisely identify specific geographical regions at unusually high risk for infectious disease transmission, facilitating the allocation of resources and implementation of targeted preventive actions (e.g., vaccination campaigns, enhanced surveillance). These models can also help clinical epidemiologists and public health researchers formulate and test etiological hypotheses regarding the spatially varying drivers of disease, leading to a deeper understanding of transmission dynamics and more effective disease control strategies.

Limitations

The abstract primarily highlights the limitations of *previous* ILMs (their inability to account for spatial location) that the new model aims to address, rather than explicitly stating limitations of the GD-ILMs themselves. It focuses on the benefits and reliability of the developed models.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated as 'future research directions,' the abstract implies that the developed GD-ILMs offer flexible tools that can be utilized for formulating etiological hypotheses in various infectious disease contexts and for identifying specific high-risk geographical regions, guiding future targeted public health interventions and preventive actions.

Abstract

Infectious disease models can be of great use for understanding the underlying mechanisms that influence the spread of diseases and predicting future disease progression. Modeling has been increasingly used to evaluate the potential impact of different control measures and to guide public health policy decisions. In recent years, there has been rapid progress in developing spatio-temporal modeling of infectious diseases and an example of such recent developments is the discrete time individual-level models (ILMs). These models are well developed and provide a common framework for modeling many disease systems, however, they assume the probability of disease transmission between two individuals depends only on their spatial separation and not on their spatial locations. In cases where spatial location itself is important for understanding the spread of emerging infectious diseases and identifying their causes, it would be beneficial to incorporate the effect of spatial location in the model. In this study, we thus generalize the ILMs to a new class of geographically-dependent ILMs (GD-ILMs), to allow for the evaluation of the effect of spatially varying risk factors (e.g., education, environmental), as well as unobserved spatial structure, upon the transmission of infectious disease. Specifically, we consider a conditional autoregressive model to capture the effects of unobserved spatially structured latent covariates or measurement error. This results in flexible infectious disease models that can be used for formulating etiological hypotheses and identifying geographical regions of unusually high risk to formulate preventive action. The reliability of these models are investigated on a combination of simulated epidemic data and Alberta seasonal influenza outbreak data (2009). This new class of models is fitted to data within a Bayesian statistical framework using MCMC methods.
