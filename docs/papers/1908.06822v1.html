Geographically-dependent individual-level models for infectious diseases transmission

arXiv ID: 1908.06822v1
Published: 2019-08-15
Authors: Md Mahsin, Rob Deardon, Patrick Brown
Categories: stat.AP, physics.soc-ph, q-bio.PE

Summary

This paper introduces Geographically-Dependent Individual-Level Models (GD-ILMs), an extension of existing discrete-time individual-level models (ILMs), to incorporate the effect of spatial location itself on infectious disease transmission, rather than just spatial separation. These models allow for the evaluation of spatially varying risk factors and unobserved spatial structure using a Conditional Autoregressive (CAR) model, aiming to formulate etiological hypotheses and identify high-risk geographical regions.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a more sophisticated tool for understanding the complex spatial dynamics of infectious diseases, which is crucial for public health agencies to develop targeted interventions, predict outbreaks, and allocate resources effectively.

AI Health Application

The research develops advanced computational models using a Bayesian statistical framework with MCMC methods to analyze complex health data for prediction and risk assessment. While the methodology is rooted in statistics and computational epidemiology rather than explicitly 'AI' in the modern machine learning sense, the application directly aligns with the goals of AI in health: providing data-driven insights for disease prediction, identifying high-risk populations/regions, and informing public health interventions. This framework could be integrated into or augment AI-driven surveillance and decision-support systems for public health.

Key Points

  • Traditional Individual-Level Models (ILMs) are generalized to Geographically-Dependent ILMs (GD-ILMs) to address the limitation that transmission probability depends only on spatial separation, not on spatial location itself.
  • GD-ILMs explicitly incorporate the effect of spatial location, allowing for the evaluation of spatially varying risk factors (e.g., education, environmental) on disease transmission.
  • The models account for unobserved spatially structured latent covariates or measurement error by integrating a Conditional Autoregressive (CAR) model.
  • The resulting GD-ILMs are flexible, enabling the formulation of etiological hypotheses regarding infectious disease spread and the identification of high-risk geographical regions.
  • Model reliability is investigated using a combination of simulated epidemic data and real-world Alberta seasonal influenza outbreak data from 2009.
  • Model fitting is performed within a Bayesian statistical framework, employing Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods.

Methodology

The study generalizes discrete-time individual-level models (ILMs) to Geographically-Dependent Individual-Level Models (GD-ILMs), allowing transmission probability to depend on spatial location. This generalization integrates the effects of observed spatially varying risk factors and unobserved spatial structure, the latter modeled using a Conditional Autoregressive (CAR) model. The models are fitted using a Bayesian statistical framework with Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods and validated on both simulated epidemic data and real-world 2009 Alberta seasonal influenza outbreak data.

Key Findings

The developed Geographically-Dependent Individual-Level Models (GD-ILMs) are found to be flexible and reliable. They successfully incorporate the effect of spatial location, account for both observed spatially varying risk factors and unobserved spatial structure, and can be effectively used for formulating etiological hypotheses and identifying high-risk geographical regions for infectious diseases.

Clinical Impact

The GD-ILMs can lead to more precise identification of disease hotspots, enabling public health officials to target preventive actions and allocate resources more efficiently. They also facilitate a deeper understanding of how geographical factors, including environmental and socioeconomic variables, influence disease spread, thus informing better policy decisions for disease control and prevention.

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations of the proposed GD-ILMs. It highlights a limitation of prior ILMs (dependency only on spatial separation).

Abstract

Infectious disease models can be of great use for understanding the underlying mechanisms that influence the spread of diseases and predicting future disease progression. Modeling has been increasingly used to evaluate the potential impact of different control measures and to guide public health policy decisions. In recent years, there has been rapid progress in developing spatio-temporal modeling of infectious diseases and an example of such recent developments is the discrete time individual-level models (ILMs). These models are well developed and provide a common framework for modeling many disease systems, however, they assume the probability of disease transmission between two individuals depends only on their spatial separation and not on their spatial locations. In cases where spatial location itself is important for understanding the spread of emerging infectious diseases and identifying their causes, it would be beneficial to incorporate the effect of spatial location in the model. In this study, we thus generalize the ILMs to a new class of geographically-dependent ILMs (GD-ILMs), to allow for the evaluation of the effect of spatially varying risk factors (e.g., education, environmental), as well as unobserved spatial structure, upon the transmission of infectious disease. Specifically, we consider a conditional autoregressive model to capture the effects of unobserved spatially structured latent covariates or measurement error. This results in flexible infectious disease models that can be used for formulating etiological hypotheses and identifying geographical regions of unusually high risk to formulate preventive action. The reliability of these models are investigated on a combination of simulated epidemic data and Alberta seasonal influenza outbreak data (2009). This new class of models is fitted to data within a Bayesian statistical framework using MCMC methods.
