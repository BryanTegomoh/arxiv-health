Geographically-dependent individual-level models for infectious diseases transmission

Published: 2019-08-15
Authors: Md Mahsin, Rob Deardon, Patrick Brown

Summary

This study introduces Geographically-Dependent Individual-Level Models (GD-ILMs), an advancement over traditional individual-level models (ILMs), to explicitly incorporate the impact of spatial location on infectious disease transmission. By integrating a conditional autoregressive model, GD-ILMs allow for the evaluation of spatially varying risk factors and unobserved spatial structures, enhancing the understanding of disease spread and facilitating the identification of high-risk geographical regions for targeted interventions.

Medical Relevance

This research significantly enhances infectious disease modeling by providing a robust framework to identify geographical hotspots and spatially varying risk factors, which is crucial for targeted public health interventions, resource allocation, and a deeper understanding of disease etiology and epidemiology.

  • Traditional discrete time individual-level models (ILMs) are limited as they assume disease transmission probability depends only on spatial separation, not on actual spatial location.
  • The study proposes Geographically-Dependent ILMs (GD-ILMs) which generalize ILMs to account for the direct effect of spatial location on disease transmission.
  • GD-ILMs incorporate a conditional autoregressive (CAR) model to capture the effects of unobserved spatially structured latent covariates or measurement error, making them flexible.
  • The models are designed to evaluate the impact of spatially varying risk factors (e.g., education, environmental) and unobserved spatial structure upon disease transmission.

  • The models are fitted using a Bayesian statistical framework, employing Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods for parameter estimation.

Methodology

The study generalizes discrete time individual-level models (ILMs) to a new class called Geographically-Dependent ILMs (GD-ILMs). This is achieved by incorporating a conditional autoregressive (CAR) model to explicitly account for location-specific effects, unobserved spatially structured latent covariates, or measurement error. The models are then fitted to data within a Bayesian statistical framework using Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) methods for inference and parameter estimation.

Key Findings

The reliability and efficacy of the proposed Geographically-Dependent Individual-Level Models (GD-ILMs) were successfully investigated and demonstrated. This validation was performed using a combination of simulated epidemic data and real-world Alberta seasonal influenza outbreak data from 2009, suggesting that these models are robust and capable of accurately identifying spatially varying transmission dynamics and high-risk geographical regions.

Abstract

Infectious disease models can be of great use for understanding the underlying mechanisms that influence the spread of diseases and predicting future disease progression. Modeling has been increasingly used to evaluate the potential impact of different control measures and to guide public health policy decisions. In recent years, there has been rapid progress in developing spatio-temporal modeling of infectious diseases and an example of such recent developments is the discrete time individual-level models (ILMs). These models are well developed and provide a common framework for modeling many disease systems, however, they assume the probability of disease transmission between two individuals depends only on their spatial separation and not on their spatial locations. In cases where spatial location itself is important for understanding the spread of emerging infectious diseases and identifying their causes, it would be beneficial to incorporate the effect of spatial location in the model. In this study, we thus generalize the ILMs to a new class of geographically-dependent ILMs (GD-ILMs), to allow for the evaluation of the effect of spatially varying risk factors (e.g., education, environmental), as well as unobserved spatial structure, upon the transmission of infectious disease. Specifically, we consider a conditional autoregressive model to capture the effects of unobserved spatially structured latent covariates or measurement error. This results in flexible infectious disease models that can be used for formulating etiological hypotheses and identifying geographical regions of unusually high risk to formulate preventive action. The reliability of these models are investigated on a combination of simulated epidemic data and Alberta seasonal influenza outbreak data (2009). This new class of models is fitted to data within a Bayesian statistical framework using MCMC methods.
