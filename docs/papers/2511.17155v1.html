UI-Styler: Ultrasound Image Style Transfer with Class-Aware Prompts for Cross-Device Diagnosis Using a Frozen Black-Box Inference Network

arXiv ID: 2511.17155v1
Published: 2025-11-21
Authors: Nhat-Tuong Do-Tran, Ngoc-Hoang-Lam Le, Ching-Chun Huang
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces UI-Styler, a novel ultrasound image style transfer framework designed to address domain shifts caused by variations in acquisition devices. By leveraging a pattern-matching mechanism and a class-aware prompting strategy guided by pseudo labels, UI-Styler effectively aligns statistical distributions and enforces accurate semantic content alignment, enabling robust cross-device diagnosis using pre-trained black-box inference models. The method achieves state-of-the-art performance in distribution distance and improves downstream tasks like classification and segmentation.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medical imaging by enabling the robust and reliable reuse of powerful AI diagnostic models across diverse clinical settings, regardless of the specific ultrasound device used for image acquisition. It directly addresses a critical barrier to AI adoption in ultrasound, improving diagnostic consistency and efficiency.

AI Health Application

The AI application described is enhancing the reliability and accuracy of AI models (inference networks) used for medical diagnosis from ultrasound images. Specifically, it enables these models to perform consistently across different ultrasound devices, mitigating performance issues caused by domain shifts. This directly supports the development of more robust and deployable AI tools for clinical diagnostic tasks like disease classification and anatomical segmentation.

Key Points

  • **Problem Addressed:** Appearance variations in ultrasound images across different acquisition devices create domain shifts, degrading the performance of fixed black-box downstream inference models when applied to data from new devices.
  • **Limitation of Existing UIT:** Current unpaired image translation (UIT) methods often lack class-specific semantic alignment during domain adaptation, leading to misaligned content-class mappings and reduced diagnostic accuracy.
  • **Proposed Solution (UI-Styler):** UI-Styler is an ultrasound-specific, class-aware image style transfer framework designed to perform domain adaptation while preserving diagnostic information.
  • **Methodology - Texture Transfer:** It employs a pattern-matching mechanism to transfer texture patterns from target domain images onto source images, meticulously preserving the original source image's structural content.
  • **Methodology - Semantic Alignment:** A novel class-aware prompting strategy, utilizing pseudo labels from the target domain, is introduced to enforce accurate semantic alignment with specific diagnostic categories.
  • **Performance Improvement:** UI-Styler consistently outperforms existing UIT methods across various ultrasound cross-device tasks, demonstrating superior performance in reducing distribution distance between domains.
  • **Downstream Task Benefits:** The enhanced domain adaptation translates to state-of-the-art results in critical downstream diagnostic tasks, specifically classification and segmentation, thereby improving the reusability of frozen black-box models.

Methodology

The UI-Styler framework utilizes an unpaired image translation approach incorporating a pattern-matching mechanism for texture transfer and a class-aware prompting strategy. The texture transfer component ensures that target domain's visual style is adopted while preserving the source's anatomical structure. The class-aware prompting, guided by pseudo-labels generated from the target domain, explicitly enforces semantic alignment of transferred images with diagnostic categories, crucial for maintaining diagnostic accuracy for a frozen black-box inference network.

Key Findings

UI-Styler significantly improves upon existing unpaired image translation methods by achieving superior performance in aligning statistical distributions across ultrasound domains. This leads to state-of-the-art results in downstream tasks such as medical image classification and segmentation when using models trained on one device and applied to data from another, overcoming the challenge of device-induced domain shifts.

Clinicians could deploy sophisticated AI models without needing extensive retraining or re-annotation for every new ultrasound device, saving time and resources. This would lead to more consistent, accurate, and rapid diagnoses across different hospitals or clinics, ultimately improving patient care by making advanced diagnostic tools more accessible and reliable.

Abstract

The appearance of ultrasound images varies across acquisition devices, causing domain shifts that degrade the performance of fixed black-box downstream inference models when reused. To mitigate this issue, it is practical to develop unpaired image translation (UIT) methods that effectively align the statistical distributions between source and target domains, particularly under the constraint of a reused inference-blackbox setting. However, existing UIT approaches often overlook class-specific semantic alignment during domain adaptation, resulting in misaligned content-class mappings that can impair diagnostic accuracy. To address this limitation, we propose UI-Styler, a novel ultrasound-specific, class-aware image style transfer framework. UI-Styler leverages a pattern-matching mechanism to transfer texture patterns embedded in the target images onto source images while preserving the source structural content. In addition, we introduce a class-aware prompting strategy guided by pseudo labels of the target domain, which enforces accurate semantic alignment with diagnostic categories. Extensive experiments on ultrasound cross-device tasks demonstrate that UI-Styler consistently outperforms existing UIT methods, achieving state-of-the-art performance in distribution distance and downstream tasks, such as classification and segmentation.
