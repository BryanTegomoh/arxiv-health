Factuality and Transparency Are All RAG Needs! Self-Explaining Contrastive Evidence Re-ranking

arXiv ID: 2512.05012v1
Published: 2025-12-04
Authors: Francielle Vargas, Daniel Pedronette

Summary

This paper introduces Self-Explaining Contrastive Evidence Re-Ranking (CER), a novel method that fine-tunes retrieval embeddings using contrastive learning with subjectivity-based hard negatives to enhance factuality and transparency in RAG systems. Evaluated on clinical trial reports, CER demonstrates improved retrieval accuracy, mitigates hallucinations, and provides transparent, evidence-based retrieval crucial for safety-critical medical domains.

Medical Relevance

This research directly addresses critical challenges in applying AI, specifically RAG systems, to healthcare by ensuring factual accuracy and transparency. By mitigating hallucinations and providing evidential traceability in clinical trial data analysis, CER enhances the trustworthiness of AI-assisted medical decision-making, which is paramount for patient safety.

AI Health Application

This research contributes to making AI-powered information retrieval and generation (RAG) systems more reliable, factual, and transparent when applied to medical documents like clinical trial reports. This can be used to assist clinicians, researchers, and pharmaceutical companies in extracting accurate, evidence-based information, making informed decisions, identifying potential adverse events, and reducing the risk of generating misleading or incorrect medical information (hallucinations) from AI models.

Key Points

  • CER is a novel method for re-ranking retrieved evidence, specifically designed to align with evidential reasoning.
  • It fine-tunes embeddings through contrastive learning, utilizing automatically selected hard negatives based on a subjectivity criterion.
  • The method generates token-level attribution rationales for each retrieved passage, enhancing transparency.
  • This approach creates an embedding space where factual rationales are pulled closer and subjective/misleading explanations are pushed apart.
  • Evaluation was conducted using data from clinical trial reports.
  • Results show improved retrieval accuracy and a reduction in the potential for hallucinations in RAG systems.
  • CER provides transparent, evidence-based retrieval, significantly enhancing reliability in safety-critical domains like medicine.

Methodology

Self-Explaining Contrastive Evidence Re-Ranking (CER) restructures retrieval by fine-tuning contextual embeddings using contrastive learning. The core mechanism involves automatically selecting hard negative examples based on a subjectivity-based criterion. This process forces the model to learn an embedding space where factual and evidential rationales are semantically proximate, while subjective or misleading explanations are pushed further apart. Additionally, CER generates token-level attribution rationales for each retrieved passage, offering granular transparency into why specific evidence is deemed relevant.

Key Findings

Initial experimental results showed that CER: 1) improved retrieval accuracy, 2) mitigated the potential for hallucinations in RAG systems, and 3) provided transparent, evidence-based retrieval.

Clinical Impact

CER's ability to ensure factual accuracy, provide transparent rationales, and reduce AI hallucinations has profound implications for clinical practice and medical research. It can significantly enhance the reliability of AI systems used in tasks such as synthesizing information from vast clinical trial literature for treatment guidelines, drug safety analysis, or personalized medicine, where the consequences of misinformation are severe. This increased trustworthiness facilitates broader adoption of AI in safety-critical medical applications.

Abstract

This extended abstract introduces Self-Explaining Contrastive Evidence Re-Ranking (CER), a novel method that restructures retrieval around factual evidence by fine-tuning embeddings with contrastive learning and generating token-level attribution rationales for each retrieved passage. Hard negatives are automatically selected using a subjectivity-based criterion, forcing the model to pull factual rationales closer while pushing subjective or misleading explanations apart. As a result, the method creates an embedding space explicitly aligned with evidential reasoning. We evaluated our method on clinical trial reports, and initial experimental results show that CER improves retrieval accuracy, mitigates the potential for hallucinations in RAG systems, and provides transparent, evidence-based retrieval that enhances reliability, especially in safety-critical domains.
