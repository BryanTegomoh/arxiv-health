Summary

This paper provides a comprehensive survey of deep learning-based super-resolution (SR) techniques applied to Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI). It systematically reviews recent advancements, proposing a taxonomy for these methods while examining their theoretical foundations, architectural designs, and learning strategies to overcome the technical and cost constraints of acquiring high-resolution MRI. The ultimate goal is to improve diagnostic accuracy and efficiency without requiring additional hardware.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medicine as it seeks to computationally enhance MRI quality, potentially improving diagnostic accuracy, enabling earlier disease detection, and increasing the efficiency of MRI scanning without additional hardware costs, thereby making high-resolution imaging more accessible and effective for patient care.

AI Health Application

The AI application is to enhance the resolution of medical MRI images using deep learning algorithms. This aims to improve diagnostic accuracy and efficiency by generating high-resolution images from more affordable low-resolution scans, thereby potentially making advanced medical imaging more accessible and cost-effective within healthcare systems.

Methodology

The paper employs a comprehensive survey methodology, systematically reviewing and synthesizing a broad spectrum of existing literature on deep learning-based MRI super-resolution. It analyzes these methods from various conceptual viewpoints (computer vision, computational imaging, inverse problems, MR physics), proposes a novel categorization taxonomy, and identifies key challenges and future directions based on this extensive literature review and analysis.

Key Findings

The survey's key findings include a detailed synthesis of the current state-of-the-art in DL-based MRI SR techniques, the establishment of a systematic taxonomy for categorizing diverse methodologies, and a thorough identification of unique challenges within clinical and research contexts. It also synthesizes the theoretical underpinnings, architectural designs, learning strategies, and evaluation metrics commonly employed across the field, providing a foundational understanding and roadmap for future work.

Clinical Impact

The successful development and clinical integration of deep learning MRI SR could significantly enhance diagnostic capabilities by providing higher-resolution images from standard or even lower-resolution scans. This could lead to earlier and more accurate disease detection (e.g., small lesions, subtle anatomical changes), better planning for surgical interventions, more precise monitoring of disease progression, and optimization of scanner time and resources, ultimately improving patient outcomes and reducing healthcare costs.

Limitations

The survey implicitly highlights current limitations of the MRI SR field itself, such as the need for improved generalization and robustness of DL models across diverse patient populations, varying scanner hardware, and different acquisition protocols. Other limitations include addressing motion artifacts, maintaining computational efficiency for real-time applications, and robustly integrating MR physics knowledge to ensure clinical fidelity and interpretability of super-resolved images.

Future Directions

Suggested future directions emphasize addressing the identified open challenges, including the development of more robust and generalizable DL architectures for varied clinical scenarios, deeper integration of MR physics priors into SR models, exploration of novel learning strategies (e.g., unsupervised, few-shot learning), and the creation of standardized, large-scale benchmark datasets and rigorous evaluation metrics to facilitate comparable progress within the field.

Abstract

High-resolution (HR) magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) is crucial for many clinical and research applications. However, achieving it remains costly and constrained by technical trade-offs and experimental limitations. Super-resolution (SR) presents a promising computational approach to overcome these challenges by generating HR images from more affordable low-resolution (LR) scans, potentially improving diagnostic accuracy and efficiency without requiring additional hardware. This survey reviews recent advances in MRI SR techniques, with a focus on deep learning (DL) approaches. It examines DL-based MRI SR methods from the perspectives of computer vision, computational imaging, inverse problems, and MR physics, covering theoretical foundations, architectural designs, learning strategies, benchmark datasets, and performance metrics. We propose a systematic taxonomy to categorize these methods and present an in-depth study of both established and emerging SR techniques applicable to MRI, considering unique challenges in clinical and research contexts. We also highlight open challenges and directions that the community needs to address. Additionally, we provide a collection of essential open-access resources, tools, and tutorials, available on our GitHub: https://github.com/mkhateri/Awesome-MRI-Super-Resolution.
