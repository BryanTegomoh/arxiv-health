Balanced Few-Shot Episodic Learning for Accurate Retinal Disease Diagnosis

arXiv ID: 2512.04967v1
Published: 2025-12-04
Authors: Jasmaine Khale, Ravi Prakash Srivastava
Categories: cs.CV, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper proposes a balanced few-shot episodic learning framework for accurate retinal disease diagnosis, addressing challenges of data scarcity and class imbalance inherent in medical imaging datasets. By integrating balanced sampling, targeted augmentation (including CLAHE), and a ResNet-50 encoder, the method significantly improves diagnostic accuracy and reduces bias towards majority classes, particularly benefiting underrepresented retinal conditions on the RFMiD dataset.

Medical Relevance

Automated retinal disease diagnosis is vital for managing widespread conditions like diabetic retinopathy and macular degeneration. This research provides a method to develop reliable AI diagnostic tools even when extensive, balanced datasets are unavailable, improving diagnostic accessibility and fairness in ophthalmology.

AI Health Application

The research proposes a balanced few-shot episodic learning framework to enable accurate and fair automated diagnosis of retinal diseases from fundus images, even with limited and imbalanced medical data. This application aims to assist clinicians in identifying conditions like diabetic retinopathy, macular degeneration, optic disc edema, and branch retinal vein occlusion, thereby improving healthcare delivery and patient outcomes, especially in settings with limited specialist access or data.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical need for automated retinal disease diagnosis by overcoming limitations of conventional deep learning, namely large data requirements and class imbalance.
  • Introduces a balanced few-shot episodic learning framework specifically tailored for the Retinal Fundus Multi-Disease Image Dataset (RFMiD), focusing on its ten most represented yet imbalanced classes.
  • Incorporates three key methodological components: (i) balanced episodic sampling to ensure equal participation of all classes in 5-way 5-shot episodes; (ii) targeted augmentation using CLAHE and other transformations to enhance minority-class diversity; and (iii) a pre-trained ResNet-50 encoder for robust feature extraction.
  • Utilizes prototype computation in the embedding space with cosine similarity for stable and effective classification.
  • Demonstrates substantial accuracy gains and a significant reduction in bias towards majority disease classes.
  • Achieves notable diagnostic improvements specifically for underrepresented retinal diseases (e.g., Optic Disc Edema, Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion).
  • Concludes that dataset-aware few-shot pipelines, combined with balanced sampling and CLAHE-enhanced preprocessing, can deliver more robust and clinically fair retinal disease diagnosis under data-constrained conditions.

Methodology

The study proposes a balanced few-shot episodic learning framework. It samples 5-way 5-shot episodes with balanced class participation. Targeted augmentation, including Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE) and color/geometry transformations, is applied to minority classes. A ResNet-50 encoder, pre-trained on ImageNet, extracts features. Prototypes are computed in the embedding space, and classification is performed using cosine similarity. The framework was trained on 100 episodes and evaluated on 1,000 test episodes using the RFMiD dataset.

Key Findings

The framework achieved substantial accuracy gains and effectively reduced diagnostic bias toward majority classes, demonstrating notable improvements in identifying underrepresented retinal diseases. The combination of balanced sampling, CLAHE-enhanced preprocessing, and a dataset-aware few-shot pipeline proved crucial for robust and clinically fair diagnosis under limited data conditions.

Clinical Impact

This research offers a significant step towards developing more reliable and equitable AI diagnostic tools for retinal diseases, particularly where data for rare or less common conditions is scarce. It can facilitate earlier and more accurate diagnosis for a broader spectrum of patients, potentially reducing diagnostic disparities and improving overall patient outcomes in ophthalmology, especially in resource-limited or specialized clinical settings.

Abstract

Automated retinal disease diagnosis is vital given the rising prevalence of conditions such as diabetic retinopathy and macular degeneration. Conventional deep learning approaches require large annotated datasets, which are costly and often imbalanced across disease categories, limiting their reliability in practice. Few-shot learning (FSL) addresses this challenge by enabling models to generalize from only a few labeled samples per class. In this study,we propose a balanced few-shot episodic learning framework tailored to the Retinal Fundus Multi-Disease Image Dataset (RFMiD). Focusing on the ten most represented classes, which still show substantial imbalance between majority diseases (e.g., Diabetic Retinopathy, Macular Hole) and minority ones (e.g., Optic Disc Edema, Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion), our method integrates three key components: (i) balanced episodic sampling, ensuring equal participation of all classes in each 5-way 5-shot episode; (ii) targeted augmentation, including Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE) and color/geometry transformations, to improve minority-class di- versity; and (iii) a ResNet-50 encoder pretrained on ImageNet, selected for its superior ability to capture fine-grained retinal features. Prototypes are computed in the embedding space and classification is performed with cosine similarity for improved stability. Trained on 100 episodes and evaluated on 1,000 test episodes, our framework achieves substantial accuracy gains and reduces bias toward majority classes, with notable improvements for underrepresented diseases. These results demonstrate that dataset-aware few-shot pipelines, combined with balanced sampling and CLAHE-enhanced preprocessing, can deliver more robust and clinically fair retinal disease diagnosis under data-constrained conditions.
