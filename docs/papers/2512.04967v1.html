Balanced Few-Shot Episodic Learning for Accurate Retinal Disease Diagnosis

arXiv ID: 2512.04967v1
Published: 2025-12-04
Authors: Jasmaine Khale, Ravi Prakash Srivastava
Categories: cs.CV, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper proposes a balanced few-shot episodic learning framework for accurate retinal disease diagnosis, specifically tackling the challenges of data scarcity and class imbalance in medical datasets. By integrating balanced sampling, targeted augmentation (including CLAHE), and an ImageNet-pretrained ResNet-50 encoder, the method significantly enhances diagnostic accuracy and reduces bias toward majority classes, notably improving the detection of underrepresented retinal diseases.

Medical Relevance

Automated diagnosis of retinal diseases like diabetic retinopathy and macular degeneration is crucial for preventing vision loss. This research offers a robust solution for deploying AI diagnostic tools even when large, balanced medical datasets are unavailable, making advanced diagnosis more accessible and reliable, particularly benefiting less common conditions often overlooked by conventional models.

AI Health Application

The AI application is an automated diagnostic system for retinal diseases using fundus images. It aims to accurately identify various conditions, including common and rare ones, by generalizing from limited data samples per disease, thereby enhancing diagnostic capabilities and clinical fairness in ophthalmology.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical problem of imbalanced and limited annotated data for automated retinal disease diagnosis using Few-Shot Learning (FSL).
  • Introduces a novel balanced few-shot episodic learning framework tailored for the Retinal Fundus Multi-Disease Image Dataset (RFMiD), focusing on 10 inherently imbalanced disease classes.
  • Implements a tripartite methodology: (i) balanced episodic sampling to ensure equal class representation in 5-way 5-shot episodes, (ii) targeted augmentation (CLAHE and color/geometry transforms) specifically for minority classes to enhance diversity, and (iii) an ImageNet-pretrained ResNet-50 encoder for fine-grained feature extraction.
  • Utilizes prototype-based classification in the embedding space, employing cosine similarity for improved stability in decision-making.
  • Achieves substantial accuracy gains and effectively mitigates diagnostic bias toward majority disease categories.
  • Demonstrates notable improvements in the diagnosis of underrepresented retinal diseases such as Optic Disc Edema and Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion.
  • Concludes that dataset-aware FSL pipelines, combined with balanced sampling and CLAHE-enhanced preprocessing, can deliver more robust and clinically fair retinal disease diagnosis under data-constrained conditions.

Methodology

The study proposes a balanced few-shot episodic learning framework applied to the Retinal Fundus Multi-Disease Image Dataset (RFMiD), focusing on its ten most represented but still significantly imbalanced classes. The core methodology involves three components: (i) **Balanced Episodic Sampling** to ensure equal participation of all classes within each 5-way 5-shot learning episode. (ii) **Targeted Augmentation** specifically for minority classes, incorporating Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE) along with standard color and geometry transformations to improve data diversity. (iii) A **ResNet-50 encoder**, pre-trained on ImageNet, is used for robust feature extraction of fine-grained retinal details. Classification is performed by computing prototypes in the embedding space and using cosine similarity for stability. The framework was trained on 100 episodes and evaluated on 1,000 test episodes.

Key Findings

The framework achieved substantial accuracy gains in the automated diagnosis of retinal diseases. A significant finding was the successful reduction of bias toward majority disease classes, resulting in notable improvements in diagnostic performance for underrepresented conditions, specifically citing diseases like Optic Disc Edema and Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion. These results highlight the efficacy of combining dataset-aware few-shot learning with balanced sampling and CLAHE-enhanced preprocessing for robust and clinically fair diagnoses in data-scarce environments.

By enabling reliable AI diagnosis with fewer labeled examples, it can facilitate earlier detection and intervention, particularly in settings with constrained resources or specialized conditions, thus leading to better patient outcomes and more equitable healthcare access.

Abstract

Automated retinal disease diagnosis is vital given the rising prevalence of conditions such as diabetic retinopathy and macular degeneration. Conventional deep learning approaches require large annotated datasets, which are costly and often imbalanced across disease categories, limiting their reliability in practice. Few-shot learning (FSL) addresses this challenge by enabling models to generalize from only a few labeled samples per class. In this study,we propose a balanced few-shot episodic learning framework tailored to the Retinal Fundus Multi-Disease Image Dataset (RFMiD). Focusing on the ten most represented classes, which still show substantial imbalance between majority diseases (e.g., Diabetic Retinopathy, Macular Hole) and minority ones (e.g., Optic Disc Edema, Branch Retinal Vein Occlusion), our method integrates three key components: (i) balanced episodic sampling, ensuring equal participation of all classes in each 5-way 5-shot episode; (ii) targeted augmentation, including Contrast Limited Adaptive Histogram Equalization (CLAHE) and color/geometry transformations, to improve minority-class di- versity; and (iii) a ResNet-50 encoder pretrained on ImageNet, selected for its superior ability to capture fine-grained retinal features. Prototypes are computed in the embedding space and classification is performed with cosine similarity for improved stability. Trained on 100 episodes and evaluated on 1,000 test episodes, our framework achieves substantial accuracy gains and reduces bias toward majority classes, with notable improvements for underrepresented diseases. These results demonstrate that dataset-aware few-shot pipelines, combined with balanced sampling and CLAHE-enhanced preprocessing, can deliver more robust and clinically fair retinal disease diagnosis under data-constrained conditions.
