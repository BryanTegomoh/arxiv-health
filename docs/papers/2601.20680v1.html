Summary

This paper addresses the scalability limitations of batch clustering algorithms like HDBSCAN for real-time social media narrative monitoring by proposing a replacement with online (streaming/incremental) density-based clustering methods. It outlines a three-stage system architecture and a comprehensive evaluation framework, balancing traditional and narrative-specific metrics, to assess online algorithms across critical dimensions like cluster quality, efficiency, and memory footprint using sliding-window simulations. The research aims to bridge the gap between static topic modeling and the dynamic nature of social media data, with broad implications for real-time information analysis.

Medical Relevance

Real-time monitoring of evolving narratives is critical in medicine for early detection of health misinformation (infodemics), tracking public sentiment towards vaccines or treatments, identifying emerging public health concerns, and monitoring adverse drug events reported on social platforms, all of which can inform rapid public health responses and communication strategies.

AI Health Application

This AI system uses online density-based clustering to process continuous social media data streams for real-time monitoring of evolving narratives. In a health context, this AI could be deployed for public health surveillance to detect early signals of disease outbreaks, track the spread and evolution of health-related misinformation (e.g., vaccine hesitancy, unproven treatments), monitor public sentiment regarding health policies or interventions, and support rapid response in public health emergencies. For biosecurity, it could serve as an intelligence tool to identify unusual health-related discussions, potential threats, or disinformation campaigns that might indicate biological hazards or undermine biosecurity measures.

Key Points

  • Traditional batch clustering algorithms (e.g., HDBSCAN) are inefficient and non-scalable for continuous social media data streams due to complete retraining requirements, memory constraints, and inability to adapt to evolving narratives in real-time.
  • The proposed solution involves replacing offline HDBSCAN with online (streaming/incremental) density-based clustering methods to enable real-time narrative evolution monitoring.
  • The system employs a three-stage architecture: data collection of multilingual social media documents, online clustering/modeling, and dashboard generation, designed to process thousands of documents daily.
  • Evaluation criteria are comprehensive, combining traditional clustering metrics (Silhouette Coefficient, Davies-Bouldin Index) with novel narrative-specific metrics (narrative distinctness, contingency, variance).
  • Methodology includes sliding-window simulations on historical datasets (from the Ukraine information space) to provide a comparative analysis of algorithmic trade-offs in realistic operational contexts.
  • The research addresses a critical gap between batch-oriented topic modeling frameworks and the inherent streaming nature of social media monitoring.
  • It has implications for improving real-time information processing in areas like computational social science, crisis informatics, and narrative surveillance systems.

Methodology

The proposed system utilizes a three-stage architecture comprising data collection from multilingual social media, real-time modeling via online density-based clustering algorithms, and dashboard generation for narrative visualization. The methodology focuses on evaluating various online clustering algorithms through sliding-window simulations on historical datasets, using a balanced set of traditional clustering metrics (Silhouette, Davies-Bouldin) and novel narrative-specific metrics (distinctness, contingency, variance) to assess performance.

Key Findings

The paper identifies a critical architectural gap where existing batch-oriented topic modeling frameworks (like HDBSCAN) are ill-suited for the dynamic, streaming nature of social media data, leading to significant scalability and real-time adaptation issues for narrative monitoring. It proposes a robust framework for the comprehensive evaluation of online clustering algorithms, integrating both established quality measures and new narrative-centric metrics to address these challenges in a production pipeline.

Clinical Impact

This technology could significantly enhance the capabilities of public health agencies and healthcare organizations by enabling the real-time detection and analysis of health-related narratives on social media. This would allow for rapid identification of emerging health threats, misinformation campaigns, or shifts in public perception regarding health interventions, facilitating timely and targeted public health communication, interventions, and policy adjustments.

Limitations

The primary limitations highlighted are those of traditional batch-oriented clustering algorithms like HDBSCAN, which suffer from a batch-only nature requiring complete retraining for each time window, leading to memory constraints, computational inefficiency, and an inability to adapt to the real-time evolution of narratives in continuous data streams.

Future Directions

The research implies future applications in improving real-time information processing for public health surveillance, crisis communication, and understanding the spread of health-related misinformation (infodemics). Further work would likely involve the deployment and rigorous real-world validation of the most effective online clustering algorithms in diverse operational contexts, particularly within health and crisis informatics for actionable insights.

Abstract

Automated narrative intelligence systems for social media monitoring face significant scalability challenges when processing continuous data streams using traditional batch clustering algorithms. We investigate the replacement of HDBSCAN (offline clustering) with online (streaming/incremental) clustering methods in a production narrative report generation pipeline. The proposed system employs a three-stage architecture (data collection, modeling, dashboard generation) that processes thousands of multilingual social media documents daily. While HDBSCAN excels at discovering hierarchical density-based clusters and handling noise, its batch-only nature necessitates complete retraining for each time window, resulting in memory constraints, computational inefficiency, and inability to adapt to evolving narratives in real-time. This work evaluates a bunch of online clustering algorithms across dimensions of cluster quality preservation, computational efficiency, memory footprint, and integration compatibility with existing workflows. We propose evaluation criteria that balance traditional clustering metrics (Silhouette Coefficient, Davies-Bouldin Index) with narrative metrics (narrative distinctness, contingency and variance). Our methodology includes sliding-window simulations on historical datasets from Ukraine information space, enabling comparative analysis of algorithmic trade-offs in realistic operational contexts. This research addresses a critical gap between batch-oriented topic modeling frameworks and the streaming nature of social media monitoring, with implications for computational social science, crisis informatics, and narrative surveillance systems.
