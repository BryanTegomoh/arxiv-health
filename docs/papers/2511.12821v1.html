BioMedJImpact: A Comprehensive Dataset and LLM Pipeline for AI Engagement and Scientific Impact Analysis of Biomedical Journals

arXiv ID: 2511.12821v1
Published: 2025-11-16
Authors: Ruiyu Wang, Yuzhang Xie, Xiao Hu, Carl Yang, Jiaying Lu
Categories: cs.CL
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces BioMedJImpact, a large-scale biomedical dataset comprising 1.74 million PubMed Central articles to analyze scientific impact and AI engagement of 2,744 journals. It proposes a reproducible three-stage LLM pipeline to derive AI engagement features and uses the dataset to reveal consistent trends: higher collaboration intensity leads to greater citation impact, and AI engagement is an increasingly strong correlate of journal prestige.

Medical Relevance

Understanding how collaboration and AI engagement drive journal impact is crucial for identifying influential research trends and venues in biomedicine, guiding researchers, funding bodies, and policymakers in resource allocation and strategic planning for medical innovation.

AI Health Application

The AI (LLM) pipeline developed in this research is applied to a large dataset of biomedical literature to identify, classify, and quantify 'AI engagement' within those publications. This enables a content-aware scientometric analysis of how AI research is emerging and influencing scientific impact and innovation dynamics within the medical and health fields. It serves as an AI tool for meta-analysis and trend detection of AI's presence and impact in the health/biomedical scientific communication ecosystem.

Key Points

  • BioMedJImpact is a novel, large-scale biomedical dataset integrating bibliometric indicators, collaboration features, and LLM-derived semantic AI engagement indicators for 2,744 journals from 1.74 million PubMed Central articles.
  • A reproducible three-stage Large Language Model (LLM) pipeline is proposed and validated for extracting AI engagement features from scientific articles, classifying AI relevance and subfield.
  • The study analyzes the joint influence of collaboration intensity and AI engagement on scientific impact across pre- (2016-2019) and post-pandemic (2020-2023) periods.
  • Key finding: Journals with higher collaboration intensity, especially those with larger and more diverse author teams, consistently achieve greater citation impact.
  • Key finding: AI engagement has become an increasingly strong correlate of journal prestige, particularly influencing quartile rankings, demonstrating its growing importance in modern biomedical research.
  • Human evaluation validates the three-stage LLM pipeline, confirming substantial agreement in AI relevance detection and consistent subfield classification.
  • The dataset and methodological framework enable scalable, content-aware scientometric analysis of scientific impact and innovation dynamics at the journal level.

Methodology

The methodology involves constructing the BioMedJImpact dataset from 1.74 million PubMed Central articles across 2,744 journals, integrating bibliometric indicators (e.g., citations), collaboration features (e.g., author team size, diversity), and LLM-derived semantic indicators for AI engagement. A three-stage LLM pipeline extracts AI engagement by classifying relevance and subfields. Statistical analysis is then performed to examine the correlation between collaboration intensity, AI engagement, and scientific impact (citation impact, quartile rankings) across two time periods (2016-2019, 2020-2023). The LLM pipeline is validated through human evaluation for agreement in AI relevance and subfield classification.

Key Findings

Two consistent trends emerged: journals exhibiting higher collaboration intensity, particularly those with larger and more diverse author teams, consistently achieve greater citation impact. Furthermore, AI engagement has become an increasingly significant correlate of journal prestige, especially evident in quartile rankings, indicating its rising influence on scientific recognition in biomedicine.

Clinical Impact

This research provides insights into the dynamics of scientific impact within biomedicine, which can help clinicians identify leading journals for evidence-based practice and stay abreast of high-impact AI-driven innovations. For medical researchers, it underscores the importance of interdisciplinary collaboration and integrating AI to enhance research visibility and impact. Healthcare policymakers and funding agencies can leverage these findings to direct resources towards collaborative and AI-focused research areas, accelerating the translation of scientific discoveries into clinical applications and improving patient care.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state any limitations of the study or dataset, beyond its focus on PubMed Central articles and the specific timeframes analyzed.

Future Directions

The authors suggest that BioMedJImpact serves as a validated methodological framework enabling scalable, content-aware scientometric analysis, implying future applications for deeper analysis of scientific impact and innovation dynamics across various research domains beyond the current scope.

Abstract

Assessing journal impact is central to scholarly communication, yet existing open resources rarely capture how collaboration structures and artificial intelligence (AI) research jointly shape venue prestige in biomedicine. We present BioMedJImpact, a large-scale, biomedical-oriented dataset designed to advance journal-level analysis of scientific impact and AI engagement. Built from 1.74 million PubMed Central articles across 2,744 journals, BioMedJImpact integrates bibliometric indicators, collaboration features, and LLM-derived semantic indicators for AI engagement. Specifically, the AI engagement feature is extracted through a reproducible three-stage LLM pipeline that we propose. Using this dataset, we analyze how collaboration intensity and AI engagement jointly influence scientific impact across pre- and post-pandemic periods (2016-2019, 2020-2023). Two consistent trends emerge: journals with higher collaboration intensity, particularly those with larger and more diverse author teams, tend to achieve greater citation impact, and AI engagement has become an increasingly strong correlate of journal prestige, especially in quartile rankings. To further validate the three-stage LLM pipeline we proposed for deriving the AI engagement feature, we conduct human evaluation, confirming substantial agreement in AI relevance detection and consistent subfield classification. Together, these contributions demonstrate that BioMedJImpact serves as both a comprehensive dataset capturing the intersection of biomedicine and AI, and a validated methodological framework enabling scalable, content-aware scientometric analysis of scientific impact and innovation dynamics. Code is available at https://github.com/JonathanWry/BioMedJImpact.
