I Detect What I Don't Know: Incremental Anomaly Learning with Stochastic Weight Averaging-Gaussian for Oracle-Free Medical Imaging

arXiv ID: 2511.03912v1
Published: 2025-11-05
Authors: Nand Kumar Yadav, Rodrigue Rizk, William CW Chen, KC Santosh
Categories: cs.CV, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces an unsupervised, oracle-free incremental learning framework for unknown anomaly detection in medical imaging, addressing the challenge of scarce labeled anomalies. The method iteratively expands a trusted set of normal samples by combining lightweight adapter updates with a dual probabilistic uncertainty-gated admission mechanism, utilizing a frozen pretrained backbone and a compact coreset for efficient k-NN scoring. This approach significantly improves anomaly detection performance across various medical imaging datasets, including COVID-CXR, Pneumonia CXR, and Brain MRI, by continually refining the notion of normality without relying on anomaly labels.

Medical Relevance

This framework offers a critical advancement for medical diagnosis by enabling the automated detection of rare, novel, or unknown anomalies without requiring prior labeled examples. It can significantly improve diagnostic accuracy, reduce expert workload, and facilitate early detection of emerging diseases or subtle conditions in clinical settings where labeled anomaly data is scarce or impossible to obtain.

AI Health Application

The AI application is an unsupervised, incremental anomaly detection system for medical images. It aims to assist healthcare professionals by automatically identifying deviations from normal patterns in X-rays (e.g., for COVID-19 or pneumonia) and MRIs (e.g., for brain anomalies), without requiring extensive labeled anomaly data. This can improve diagnostic efficiency, reduce reliance on costly expert supervision, and potentially lead to earlier or more accurate detection of diseases, particularly in settings with limited resources or for rare conditions.

Key Points

  • Addresses the fundamental challenge of unknown anomaly detection in medical imaging due to scarce labels and high supervision costs.
  • Proposes an unsupervised, oracle-free framework that incrementally expands a trusted set of normal samples without requiring any anomaly labels.
  • Utilizes a frozen pretrained vision backbone augmented with tiny convolutional adapters for rapid, computationally efficient domain adaptation.
  • Employs dual probabilistic gates for safe incremental expansion: samples are admitted only if their distance to the existing coreset is within a calibrated z-score threshold and their SWAG-based epistemic uncertainty is below a seed-calibrated bound.
  • Stores extracted embeddings in a compact coreset, enabling efficient k-nearest neighbor (k-NN) anomaly scoring.
  • Demonstrates substantial performance gains: COVID-CXR ROC-AUC improves from 0.9489 to 0.9982 (F1: 0.8048 to 0.9746); Pneumonia CXR ROC-AUC rises from 0.6834 to 0.8968; and Brain MRI ND-5 ROC-AUC increases from 0.6041 to 0.7269 (PR-AUC from 0.7539 to 0.8211).
  • The mechanism prevents model drift and false inclusions without relying on generative reconstruction or replay buffers.

Methodology

The proposed method is an unsupervised, incremental anomaly learning framework. It starts with a small, verified seed of normal images. The core process alternates between lightweight updates to tiny convolutional adapters (attached to a frozen pretrained vision backbone) and uncertainty-gated sample admission. Extracted embeddings from these adapted models are stored in a compact coreset. Safety during incremental expansion is enforced by dual probabilistic gates: a sample is admitted into the normal memory only if its distance to the existing coreset is within a calibrated z-score threshold, and its Stochastic Weight Averaging-Gaussian (SWAG)-based epistemic uncertainty remains below a seed-calibrated bound. Anomaly scoring is performed efficiently using k-nearest neighbors (k-NN) on the coreset embeddings.

Key Findings

The system consistently refines its understanding of normality as unlabeled data are processed. It achieved substantial performance improvements across diverse medical imaging tasks: ROC-AUC on COVID-CXR improved from 0.9489 to 0.9982 (F1: 0.8048 to 0.9746); on Pneumonia CXR, ROC-AUC rose from 0.6834 to 0.8968; and on Brain MRI ND-5, ROC-AUC increased from 0.6041 to 0.7269 and PR-AUC from 0.7539 to 0.8211. These results underscore the framework's effectiveness and efficiency in real-world, label-scarce medical imaging scenarios.

Clinical Impact

This framework has significant potential for clinical application, enabling medical imaging systems to continuously learn and adapt to new presentations of 'normality' in an unsupervised manner. This capability facilitates the earlier detection of unknown pathologies, rare diseases, or emerging conditions (like new strains of viruses) that lack specific training data. It can reduce the diagnostic burden on expert radiologists, provide decision support in resource-limited settings, and accelerate the identification of critical anomalies, ultimately improving patient outcomes.

Abstract

Unknown anomaly detection in medical imaging remains a fundamental challenge due to the scarcity of labeled anomalies and the high cost of expert supervision. We introduce an unsupervised, oracle-free framework that incrementally expands a trusted set of normal samples without any anomaly labels. Starting from a small, verified seed of normal images, our method alternates between lightweight adapter updates and uncertainty-gated sample admission. A frozen pretrained vision backbone is augmented with tiny convolutional adapters, ensuring rapid domain adaptation with negligible computational overhead. Extracted embeddings are stored in a compact coreset enabling efficient k-nearest neighbor anomaly (k-NN) scoring. Safety during incremental expansion is enforced by dual probabilistic gates, a sample is admitted into the normal memory only if its distance to the existing coreset lies within a calibrated z-score threshold, and its SWAG-based epistemic uncertainty remains below a seed-calibrated bound. This mechanism prevents drift and false inclusions without relying on generative reconstruction or replay buffers. Empirically, our system steadily refines the notion of normality as unlabeled data arrive, producing substantial gains over baselines. On COVID-CXR, ROC-AUC improves from 0.9489 to 0.9982 (F1: 0.8048 to 0.9746); on Pneumonia CXR, ROC-AUC rises from 0.6834 to 0.8968; and on Brain MRI ND-5, ROC-AUC increases from 0.6041 to 0.7269 and PR-AUC from 0.7539 to 0.8211. These results highlight the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed framework for real-world, label-scarce medical imaging applications.
