I Detect What I Don't Know: Incremental Anomaly Learning with Stochastic Weight Averaging-Gaussian for Oracle-Free Medical Imaging

arXiv ID: 2511.03912v1
Published: 2025-11-05
Authors: Nand Kumar Yadav, Rodrigue Rizk, William CW Chen, KC Santosh
Categories: cs.CV, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces an unsupervised, oracle-free framework for incremental anomaly learning in medical imaging, designed to identify unknown anomalies despite scarce labeled data. It achieves this by iteratively expanding a trusted set of normal samples through lightweight adapter updates and a robust dual probabilistic gating mechanism, significantly improving anomaly detection performance across various medical imaging tasks.

Medical Relevance

This framework directly addresses a critical bottleneck in medical AI: the prohibitive cost and scarcity of anomaly labels for rare diseases or novel conditions. By enabling robust and unsupervised detection of unknown anomalies, it has the potential to significantly enhance early disease diagnosis, reduce diagnostic errors, and optimize clinical workflows in resource-constrained healthcare environments.

AI Health Application

This AI system provides an unsupervised, oracle-free method for detecting anomalies in medical images, such as chest X-rays (for COVID-19, Pneumonia) and brain MRIs. Its application is to assist healthcare professionals by automatically identifying potentially pathological findings, even when labeled anomaly data is scarce. This can lead to earlier detection, improved diagnostic accuracy, and more efficient screening processes in various medical specialties.

Key Points

  • **Oracle-Free Incremental Learning:** The framework learns to define "normality" incrementally from unlabeled medical images, starting with a small verified seed of normal samples, without requiring any anomaly labels for training.
  • **Lightweight Adapter-Based Domain Adaptation:** A frozen, pretrained vision backbone is augmented with tiny convolutional adapters, enabling rapid and computationally efficient domain adaptation to specific medical imaging modalities.
  • **Efficient k-NN Anomaly Scoring:** Extracted image embeddings are stored in a compact coreset, facilitating efficient k-nearest neighbor (k-NN) based scoring for anomaly detection.
  • **Dual Probabilistic Safety Gates:** Incremental expansion of the normal memory is strictly governed by two safety mechanisms: a calibrated z-score threshold for sample distance to the existing coreset, and a seed-calibrated bound on Stochastic Weight Averaging-Gaussian (SWAG)-based epistemic uncertainty.
  • **Drift Prevention without Replay/Generative Models:** This dual gating mechanism effectively prevents model drift and the false inclusion of anomalous samples, crucially without relying on computationally intensive generative reconstruction methods or replay buffers.
  • **Significant Performance Improvements:** The method demonstrates substantial gains, with ROC-AUC improving from 0.9489 to 0.9982 on COVID-CXR, 0.6834 to 0.8968 on Pneumonia CXR, and 0.6041 to 0.7269 on Brain MRI ND-5.
  • **Applicability in Label-Scarce Settings:** The framework is highly efficient and effective for real-world medical imaging applications characterized by extreme scarcity of labeled anomalies and high expert supervision costs.

Methodology

The proposed method is an unsupervised, oracle-free incremental learning framework. It initializes with a small, verified seed of normal medical images. A frozen, pretrained vision backbone is augmented with tiny convolutional adapters, which are updated during learning. Extracted embeddings are stored in a compact coreset for efficient k-NN anomaly scoring. For incremental expansion, new unlabeled samples are admitted into the normal memory only if they pass two probabilistic safety gates: their distance to the existing coreset is within a calibrated z-score threshold, and their epistemic uncertainty (estimated via Stochastic Weight Averaging-Gaussian, SWAG) remains below a seed-calibrated bound. This iterative process refines the notion of normality.

Key Findings

The system consistently refined its understanding of normality, leading to substantial performance gains. On COVID-CXR, ROC-AUC improved from 0.9489 to 0.9982 (with F1 improving from 0.8048 to 0.9746). For Pneumonia CXR, ROC-AUC rose from 0.6834 to 0.8968. On Brain MRI ND-5, ROC-AUC increased from 0.6041 to 0.7269, and PR-AUC from 0.7539 to 0.8211.

Clinical Impact

This framework offers a practical solution for automating anomaly detection in medical imaging, particularly for rare conditions or when labeled anomaly data is sparse. It could enable more efficient screening, helping clinicians to quickly identify and prioritize abnormal cases for expert review, thereby potentially leading to earlier diagnoses, more timely interventions, and improved patient outcomes, especially in settings with limited access to specialized radiologists.

Abstract

Unknown anomaly detection in medical imaging remains a fundamental challenge due to the scarcity of labeled anomalies and the high cost of expert supervision. We introduce an unsupervised, oracle-free framework that incrementally expands a trusted set of normal samples without any anomaly labels. Starting from a small, verified seed of normal images, our method alternates between lightweight adapter updates and uncertainty-gated sample admission. A frozen pretrained vision backbone is augmented with tiny convolutional adapters, ensuring rapid domain adaptation with negligible computational overhead. Extracted embeddings are stored in a compact coreset enabling efficient k-nearest neighbor anomaly (k-NN) scoring. Safety during incremental expansion is enforced by dual probabilistic gates, a sample is admitted into the normal memory only if its distance to the existing coreset lies within a calibrated z-score threshold, and its SWAG-based epistemic uncertainty remains below a seed-calibrated bound. This mechanism prevents drift and false inclusions without relying on generative reconstruction or replay buffers. Empirically, our system steadily refines the notion of normality as unlabeled data arrive, producing substantial gains over baselines. On COVID-CXR, ROC-AUC improves from 0.9489 to 0.9982 (F1: 0.8048 to 0.9746); on Pneumonia CXR, ROC-AUC rises from 0.6834 to 0.8968; and on Brain MRI ND-5, ROC-AUC increases from 0.6041 to 0.7269 and PR-AUC from 0.7539 to 0.8211. These results highlight the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed framework for real-world, label-scarce medical imaging applications.
