I Detect What I Don't Know: Incremental Anomaly Learning with Stochastic Weight Averaging-Gaussian for Oracle-Free Medical Imaging

arXiv ID: 2511.03912v1
Published: 2025-11-05
Authors: Nand Kumar Yadav, Rodrigue Rizk, William CW Chen, KC Santosh
Categories: cs.CV, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces an unsupervised, oracle-free framework for incremental unknown anomaly detection in medical imaging, specifically designed to address label scarcity and high supervision costs. The method iteratively expands a trusted set of normal samples using lightweight adapter updates and dual uncertainty-gated admission, preventing drift and achieving significant performance gains across various medical imaging tasks. It offers an efficient and effective solution for real-world medical applications requiring anomaly detection without extensive labeled data.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medicine as it tackles the critical challenge of detecting unknown or rare medical conditions in imaging diagnostics where labeled anomaly data is scarce and expert supervision is costly. By incrementally learning what constitutes 'normal' without explicit anomaly labels, it provides a scalable and robust method to enhance diagnostic capabilities, potentially identifying novel diseases or subtle variations that might otherwise be missed.

AI Health Application

The AI application is an unsupervised, incremental anomaly detection system for medical imaging. It aims to automatically identify pathological conditions or abnormalities in medical scans (e.g., X-rays, MRIs) without requiring extensive pre-labeled anomaly data. This can assist clinicians in diagnosing diseases like COVID-19, pneumonia, or neurological disorders more efficiently and accurately, especially in environments where expert labels are scarce, thereby improving diagnostic workflows and patient outcomes.

Key Points

  • **Unsupervised & Oracle-Free Incremental Learning:** The framework learns to identify unknown anomalies by incrementally expanding a set of 'normal' samples without requiring any anomaly labels or expert intervention during the expansion process.
  • **Lightweight Adapter-Based Domain Adaptation:** It utilizes tiny convolutional adapters appended to a frozen, pretrained vision backbone, ensuring rapid domain adaptation with negligible computational overhead.
  • **Coreset for Efficient k-NN Scoring:** Extracted image embeddings are stored in a compact coreset, enabling highly efficient k-nearest neighbor (k-NN) based anomaly scoring for new samples.
  • **Dual Probabilistic Safety Gates:** Safety during incremental normal set expansion is enforced by two mechanisms: (1) a z-score threshold on the sample's distance to the existing coreset, and (2) a Stochastic Weight Averaging-Gaussian (SWAG)-based epistemic uncertainty bound, both calibrated to prevent drift and false inclusion of anomalies.
  • **Avoids Generative Models & Replay Buffers:** The methodology prevents drift and false inclusions without relying on computationally intensive generative reconstruction techniques or memory-heavy replay buffers.
  • **Significant Performance Improvements:** Achieves substantial gains in anomaly detection metrics (ROC-AUC, F1, PR-AUC) on diverse medical imaging datasets, including COVID-CXR, Pneumonia CXR, and Brain MRI ND-5.
  • **Addresses Label Scarcity Challenge:** Provides a practical and efficient solution for real-world medical imaging applications where obtaining labeled anomalies is prohibitively expensive or impossible.

Methodology

The proposed framework starts with a small, verified seed set of normal medical images. It alternates between updating lightweight convolutional adapters (attached to a frozen pretrained vision backbone) and admitting new, unlabeled samples into its 'normal' memory. Extracted image embeddings are stored in a compact coreset to facilitate efficient k-nearest neighbor (k-NN) anomaly scoring. New samples are admitted only if they pass dual probabilistic safety gates: their distance to the existing coreset must be within a calibrated z-score threshold, and their epistemic uncertainty (estimated using Stochastic Weight Averaging-Gaussian, SWAG) must remain below a seed-calibrated bound. This mechanism ensures safe, oracle-free expansion without generative models or replay buffers.

Key Findings

The system consistently refines its notion of normality, yielding substantial performance improvements over baselines. For COVID-CXR, ROC-AUC improved from 0.9489 to 0.9982 (F1 from 0.8048 to 0.9746). On Pneumonia CXR, ROC-AUC increased from 0.6834 to 0.8968. For Brain MRI ND-5, ROC-AUC rose from 0.6041 to 0.7269 and PR-AUC from 0.7539 to 0.8211. These results demonstrate the framework's effectiveness and efficiency in accurately detecting unknown anomalies across different medical imaging modalities.

Clinical Impact

This framework offers a significant potential clinical impact by enabling earlier and more accurate detection of unknown or emerging medical anomalies in imaging, even when labeled data is scarce. It could reduce the diagnostic burden on highly skilled radiologists, improve diagnostic consistency, and facilitate the deployment of AI-powered diagnostic tools in resource-constrained or remote clinical settings. Its ability to adapt to new definitions of 'normality' without human intervention makes it valuable for dynamic healthcare environments, potentially speeding up the identification of novel disease patterns.

Abstract

Unknown anomaly detection in medical imaging remains a fundamental challenge due to the scarcity of labeled anomalies and the high cost of expert supervision. We introduce an unsupervised, oracle-free framework that incrementally expands a trusted set of normal samples without any anomaly labels. Starting from a small, verified seed of normal images, our method alternates between lightweight adapter updates and uncertainty-gated sample admission. A frozen pretrained vision backbone is augmented with tiny convolutional adapters, ensuring rapid domain adaptation with negligible computational overhead. Extracted embeddings are stored in a compact coreset enabling efficient k-nearest neighbor anomaly (k-NN) scoring. Safety during incremental expansion is enforced by dual probabilistic gates, a sample is admitted into the normal memory only if its distance to the existing coreset lies within a calibrated z-score threshold, and its SWAG-based epistemic uncertainty remains below a seed-calibrated bound. This mechanism prevents drift and false inclusions without relying on generative reconstruction or replay buffers. Empirically, our system steadily refines the notion of normality as unlabeled data arrive, producing substantial gains over baselines. On COVID-CXR, ROC-AUC improves from 0.9489 to 0.9982 (F1: 0.8048 to 0.9746); on Pneumonia CXR, ROC-AUC rises from 0.6834 to 0.8968; and on Brain MRI ND-5, ROC-AUC increases from 0.6041 to 0.7269 and PR-AUC from 0.7539 to 0.8211. These results highlight the effectiveness and efficiency of the proposed framework for real-world, label-scarce medical imaging applications.
