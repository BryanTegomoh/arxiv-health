Summary

This paper introduces MedImageInsight, a medical imaging foundational model, for automated binary classification of chest X-rays into Normal or Abnormal categories to address increasing radiologist workload. The fine-tuned MedImageInsight achieved superior performance with an ROC-AUC of 0.888 and better calibration compared to transfer learning approaches, demonstrating its effectiveness in reducing training requirements while maintaining diagnostic reliability comparable to established models like CheXNet.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medical diagnostics as it provides an AI-driven solution to enhance the efficiency and timeliness of chest X-ray interpretation, directly addressing the growing demand for imaging services and alleviating radiologist burnout.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the automated binary classification of chest X-rays into 'Normal' or 'Abnormal' categories using a foundational medical imaging model (MedImageInsight). This system is designed to act as a preliminary diagnostic tool, support triage, and alleviate the workload of radiologists in clinical environments by integrating into existing hospital PACS and web-based workflows.

Methodology

The study utilized MedImageInsight, a medical imaging foundational model, to perform automated binary classification of chest X-rays. Two distinct methodologies were evaluated: (1) fine-tuning the MedImageInsight model for end-to-end classification, and (2) employing MedImageInsight as a feature extractor for a transfer learning pipeline that fed these features into traditional machine learning classifiers. The models were trained and tested on a dataset comprising ChestX-ray14 and real-world clinical data.

Key Findings

The fine-tuned MedImageInsight classifier achieved the best performance with an ROC-AUC of 0.888 and demonstrated superior calibration compared to the transfer learning models. Its diagnostic reliability was found to be comparable to established state-of-the-art architectures like CheXNet, affirming the effectiveness of foundational medical imaging models in reducing task-specific training requirements.

Clinical Impact

The system holds significant potential for clinical integration into existing hospital PACS and web-based workflows. It can effectively support the triage of chest X-rays, allowing for quicker identification of abnormal cases and significantly reducing the interpretative workload on radiologists, thereby improving diagnostic throughput and patient care.

Limitations

While not explicitly stated as a limitation, the current model primarily focuses on binary classification (Normal/Abnormal). The abstract indicates that multi-label pathology classification is an area for future work, implying this is a current scope boundary.

Future Directions

Future research will focus on extending the MedImageInsight model to perform multi-label pathology classification. This expansion aims to provide more detailed and preliminary diagnostic interpretations directly within clinical environments, moving beyond simple normal/abnormal distinctions.

Abstract

Chest radiography remains one of the most widely used imaging modalities for thoracic diagnosis, yet increasing imaging volumes and radiologist workload continue to challenge timely interpretation. In this work, we investigate the use of MedImageInsight, a medical imaging foundational model, for automated binary classification of chest X-rays into Normal and Abnormal categories. Two approaches were evaluated: (1) fine-tuning MedImageInsight for end-to-end classification, and (2) employing the model as a feature extractor for a transfer learning pipeline using traditional machine learning classifiers. Experiments were conducted using a combination of the ChestX-ray14 dataset and real-world clinical data sourced from partner hospitals. The fine-tuned classifier achieved the highest performance, with an ROC-AUC of 0.888 and superior calibration compared to the transfer learning models, demonstrating performance comparable to established architectures such as CheXNet. These results highlight the effectiveness of foundational medical imaging models in reducing task-specific training requirements while maintaining diagnostic reliability. The system is designed for integration into web-based and hospital PACS workflows to support triage and reduce radiologist burden. Future work will extend the model to multi-label pathology classification to provide preliminary diagnostic interpretation in clinical environments.
