A Computer Vision Hybrid Approach: CNN and Transformer Models for Accurate Alzheimer's Detection from Brain MRI Scans

Summary

This study introduces Evan_V2, a novel hybrid deep learning model combining CNN and Transformer architectures through feature-level fusion, for the accurate four-class classification of Alzheimer's disease (AD) from brain MRI scans. Achieving an exceptional 99.99% accuracy and superior F1-score and ROC AUC, Evan_V2 significantly outperforms individual CNN and Transformer models, highlighting the potential of ensemble strategies for highly reliable AD diagnosis.

Medical Relevance

Early and accurate classification of Alzheimer's disease from brain MRI scans is crucial for timely clinical intervention, enabling better disease management strategies, and ultimately improving patient outcomes and quality of life.

AI Health Application

The research develops and evaluates hybrid AI models (combining CNN and Transformer architectures) for automated, highly accurate detection and classification of Alzheimer's disease stages (Mild Dementia, Moderate Dementia, Non-Demented, Very Mild Dementia) from brain MRI scans. This application aims to provide a reliable AI-driven diagnostic tool to assist clinicians in the early diagnosis and management of Alzheimer's disease.

Key Points

  • The study conducted a comprehensive comparative analysis of five CNN architectures (EfficientNetB0, ResNet50, DenseNet201, MobileNetV3, VGG16) and five Transformer-based models (ViT, ConvTransformer, PatchTransformer, MLP-Mixer, SimpleTransformer) for AD detection.
  • The classification task involved distinguishing between four AD stages: Mild Dementia, Moderate Dementia, Non-Demented, and Very Mild Dementia, utilizing brain MRI scans.
  • Individual CNN architectures demonstrated strong performance, with ResNet50 achieving the highest accuracy among them at 98.83%.
  • Transformer models showed competitive generalization capabilities, with ViT achieving 95.38% accuracy as the top performer in its category, though individual Transformers exhibited greater class-specific instability.
  • The proposed Evan_V2 hybrid model integrates outputs from all ten CNN and Transformer architectures through a feature-level fusion technique.
  • Evan_V2 achieved the best overall performance, boasting 99.99% accuracy, a 0.9989 F1-score, and a 0.9968 ROC AUC.
  • Confusion matrix analysis confirmed that Evan_V2 substantially reduced misclassification errors across all dementia stages, outperforming every standalone model evaluated.

Methodology

The study utilized a comparative experimental design, evaluating five established CNN architectures and five Transformer-based models for a four-class classification of Alzheimer's disease from brain MRI scans. A novel hybrid model, Evan_V2, was then proposed, which integrated the outputs of these ten individual deep learning models via a feature-level fusion approach to enhance diagnostic accuracy.

Key Findings

The Evan_V2 hybrid model achieved an unprecedented 99.99% accuracy, 0.9989 F1-score, and 0.9968 ROC AUC, significantly surpassing the performance of the best individual CNN (ResNet50 at 98.83% accuracy) and Transformer (ViT at 95.38% accuracy) models. This hybrid approach demonstrated superior capability in reducing misclassification across all stages of dementia.

Clinical Impact

This highly accurate and robust hybrid deep learning solution offers a significant advancement in diagnostic tools for Alzheimer's disease. Its potential for near-perfect accuracy in AD classification from MRI could enable earlier and more precise diagnosis, leading to timely therapeutic interventions, personalized patient management, improved monitoring of disease progression, and ultimately better patient care and outcomes.

Abstract

Early and accurate classification of Alzheimers disease (AD) from brain MRI scans is essential for timely clinical intervention and improved patient outcomes. This study presents a comprehensive comparative analysis of five CNN architectures (EfficientNetB0, ResNet50, DenseNet201, MobileNetV3, VGG16), five Transformer-based models (ViT, ConvTransformer, PatchTransformer, MLP-Mixer, SimpleTransformer), and a proposed hybrid model named Evan_V2. All models were evaluated on a four-class AD classification task comprising Mild Dementia, Moderate Dementia, Non-Demented, and Very Mild Dementia categories. Experimental findings show that CNN architectures consistently achieved strong performance, with ResNet50 attaining 98.83% accuracy. Transformer models demonstrated competitive generalization capabilities, with ViT achieving the highest accuracy among them at 95.38%. However, individual Transformer variants exhibited greater class-specific instability. The proposed Evan_V2 hybrid model, which integrates outputs from ten CNN and Transformer architectures through feature-level fusion, achieved the best overall performance with 99.99% accuracy, 0.9989 F1-score, and 0.9968 ROC AUC. Confusion matrix analysis further confirmed that Evan_V2 substantially reduced misclassification across all dementia stages, outperforming every standalone model. These findings highlight the potential of hybrid ensemble strategies in producing highly reliable and clinically meaningful diagnostic tools for Alzheimers disease classification.
