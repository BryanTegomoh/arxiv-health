Anomaly Detection with Adaptive and Aggressive Rejection for Contaminated Training Data

arXiv ID: 2511.21378v1
Published: 2025-11-26
Authors: Jungi Lee, Jungkwon Kim, Chi Zhang, Kwangsun Yoo, Seok-Joo Byun
Categories: cs.LG, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces Adaptive and Aggressive Rejection (AAR), a novel anomaly detection method designed to overcome the critical challenge of contaminated training data by dynamically excluding anomalies. AAR utilizes a modified z-score and Gaussian Mixture Model-based thresholds, integrating both hard and soft rejection strategies to effectively balance data preservation and anomaly removal. The method significantly enhances robustness and performance, outperforming state-of-the-art approaches, making it highly relevant for real-world applications, particularly in healthcare.

Medical Relevance

Anomaly detection is vital in healthcare for early disease diagnosis, adverse event monitoring, and fraud detection. Contaminated training data (e.g., mislabeled patient records, atypical presentations) can severely degrade the reliability of medical AI, making AAR's robust approach crucial for trustworthy and accurate clinical decision support systems.

AI Health Application

This AI method (AAR) can be applied to build more robust anomaly detection systems in healthcare. For instance, it can be used to identify rare diseases from electronic health records, detect subtle or unusual anomalies in medical images (e.g., MRI, CT scans, X-rays), flag abnormal physiological patterns from wearable devices or ICU monitors, or identify unexpected adverse drug reactions in clinical trial data, even when the 'normal' training datasets are not perfectly clean or contain outliers.

Methodology

The Adaptive and Aggressive Rejection (AAR) method dynamically purifies training data by identifying and excluding anomalies without relying on fixed contamination ratios. It employs a modified z-score to quantify data point deviation and utilizes Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM)-based thresholds, which adaptively adjust to the data distribution, to distinguish between normal and anomalous instances. The method integrates a dual rejection strategy: 'hard rejection' for clear anomalies and 'soft rejection' for ambiguous cases, ensuring a balanced approach to data cleansing that preserves normal data integrity while aggressively removing contamination.

Key Findings

AAR demonstrates a significant performance improvement, outperforming the state-of-the-art anomaly detection methods by an average of 0.041 AUROC. This enhanced performance was consistently observed across a diverse range of datasets, including two image datasets and thirty tabular datasets. The core finding is that AAR effectively enhances the robustness of anomaly detection models when trained on contaminated data, making them more reliable in real-world scenarios.

By enabling robust anomaly detection despite contaminated training data, AAR can lead to more accurate and earlier detection of rare diseases, improved monitoring for adverse drug reactions, and better identification of subtle changes indicative of disease progression. This directly translates to improved patient safety, more efficient healthcare resource allocation, and a reduction in diagnostic errors or delays caused by unreliable AI models.

Abstract

Handling contaminated data poses a critical challenge in anomaly detection, as traditional models assume training on purely normal data. Conventional methods mitigate contamination by relying on fixed contamination ratios, but discrepancies between assumed and actual ratios can severely degrade performance, especially in noisy environments where normal and abnormal data distributions overlap. To address these limitations, we propose Adaptive and Aggressive Rejection (AAR), a novel method that dynamically excludes anomalies using a modified z-score and Gaussian mixture model-based thresholds. AAR effectively balances the trade-off between preserving normal data and excluding anomalies by integrating hard and soft rejection strategies. Extensive experiments on two image datasets and thirty tabular datasets demonstrate that AAR outperforms the state-of-the-art method by 0.041 AUROC. By providing a scalable and reliable solution, AAR enhances robustness against contaminated datasets, paving the way for broader real-world applications in domains such as security and healthcare.
