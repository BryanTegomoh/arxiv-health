Anomaly Detection with Adaptive and Aggressive Rejection for Contaminated Training Data

Published: 2025-11-26
Authors: Jungi Lee, Jungkwon Kim, Chi Zhang, Kwangsun Yoo, Seok-Joo Byun

Summary

This paper introduces Adaptive and Aggressive Rejection (AAR), a novel method designed to robustly perform anomaly detection by handling contaminated training data. AAR dynamically identifies and excludes anomalies using modified z-scores and Gaussian Mixture Model-based thresholds, significantly outperforming state-of-the-art methods by 0.041 AUROC. This approach enhances the reliability of AI models in real-world applications, particularly in sensitive domains like healthcare.

Medical Relevance

This method is critically important for medical/health AI, as clinical datasets are often noisy, incomplete, or contain mislabeled anomalies (e.g., rare diseases, artifacts in imaging). AAR ensures that AI models trained on such imperfect data can still accurately identify anomalies (e.g., disease onset, unusual patient vital signs), leading to more reliable diagnostic tools and improved patient safety.

AI Health Application

This research provides a method to improve the robustness and reliability of AI models in healthcare by effectively handling contaminated training data. For medical AI, this means models can be trained on 'normal' data that has been rigorously cleaned of subtle anomalies, leading to more accurate and trustworthy performance in tasks such as disease diagnosis, personalized treatment recommendation, real-time patient monitoring, and identifying potential biosecurity threats by detecting unusual patterns in health data.

Key Points

  • AAR addresses the critical challenge of contaminated training data in anomaly detection, where traditional models assume purely normal data, leading to performance degradation.
  • The method dynamically excludes anomalies by employing a modified z-score, providing a robust statistical measure of data point deviation.
  • It utilizes Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM)-based thresholds, which adaptively determine rejection criteria based on the underlying data distribution.
  • AAR integrates both 'hard' and 'soft' rejection strategies to strike a balance between aggressively removing clear anomalies and carefully preserving potentially normal, yet borderline, data points.
  • Extensive experiments on two image datasets and thirty tabular datasets demonstrated that AAR significantly outperforms state-of-the-art anomaly detection methods by 0.041 AUROC.
  • The proposed solution is scalable and enhances robustness against contaminated datasets, particularly in noisy environments where normal and abnormal data distributions overlap.
  • AAR's ability to provide a reliable solution has broad implications for real-world applications, especially in high-stakes fields such as security and healthcare.

Methodology

The Adaptive and Aggressive Rejection (AAR) method dynamically excludes anomalies during the training phase. It leverages a modified z-score to quantify the statistical deviation of data points from the dataset's median. Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) are then employed to learn the underlying distribution of the 'normal' data and establish adaptive thresholds for anomaly rejection. AAR combines 'hard rejection' for clearly anomalous data points with 'soft rejection' for borderline cases, effectively balancing the need to remove contaminants while preserving legitimate normal data.

Key Findings

The AAR method significantly improves anomaly detection performance, outperforming the state-of-the-art method by 0.041 AUROC. This improvement was consistently demonstrated across a diverse range of datasets, including two image datasets and thirty tabular datasets. AAR proves to be a scalable and robust solution for handling contaminated training data, particularly effective in noisy environments where distinguishing normal from abnormal data is challenging due to overlapping distributions.

Abstract

Handling contaminated data poses a critical challenge in anomaly detection, as traditional models assume training on purely normal data. Conventional methods mitigate contamination by relying on fixed contamination ratios, but discrepancies between assumed and actual ratios can severely degrade performance, especially in noisy environments where normal and abnormal data distributions overlap. To address these limitations, we propose Adaptive and Aggressive Rejection (AAR), a novel method that dynamically excludes anomalies using a modified z-score and Gaussian mixture model-based thresholds. AAR effectively balances the trade-off between preserving normal data and excluding anomalies by integrating hard and soft rejection strategies. Extensive experiments on two image datasets and thirty tabular datasets demonstrate that AAR outperforms the state-of-the-art method by 0.041 AUROC. By providing a scalable and reliable solution, AAR enhances robustness against contaminated datasets, paving the way for broader real-world applications in domains such as security and healthcare.
