Anomaly Detection with Adaptive and Aggressive Rejection for Contaminated Training Data

arXiv ID: 2511.21378v1
Published: 2025-11-26
Authors: Jungi Lee, Jungkwon Kim, Chi Zhang, Kwangsun Yoo, Seok-Joo Byun
Categories: cs.LG, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces Adaptive and Aggressive Rejection (AAR), a novel anomaly detection method designed to robustly handle contaminated training data without relying on fixed contamination ratios. AAR dynamically excludes anomalies using modified z-scores and Gaussian mixture model-based thresholds, employing both hard and soft rejection strategies. Experiments demonstrate AAR significantly improves performance by 0.041 AUROC compared to state-of-the-art methods, enhancing reliability for real-world applications in domains like healthcare.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medicine as healthcare datasets are frequently contaminated with noise, outliers, or mislabeled instances, making reliable anomaly detection crucial. AAR's ability to robustly learn from and identify anomalies even with contaminated training data is essential for accurate disease diagnosis, effective patient monitoring, and medical fraud detection, areas where traditional methods often degrade due to data impurity.

AI Health Application

AAR can be applied to improve the robustness and accuracy of AI models performing anomaly detection in healthcare. For instance, in medical imaging, it can ensure that AI models trained to distinguish 'normal' scans are not inadvertently contaminated by images containing subtle, undiagnosed anomalies, leading to more precise detection of diseases. Similarly, for patient monitoring, it can help develop more reliable AI systems for identifying unusual physiological events (e.g., arrhythmias, sepsis onset) by filtering out noisy or mislabeled 'normal' baseline data. This enhances the overall trustworthiness and performance of AI in clinical decision support, diagnostics, and preventative care.

Methodology

AAR dynamically identifies and excludes anomalous instances from training data. It utilizes a modified z-score for initial outlier scoring to identify potential anomalies. Subsequently, Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM)-based thresholds are employed to set adaptive and data-driven boundaries for anomaly rejection. The method integrates both 'hard rejection' (for clearly anomalous data) and 'soft rejection' (for borderline cases to prevent loss of valuable normal data) strategies to optimize the trade-off between contamination removal and data preservation.

Key Findings

The study found that AAR effectively mitigates performance degradation in anomaly detection caused by contaminated training data, adapting dynamically without requiring a fixed contamination ratio. It significantly outperformed the state-of-the-art method by 0.041 AUROC across a diverse range of image and tabular datasets. This demonstrates enhanced robustness, reliability, and scalability of the proposed method in handling real-world noisy environments.

This can improve diagnostic accuracy by ensuring anomaly detection models are trained on cleaner, more representative 'normal' data, thus better identifying true medical anomalies (e.g., early disease markers, critical patient changes). It also reduces the need for costly and time-consuming manual data cleaning, accelerating the deployment and integration of AI in various healthcare applications.

Abstract

Handling contaminated data poses a critical challenge in anomaly detection, as traditional models assume training on purely normal data. Conventional methods mitigate contamination by relying on fixed contamination ratios, but discrepancies between assumed and actual ratios can severely degrade performance, especially in noisy environments where normal and abnormal data distributions overlap. To address these limitations, we propose Adaptive and Aggressive Rejection (AAR), a novel method that dynamically excludes anomalies using a modified z-score and Gaussian mixture model-based thresholds. AAR effectively balances the trade-off between preserving normal data and excluding anomalies by integrating hard and soft rejection strategies. Extensive experiments on two image datasets and thirty tabular datasets demonstrate that AAR outperforms the state-of-the-art method by 0.041 AUROC. By providing a scalable and reliable solution, AAR enhances robustness against contaminated datasets, paving the way for broader real-world applications in domains such as security and healthcare.
