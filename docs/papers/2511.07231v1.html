Summary

This study develops a remote sensing-driven framework using sub-meter satellite imagery and a semi-supervised segmentation model to quantify Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene (WASH) accessibility in dense Rohingya refugee camps. The research reveals declining WASH accessibility over time, with an increase from 25 to 29.4 people per facility between 2022 and 2025, and highlights significant gender disparities where women and girls face reduced access due to inadequate safety segregation.

Medical Relevance

Reduced WASH accessibility is a major public health concern in refugee camps, directly impacting disease transmission, morbidity, and mortality. This study provides a method to identify populations at highest risk due to poor access, enabling targeted interventions to mitigate health crises.

AI Health Application

The research applies machine learning (semi-supervised segmentation framework) to sub-meter satellite imagery to detect individual refugee shelters and subsequently quantify WASH facility accessibility. This AI-driven framework is used to monitor public health infrastructure, identify underserved populations (e.g., women and girls), and inform demand-responsive allocation strategies for WASH facilities, thereby mitigating health risks and improving public health outcomes in complex humanitarian settings.

Key Points

  • A novel remote sensing framework was developed to quantify WASH accessibility (water pumps, latrines, bathing cubicles) in complex, densely populated refugee camps.
  • The framework utilizes sub-meter satellite images and a semi-supervised segmentation model, achieving an F1-score of 76.4% for detecting individual refugee shelters.
  • Analysis of multi-year data for Rohingya camps in Cox's Bazar showed a decline in WASH accessibility, with the ratio of people per facility increasing from 25 in 2022 to a projected 29.4 in 2025.
  • Rapid refugee population growth and reduced facility availability were identified as primary drivers for the decreasing accessibility.
  • Gender-disaggregated analysis revealed that women and girls experience reduced WASH accessibility, particularly in facilities lacking adequate safety-related segregation.
  • The findings emphasize the critical need for demand-responsive allocation strategies to identify and prioritize underserved populations, such as women and girls.
  • The study advocates for the use of high-resolution remote sensing and machine learning as valuable tools for detecting inequality and informing equitable resource planning in humanitarian contexts, especially with fixed or shrinking budgets.

Methodology

The study employed a remote sensing-driven framework utilizing sub-meter satellite images. A semi-supervised segmentation framework was developed and applied to detect individual refugee shelters, achieving an F1-score of 76.4%. This framework was then used to quantify accessibility to specific WASH facilities (water pumps, latrines, bathing cubicles) over multiple years, integrating data on refugee population growth and facility availability. Gender-disaggregated analysis was performed to identify specific vulnerabilities.

Key Findings

WASH accessibility in Rohingya camps is declining, with the ratio of people per facility increasing from 25 in 2022 to an estimated 29.4 in 2025. This decline is attributed to rapid population growth and reduced facility availability. Crucially, women and girls consistently experience reduced WASH accessibility, especially where safety-related segregation in facilities is inadequate.

Clinical Impact

This research provides a powerful tool for humanitarian organizations and public health practitioners to monitor and manage WASH infrastructure in dynamic refugee settings. It enables the identification of 'hotspots' or specific demographic groups (e.g., women and girls) with critical unmet WASH needs, allowing for data-driven, demand-responsive allocation of limited resources, thereby potentially reducing outbreaks of WASH-related diseases and improving overall camp health and safety.

Abstract

Access to Water, Sanitation, and Hygiene (WASH) services remains a major public health concern in refugee camps. This study introduces a remote sensing-driven framework to quantify WASH accessibility-specifically to water pumps, latrines, and bathing cubicles-in the Rohingya camps of Cox's Bazar, one of the world's most densely populated displacement settings. Detecting refugee shelters in such emergent camps presents substantial challenges, primarily due to their dense spatial configuration and irregular geometric patterns. Using sub-meter satellite images, we develop a semi-supervised segmentation framework that achieves an F1-score of 76.4% in detecting individual refugee shelters. Applying the framework across multi-year data reveals declining WASH accessibility, driven by rapid refugee population growth and reduced facility availability, rising from 25 people per facility in 2022 to 29.4 in 2025. Gender-disaggregated analysis further shows that women and girls experience reduced accessibility, in scenarios with inadequate safety-related segregation in WASH facilities. These findings suggest the importance of demand-responsive allocation strategies that can identify areas with under-served populations-such as women and girls-and ensure that limited infrastructure serves the greatest number of people in settings with fixed or shrinking budgets. We also discuss the value of high-resolution remote sensing and machine learning to detect inequality and inform equitable resource planning in complex humanitarian environments.
