Learning to reason about rare diseases through retrieval-augmented agents

Summary

RADAR (Retrieval Augmented Diagnostic Reasoning Agents) is an agentic AI system designed to improve rare disease detection in brain MRI, specifically addressing the challenges of data scarcity. It enhances diagnostic decision-making by enabling AI agents to access and retrieve clinically relevant evidence from external medical knowledge sources like case reports and literature. This model-agnostic system achieved up to a 10.2% performance gain on 280 distinct rare diseases in the NOVA dataset, particularly benefiting open-source language models, while also providing interpretable, literature-grounded explanations.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medicine as it offers a solution to improve the challenging and often delayed diagnosis of rare diseases, which current AI models struggle with due to data scarcity. Enhanced accuracy and interpretability in this domain can lead to earlier interventions and better patient outcomes for conditions that typically have high diagnostic burdens.

AI Health Application

The paper describes an AI agentic system (RADAR) that utilizes retrieval-augmented reasoning and large language models to improve the detection and diagnosis of rare diseases in brain MRI. It serves as a medical AI application to provide enhanced diagnostic capabilities and interpretable, literature-grounded explanations for radiologists, thereby aiding clinical workflows and addressing a critical unmet need in healthcare for low-prevalence conditions.

Key Points

  • Introduces RADAR, a novel Retrieval Augmented Diagnostic Reasoning Agent system, specifically for rare disease detection in brain MRI.
  • Utilizes AI agents that access external medical knowledge (case reports and literature) by embedding it with sentence transformers and indexing it with FAISS for efficient similarity search.
  • Enables diagnostic decision-making by retrieving clinically relevant evidence to guide reasoning on unseen diseases, without requiring additional model training.
  • Designed as a model-agnostic reasoning module, allowing seamless integration with diverse large language models (LLMs) to consistently improve their performance.

Methodology

The RADAR system employs AI agents that leverage a retrieval-augmented approach. External medical knowledge, comprising case reports and literature, is first processed by embedding it using sentence transformers. This embedded knowledge is then indexed with FAISS, enabling efficient similarity search. During diagnostic reasoning, these agents retrieve contextually relevant evidence from the indexed knowledge base to guide decision-making. The system is designed to be model-agnostic, allowing integration with various large language models to enhance their rare pathology recognition capabilities without the need for additional training.

Key Findings

The RADAR system demonstrated a substantial performance gain of up to 10.2% in rare disease detection when evaluated on the NOVA dataset, which includes 280 distinct rare diseases. The improvements were particularly pronounced for open-source large language models such as DeepSeek. Crucially, beyond accuracy, the system provided interpretable, literature-grounded explanations derived directly from the retrieved medical evidence, highlighting the transparency of its reasoning.

Clinical Impact

RADAR has the potential to significantly improve the accuracy and efficiency of diagnosing rare diseases from brain MRI scans, thereby reducing diagnostic delays and ultimately leading to better patient management and outcomes. Its ability to provide interpretable, literature-grounded explanations can increase clinician trust in AI-assisted diagnoses, serving as a valuable decision-support tool for radiologists in complex cases and potentially aiding in their continuous learning.

Abstract

Rare diseases represent the long tail of medical imaging, where AI models often fail due to the scarcity of representative training data. In clinical workflows, radiologists frequently consult case reports and literature when confronted with unfamiliar findings. Following this line of reasoning, we introduce RADAR, Retrieval Augmented Diagnostic Reasoning Agents, an agentic system for rare disease detection in brain MRI. Our approach uses AI agents with access to external medical knowledge by embedding both case reports and literature using sentence transformers and indexing them with FAISS to enable efficient similarity search. The agent retrieves clinically relevant evidence to guide diagnostic decision making on unseen diseases, without the need of additional training. Designed as a model-agnostic reasoning module, RADAR can be seamlessly integrated with diverse large language models, consistently improving their rare pathology recognition and interpretability. On the NOVA dataset comprising 280 distinct rare diseases, RADAR achieves up to a 10.2% performance gain, with the strongest improvements observed for open source models such as DeepSeek. Beyond accuracy, the retrieved examples provide interpretable, literature grounded explanations, highlighting retrieval-augmented reasoning as a powerful paradigm for low-prevalence conditions in medical imaging.
