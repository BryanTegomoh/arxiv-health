A Data-Validated Host-Parasite Model for Infectious Disease Outbreaks

arXiv ID: 1907.00249v2
Published: 2019-06-29
Authors: Christina P. Tadiri, Jude D. Kong, Gregor F. Fussmann, Marilyn E. Scott, Hao Wang

Summary

This paper develops a data-validated mathematical model for host-parasite dynamics using the guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system, a quantifiable model for infectious diseases. It estimates the basic reproduction number (R0) for this system and identifies parasite growth rate and host immune response rate as critical factors influencing outbreak peak and timing. These findings highlight the importance of host resistance and parasite virulence in disease control strategies.

Medical Relevance

Understanding the fundamental host-parasite dynamics, pathogen virulence, and host immune responses in this tractable model system provides valuable mechanistic insights directly applicable to human infectious disease epidemiology, outbreak prediction, and the development of effective mitigation strategies.

AI Health Application

Not applicable. This paper describes a traditional mathematical modeling and experimental validation approach, without incorporating AI or machine learning methods or applications.

Key Points

  • A mathematical model was designed and data-validated to describe host-parasite dynamics using the well-studied guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system.
  • The guppy-Gyrodactylus system serves as an advantageous model due to its quantifiable and trackable helminth ectoparasites, offering insights into within-host and epidemic dynamics relevant to microparasites.
  • Experimental data was explicitly used to estimate model parameters and validate the model's accuracy.
  • The basic reproduction number (R_0) was estimated for the guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system, quantifying its epidemic potential.
  • Sensitivity analysis revealed that the parasite growth rate and the rate at which the guppy mounts an immune response are the most impactful parameters on both initial outbreak peak/timing and longer-term dynamics.
  • The findings underscore host population resistance and parasite virulence as key determinants in the effectiveness of disease control measures.
  • Future work is proposed to incorporate heterogeneity in host resistance into disease models and extrapolate the framework to other host-parasite systems.

Methodology

The study involved the design of a mathematical model to describe host-parasite interactions. This model was rigorously parameterized and validated using empirical experimental data collected from the guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system. Key analytical techniques included the estimation of the basic reproduction number (R_0) and a comprehensive sensitivity analysis to determine the relative impact of various model parameters on outbreak characteristics.

Key Findings

The study successfully developed a data-validated host-parasite model and estimated the basic reproduction number (R_0) for the guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system. A critical finding from sensitivity analysis was that the parasite's intrinsic growth rate and the host's immune response rate are the predominant factors dictating the magnitude and timing of outbreak peaks, both for initial emergence and over longer temporal scales.

Clinical Impact

While focused on a specific aquatic model, the identified key factors (parasite growth rate/virulence and host immune response) are universal principles in infectious disease. These insights provide a foundational understanding for developing more effective disease control strategies in clinical and public health settings, by informing interventions that target pathogen replication rates or enhance host immunity to either prevent outbreaks or mitigate their severity.

Limitations

The primary limitation noted, implicitly, is the focus on a specific experimental model system (guppy-Gyrodactylus), which may not fully encompass the biological complexity of all host-parasite interactions. The abstract also suggests a current limitation in not yet incorporating heterogeneity in host resistance into the developed models.

Future Directions

Future research should prioritize incorporating heterogeneity in host resistance into disease models to better reflect natural population dynamics. Additionally, the developed modeling framework and the insights gained should be extrapolated and tested across other diverse host-parasite systems to assess generalizability and broader applicability.

Abstract

The use of model experimental systems and mathematical models is important to further understanding of infectious disease dynamics and strategize disease mitigation. Gyrodactylids are helminth ectoparasites of teleost fish which have many dynamical characteristics of microparasites but offer the advantage that they can be quantified and tracked over time, allowing further insight into within-host and epidemic dynamics. In this paper, we design a model to describe host-parasite dynamics of the well-studied guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system, using experimental data to estimate parameters and validate it. We estimate the basic reproduction number (R_0), for this system. Sensitivity analysis reveals that parasite growth rate, and the rate at which the guppy mounts an immune response have the greatest impact on outbreak peak and timing both for initial outbreaks and on longer time scales. These findings highlight guppy population resistance and parasite virulence as key factors in disease control, and future work should focus on incorporating heterogeneity in host resistance into disease models and extrapolating to other host-parasite systems.
