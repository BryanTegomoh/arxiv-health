A Data-Validated Host-Parasite Model for Infectious Disease Outbreaks

arXiv ID: 1907.00249v2
Published: 2019-06-29
Authors: Christina P. Tadiri, Jude D. Kong, Gregor F. Fussmann, Marilyn E. Scott, Hao Wang
Categories: q-bio.PE, math.DS, stat.CO
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00

Summary

This paper designs and validates a mathematical host-parasite model using experimental data from the guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system, to understand infectious disease dynamics. The study estimates the basic reproduction number (R_0) and identifies parasite growth rate and host immune response as key determinants of outbreak peak and timing, highlighting their importance in disease control strategies.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a robust, data-validated modeling framework for understanding infectious disease outbreaks, which can be extrapolated to human pathogens. By identifying critical parameters like pathogen virulence and host immune response, it offers insights fundamental for developing effective public health interventions and strategies for disease mitigation.

AI Health Application

While the paper describes a mathematical model rather than an AI model, the methodologies for data-validated host-parasite modeling, parameter estimation, and sensitivity analysis of infectious disease dynamics are foundational. These principles and models are critical components for developing AI/ML-driven epidemiological forecasting tools, predicting outbreak severity and timing, optimizing intervention strategies, and understanding complex host-pathogen interactions in real-world public health scenarios.

Key Points

  • A data-validated mathematical model was developed to describe host-parasite dynamics using the well-studied guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system.
  • The model's parameters were estimated and validated using specific experimental data from this host-parasite interaction.
  • The basic reproduction number (R_0) was estimated for the guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system, providing a measure of transmission potential.
  • Sensitivity analysis revealed that the parasite growth rate has a significant impact on both the peak and timing of disease outbreaks.
  • The rate at which the guppy mounts an immune response was also identified as a crucial factor influencing outbreak dynamics on both initial and longer time scales.
  • The findings underscore the critical roles of host population resistance and parasite virulence in the effective control of infectious diseases.
  • Future research is recommended to incorporate heterogeneity in host resistance into disease models and to extrapolate these findings to other host-parasite systems, including human-relevant ones.

Methodology

A mathematical model was designed to describe host-parasite dynamics, specifically for the guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system. Experimental data was utilized to estimate model parameters and to validate the model's accuracy. The basic reproduction number (R_0) was calculated, and a sensitivity analysis was performed to identify parameters that most significantly impact outbreak characteristics such as peak and timing.

Key Findings

The study successfully developed and validated a host-parasite model for the guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system, providing an estimated R_0. Sensitivity analysis prominently demonstrated that parasite growth rate and the host's immune response rate are the most influential factors determining the peak and timing of disease outbreaks. These findings highlight host resistance and parasite virulence as pivotal for disease control.

Clinical Impact

This modeling approach can inform public health strategies by predicting outbreak dynamics and identifying key intervention points for human infectious diseases. Understanding the relative impact of pathogen virulence and host immunity allows for targeted development of vaccines, immunomodulators, or antiparasitic/antiviral therapies, thereby optimizing resource allocation for disease prevention and control.

Limitations

The abstract implicitly suggests limitations by noting future directions; specifically, the current model may lack explicit incorporation of heterogeneity in host resistance, and its direct applicability has thus far been demonstrated only in a specific fish ectoparasite system.

Future Directions

Future research should prioritize incorporating heterogeneity in host resistance into disease models to better reflect natural populations. Additionally, the developed model and its insights should be extrapolated and applied to a broader range of host-parasite systems, including those directly relevant to human health.

Abstract

The use of model experimental systems and mathematical models is important to further understanding of infectious disease dynamics and strategize disease mitigation. Gyrodactylids are helminth ectoparasites of teleost fish which have many dynamical characteristics of microparasites but offer the advantage that they can be quantified and tracked over time, allowing further insight into within-host and epidemic dynamics. In this paper, we design a model to describe host-parasite dynamics of the well-studied guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system, using experimental data to estimate parameters and validate it. We estimate the basic reproduction number (R_0), for this system. Sensitivity analysis reveals that parasite growth rate, and the rate at which the guppy mounts an immune response have the greatest impact on outbreak peak and timing both for initial outbreaks and on longer time scales. These findings highlight guppy population resistance and parasite virulence as key factors in disease control, and future work should focus on incorporating heterogeneity in host resistance into disease models and extrapolating to other host-parasite systems.
