A Data-Validated Host-Parasite Model for Infectious Disease Outbreaks

arXiv ID: 1907.00249v2
Published: 2019-06-29
Authors: Christina P. Tadiri, Jude D. Kong, Gregor F. Fussmann, Marilyn E. Scott, Hao Wang
Categories: q-bio.PE, math.DS, stat.CO
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00

Summary

This paper develops and validates a mathematical host-parasite model using experimental data from the guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system to understand infectious disease dynamics. The model estimates the basic reproduction number (R_0) and identifies parasite growth rate and host immune response as critical factors influencing outbreak peak and timing. These findings underscore the importance of host resistance and parasite virulence in disease control strategies.

Medical Relevance

While focused on a fish-parasite system, this research provides a robust framework and generalizable insights into infectious disease dynamics, pathogen virulence, and host immunity. The findings can inform the development of strategies for mitigating human infectious disease outbreaks by identifying key drivers of disease progression and control.

Key Points

  • A data-validated mathematical model was designed to describe host-parasite dynamics using the guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system as an experimental model.
  • Experimental data was utilized for parameter estimation and validation of the host-parasite model.
  • The basic reproduction number (R_0) for the guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system was estimated.
  • Sensitivity analysis revealed that parasite growth rate and the rate of guppy immune response are the most impactful factors on outbreak peak and timing.
  • The study highlights guppy population resistance and parasite virulence as key determinants for disease control.
  • The tractable Gyrodactylid system, with quantifiable and trackable parasites, provides insights into both within-host and epidemic dynamics relevant to microparasites.

Methodology

The study involved designing a mathematical host-parasite model specific to the guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system. This model was then rigorously validated and had its parameters estimated using empirical data from experimental observations. A sensitivity analysis was performed to determine the impact of various parameters on outbreak dynamics.

Key Findings

The paper successfully developed a data-validated host-parasite model and estimated the basic reproduction number (R_0) for the system. Crucially, it identified that the parasite's intrinsic growth rate and the host's rate of mounting an immune response are the most significant factors influencing the timing and magnitude of disease outbreaks. These findings underscore the critical roles of host resistance and parasite virulence in disease control.

Clinical Impact

This research contributes to the fundamental understanding of infectious disease epidemiology and dynamics. The insights into the critical role of host resistance and parasite virulence can guide public health strategies by emphasizing the importance of host immunity and pathogen characteristics in predicting and controlling outbreaks. Though not directly clinical, the modeling approach and identified key parameters offer a blueprint for analyzing and strategizing against human pathogens.

Limitations

The current model is specifically validated for the guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system, meaning its direct applicability to other, more complex host-parasite systems (including human ones) requires further adaptation and validation. Additionally, the model, in its current form, does not incorporate heterogeneity in host resistance, which is acknowledged as a factor for future consideration.

Future Directions

Future research should focus on incorporating heterogeneity in host resistance into disease models to better reflect real-world population dynamics. Furthermore, the findings and modeling approach should be extrapolated and adapted to other host-parasite systems, including those relevant to human health, to broaden their applicability and impact.

Abstract

The use of model experimental systems and mathematical models is important to further understanding of infectious disease dynamics and strategize disease mitigation. Gyrodactylids are helminth ectoparasites of teleost fish which have many dynamical characteristics of microparasites but offer the advantage that they can be quantified and tracked over time, allowing further insight into within-host and epidemic dynamics. In this paper, we design a model to describe host-parasite dynamics of the well-studied guppy-Gyrodactylus turnbulli system, using experimental data to estimate parameters and validate it. We estimate the basic reproduction number (R_0), for this system. Sensitivity analysis reveals that parasite growth rate, and the rate at which the guppy mounts an immune response have the greatest impact on outbreak peak and timing both for initial outbreaks and on longer time scales. These findings highlight guppy population resistance and parasite virulence as key factors in disease control, and future work should focus on incorporating heterogeneity in host resistance into disease models and extrapolating to other host-parasite systems.
