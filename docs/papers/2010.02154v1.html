Medical Imaging and Computational Image Analysis in COVID-19 Diagnosis: A Review

Summary

This review paper synthesizes studies on automated medical image analysis for COVID-19 diagnosis, focusing on characteristic imaging signs and the performance of AI-based approaches. It highlights that medical imaging can detect COVID-19 early, even in asymptomatic patients, and that automated machine learning methods offer high accuracy, reduced diagnostic time, cost, and error.

Medical Relevance

Early and accurate diagnosis of COVID-19, particularly in asymptomatic individuals, is paramount for effective disease containment, patient isolation, and timely treatment. This review validates the significant potential of automated medical image analysis to provide rapid, objective, and reliable diagnostic insights, thereby improving public health outcomes and optimizing clinical resource allocation.

AI Health Application

The paper focuses on the application of artificial intelligence and machine learning, specifically computational image analysis and computer vision techniques, to automatically analyze medical images (e.g., CT scans, X-rays) for the early and accurate diagnosis of COVID-19. This aims to reduce diagnostic time, cost, and error in clinical settings, including the detection of asymptomatic cases.

Key Points

  • The review serves as a tutorial for both clinicians and technologists regarding medical imaging and computational analysis in COVID-19 diagnosis.
  • It provides a comprehensive overview of the characteristic features and signs of COVID-19 as presented in various medical images.
  • Automated artificial intelligence-based approaches for COVID-19 diagnosis are critically examined based on their reported accuracy and the specific methods employed.
  • Medical imaging is identified as a valuable tool for the early diagnosis of COVID-19, including in asymptomatic patients, which is critical for mitigating disease transmission.
  • Automated machine learning methods are shown to achieve high diagnostic accuracy from medical images, contributing to significant reductions in diagnostic time, cost, and potential human error.
  • The paper addresses existing research limitations within the field of automated COVID-19 imaging diagnosis and discusses strategies to overcome these challenges.
  • A key recommendation is the rapid collection of large-scale imaging datasets from COVID-19 patients to enhance the performance and robustness of automated diagnostic algorithms.

Methodology

This study conducted a comprehensive review by systematically searching major academic databases including PubMed, Google Scholar, arXiv, and medRxiv for relevant research papers published by the end of April 2020. The selected studies focused on automated methods for medical image analysis specifically applied to COVID-19 diagnosis.

Key Findings

The review concluded that COVID-19 exhibits distinct signs in medical images that can be effectively utilized for early diagnosis, even in patients who do not yet display symptoms. Furthermore, automated machine learning-based methods have demonstrated a high level of accuracy in diagnosing the disease from these medical images, leading to efficiencies in terms of reduced diagnostic time, cost, and potential for human error.

Clinical Impact

The integration of automated AI-based medical image analysis into clinical practice could dramatically accelerate COVID-19 diagnostic workflows, offering a rapid, standardized, and potentially more accessible screening tool. This could enable earlier initiation of isolation and treatment protocols, alleviate diagnostic burdens on radiologists, and improve resource management, particularly in high-volume settings or during outbreaks.

Limitations

The abstract indicates that the review addresses research limitations within the field of automated COVID-19 diagnosis and discusses methods to overcome them. However, specific limitations of the reviewed studies or the field itself are not explicitly detailed within the abstract. The search timeframe up to April 2020 implies that research published subsequently is not included, which is a temporal limitation for a rapidly evolving field.

Future Directions

The most emphasized future direction is the urgent and rapid collection of large volumes of medical imaging data from COVID-19 patients. This extensive dataset is crucial for the continuous improvement, validation, and generalization of automated diagnostic methods based on artificial intelligence and machine learning.

Abstract

Coronavirus disease (COVID-19) is an infectious disease caused by a newly discovered coronavirus. The disease presents with symptoms such as shortness of breath, fever, dry cough, and chronic fatigue, amongst others. Sometimes the symptoms of the disease increase so much they lead to the death of the patients. The disease may be asymptomatic in some patients in the early stages, which can lead to increased transmission of the disease to others. Many studies have tried to use medical imaging for early diagnosis of COVID-19. This study attempts to review papers on automatic methods for medical image analysis and diagnosis of COVID-19. For this purpose, PubMed, Google Scholar, arXiv and medRxiv were searched to find related studies by the end of April 2020, and the essential points of the collected studies were summarised. The contribution of this study is four-fold: 1) to use as a tutorial of the field for both clinicians and technologists, 2) to comprehensively review the characteristics of COVID-19 as presented in medical images, 3) to examine automated artificial intelligence-based approaches for COVID-19 diagnosis based on the accuracy and the method used, 4) to express the research limitations in this field and the methods used to overcome them. COVID-19 reveals signs in medical images can be used for early diagnosis of the disease even in asymptomatic patients. Using automated machine learning-based methods can diagnose the disease with high accuracy from medical images and reduce time, cost and error of diagnostic procedure. It is recommended to collect bulk imaging data from patients in the shortest possible time to improve the performance of COVID-19 automated diagnostic methods.
