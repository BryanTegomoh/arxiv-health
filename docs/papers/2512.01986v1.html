A robust generalizable device-agnostic deep learning model for sleep-wake determination from triaxial wrist accelerometry

arXiv ID: 2512.01986v1
Published: 2025-12-01
Authors: Nasim Montazeri, Stone Yang, Dominik Luszczynski, John Zhang, Dharmendra Gurve, Andrew Centen, Maged Goubran, Andrew Lim
Categories: q-bio.QM, cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This study developed and validated a robust, generalizable, and device-agnostic deep learning model for accurate sleep-wake determination from triaxial wrist accelerometry. The model demonstrated high performance in detecting sleep and wakefulness, with improved wake detection, and showed consistency across different accelerometer devices and in adults with various sleep disorders.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a significantly improved and validated tool for objective, real-world sleep monitoring, which is crucial for diagnosing and managing sleep disorders, evaluating treatment efficacy, and advancing research in chronobiology and public health without the need for cumbersome PSG.

AI Health Application

The AI application is a deep learning model that processes triaxial wrist accelerometry data to determine sleep-wakefulness, including the presence of sleep with arousals. This model is designed to be device-agnostic and robust to various sleep disorders, serving as an advanced diagnostic and monitoring tool for individuals with and without sleep disorders, potentially aiding clinicians in assessing sleep health and guiding treatment.

Key Points

  • The study aimed to address limitations of previous actigraphy models, specifically poor wake detection, lack of cross-device generalizability, and limited validation across diverse populations (age, sleep disorders).
  • A deep learning model was trained on triaxial wrist accelerometry data collected simultaneously with Polysomnography (PSG) from 453 adults undergoing clinical sleep testing, using three different accelerometer devices.
  • The model initially classified three states (wake, sleep, sleep with arousals), which were subsequently collapsed into a binary wake vs. sleep classification using a decision tree.
  • To enhance wake detection, the model incorporated a specific training strategy utilizing data from subjects with low sleep efficiency and/or high arousal index.
  • Key performance metrics include an F1 Score of 0.86, sleep sensitivity of 0.87, and wakefulness specificity of 0.78, indicating strong agreement with PSG.
  • The model showed significant and moderate correlation with PSG for Total Sleep Time (R=0.69) and Sleep Efficiency (R=0.63).
  • Crucially, the model's performance was robust to common sleep disorders (e.g., sleep apnea, periodic limb movements) and remained consistent across all three different accelerometer models used, demonstrating device-agnostic generalizability.

Methodology

The study utilized a cohort of 453 adults undergoing clinical PSG at a tertiary sleep center, collecting simultaneous triaxial wrist accelerometry data from three distinct devices. Features were extracted from 30-second epochs. A deep learning model was initially trained to differentiate between wake, sleep, and sleep with arousals, then collapsed to a binary wake/sleep output via a decision tree. A targeted training approach on subjects with low sleep efficiency and/or high arousal index was employed to specifically enhance wake detection. The model's performance was evaluated against PSG and tested for generalizability across devices and robustness in populations with various sleep disorders.

Key Findings

The deep learning model achieved an F1 Score of 0.86, sleep sensitivity of 0.87, and wakefulness specificity of 0.78. It demonstrated significant correlations with PSG for Total Sleep Time (R=0.69) and Sleep Efficiency (R=0.63). The model's performance was robust against the presence of common sleep disorders (e.g., sleep apnea, periodic limb movements in sleep) and maintained consistent accuracy across three different models of wrist accelerometers, indicating strong device generalizability.

Clinical Impact

This robust and generalizable deep learning model offers a non-invasive, cost-effective, and accurate alternative to PSG for long-term sleep-wake monitoring in clinical and research settings. It can significantly improve the diagnosis, management, and longitudinal assessment of sleep disorders, particularly in primary care or home-based settings where PSG is impractical, and enhance precision in epidemiological sleep studies by providing reliable data across diverse populations and devices.

Abstract

Study Objectives: Wrist accelerometry is widely used for inferring sleep-wake state. Previous works demonstrated poor wake detection, without cross-device generalizability and validation in different age range and sleep disorders. We developed a robust deep learning model for to detect sleep-wakefulness from triaxial accelerometry and evaluated its validity across three devices and in a large adult population spanning a wide range of ages with and without sleep disorders. Methods: We collected wrist accelerometry simultaneous to polysomnography (PSG) in 453 adults undergoing clinical sleep testing at a tertiary care sleep laboratory, using three devices. We extracted features in 30-second epochs and trained a 3-class model to detect wake, sleep, and sleep with arousals, which was then collapsed into wake vs. sleep using a decision tree. To enhance wake detection, the model was specifically trained on randomly selected subjects with low sleep efficiency and/or high arousal index from one device recording and then tested on the remaining recordings. Results: The model showed high performance with F1 Score of 0.86, sensitivity (sleep) of 0.87, and specificity (wakefulness) of 0.78, and significant and moderate correlation to PSG in predicting total sleep time (R=0.69) and sleep efficiency (R=0.63). Model performance was robust to the presence of sleep disorders, including sleep apnea and periodic limb movements in sleep, and was consistent across all three models of accelerometer. Conclusions: We present a deep model to detect sleep-wakefulness from actigraphy in adults with relative robustness to the presence of sleep disorders and generalizability across diverse commonly used wrist accelerometers.
