A robust generalizable device-agnostic deep learning model for sleep-wake determination from triaxial wrist accelerometry

arXiv ID: 2512.01986v1
Published: 2025-12-01
Authors: Nasim Montazeri, Stone Yang, Dominik Luszczynski, John Zhang, Dharmendra Gurve, Andrew Centen, Maged Goubran, Andrew Lim
Categories: q-bio.QM, cs.LG
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00

Summary

This paper presents a novel, robust deep learning model designed to accurately determine sleep-wake states from triaxial wrist accelerometry. The model demonstrated high performance and strong generalizability, effectively overcoming previous limitations related to poor wake detection and lack of cross-device compatibility, even in diverse adult populations including those with various sleep disorders.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a highly accurate, non-invasive, and device-agnostic tool for objective sleep assessment, which is crucial for scalable, long-term monitoring, diagnosis, and management of sleep disorders outside of traditional laboratory settings.

AI Health Application

The paper presents an AI (deep learning) model designed for automated, accurate, and device-agnostic determination of sleep-wake states from wearable accelerometers. This application aims to provide a more accessible, less intrusive, and scalable alternative or complement to traditional polysomnography for monitoring sleep health, aiding in the screening and management of sleep disorders, and potentially facilitating longitudinal tracking of sleep patterns in both clinical and home settings.

Key Points

  • A deep learning model was developed to detect sleep-wakefulness from triaxial wrist accelerometry, addressing prior limitations in wake detection and generalizability.
  • The model was trained on data from 453 adults undergoing simultaneous polysomnography (PSG) and accelerometry from three different wrist devices at a tertiary sleep center.
  • It employed a 3-class classification (wake, sleep, sleep with arousals), subsequently collapsed to a binary wake/sleep state using a decision tree.
  • To enhance wake detection, the model utilized a targeted training approach on subjects with low sleep efficiency and/or high arousal index.
  • The model achieved high performance metrics: F1 Score of 0.86, sleep sensitivity of 0.87, and wakefulness specificity of 0.78.
  • Significant and moderate correlation with PSG was observed for Total Sleep Time (R=0.69) and Sleep Efficiency (R=0.63).
  • Performance was robust across different accelerometer devices and maintained in the presence of sleep disorders such as sleep apnea and periodic limb movements in sleep.

Methodology

The study collected simultaneous triaxial wrist accelerometry data from three different devices alongside polysomnography (PSG) in 453 adult patients at a tertiary care sleep laboratory. Features were extracted from 30-second epochs of accelerometry data. A deep learning model was trained as a 3-class classifier (wake, sleep, sleep with arousals), which was then collapsed into a binary wake vs. sleep using a decision tree. To specifically improve wake detection, the model was strategically trained on a subset of subjects exhibiting low sleep efficiency and/or high arousal index from one device, followed by testing on the remaining recordings. Model performance was assessed using F1 score, sensitivity, specificity, and correlation with PSG-derived sleep metrics.

Key Findings

The deep learning model demonstrated high overall performance with an F1 Score of 0.86, a sensitivity for sleep of 0.87, and a specificity for wakefulness of 0.78. It showed significant and moderate correlation with PSG for predicting total sleep time (R=0.69) and sleep efficiency (R=0.63). Crucially, the model's performance proved robust to the presence of common sleep disorders (e.g., sleep apnea, periodic limb movements in sleep) and was consistent across all three different models of wrist accelerometers used in the study.

Clinical Impact

This robust and generalizable deep learning model significantly advances the capabilities of actigraphy for sleep-wake determination. It offers a more accurate, accessible, and scalable method for objective sleep assessment in clinical practice, reducing reliance on costly and labor-intensive PSG for routine monitoring or screening. Its device-agnostic nature and effectiveness across diverse patient populations (including those with sleep disorders) facilitate broader adoption in personalized sleep medicine, remote patient monitoring, and the development of more reliable digital health solutions for sleep health management.

Abstract

Study Objectives: Wrist accelerometry is widely used for inferring sleep-wake state. Previous works demonstrated poor wake detection, without cross-device generalizability and validation in different age range and sleep disorders. We developed a robust deep learning model for to detect sleep-wakefulness from triaxial accelerometry and evaluated its validity across three devices and in a large adult population spanning a wide range of ages with and without sleep disorders. Methods: We collected wrist accelerometry simultaneous to polysomnography (PSG) in 453 adults undergoing clinical sleep testing at a tertiary care sleep laboratory, using three devices. We extracted features in 30-second epochs and trained a 3-class model to detect wake, sleep, and sleep with arousals, which was then collapsed into wake vs. sleep using a decision tree. To enhance wake detection, the model was specifically trained on randomly selected subjects with low sleep efficiency and/or high arousal index from one device recording and then tested on the remaining recordings. Results: The model showed high performance with F1 Score of 0.86, sensitivity (sleep) of 0.87, and specificity (wakefulness) of 0.78, and significant and moderate correlation to PSG in predicting total sleep time (R=0.69) and sleep efficiency (R=0.63). Model performance was robust to the presence of sleep disorders, including sleep apnea and periodic limb movements in sleep, and was consistent across all three models of accelerometer. Conclusions: We present a deep model to detect sleep-wakefulness from actigraphy in adults with relative robustness to the presence of sleep disorders and generalizability across diverse commonly used wrist accelerometers.
