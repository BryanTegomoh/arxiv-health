From Cold Start to Active Learning: Embedding-Based Scan Selection for Medical Image Segmentation

arXiv ID: 2601.18532v1
Published: 2026-01-26
Authors: Devon Levy, Bar Assayag, Laura Gaspar, Ilan Shimshoni, Bella Specktor-Fadida
Categories: cs.CV, cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a novel active learning framework for medical image segmentation, addressing the critical bottleneck of manual annotation. It proposes an embedding-based cold-start strategy using foundation models and clustering for diverse initial training, followed by an uncertainty and spatial diversity-guided sample selection. The method consistently improves segmentation accuracy, as measured by Dice coefficient and Hausdorff distance, over baseline methods across various X-ray and MRI datasets, particularly in low-data regimes.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medicine as it significantly accelerates the development and deployment of accurate AI-powered medical image segmentation models. By reducing the reliance on extensive manual annotations, it can lead to faster, more efficient, and potentially more precise disease monitoring, diagnosis, and treatment planning in clinical practice.

AI Health Application

The research proposes an AI-driven active learning framework to efficiently and accurately segment medical images (X-ray, MRI). This directly supports the development of AI tools for disease diagnosis, monitoring, and treatment planning by reducing the burden of manual annotation, thus accelerating the creation of robust medical AI models.

Key Points

  • Addresses the significant bottleneck of manual, time-consuming, and expertise-intensive medical image segmentation annotation using Active Learning (AL).
  • Proposes a novel 'cold-start' sampling strategy for initial training, leveraging foundation model embeddings combined with automated clustering (including automatic cluster count selection and proportional sampling).
  • Develops an active learning framework for subsequent sample selection that integrates uncertainty (e.g., entropy) with spatial diversity to prioritize informative samples.
  • Evaluated on three diverse medical imaging datasets: CheXmask (X-ray), Montgomery (X-ray), and SynthStrip (MRI), covering different modalities and anatomical regions.
  • Demonstrates significant improvements in segmentation accuracy, measured by Dice coefficient and Hausdorff distance, for both the cold-start phase and the full AL framework compared to random selection.
  • On CheXmask, the cold-start strategy alone improved Dice from 0.918 to 0.929 and reduced Hausdorff distance from 32.41 to 27.66 mm, while the full AL framework further enhanced Dice to 0.939 and reduced HD to 19.16 mm.
  • The proposed method is intuitive, interpretable, and allows visualization of candidate samples' distribution in the feature space.

Methodology

The methodology comprises a two-stage active learning pipeline. The initial 'cold-start' phase selects a diverse and representative dataset by first extracting foundation model embeddings for all unlabeled samples. These embeddings are then clustered, with the number of clusters automatically determined, and samples are proportionally selected from each cluster. The subsequent active learning phase employs an uncertainty-based sampling strategy, which is enhanced by integrating spatial diversity to guide the selection of new samples for annotation. The framework is designed to be interpretable, allowing visualization of the feature-space distribution of candidate samples.

Key Findings

The proposed framework consistently outperformed baseline methods across all tested datasets. On CheXmask, the cold-start improved Dice from 0.918 to 0.929 (HD 32.41 to 27.66 mm), and the AL framework further improved Dice to 0.939 (HD 30.10 to 19.16 mm). For Montgomery, the cold-start substantially improved Dice from 0.928 to 0.950 and reduced HD from 14.22 to 9.38 mm. On SynthStrip, while cold-start's Dice effect was slight, it reduced HD from 9.43 to 8.69 mm, and the AL phase improved Dice from 0.816 to 0.826 and reduced HD from 7.76 to 6.38 mm. These results indicate superior segmentation accuracy, particularly in low-data regimes, for both X-ray and MRI modalities.

Clinical Impact

This research has the potential to significantly alleviate the annotation burden on clinical experts, enabling faster iteration and development of high-performing medical image segmentation models. By requiring less labeled data to achieve high accuracy, it can accelerate the adoption of AI in clinical workflows, leading to more efficient disease diagnosis, monitoring of treatment response, and precise surgical planning. This translates to cost savings, faster access to advanced diagnostics, and potentially improved patient outcomes by enabling earlier and more accurate interventions.

Abstract

Accurate segmentation annotations are critical for disease monitoring, yet manual labeling remains a major bottleneck due to the time and expertise required. Active learning (AL) alleviates this burden by prioritizing informative samples for annotation, typically through a diversity-based cold-start phase followed by uncertainty-driven selection. We propose a novel cold-start sampling strategy that combines foundation-model embeddings with clustering, including automatic selection of the number of clusters and proportional sampling across clusters, to construct a diverse and representative initial training. This is followed by an uncertainty-based AL framework that integrates spatial diversity to guide sample selection. The proposed method is intuitive and interpretable, enabling visualization of the feature-space distribution of candidate samples. We evaluate our approach on three datasets spanning X-ray and MRI modalities. On the CheXmask dataset, the cold-start strategy outperforms random selection, improving Dice from 0.918 to 0.929 and reducing the Hausdorff distance from 32.41 to 27.66 mm. In the AL setting, combined entropy and diversity selection improves Dice from 0.919 to 0.939 and reduces the Hausdorff distance from 30.10 to 19.16 mm. On the Montgomery dataset, cold-start gains are substantial, with Dice improving from 0.928 to 0.950 and Hausdorff distance decreasing from 14.22 to 9.38 mm. On the SynthStrip dataset, cold-start selection slightly affects Dice but reduces the Hausdorff distance from 9.43 to 8.69 mm, while active learning improves Dice from 0.816 to 0.826 and reduces the Hausdorff distance from 7.76 to 6.38 mm. Overall, the proposed framework consistently outperforms baseline methods in low-data regimes, improving segmentation accuracy.
