Summary

This paper introduces a 2-parameter generalized-growth model to accurately characterize the early ascending phase of infectious disease outbreaks, encompassing profiles from sub-exponential to exponential growth. By applying this model to 20 diverse outbreaks, the study reveals significant variability in growth patterns and identifies sub-exponential growth as a common phenomenon. This improved understanding is crucial for dissecting transmission drivers and enhancing disease forecasting capabilities.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant for public health and infectious disease management as it provides a more nuanced and accurate framework for modeling early outbreak dynamics. A better characterization of growth patterns, especially the prevalence of sub-exponential growth, allows for more precise forecasting, improved evaluation of intervention effectiveness, and more informed resource allocation during epidemics.

AI Health Application

The generalized-growth model presented in the paper provides a refined method for infectious disease forecasting. This type of computational model is crucial for informing public health interventions and resource planning, areas where AI and advanced analytics are increasingly applied. While the model itself is not an AI, its outputs (improved disease forecasts) are direct inputs for AI-driven disease surveillance systems, outbreak detection, and predictive analytics tools used in medical AI applications for public health and biosecurity.

Key Points

  • A novel 2-parameter generalized-growth model is introduced to characterize the ascending phase of outbreaks, capable of capturing growth dynamics from sub-exponential to exponential.
  • The model was tested against empirical data from 20 diverse infectious disease outbreaks, including viral pathogens like Ebola, smallpox, and influenza, across various transmission routes.
  • Significant variation in epidemic growth patterns was observed, with 'p' values ranging from very slow sub-exponential (e.g., Ebola in Bomi, Liberia, p=0.14) to near exponential (e.g., smallpox in Khulna, p>0.9).
  • Sub-exponential growth profiles are highlighted as a common phenomenon in the early stages of outbreaks, challenging the sole reliance on exponential growth assumptions.
  • Geographic heterogeneity in growth profiles was demonstrated through analysis of the West African Ebola epidemic, revealing substantial district-level variations (mean p=0.59, range: 0.14-0.97).
  • Potential drivers for sub-exponential growth include heterogeneity in contact structures, varied risk groups, reactive behavioral changes within populations, or the early implementation of intervention strategies.
  • The findings emphasize the utility of considering "deceleration parameters" to refine existing mathematical transmission models and improve the accuracy of disease forecasts.

Methodology

The study involved introducing a novel 2-parameter generalized-growth model designed to characterize the ascending phase of outbreaks and capture growth profiles ranging from sub-exponential to exponential. This model was then rigorously tested and applied to empirical data from 20 distinct infectious disease outbreaks caused by a variety of viral pathogens and across different transmission routes. Simulations were also utilized to underscore the importance of accurately capturing sub-exponential growth for forecasting purposes.

Key Findings

The study's key findings include the observation of significant variability in epidemic growth patterns across different infectious disease outbreaks. It highlights that sub-exponential growth is a common phenomenon in the early ascending phase, with growth profiles ('p' values) ranging widely from very slow (e.g., p=0.14 for Ebola in Bomi, Liberia) to near exponential (e.g., p>0.9 for smallpox in Khulna). Geographic heterogeneity in growth was also evident, as shown by substantial variations in district-level outbreaks during the West African Ebola epidemic. These sub-exponential patterns are attributed to factors such as heterogeneity in contact structures, risk groups, reactive behavioral changes, or early interventions.

Clinical Impact

The findings have direct clinical and public health impact by enabling more accurate disease forecasting, which is critical for timely resource allocation (e.g., hospital beds, medical personnel, vaccines, therapeutics) during outbreaks. By accounting for sub-exponential growth and 'deceleration parameters', public health interventions can be better designed and their efficacy more precisely evaluated. This refined modeling approach can lead to more effective outbreak control strategies and improved patient outcomes through better preparation and response.

Limitations

The abstract itself does not explicitly state limitations of the generalized-growth model. However, as the model is designed to characterize the 'early ascending phase', its applicability to the peak or descending phases of an epidemic, or to outbreaks with highly complex, multi-wave dynamics, may require further validation or modifications.

Future Directions

The paper suggests that future research should focus on incorporating "deceleration parameters" into existing mathematical transmission models to refine their accuracy and improve disease forecasts. This implies a need for further work in integrating the insights from generalized-growth models into more complex epidemiological frameworks and exploring the specific contextual factors driving sub-exponential growth in various disease systems.

Abstract

A better characterization of the early growth dynamics of an epidemic is needed to dissect the important drivers of disease transmission. We introduce a 2-parameter generalized-growth model to characterize the ascending phase of an outbreak and capture epidemic profiles ranging from sub-exponential to exponential growth. We test the model against empirical outbreak data representing a variety of viral pathogens and provide simulations highlighting the importance of sub-exponential growth for forecasting purposes. We applied the generalized-growth model to 20 infectious disease outbreaks representing a range of transmission routes. We uncovered epidemic profiles ranging from very slow growth (p=0.14 for the Ebola outbreak in Bomi, Liberia (2014)) to near exponential (p>0.9 for the smallpox outbreak in Khulna (1972), and the 1918 pandemic influenza in San Francisco). The foot-and-mouth disease outbreak in Uruguay displayed a profile of slower growth while the growth pattern of the HIV/AIDS epidemic in Japan was approximately linear. The West African Ebola epidemic provided a unique opportunity to explore how growth profiles vary by geography; analysis of the largest district-level outbreaks revealed substantial growth variations (mean p=0.59, range: 0.14-0.97). Our findings reveal significant variation in epidemic growth patterns across different infectious disease outbreaks and highlights that sub-exponential growth is a common phenomenon. Sub-exponential growth profiles may result from heterogeneity in contact structures or risk groups, reactive behavior changes, or the early onset of interventions strategies, and consideration of "deceleration parameters" may be useful to refine existing mathematical transmission models and improve disease forecasts.
