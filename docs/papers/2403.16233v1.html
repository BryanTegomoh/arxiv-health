An early warning indicator trained on stochastic disease-spreading models with different noises

Summary

This paper introduces a novel deep learning-based early warning indicator (EWS) for infectious disease outbreaks, specifically designed to function robustly amidst diverse real-world noise sources like additive white, multiplicative environmental, and demographic noise. By training on stochastic disease-spreading models incorporating these noise types, the indicator demonstrates superior performance in capturing impending outbreak transitions compared to existing methods, as validated on real-world COVID-19 data and simulated time series.

Medical Relevance

Reliable and timely early warning signals for infectious disease outbreaks are crucial for implementing effective public health mitigation strategies, reducing morbidity and mortality, and optimizing resource allocation during epidemics and pandemics.

AI Health Application

A deep learning algorithm is developed and trained on noisy disease-spreading models to provide reliable early warning signals for infectious disease outbreaks. This AI application helps in the timely detection of impending outbreaks, thereby enhancing public health preparedness and enabling more effective response efforts, as demonstrated with real-world COVID-19 data.

Methodology

The methodology involves integrating additive white noise, multiplicative environmental noise, and demographic noise into standard epidemic mathematical models to generate realistic, noise-corrupted disease spread time series. A deep learning algorithm is then trained on these synthetic, noise-induced disease-spreading models to develop an early warning indicator. The trained indicator's performance is subsequently evaluated on real-world COVID-19 case data and additional simulated noisy time series, comparing its efficacy against existing EWS methods.

Key Findings

The deep learning-based early warning indicator successfully captures impending transitions within disease outbreak time series, even when measurements are corrupted by significant noise. The indicator consistently outperforms existing early warning indicators in terms of reliability and timeliness in detecting these critical transitions.

This enhanced capability can lead to more proactive and effective public health interventions, such as targeted testing, contact tracing, vaccination campaigns, and non-pharmaceutical interventions, thereby potentially reducing the scale and impact of future epidemics and improving patient outcomes through timely resource mobilization.

Abstract

The timely detection of disease outbreaks through reliable early warning signals (EWSs) is indispensable for effective public health mitigation strategies. Nevertheless, the intricate dynamics of real-world disease spread, often influenced by diverse sources of noise and limited data in the early stages of outbreaks, pose a significant challenge in developing reliable EWSs, as the performance of existing indicators varies with extrinsic and intrinsic noises. Here, we address the challenge of modeling disease when the measurements are corrupted by additive white noise, multiplicative environmental noise, and demographic noise into a standard epidemic mathematical model. To navigate the complexities introduced by these noise sources, we employ a deep learning algorithm that provides EWS in infectious disease outbreak by training on noise-induced disease-spreading models. The indicator's effectiveness is demonstrated through its application to real-world COVID-19 cases in Edmonton and simulated time series derived from diverse disease spread models affected by noise. Notably, the indicator captures an impending transition in a time series of disease outbreaks and outperforms existing indicators. This study contributes to advancing early warning capabilities by addressing the intricate dynamics inherent in real-world disease spread, presenting a promising avenue for enhancing public health preparedness and response efforts.
