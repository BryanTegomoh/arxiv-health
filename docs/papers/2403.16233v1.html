An early warning indicator trained on stochastic disease-spreading models with different noises

Published: 2024-03-24
Authors: Amit K. Chakraborty, Shan Gao, Reza Miry, Pouria Ramazi, Russell Greiner, Mark A. Lewis, Hao Wang

Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00

Summary

This paper introduces a novel deep learning-based early warning indicator (EWI) designed for timely detection of infectious disease outbreaks, specifically addressing the challenges posed by diverse noise sources and limited early-stage data. The indicator, trained on stochastic disease-spreading models incorporating additive white, multiplicative environmental, and demographic noise, demonstrates superior performance in capturing impending transitions compared to existing indicators, as validated on real-world COVID-19 cases and simulated time series.

Medical Relevance

Reliable and timely detection of disease outbreaks is paramount for effective public health intervention, enabling rapid implementation of mitigation strategies to control spread, reduce morbidity, and save lives. This research offers a robust tool for improving outbreak surveillance in realistic, noisy data environments.

AI Health Application

The paper describes a deep learning algorithm that provides early warning signals (EWS) for infectious disease outbreaks. It achieves this by training on stochastic disease-spreading models that incorporate various noise sources, and its effectiveness is validated on real-world COVID-19 case data. This is a direct application of AI (deep learning) to improve the detection and prediction of disease outbreaks, thereby enhancing public health preparedness and response.

Key Points

  • The research addresses the critical challenge of developing reliable early warning signals (EWSs) for disease outbreaks amidst complex real-world dynamics, various noise sources, and data scarcity.
  • It explicitly models disease spread by integrating additive white noise, multiplicative environmental noise, and demographic noise into a standard epidemic mathematical framework.
  • A deep learning algorithm is developed and trained on synthetic time series generated from these noise-corrupted stochastic disease-spreading models to provide early warning signals.
  • The indicator's efficacy is rigorously tested using both simulated time series derived from diverse noisy disease spread models and real-world COVID-19 case data from Edmonton.
  • A significant finding is the indicator's ability to effectively capture an impending transition within disease outbreak time series, signifying an early detection capability.
  • The proposed deep learning-based EWI consistently outperforms existing early warning indicators, demonstrating enhanced robustness and accuracy in noisy environments.
  • This study advances public health preparedness by providing a promising tool for early detection, contributing to more effective mitigation strategies against infectious disease outbreaks.

Methodology

The methodology involves training a deep learning algorithm on a synthetic dataset generated from standard epidemic mathematical models. These models are perturbed by three distinct types of noise: additive white noise, multiplicative environmental noise, and demographic noise. This noisy data simulates real-world disease spread dynamics. The trained deep learning model then acts as an early warning indicator by analyzing time series data to detect critical transitions indicative of an impending outbreak. Validation was performed on both diverse simulated time series and real-world COVID-19 case data.

Key Findings

The deep learning-based early warning indicator effectively identifies and captures impending critical transitions within disease outbreak time series. Crucially, it demonstrates superior performance and robustness compared to existing early warning indicators across scenarios involving various noise types and limited data, validated by both synthetic noisy datasets and real-world COVID-19 incidence data.

Clinical Impact

This EWI can significantly enhance public health surveillance systems by providing more reliable and earlier alerts of emerging infectious disease outbreaks. This allows public health authorities to initiate containment measures, allocate resources, and communicate risks more proactively, thereby reducing the peak burden on healthcare systems, minimizing disease spread, and ultimately saving lives and mitigating socioeconomic impact.

Abstract

The timely detection of disease outbreaks through reliable early warning signals (EWSs) is indispensable for effective public health mitigation strategies. Nevertheless, the intricate dynamics of real-world disease spread, often influenced by diverse sources of noise and limited data in the early stages of outbreaks, pose a significant challenge in developing reliable EWSs, as the performance of existing indicators varies with extrinsic and intrinsic noises. Here, we address the challenge of modeling disease when the measurements are corrupted by additive white noise, multiplicative environmental noise, and demographic noise into a standard epidemic mathematical model. To navigate the complexities introduced by these noise sources, we employ a deep learning algorithm that provides EWS in infectious disease outbreak by training on noise-induced disease-spreading models. The indicator's effectiveness is demonstrated through its application to real-world COVID-19 cases in Edmonton and simulated time series derived from diverse disease spread models affected by noise. Notably, the indicator captures an impending transition in a time series of disease outbreaks and outperforms existing indicators. This study contributes to advancing early warning capabilities by addressing the intricate dynamics inherent in real-world disease spread, presenting a promising avenue for enhancing public health preparedness and response efforts.
