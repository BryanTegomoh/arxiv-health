Summary

SCORE (Segmentation COrrection from Regional Evaluations) is a novel weakly supervised framework designed to refine automated medical image segmentations using only light feedback during training, significantly reducing the need for dense annotations. It introduces a unique loss function leveraging region-wise quality scores and over/under-segmentation error labels, demonstrating considerable improvement over initial predictions from foundation models like TotalSegmentator while matching existing refinement methods' performance with much less supervision.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medical imaging as it tackles the crucial challenge of obtaining clinically accurate anatomical segmentations efficiently. By reducing the manual workload for refining automated segmentations, it can accelerate diagnostic processes, improve the precision of treatment planning, and enhance patient care across various medical specialties.

AI Health Application

SCORE is an AI framework that uses weakly supervised learning to refine automated segmentations of anatomical regions in medical images (e.g., humerus CT scans). This application aims to improve the accuracy and efficiency of medical image analysis, reduce labor-intensity and variability in clinical workflows, and enhance the reliability of automated diagnostic tools.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical need for accurate medical image segmentation, overcoming the limitations of manual methods (labor-intensive, variable) and fully automated foundation models (insufficient clinical accuracy).
  • Introduces SCORE, a weakly supervised framework specifically for refining initial segmentation mask predictions from automated tools.
  • Employs a novel loss function that leverages 'light feedback' in the form of region-wise quality scores and over/under-segmentation error labels, rather than dense pixel-level annotations.
  • Demonstrated on humerus CT scans, SCORE considerably improved initial segmentation predictions generated by TotalSegmentator.
  • Achieves refinement performance on par with existing, more heavily supervised segmentation refinement methods.
  • Significantly reduces the supervision requirements and manual annotation time typically associated with training segmentation refinement models, enhancing resource efficiency.
  • The approach aims to bridge the gap between initial automated segmentations and the high accuracy standards required in clinical practice with minimal user interaction.

Methodology

SCORE is a weakly supervised deep learning framework designed for segmentation refinement. Instead of relying on dense, pixel-level ground truth annotations for training, it utilizes a novel loss function incorporating 'light feedback' signals. These signals include region-wise quality scores and binary labels indicating over-segmentation or under-segmentation errors. The framework was evaluated by refining initial humerus segmentations generated by the TotalSegmentator model on CT scans.

Key Findings

SCORE considerably improves the accuracy of initial automated segmentations, as demonstrated on humerus CT scans using TotalSegmentator predictions. It achieves a level of refinement performance comparable to existing methods, while critically reducing their supervision requirements and annotation time. This validates its effectiveness in enhancing segmentation quality with greater resource efficiency.

Clinical Impact

The clinical impact lies in enabling more accurate and efficient anatomical segmentation for medical professionals. By providing a method to refine automated segmentations with minimal expert input, SCORE can significantly reduce the manual workload for radiologists and clinicians. This could lead to faster turnaround times for image analysis, more precise diagnosis, improved surgical planning, and a higher standard of care, especially in scenarios where manual segmentation is prohibitively time-consuming.

Limitations

The demonstration of SCORE is specifically on humerus CT scans, implying that its generalizability to a broader range of anatomies, different imaging modalities (e.g., MRI, ultrasound), or various pathologies has not been explicitly shown in this work. The precise nature and effort involved in generating the 'light feedback' (region-wise quality scores and over/under-segmentation error labels) are not fully detailed.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated in the abstract, future work would likely involve validating SCORE's performance and generalizability across diverse anatomical structures, imaging modalities, and clinical applications. Further research could also explore automated or semi-automated methods for generating the 'light feedback' to minimize human intervention even further, and integrating the framework into clinical workflows.

Abstract

Delineating anatomical regions is a key task in medical image analysis. Manual segmentation achieves high accuracy but is labor-intensive and prone to variability, thus prompting the development of automated approaches. Recently, a breadth of foundation models has enabled automated segmentations across diverse anatomies and imaging modalities, but these may not always meet the clinical accuracy standards. While segmentation refinement strategies can improve performance, current methods depend on heavy user interactions or require fully supervised segmentations for training. Here, we present SCORE (Segmentation COrrection from Regional Evaluations), a weakly supervised framework that learns to refine mask predictions only using light feedback during training. Specifically, instead of relying on dense training image annotations, SCORE introduces a novel loss that leverages region-wise quality scores and over/under-segmentation error labels. We demonstrate SCORE on humerus CT scans, where it considerably improves initial predictions from TotalSegmentator, and achieves performance on par with existing refinement methods, while greatly reducing their supervision requirements and annotation time. Our code is available at: https://gitlab.inria.fr/adelangl/SCORE.
