DANIEL: A Distributed and Scalable Approach for Global Representation Learning with EHR Applications

Summary

This paper introduces DANIEL, a distributed and scalable framework that leverages a revisited Ising model to perform privacy-preserving representation learning from large-scale, high-dimensional binary medical data. It addresses challenges of data heterogeneity and sharing constraints in modern healthcare by optimizing a non-convex surrogate loss function via bi-factored gradient descent. Evaluated on multi-institutional EHR data from 58,248 patients, DANIEL demonstrates superior performance in global representation learning and critical downstream clinical tasks like patient phenotyping and clustering.

Medical Relevance

This research offers a critical solution for integrating and analyzing vast, disparate healthcare datasets while maintaining patient privacy and data security. It enables the extraction of meaningful, globally representative insights from multi-institutional EHRs, which can lead to better understanding of diseases, more precise patient stratification, and improved clinical decision support.

The DANIEL framework revisits the Ising model, a probabilistic graphical model, and implements a distributed approach. It optimizes a non-convex surrogate loss function using bi-factored gradient descent to achieve scalable and privacy-preserving representation learning from large-scale binary data with low-rank structure. This methodology prioritizes computational and communication efficiency, making it suitable for federated settings.

DANIEL successfully overcomes challenges associated with high-dimensionality and data-sharing constraints in multi-institutional EHR data. The framework demonstrates superior performance in learning global representations from diverse patient data and significantly improves the accuracy of critical downstream clinical tasks, including relationship detection among clinical entities, patient phenotyping, and clustering patients based on shared characteristics.

Abstract

Classical probabilistic graphical models face fundamental challenges in modern data environments, which are characterized by high dimensionality, source heterogeneity, and stringent data-sharing constraints. In this work, we revisit the Ising model, a well-established member of the Markov Random Field (MRF) family, and develop a distributed framework that enables scalable and privacy-preserving representation learning from large-scale binary data with inherent low-rank structure. Our approach optimizes a non-convex surrogate loss function via bi-factored gradient descent, offering substantial computational and communication advantages over conventional convex approaches. We evaluate our algorithm on multi-institutional electronic health record (EHR) datasets from 58,248 patients across the University of Pittsburgh Medical Center (UPMC) and Mass General Brigham (MGB), demonstrating superior performance in global representation learning and downstream clinical tasks, including relationship detection, patient phenotyping, and patient clustering. These results highlight a broader potential for statistical inference in federated, high-dimensional settings while addressing the practical challenges of data complexity and multi-institutional integration.
