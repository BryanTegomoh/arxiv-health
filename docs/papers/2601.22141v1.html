Routing the Lottery: Adaptive Subnetworks for Heterogeneous Data

arXiv ID: 2601.22141v1
Published: 2026-01-29
Authors: Grzegorz Stefanski, Alberto Presta, Michal Byra
Categories: cs.AI, cs.CV, cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.80 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces Routing the Lottery (RTL), an adaptive pruning framework that generates multiple specialized subnetworks, or 'adaptive tickets', tailored to specific classes, semantic clusters, or conditions within heterogeneous datasets. RTL consistently outperforms traditional single and multi-model baselines in accuracy and recall, while drastically reducing parameter count, offering a novel approach to align model structure with inherent data diversity.

Medical Relevance

Medical data is inherently heterogeneous (e.g., varying patient populations, imaging protocols, disease manifestations, genetic profiles). This framework enables more robust and efficient AI models that can adapt to specific medical contexts or patient subgroups without requiring multiple full-sized models, thereby improving diagnostic accuracy, treatment personalization, and computational efficiency in healthcare applications.

AI Health Application

The proposed 'Routing the Lottery' framework could significantly enhance medical AI applications by creating more efficient and adaptable models. For instance, in medical imaging, it could allow a single AI model to adapt to different scanner types, image resolutions, or patient populations, providing more reliable diagnoses across diverse clinical settings while requiring fewer computational resources. In personalized medicine, it could enable models to specialize in specific patient subgroups or disease presentations, leading to more tailored treatment recommendations. Its ability to improve balanced accuracy and recall is crucial for detecting rare diseases or critical anomalies in imbalanced medical datasets. The efficiency gains (fewer parameters) would also facilitate deployment on edge devices for point-of-care diagnostics or wearables.

Key Points

  • Addresses the limitation of the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis (LTH) by moving beyond a single universal winning ticket to accommodate data heterogeneity.
  • Proposes Routing the Lottery (RTL), an adaptive pruning framework that discovers multiple specialized 'adaptive tickets' for different data subsets.
  • RTL demonstrates superior performance in balanced accuracy and recall compared to single- and multi-model baselines across diverse datasets and tasks.
  • Achieves significant model compression, utilizing up to 10 times fewer parameters than a collection of independent full models.
  • Identifies 'subnetwork collapse', a performance degradation under aggressive pruning, and introduces a 'subnetwork similarity score' for its label-free diagnosis.
  • The discovered subnetworks are shown to be semantically aligned with their respective data subsets.
  • Recasts neural network pruning as a mechanism for better aligning model architecture with data heterogeneity, promoting modular and context-aware deep learning.

Methodology

The authors propose Routing the Lottery (RTL), an adaptive pruning framework that dynamically identifies and trains multiple specialized subnetworks ('adaptive tickets'). These subnetworks are designed to be optimally suited for different data classes, semantic clusters, or environmental conditions. The framework also introduces a 'subnetwork similarity score' to diagnose 'subnetwork collapse' during aggressive pruning.

Key Findings

RTL consistently outperforms conventional single-model and multi-model baselines in terms of balanced accuracy and recall. It achieves these performance gains while significantly reducing model complexity, using up to 10 times fewer parameters than a set of independent models. The subnetworks generated by RTL exhibit strong semantic alignment with their target data subsets. The study also identifies and provides a diagnostic tool for 'subnetwork collapse', a common issue in aggressive pruning.

Clinical Impact

This research could lead to more accurate, reliable, and computationally efficient AI models in medicine. For instance, in medical imaging, one AI model could dynamically adapt its underlying structure to different scanner types, patient demographics, or disease stages, improving diagnostic consistency. In personalized medicine, it could create tailored predictive models for specific genetic variations or patient cohorts without the overhead of maintaining many large, independent models, facilitating more precise clinical decision-making and resource allocation.

Limitations

While the abstract highlights the identification of 'subnetwork collapse' as a phenomenon to be addressed in aggressive pruning, it does not explicitly state limitations of the RTL framework itself. Further research would be needed to understand the computational overhead of routing and training multiple subnetworks, and its generalization to extremely complex, multi-modal medical datasets with highly granular heterogeneity.

Future Directions

The work paves the way for more modular and context-aware deep learning systems. Future research could explore the application of RTL to real-world, highly complex medical datasets, investigating its ability to discover novel disease subtypes or treatment response groups, and to further optimize the routing mechanism for practical clinical deployment.

Abstract

In pruning, the Lottery Ticket Hypothesis posits that large networks contain sparse subnetworks, or winning tickets, that can be trained in isolation to match the performance of their dense counterparts. However, most existing approaches assume a single universal winning ticket shared across all inputs, ignoring the inherent heterogeneity of real-world data. In this work, we propose Routing the Lottery (RTL), an adaptive pruning framework that discovers multiple specialized subnetworks, called adaptive tickets, each tailored to a class, semantic cluster, or environmental condition. Across diverse datasets and tasks, RTL consistently outperforms single- and multi-model baselines in balanced accuracy and recall, while using up to 10 times fewer parameters than independent models and exhibiting semantically aligned. Furthermore, we identify subnetwork collapse, a performance drop under aggressive pruning, and introduce a subnetwork similarity score that enables label-free diagnosis of oversparsification. Overall, our results recast pruning as a mechanism for aligning model structure with data heterogeneity, paving the way toward more modular and context-aware deep learning.
