Optimizing Sensor Placement in Urban Storm Sewers: A Data-Driven Sparse Sensing Approach

arXiv ID: 2511.04556v1
Published: 2025-11-06
Authors: Zihang Ding, Kun Zhang
Categories: cs.AI, cs.CE
Relevance Score: 0.80 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This study presents a Data-Driven Sparse Sensing (DSS) framework, integrated with the EPA-SWMM model, to optimize sensor placement and accurately reconstruct peak flowrates in urban stormwater systems using minimal resources. Applied to a real-world catchment, the framework demonstrated that only three optimally placed sensors among 77 nodes could achieve high-accuracy flowrate reconstruction (NSE 0.92-0.95) and good robustness to measurement uncertainty, addressing a critical challenge in flood monitoring under practical constraints.

Methodology

The study utilized the EPA-SWMM model to generate a comprehensive training dataset consisting of peak flowrate profiles across the stormwater network of the Woodland Avenue catchment. A Data-Driven Sparse Sensing (DSS) framework was then applied, employing Singular Value Decomposition (SVD) for dimensionality reduction to capture dominant flow patterns and QR factorization for strategically allocating a minimal number of sensors. The performance of these identified optimal monitoring nodes was validated by comparing their DSS-reconstructed peak flowrate profiles against the full flowrate profiles obtained directly from the SWMM simulations.

Key Findings

The DSS framework successfully identified that only three optimally placed sensors out of 77 potential nodes were sufficient to achieve high-accuracy peak flowrate reconstruction, indicated by Nash-Sutcliffe Efficiency (NSE) values between 0.92 and 0.95. Furthermore, the model exhibited good robustness against measurement uncertainty and showed location-dependent robustness to sensor failures, which improved with the deployment of additional sensors.

Limitations

The abstract implicitly suggests limitations by noting that robustness to sensor failures is 'location-dependent' and 'improves with the number of sensors deployed,' indicating conditions under which performance may vary. The validation was performed against a simulated SWMM dataset rather than real-world sensor data, which might introduce discrepancies when applied in practice. The computational cost for SVD and QR factorization for very large networks is not explicitly discussed but could be a consideration.

Future Directions

The authors suggest that the developed DSS framework can be further integrated with advanced predictive models to achieve real-time flood early warning capabilities and facilitate real-time control within urban stormwater systems, especially under constraints of limited sensing and monitoring resources.

Abstract

Urban surface water flooding, triggered by intense rainfall overwhelming drainage systems, is increasingly frequent and widespread. While flood prediction and monitoring in high spatial-temporal resolution are desired, practical constraints in time, budget, and technology hinder its full implementation. How to monitor urban drainage networks and predict flow conditions under constrained resource is a major challenge. This study presents a data-driven sparse sensing (DSS) framework, integrated with EPA-SWMM, to optimize sensor placement and reconstruct peak flowrates in a stormwater system, using the Woodland Avenue catchment in Duluth, Minnesota, as a case study. We utilized a SWMM model to generate a training dataset of peak flowrate profiles across the stormwater network. Furthermore, we applied DSS - leveraging singular value decomposition for dimensionality reduction and QR factorization for sensor allocation - to identify the optimal monitoring nodes based on the simulated training dataset. We then validated the representativeness of these identified monitoring nodes by comparing the DSS-reconstructed peak flowrate profiles with those obtained from SWMM. Three optimally placed sensors among 77 nodes achieved satisfactory reconstruction performance with Nash-Sutcliffe Efficiency (NSE) values of 0.92-0.95 (25th to 75th percentiles). In addition, the model showed good robustness to uncertainty in measurements. Its robustness to sensor failures is location-dependent and improves with the number of sensors deployed. The framework balances computational efficiency and physical interpretability, enabling high-accuracy flow reconstruction with minimal sensors. This DSS framework can be further integrated with predictive models to realize flood early warning and real-time control under limited sensing and monitoring resource.
