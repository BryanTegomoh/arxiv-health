On Multiple Robustness of Proximal Dynamic Treatment Regimes

Summary

This paper addresses the critical challenge of estimating optimal dynamic treatment regimes (DTRs) from observational data when the standard unconfoundedness assumption, crucial for causal inference, is violated. It introduces a proximal causal inference framework to overcome this limitation, proposing novel nonparametric identification methods, establishing a semiparametric efficiency bound, and developing a (K+1)-robust estimation method for learning optimal DTRs, with K representing the number of treatment stages.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant for advancing precision medicine and personalized healthcare by enabling the robust and ethical estimation of optimal adaptive treatment strategies from readily available, but often confounded, historical observational patient data, circumventing the limitations of costly and difficult sequential randomized trials.

AI Health Application

This research provides fundamental statistical and machine learning methods for developing medical AI applications that aim to optimize dynamic treatment regimes for individual patients. Specifically, it enables AI systems to learn personalized, adaptive treatment strategies from complex, real-world observational patient data, even when traditional causal inference assumptions (like unconfoundedness) are violated. This directly supports AI for personalized treatment planning, adaptive clinical guidelines, and intelligent drug dosing.

Key Points

  • Focuses on estimating optimal dynamic treatment regimes (DTRs), which are sequential decision rules adapting treatment according to individual time-varying characteristics and outcomes.
  • Addresses the significant challenge of unmeasured confounding by utilizing the proximal causal inference framework, enabling robust causal inference even when the unconfoundedness assumption fails.
  • Proposes three distinct nonparametric identification methods for optimal DTRs, allowing for flexible estimation without strong parametric assumptions.
  • Establishes the semiparametric efficiency bound for the value function of a given DTR, providing a theoretical benchmark for estimation precision.
  • Develops a novel (K+1)-robust estimation method for learning optimal DTRs, where K is the number of stages, enhancing reliability against potential model misspecifications.
  • Includes, as a by-product for marginal structural models (MSMs), the identification and estimation of counterfactual means under static regimes.
  • Numerical experiments validate the efficiency and multiple robustness properties of the proposed methods, demonstrating their practical applicability.

Abstract

Dynamic treatment regimes are sequential decision rules that adapt treatment according to individual time-varying characteristics and outcomes to achieve optimal effects, with applications in precision medicine, personalized recommendations, and dynamic marketing. Estimating optimal dynamic treatment regimes via sequential randomized trials might face costly and ethical hurdles, often necessitating the use of historical observational data. In this work, we utilize proximal causal inference framework for learning optimal dynamic treatment regimes when the unconfoundedness assumption fails. Our contributions are four-fold: (i) we propose three nonparametric identification methods for optimal dynamic treatment regimes; (ii) we establish the semiparametric efficiency bound for the value function of a given regime; (iii) we propose a (K+1)-robust method for learning optimal dynamic treatment regimes, where K is the number of stages; (iv) as a by-product for marginal structural models, we establish identification and estimation of counterfactual means under a static regime. Numerical experiments validate the efficiency and multiple robustness of our proposed methods.
