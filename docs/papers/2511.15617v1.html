Summary

This paper demonstrates the successful application of a comprehensive framework for artifact-free Zero Echo Time (ZTE) imaging on a portable, low-cost Halbach MRI scanner. It showcases the ability to perform in vivo qualitative imaging and quantitative T1 mapping of both soft and hard musculoskeletal tissues, previously challenging at low fields, within clinically viable acquisition times, thereby broadening the capabilities of portable MRI systems.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant for medical diagnostics as it enables detailed structural imaging and quantitative assessment of hard and soft musculoskeletal tissues using portable, low-cost MRI scanners. This advancement can democratize access to advanced MRI, improve point-of-care diagnostics in various clinical settings, and facilitate monitoring of conditions like osteoarthritis or sports injuries in resource-limited environments.

Methodology

The study developed a comprehensive framework for artifact-free Zero Echo Time (ZTE) imaging at low magnetic fields. This involved: (i) optimizing RF pulse characteristics (pre/counteremphasis calibration) to mitigate ring-down and electronics switching artifacts; (ii) extending a single-point double-shot (SPDS) protocol to simultaneously map B0 and B1 field inhomogeneities; and (iii) implementing a model-based reconstruction that incorporates these acquired field maps into the image encoding matrix. ZTE imaging (using an optimized PETRA sequence) and Variable Flip Angle (VFA) T1 mapping were performed on phantoms and subsequently applied in vivo to human knees and ankles. The results were benchmarked against conventional RARE and STIR sequences.

Key Findings

The optimized PETRA sequence successfully generated 3D images of human knees and ankles within clinically acceptable acquisition times (< 15 min), clearly visualizing hard tissues like ligaments, tendons, cartilage, and bone, which are typically unobservable with conventional spin-echo sequences. The newly extended SPDS method proved effective in achieving accurate B0 and B1 field mapping. Furthermore, the VFA approach enabled the first ever in vivo T1 measurements of hard tissues at very low magnetic fields (B0 < 0.1 T).

Abstract

Purpose: To demonstrate the feasibility of performing in vivo imaging and quantitative relaxation mapping of soft and hard tissues using a low-cost, portable MRI scanner, and to establish the methodological foundations for zero echo time (ZTE) imaging in systems affected by strong field inhomogeneities. Methods: A complete framework for artifact-free ZTE imaging at low field was developed, including: (i) RF pulse pre/counteremphasis calibration to minimize ring-down and electronics switching time; (ii) an extension of a recent single-point double-shot (SPDS) protocol for simultaneous B0 and B1 mapping; and (iii) a model-based reconstruction incorporating these field maps into the encoding matrix. ZTE imaging and variable flip angle (VFA) T1 mapping were performed on phantoms and in vivo human knees and ankles, and benchmarked against standard RARE and STIR acquisitions. Results: The optimized PETRA sequence produced 3D images of knees and ankles within clinically compatible times (< 15 min), revealing hard tissues such as ligaments, tendons, cartilage, and bone that are invisible in spin-echo sequences. The extended SPDS method enabled accurate field mapping, while the VFA approach provided the first in vivo T1 measurements of hard tissues at B0 < 0.1 T. Conclusions: The proposed framework broadens the range of pulse sequences feasible in portable low-field MRI and demonstrates the potential of ZTE for quantitative and structural imaging of musculoskeletal tissues in affordable Halbach-based systems.
