Summary

This paper demonstrates the feasibility of performing high-quality in vivo imaging and quantitative relaxation mapping of both soft and hard tissues using low-cost, portable Halbach MRI scanners. It establishes a comprehensive methodological framework for artifact-free Zero Echo Time (ZTE) imaging in systems with strong field inhomogeneities, enabling clear visualization of musculoskeletal structures previously invisible at low fields and providing quantitative T1 measurements.

Medical Relevance

This research is medically relevant as it significantly lowers the barrier to advanced MRI diagnostics, making it more accessible and affordable. By enabling detailed structural and quantitative assessment of musculoskeletal tissues with portable, low-field systems, it could improve point-of-care diagnostics, particularly in underserved regions, emergency departments, or orthopedic clinics.

  • A novel and complete framework for artifact-free Zero Echo Time (ZTE) imaging at low magnetic fields was developed, specifically addressing strong field inhomogeneities inherent to such systems.
  • The methodological framework includes RF pulse pre/counteremphasis calibration, an extended Single-Point Double-Shot (SPDS) protocol for simultaneous B0 and B1 field mapping, and model-based image reconstruction incorporating these field maps.
  • Optimized PETRA sequences produced 3D images of human knees and ankles within clinically compatible times (< 15 min), successfully revealing hard tissues (ligaments, tendons, cartilage, bone) that are typically invisible in conventional spin-echo sequences.
  • The extended SPDS method enabled accurate field mapping, crucial for robust image reconstruction in low-field, inhomogeneous environments.
  • The Variable Flip Angle (VFA) approach facilitated the first reported in vivo T1 measurements of hard tissues at ultra-low magnetic fields (B0 < 0.1 T).

Methodology

A comprehensive framework for artifact-free Zero Echo Time (ZTE) imaging at low magnetic fields was developed. This included: (i) meticulous calibration of RF pulse pre/counteremphasis to mitigate ring-down and electronics switching artifacts; (ii) an extended Single-Point Double-Shot (SPDS) protocol for simultaneous and accurate B0 and B1 field mapping; and (iii) a model-based image reconstruction method that incorporates these measured field maps directly into the encoding matrix. ZTE imaging, utilizing an optimized PETRA sequence, and Variable Flip Angle (VFA) T1 mapping were performed on phantoms and in vivo human knees and ankles, with results benchmarked against standard RARE and STIR acquisitions.

Key Findings

The optimized PETRA sequence successfully generated 3D images of human knees and ankles within clinically viable times (< 15 minutes), uniquely resolving hard tissues such as ligaments, tendons, cartilage, and bone, which are typically not visible with conventional spin-echo sequences. The extended SPDS method proved effective in providing accurate B0 and B1 field maps, crucial for the model-based reconstruction. Furthermore, the VFA approach enabled the first in vivo T1 measurements of hard tissues at ultra-low magnetic fields (B0 < 0.1 T) using these portable systems.

Clinical Impact

This research has the potential to significantly impact clinical practice by enabling high-quality and quantitative musculoskeletal MRI scans with affordable, portable systems. This could facilitate earlier and more accurate diagnosis of orthopedic injuries, degenerative joint diseases, and other conditions in a wider range of clinical settings, including remote areas, underserved communities, or at the point of care. The ability to image hard tissues and provide quantitative T1 maps could lead to improved patient management, personalized treatment strategies, and reduced healthcare costs by making advanced imaging more accessible.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations. However, by establishing "methodological foundations," it implies that further validation or broader clinical application may be necessary. While a significant advance for low-field systems, intrinsic differences in image resolution or signal-to-noise ratio compared to high-field MRI systems for certain diagnostic tasks may exist.

Further research may focus on expanding the application to a wider range of anatomical regions and pathologies, optimizing image acquisition speed and resolution, and conducting larger-scale clinical trials to establish definitive diagnostic accuracy and clinical utility. Exploration of integration into routine clinical workflows and cost-effectiveness analyses are also implied next steps.

Abstract

Purpose: To demonstrate the feasibility of performing in vivo imaging and quantitative relaxation mapping of soft and hard tissues using a low-cost, portable MRI scanner, and to establish the methodological foundations for zero echo time (ZTE) imaging in systems affected by strong field inhomogeneities. Methods: A complete framework for artifact-free ZTE imaging at low field was developed, including: (i) RF pulse pre/counteremphasis calibration to minimize ring-down and electronics switching time; (ii) an extension of a recent single-point double-shot (SPDS) protocol for simultaneous B0 and B1 mapping; and (iii) a model-based reconstruction incorporating these field maps into the encoding matrix. ZTE imaging and variable flip angle (VFA) T1 mapping were performed on phantoms and in vivo human knees and ankles, and benchmarked against standard RARE and STIR acquisitions. Results: The optimized PETRA sequence produced 3D images of knees and ankles within clinically compatible times (< 15 min), revealing hard tissues such as ligaments, tendons, cartilage, and bone that are invisible in spin-echo sequences. The extended SPDS method enabled accurate field mapping, while the VFA approach provided the first in vivo T1 measurements of hard tissues at B0 < 0.1 T. Conclusions: The proposed framework broadens the range of pulse sequences feasible in portable low-field MRI and demonstrates the potential of ZTE for quantitative and structural imaging of musculoskeletal tissues in affordable Halbach-based systems.
