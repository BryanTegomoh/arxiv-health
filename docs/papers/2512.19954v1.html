HistoWAS: A Pathomics Framework for Large-Scale Feature-Wide Association Studies of Tissue Topology and Patient Outcomes

arXiv ID: 2512.19954v1
Published: 2025-12-23
Authors: Yuechen Yang, Junlin Guo, Yanfan Zhu, Jialin Yue, Junchao Zhu, Yu Wang, Shilin Zhao, Haichun Yang, Xingyi Guo, Jovan Tanevski, Laura Barisoni, Avi Z. Rosenberg, Yuankai Huo

Summary

HistoWAS is a novel computational framework designed to link intricate tissue spatial organization to patient clinical outcomes, addressing limitations in current pathomic analysis. It augments conventional feature sets with 30 topological and spatial features adapted from Geographic Information Systems (GIS) point pattern analysis, then employs a Phenome-Wide Association Study (PheWAS)-inspired engine to perform mass univariate regression. The framework's utility was demonstrated as a proof-of-concept on 385 PAS-stained Whole Slide Images (WSIs) from 206 participants in the Kidney Precision Medicine Project (KPMP).

Medical Relevance

This framework significantly advances the field of digital pathology and biomarker discovery by enabling the identification of novel diagnostic, prognostic, and predictive biomarkers based on subtle tissue spatial organization, which is often overlooked by conventional methods. It offers a powerful tool to unravel the mechanistic links between tissue micro-environment and disease progression, fostering precision medicine.

AI Health Application

HistoWAS is a medical AI/computational application that uses advanced image analysis techniques (adapting GIS point pattern analysis for topological and spatial features) and statistical modeling (mass univariate regression, inspired by PheWAS) on medical imaging data (WSIs). Its purpose is to identify novel tissue characteristics and biomarkers that associate with clinical outcomes, thereby improving disease understanding, diagnosis, and personalized treatment strategies in healthcare.

Methodology

HistoWAS is a computational framework comprising two core components: (1) a feature space that expands upon conventional object-level metrics by introducing 30 novel topological and spatial features, derived from Geographic Information Systems (GIS) point pattern analysis, to characterize tissue micro-architecture; and (2) an association study engine, inspired by Phenome-Wide Association Studies (PheWAS), which performs mass univariate regression between these features and clinical parameters, incorporating statistical correction. This framework was applied to PAS-stained Whole Slide Images (WSIs).

Key Findings

The HistoWAS framework was successfully applied to analyze a comprehensive set of 102 features (72 conventional object-level and 30 newly introduced spatial features) using 385 PAS-stained WSIs obtained from 206 participants within the Kidney Precision Medicine Project (KPMP). This application served as a robust proof-of-concept, effectively demonstrating the framework's capability to extract and associate detailed tissue spatial organization with patient outcomes.

Clinical Impact

HistoWAS has the potential to transform biomarker discovery by identifying subtle, yet clinically significant, spatial patterns within tissues that may serve as novel diagnostic, prognostic, or predictive indicators for various diseases. By providing a deeper, spatially-aware understanding of disease pathophysiology, it could lead to more accurate patient stratification, personalized treatment strategies, and improved therapeutic monitoring in precision medicine.

Limitations

As a 'proof of concept' study, the abstract does not detail specific clinical discoveries or validated biomarkers linking the novel spatial features to specific patient outcomes. This implies that further extensive validation across larger, independent cohorts and diverse disease entities is required to fully establish its clinical utility and generalizability.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated, the 'proof of concept' nature implies future directions include applying HistoWAS to larger, more diverse disease cohorts beyond kidney disease, validating specific spatial biomarkers identified, integrating findings with other multi-omics data for a holistic view, and developing predictive models for direct clinical decision support based on these advanced spatial features.

Abstract

High-throughput "pathomic" analysis of Whole Slide Images (WSIs) offers new opportunities to study tissue characteristics and for biomarker discovery. However, the clinical relevance of the tissue characteristics at the micro- and macro-environment level is limited by the lack of tools that facilitate the measurement of the spatial interaction of individual structure characteristics and their association with clinical parameters. To address these challenges, we introduce HistoWAS (Histology-Wide Association Study), a computational framework designed to link tissue spatial organization to clinical outcomes. Specifically, HistoWAS implements (1) a feature space that augments conventional metrics with 30 topological and spatial features, adapted from Geographic Information Systems (GIS) point pattern analysis, to quantify tissue micro-architecture; and (2) an association study engine, inspired by Phenome-Wide Association Studies (PheWAS), that performs mass univariate regression for each feature with statistical correction. As a proof of concept, we applied HistoWAS to analyze a total of 102 features (72 conventional object-level features and our 30 spatial features) using 385 PAS-stained WSIs from 206 participants in the Kidney Precision Medicine Project (KPMP). The code and data have been released to https://github.com/hrlblab/histoWAS.
