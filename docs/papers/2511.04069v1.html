Pediatric Appendicitis Detection from Ultrasound Images

Published: 2025-11-06
Authors: Fatemeh Hosseinabadi, Seyedhassan Sharifi
Categories: cs.LG, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00

Summary

This study developed and evaluated a deep learning model, based on a fine-tuned ResNet architecture, for automated detection of pediatric appendicitis from ultrasound images. Utilizing the Regensburg Pediatric Appendicitis Dataset, the model achieved high diagnostic performance metrics, demonstrating its ability to learn crucial discriminative features despite common imaging challenges like low contrast, speckle noise, and anatomical variability in pediatric patients.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medicine as it offers a robust, automated tool to assist in the accurate and timely diagnosis of pediatric appendicitis, a common and critical condition. Improved diagnostic accuracy can reduce unnecessary surgeries, minimize diagnostic delays, and lead to better patient outcomes by ensuring appropriate management.

AI Health Application

The AI application is an automated diagnostic tool that uses a deep learning model (ResNet) to analyze ultrasound images of the right lower quadrant in pediatric patients. Its purpose is to accurately detect and distinguish appendicitis from non-appendicitis cases, thereby aiding clinicians in the diagnosis of acute abdominal pain in children.

Key Points

  • Addresses the diagnostic challenge of pediatric appendicitis due to overlapping symptoms and variable ultrasound image quality.
  • Utilizes a deep learning approach with a pretrained ResNet architecture, fine-tuned for binary classification (appendicitis vs. non-appendicitis).
  • The model was trained on the Regensburg Pediatric Appendicitis Dataset, which includes ultrasound scans, laboratory data, and clinical scores from 1 to 15 heterogeneous ultrasound views per patient.
  • Image preprocessing included normalization, resizing, and augmentation to enhance model generalization and robustness.
  • Achieved strong performance metrics: 93.44% overall accuracy, 91.53% precision, and 89.8% recall.
  • Successfully learned discriminative spatial features, demonstrating resilience against challenges inherent in pediatric ultrasound imaging, such as low contrast, speckle noise, and anatomical variability.
  • Presents a strong potential for objective and automated diagnostic support for pediatric appendicitis, improving diagnostic confidence and timeliness.

Methodology

The study implemented a deep learning methodology by fine-tuning a pretrained ResNet architecture for an image-based binary classification task to distinguish appendicitis from non-appendicitis. The Regensburg Pediatric Appendicitis Dataset, comprising diverse ultrasound views (1 to 15 per subject) along with clinical and laboratory data, served as the training and evaluation dataset. Images underwent extensive preprocessing, including normalization, resizing, and augmentation, to improve the model's ability to generalize to new data and handle varying image qualities.

Key Findings

The proposed ResNet model demonstrated strong performance in detecting pediatric appendicitis, achieving an overall accuracy of 93.44%, a precision of 91.53%, and a recall of 89.8%. The model effectively learned discriminative spatial features, proving capable of overcoming challenges posed by low contrast, speckle noise, and anatomical variability commonly encountered in pediatric ultrasound imaging.

Clinical Impact

This deep learning model has significant potential to serve as a valuable clinical decision support tool for pediatric appendicitis diagnosis. By providing an objective and highly accurate automated assessment from ultrasound images, it could aid clinicians, particularly in emergency settings or those with less specialized expertise, in making quicker and more confident diagnostic decisions. This could lead to a reduction in misdiagnoses, unnecessary interventions, and diagnostic delays, ultimately improving patient care and resource allocation.

Abstract

Pediatric appendicitis remains one of the most common causes of acute abdominal pain in children, and its diagnosis continues to challenge clinicians due to overlapping symptoms and variable imaging quality. This study aims to develop and evaluate a deep learning model based on a pretrained ResNet architecture for automated detection of appendicitis from ultrasound images. We used the Regensburg Pediatric Appendicitis Dataset, which includes ultrasound scans, laboratory data, and clinical scores from pediatric patients admitted with abdominal pain to Children Hospital. Hedwig in Regensburg, Germany. Each subject had 1 to 15 ultrasound views covering the right lower quadrant, appendix, lymph nodes, and related structures. For the image based classification task, ResNet was fine tuned to distinguish appendicitis from non-appendicitis cases. Images were preprocessed by normalization, resizing, and augmentation to enhance generalization. The proposed ResNet model achieved an overall accuracy of 93.44, precision of 91.53, and recall of 89.8, demonstrating strong performance in identifying appendicitis across heterogeneous ultrasound views. The model effectively learned discriminative spatial features, overcoming challenges posed by low contrast, speckle noise, and anatomical variability in pediatric imaging.
