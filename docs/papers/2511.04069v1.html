Pediatric Appendicitis Detection from Ultrasound Images

arXiv ID: 2511.04069v1
Published: 2025-11-06
Authors: Fatemeh Hosseinabadi, Seyedhassan Sharifi
Categories: eess.IV, cs.AI, cs.LG
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This study developed a deep learning model based on a fine-tuned ResNet architecture for automated detection of pediatric appendicitis from ultrasound images. Leveraging the Regensburg Pediatric Appendicitis Dataset, the model achieved high diagnostic performance with 93.44% accuracy, 91.53% precision, and 89.8% recall, demonstrating its capability to extract discriminative features despite common imaging challenges.

Medical Relevance

Pediatric appendicitis requires timely and accurate diagnosis to prevent severe complications. This research provides a powerful AI-driven tool that can assist clinicians in rapidly and objectively identifying appendicitis from ultrasound images, potentially reducing misdiagnosis rates, improving patient outcomes, and optimizing healthcare resource utilization.

AI Health Application

The AI application is an automated diagnostic aid for pediatric appendicitis. Specifically, it's a deep learning model (ResNet) that analyzes ultrasound images to distinguish appendicitis from non-appendicitis cases in children, thereby assisting clinicians in making accurate diagnoses.

Key Points

  • Addresses the diagnostic challenge of pediatric appendicitis, a common cause of acute abdominal pain in children, often complicated by overlapping symptoms and variable ultrasound image quality.
  • A deep learning model, utilizing a pretrained and fine-tuned ResNet architecture, was developed for automated classification of appendicitis from ultrasound images.
  • The Regensburg Pediatric Appendicitis Dataset was used, which comprises 1 to 15 ultrasound views per patient covering the right lower quadrant, along with associated laboratory data and clinical scores.
  • Image preprocessing techniques, including normalization, resizing, and augmentation, were applied to enhance the model's generalization capabilities across heterogeneous ultrasound views.
  • The model demonstrated strong performance metrics: 93.44% overall accuracy, 91.53% precision, and 89.8% recall in distinguishing appendicitis from non-appendicitis cases.
  • The deep learning model effectively learned discriminative spatial features, successfully overcoming inherent challenges in pediatric ultrasound imaging such as low contrast, speckle noise, and anatomical variability.
  • The study highlights the potential of deep learning to significantly improve the accuracy and efficiency of diagnosing pediatric appendicitis, offering a valuable tool for clinicians.

Methodology

The study employed a deep learning approach, fine-tuning a pretrained ResNet architecture for a binary image classification task (appendicitis vs. non-appendicitis). The model was trained and evaluated on ultrasound images from the Regensburg Pediatric Appendicitis Dataset. Images underwent standard preprocessing steps including normalization, resizing, and augmentation to enhance the model's robustness and generalization ability.

Key Findings

The proposed ResNet deep learning model achieved an overall accuracy of 93.44%, a precision of 91.53%, and a recall of 89.8% in detecting pediatric appendicitis from ultrasound images. The model demonstrated effective learning of discriminative spatial features, successfully navigating challenges such as low contrast, speckle noise, and anatomical variability inherent in pediatric ultrasound imaging.

Clinical Impact

This automated detection system can serve as a valuable diagnostic aid for clinicians, particularly in emergency departments, by offering a highly accurate, objective, and consistent tool for identifying pediatric appendicitis. Its implementation could lead to faster diagnoses, reduced reliance on subjective interpretation, decreased rates of unnecessary imaging or surgical interventions, and ultimately improved management and outcomes for children presenting with acute abdominal pain.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations; however, potential limitations include the reliance on a single institutional dataset, which might limit the generalizability of the model to diverse patient populations or varied ultrasound equipment/protocols from other clinical centers. Specific details on dataset size, patient demographics, and types/severities of appendicitis cases are also not provided.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated in the abstract, potential future directions include validating the model's performance on external, multi-center datasets to assess its robustness and generalizability. Further research could explore integrating clinical and laboratory data with image features in a multi-modal deep learning framework for enhanced diagnostic accuracy, and developing methods to provide explainability for the model's predictions to foster greater clinician trust and adoption.

Abstract

Pediatric appendicitis remains one of the most common causes of acute abdominal pain in children, and its diagnosis continues to challenge clinicians due to overlapping symptoms and variable imaging quality. This study aims to develop and evaluate a deep learning model based on a pretrained ResNet architecture for automated detection of appendicitis from ultrasound images. We used the Regensburg Pediatric Appendicitis Dataset, which includes ultrasound scans, laboratory data, and clinical scores from pediatric patients admitted with abdominal pain to Children Hospital. Hedwig in Regensburg, Germany. Each subject had 1 to 15 ultrasound views covering the right lower quadrant, appendix, lymph nodes, and related structures. For the image based classification task, ResNet was fine tuned to distinguish appendicitis from non-appendicitis cases. Images were preprocessed by normalization, resizing, and augmentation to enhance generalization. The proposed ResNet model achieved an overall accuracy of 93.44, precision of 91.53, and recall of 89.8, demonstrating strong performance in identifying appendicitis across heterogeneous ultrasound views. The model effectively learned discriminative spatial features, overcoming challenges posed by low contrast, speckle noise, and anatomical variability in pediatric imaging.
