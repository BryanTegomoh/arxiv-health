Summary

This paper evaluates REFLECT, a flow-based generative model, for unsupervised detection of both focal lesions and secondary non-lesional structural brain abnormalities post-stroke. The study demonstrates that training the model on fully healthy anatomy significantly improves its ability to segment lesions (Dice = 0.37) and detect non-lesional abnormalities (FROC = 0.62) compared to training on lesion-free stroke slices. This approach aims to better capture crucial imaging biomarkers for post-stroke recovery and outcome.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing the understanding and assessment of post-stroke recovery and outcome by enabling improved, automated detection of subtle, non-lesional brain changes, which are increasingly recognized as vital imaging biomarkers. It moves beyond just lesion detection to capture a more comprehensive picture of stroke-related brain impact.

AI Health Application

The paper describes the application of a flow-based generative AI model (REFLECT) for unsupervised detection of various structural brain abnormalities (focal lesions, atrophy, ventricular enlargement) in post-stroke MRI scans. This aims to improve the identification of imaging biomarkers crucial for assessing recovery and predicting patient outcomes after a stroke, thereby enhancing diagnostic and prognostic capabilities in clinical practice.

  • The study proposes REFLECT, a flow-based generative model, for unsupervised detection of *both* focal lesions and non-lesional brain abnormalities following a stroke.
  • Performance was assessed using dual-expert central-slice annotations on ATLAS data, utilizing Free-Response ROC (FROC) for anomaly map analysis and Dice score for lesion segmentation.

Methodology

The study utilized REFLECT, a flow-based generative model, trained on MRI data. Two distinct training strategies were employed: one model trained on lesion-free slices from post-stroke patients (ATLAS dataset) and another on healthy control subjects (IXI dataset). Evaluation was performed on ATLAS test subjects, using dual-expert central-slice annotations. Performance metrics included Dice coefficient for focal lesion segmentation and Free-Response ROC (FROC) analysis for the detection of both focal and non-lesional abnormalities from generated anomaly maps.

Key Findings

The model trained on healthy control data (IXI-trained) significantly outperformed the model trained on lesion-free stroke data. Specifically, the IXI-trained model achieved a higher Dice score for lesion segmentation (0.37 compared to 0.27) and improved sensitivity for detecting non-lesional abnormalities, as indicated by a higher FROC score (0.62 compared to 0.43). This demonstrates that training on healthy anatomy is superior for capturing normal brain variability, leading to broader and more reliable detection of diverse post-stroke structural changes.

Clinical Impact

This unsupervised method has the potential to provide a more comprehensive and automated assessment of post-stroke brain changes beyond just focal lesions, including subtle atrophic and ventricular abnormalities. This could lead to more precise prognostication, personalized rehabilitation strategies, and the development of new imaging biomarkers for recovery, ultimately improving patient management and treatment efficacy by offering a richer picture of brain pathology.

Abstract

Post-stroke MRI not only delineates focal lesions but also reveals secondary structural changes, such as atrophy and ventricular enlargement. These abnormalities, increasingly recognised as imaging biomarkers of recovery and outcome, remain poorly captured by supervised segmentation methods. We evaluate REFLECT, a flow-based generative model, for unsupervised detection of both focal and non-lesional abnormalities in post-stroke patients. Using dual-expert central-slice annotations on ATLAS data, performance was assessed at the object level with Free-Response ROC analysis for anomaly maps. Two models were trained on lesion-free slices from stroke patients (ATLAS) and on healthy controls (IXI) to test the effect of training data. On ATLAS test subjects, the IXI-trained model achieved higher lesion segmentation (Dice = 0.37 vs 0.27) and improved sensitivity to non-lesional abnormalities (FROC = 0.62 vs 0.43). Training on fully healthy anatomy improves the modelling of normal variability, enabling broader and more reliable detection of structural abnormalities.
