How Mathematical Forms of Chemotherapy and Radiotherapy Bias Model-Optimized Predictions: Implications for Model Selection

Published: 2025-11-19
Authors: Changin Oh, Kathleen P. Wilkie

Summary

This study comprehensively investigates how the choice of mathematical models for chemotherapy and radiotherapy significantly biases optimized treatment protocols, comparing three distinct models for each modality. It demonstrates that inherent model assumptions critically influence optimal dosing and sequencing, often leading to contradictory and non-generalizable predictions crucial for personalized and adaptive cancer therapies.

Medical Relevance

This research is critically important for the advancement of personalized cancer therapy and the development of digital twins, as it uncovers fundamental sources of bias in the mathematical models underlying simulation-based treatment optimization. Addressing this bias is essential for ensuring that computationally derived treatment strategies are safe, effective, and clinically reliable for patients.

AI Health Application

This research directly informs the development and selection of mathematical models used in AI-powered decision support systems, digital twins, and personalized treatment planning tools for cancer therapy. By analyzing model bias, sensitivity, and uncertainty quantification, it provides critical insights for building robust and trustworthy AI applications that optimize chemotherapy and radiotherapy protocols, dosing, and sequencing to improve patient outcomes. It underpins the foundation of simulation-based strategies that leverage AI for medical treatment.

Key Points

  • The study analyzed three chemotherapy models (log-kill, Norton-Simon, Emax) and three radiotherapy models (linear-quadratic, proliferation saturation index, continuous death-rate).
  • Significant differences were identified in the optimized chemotherapy and radiotherapy protocols generated by these distinct mathematical models.
  • Model-inherent assumptions were shown to heavily influence optimal treatment dosing and sequencing, potentially yielding contradictory results.
  • Different model forms were demonstrated to produce varying and biased predictions within the context of adaptive therapy settings.
  • Unexamined model assumptions were found to introduce bias, leading to model-dependent recommendations that may not be generalizable in clinical practice.
  • The research advocates for model selection to be based on a comprehensive analysis of bias, sensitivity, practical parameter identifiability, and inferred parameter posteriors, integrated into an uncertainty quantification process, rather than solely relying on information criterion.
  • The overarching implication is that a more rigorous approach to model choice is essential for developing robust and generalizable predictions for personalized cancer treatment planning.

Methodology

The study employed a comparative computational analysis approach, examining three distinct mathematical models for chemotherapy (log-kill, Norton-Simon, Emax) and three for radiotherapy (linear-quadratic, proliferation saturation index, continuous death-rate). It investigated how these different model forms influence the optimization of treatment protocols, specifically focusing on dosing and sequencing, and their impact on predictions for adaptive therapy.

Key Findings

The study found that the choice of mathematical model significantly alters optimized chemotherapy and radiotherapy protocols. It demonstrated that inherent model assumptions critically bias optimal treatment dosing and sequencing, frequently leading to contradictory recommendations. Furthermore, different model forms were shown to produce distinct predictions in adaptive therapy scenarios, indicating that unexamined model assumptions introduce significant bias, resulting in model-dependent and potentially non-generalizable clinical advice.

Clinical Impact

This research directly impacts the clinical implementation of personalized cancer treatment strategies and digital twins by highlighting the necessity for rigorous model selection. By demonstrating how model choice introduces bias, it advocates for a shift towards a more comprehensive approach to model validation in oncology, beyond simplistic information criteria. This will lead to more robust and reliable computational treatment plans, ultimately enhancing patient safety and treatment efficacy by reducing the risk of suboptimal or harmful outcomes stemming from biased model predictions.

Abstract

The move towards personalized treatment and digital twins for cancer therapy requires a complete understanding of the mathematical models upon which these optimized simulation-based strategies are formulated. This study investigates the influence of mathematical model selection on the optimization of chemotherapy and radiotherapy protocols. By examining three chemotherapy models (log-kill, Norton-Simon, and Emax), and three radiotherapy models (linear-quadratic, proliferation saturation index, and continuous death-rate), we identify similarities and significant differences in the optimized protocols. We demonstrate how the assumptions built into the model formulations heavily influence optimal treatment dosing and sequencing, potentially leading to contradictory results. Further, we demonstrate how different model forms influence predictions in the adaptive therapy setting. As treatment decisions increasingly rely on simulation-based strategies, unexamined model assumptions can introduce bias, leading to model-dependent recommendations that may not be generalizable. This study highlights the importance of basing model selection on a full analysis of bias, sensitivity, practical parameter identifiability and/or inferred parameter posteriors, as a part of the uncertainty quantification process, rather than solely relying on information criterion. Understanding how model choice impacts predictions guiding personalized treatment planning with sufficient uncertainty quantification analysis, will lead to more robust and generalizable predictions.
