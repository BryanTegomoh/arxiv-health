How Mathematical Forms of Chemotherapy and Radiotherapy Bias Model-Optimized Predictions: Implications for Model Selection

Summary

This study demonstrates how the choice of mathematical models for chemotherapy and radiotherapy significantly biases optimized treatment protocols and predictions, potentially leading to contradictory and non-generalizable recommendations. It highlights that inherent model assumptions critically influence dosing and sequencing, underscoring the urgent need for a more comprehensive and rigorous approach to model selection in personalized cancer therapy.

Medical Relevance

This research is critically important for the development and safe implementation of personalized cancer therapy and 'digital twins', as it reveals how fundamental mathematical model choices can introduce significant bias, potentially leading to suboptimal or harmful treatment protocols for patients and impacting the reliability of clinical decision support systems.

AI Health Application

This research is foundational for medical AI applications in oncology. It directly concerns the underlying mathematical models used in 'digital twins for cancer therapy' and 'optimized simulation-based strategies' for personalized treatment. AI and machine learning algorithms are frequently employed to develop and implement such models for predictive analytics, treatment optimization, and decision support in healthcare. Understanding model biases and improving model selection, as highlighted in this paper, is critical for building reliable, generalizable, and safe AI systems that recommend or implement personalized cancer treatments.

Key Points

  • The study comparatively examined three chemotherapy models (log-kill, Norton-Simon, Emax) and three radiotherapy models (linear-quadratic, proliferation saturation index, continuous death-rate).
  • It identified both similarities and significant differences in the optimized treatment protocols generated by these distinct mathematical models.
  • Demonstrated that assumptions built into model formulations heavily influence optimal treatment dosing and sequencing, capable of producing contradictory results.
  • Showcased how different mathematical model forms specifically influence predictions within the adaptive therapy setting.
  • Warned that unexamined model assumptions in simulation-based treatment strategies introduce bias, leading to model-dependent recommendations lacking generalizability.
  • Emphasized that robust model selection must incorporate a full analysis of bias, sensitivity, practical parameter identifiability, and inferred parameter posteriors, as part of uncertainty quantification, rather than solely relying on information criterion.

Methodology

The study employed a comparative analytical approach, investigating the influence of different mathematical model forms on the optimization of chemotherapy and radiotherapy protocols. It specifically examined three established models for each therapy type, analyzing how their underlying assumptions bias optimal dosing, sequencing, and predictive outcomes, particularly in adaptive therapy contexts.

Abstract

The move towards personalized treatment and digital twins for cancer therapy requires a complete understanding of the mathematical models upon which these optimized simulation-based strategies are formulated. This study investigates the influence of mathematical model selection on the optimization of chemotherapy and radiotherapy protocols. By examining three chemotherapy models (log-kill, Norton-Simon, and Emax), and three radiotherapy models (linear-quadratic, proliferation saturation index, and continuous death-rate), we identify similarities and significant differences in the optimized protocols. We demonstrate how the assumptions built into the model formulations heavily influence optimal treatment dosing and sequencing, potentially leading to contradictory results. Further, we demonstrate how different model forms influence predictions in the adaptive therapy setting. As treatment decisions increasingly rely on simulation-based strategies, unexamined model assumptions can introduce bias, leading to model-dependent recommendations that may not be generalizable. This study highlights the importance of basing model selection on a full analysis of bias, sensitivity, practical parameter identifiability and/or inferred parameter posteriors, as a part of the uncertainty quantification process, rather than solely relying on information criterion. Understanding how model choice impacts predictions guiding personalized treatment planning with sufficient uncertainty quantification analysis, will lead to more robust and generalizable predictions.
