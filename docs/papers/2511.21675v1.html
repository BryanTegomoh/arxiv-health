On Evolution-Based Models for Experimentation Under Interference

arXiv ID: 2511.21675v1
Published: 2025-11-26
Authors: Sadegh Shirani, Mohsen Bayati
Categories: stat.ML, cs.LG, cs.SI, econ.EM
Relevance Score: 0.85 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper proposes an evolution-based approach for estimating population-level causal effects in networked systems where intervention spillovers occur through unobserved interaction pathways. It argues that precisely recovering the network structure is unnecessary; instead, characterizing how outcomes evolve across observation rounds in response to interventions is sufficient, leveraging an exposure-mapping perspective to identify conditions for low-dimensional recursive outcome equations.

Medical Relevance

This methodology is highly relevant for evaluating interventions in healthcare, especially for public health campaigns, drug trials with patient interaction, or adoption of health behaviors where network effects, social influence, or patient-provider interactions lead to unobserved spillovers. It enables robust causal inference without needing a fully mapped interaction network, which is often infeasible in complex health systems.

AI Health Application

This paper presents a novel methodological framework in machine learning (stat.ML, cs.LG) for robust causal inference. An AI system or application leveraging this methodology could: 1. **Evaluate Health Interventions:** Provide data-driven insights into the causal effects of public health campaigns, policy changes, or healthcare delivery strategies, accurately accounting for unobserved spillover effects within networked populations. 2. **Optimize Resource Allocation:** Help decision-makers allocate resources more effectively for disease control, vaccine distribution, or health promotion by understanding how interventions propagate through communities, even without full knowledge of interaction networks. 3. **Forecast Disease Spread:** Improve models for forecasting disease outbreaks and evaluating the impact of different containment strategies by incorporating the complex interplay of interventions and unobserved transmission pathways. 4. **Personalized Medicine (Macro-level):** While not directly patient-level, it could inform group-level or community-level 'personalized' interventions by identifying and learning about heterogeneous spillover effects in specific demographic or social contexts.

Key Points

  • Addresses the challenge of causal effect estimation in networked systems where intervention spillovers occur through largely unobserved interaction pathways.
  • Proposes that for population-level causal effects, it is sufficient to characterize how outcomes evolve across observation rounds in response to interventions, rather than recovering the exact network structure.
  • Introduces an 'evolution-based approach' formalizing outcome change over time using an 'exposure-mapping perspective' to derive an axiomatic characterization for low-dimensional recursive outcome equations.
  • Frames the method as a 'distributional counterpart to difference-in-differences,' exploiting parallel evolution patterns across treatment scenarios to estimate counterfactual trajectories.
  • Highlights that treatment randomization plays a crucial role beyond eliminating latent confounding by inducing an implicit sampling from hidden interference channels, enabling consistent learning about heterogeneous spillover effects.
  • Demonstrates applicability through instantiations like 'causal message passing' in dense networks and extends to general interference structures, including 'influencer networks' where a few units drive most spillovers.
  • Identifies limitations, noting that strong temporal trends or endogenous interference can undermine the identification of causal effects using this approach.

Methodology

The paper introduces an 'evolution-based approach' that analyzes how outcomes change over sequential observation rounds in response to interventions, effectively compensating for unobserved network structures. It employs an 'exposure-mapping perspective' to axiomatically characterize conditions under which the empirical outcome distribution follows a low-dimensional recursive equation. This approach is framed as a 'distributional counterpart to difference-in-differences,' leveraging parallel outcome evolution patterns across different treatment scenarios rather than assuming parallel trends for individual units.

Key Findings

The central finding is that identifying population-level causal effects under interference does not require recovering the exact network structure, but rather characterizing the evolution of outcomes. Minimal structural conditions are identified for the existence of low-dimensional recursive evolution mappings. Furthermore, treatment randomization is shown to be crucial for learning about heterogeneous spillover effects by implicitly sampling hidden interference channels, extending its traditional role beyond confounding control. The approach is versatile, applicable to both dense and influencer networks.

Clinical Impact

This approach offers a more robust and practical framework for evaluating healthcare interventions where direct and indirect effects are intertwined through unobserved social or physical networks, such as assessing the impact of vaccination campaigns, health education programs, or new treatment protocols within communities. It allows for valid causal conclusions even when detailed interaction data is unavailable, making evidence-based policy and clinical decision-making more feasible and reliable in real-world, complex settings.

Limitations

The identification of causal effects using this evolution-based approach can be undermined by the presence of strong temporal trends in outcomes that are independent of the intervention, or by endogenous interference, where units actively choose their exposure or interaction based on previous outcomes, violating assumptions about exogenous intervention.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated as future directions, the identified limitations suggest avenues for further research, such as developing methods to robustly account for strong temporal confounding or designing interventions and analyses that can disentangle endogenous interference from exogenous spillover effects. Further empirical applications across diverse medical contexts would also validate the practical utility of the framework.

Abstract

Causal effect estimation in networked systems is central to data-driven decision making. In such settings, interventions on one unit can spill over to others, and in complex physical or social systems, the interaction pathways driving these interference structures remain largely unobserved. We argue that for identifying population-level causal effects, it is not necessary to recover the exact network structure; instead, it suffices to characterize how those interactions contribute to the evolution of outcomes. Building on this principle, we study an evolution-based approach that investigates how outcomes change across observation rounds in response to interventions, hence compensating for missing network information. Using an exposure-mapping perspective, we give an axiomatic characterization of when the empirical distribution of outcomes follows a low-dimensional recursive equation, and identify minimal structural conditions under which such evolution mappings exist. We frame this as a distributional counterpart to difference-in-differences. Rather than assuming parallel paths for individual units, it exploits parallel evolution patterns across treatment scenarios to estimate counterfactual trajectories. A key insight is that treatment randomization plays a role beyond eliminating latent confounding; it induces an implicit sampling from hidden interference channels, enabling consistent learning about heterogeneous spillover effects. We highlight causal message passing as an instantiation of this method in dense networks while extending to more general interference structures, including influencer networks where a small set of units drives most spillovers. Finally, we discuss the limits of this approach, showing that strong temporal trends or endogenous interference can undermine identification.
