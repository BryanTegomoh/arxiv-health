Simulations Predict Improved Valve Performance Without Direct Leaflet Intervention After Neonatal Truncus Arteriosus Repair

arXiv ID: 2601.08932v1
Published: 2026-01-13
Authors: Karoline-Marie Bornemann, Perry S. Choi, Jay Huber, Alexander K. Reed, Amit Sharir, Shiraz A. Maskatia, Alison L. Marsden, Michael R. Ma, Alexander D. Kaiser
Categories: q-bio.TO

Summary

This study employed computational simulations to investigate the impact of hemodynamic changes following neonatal Truncus Arteriosus (TA) repair on the performance of an associated quadricuspid valve, without direct leaflet intervention. The simulations accurately predicted that altered postoperative hemodynamics significantly improved valve coaptation and eliminated regurgitation, mirroring in-vivo clinical observations.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a powerful in-silico tool to predict and understand the functional outcomes of truncal valve performance after TA repair, offering crucial insights that could help surgeons determine which patients may benefit from TA repair alone versus those requiring additional direct leaflet intervention, potentially improving surgical planning and long-term patient outcomes.

AI Health Application

The paper describes using 'simulations' and 'in-silico modeling' to predict patient-specific valve performance and identify mechanisms of improvement after surgery. This falls under the umbrella of computational modeling and predictive analytics in healthcare. While not explicitly labeled as 'AI' (e.g., deep learning), these advanced computational methods are foundational for personalized medicine, 'digital twin' applications, and data-driven clinical decision support, which are key areas of medical AI applications.

Key Points

  • Truncus Arteriosus (TA) is a severe congenital heart defect where a quadricuspid valve morphology, present in 25% of patients, is linked to regurgitation and increased re-operation risk.
  • Patient-specific computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulations were conducted using pre- and postoperative CT images and echocardiogram data from a neonatal TA patient with a quadricuspid valve.
  • Preoperative simulations accurately predicted mild valve regurgitation, identifying the mechanism as asymmetric flow interaction with the surrounding vessel leading to asymmetric leaflet opening/closing and poor central coaptation, resulting in a central regurgitant jet.
  • Postoperative simulations, incorporating modified boundary conditions mimicking patient-specific hemodynamics after TA repair, demonstrated a significant improvement in valve coaptation and the complete elimination of regurgitation.
  • The in-silico model successfully reproduced both preoperative regurgitation and postoperative absence of regurgitation, aligning precisely with in-vivo imaging findings.
  • The primary mechanism identified for improved valve function was enhanced central coaptation driven by the normalized hemodynamic conditions post-TA repair.
  • The findings suggest that the hemodynamic alterations achieved through TA repair itself can intrinsically improve the performance of a quadricuspid valve, potentially negating the need for direct surgical intervention on the valve leaflets.

Methodology

The study utilized patient-specific computational simulations based on pre- and postoperative CT images of a neonatal patient with Truncus Arteriosus and a quadricuspid valve. The valve itself was modeled using geometric parameters (free edge length, geometric height) derived from the patient's echocardiogram. Preoperative boundary conditions were set with equal ventricular pressures (mimicking an unrestricted VSD) and systemic/pulmonary resistances tuned to the patient's Qp:Qs ratio. Postoperative boundary conditions were modified to reflect patient-specific hemodynamics after TA repair.

Key Findings

The preoperative simulation successfully confirmed mild valve regurgitation, attributing it to asymmetric flow leading to poor central coaptation and a central regurgitant jet. Critically, the postoperative simulations demonstrated that altered hemodynamics following TA repair led to significantly improved central coaptation and the complete elimination of regurgitation, consistently matching in-vivo observations.

Clinical Impact

This research suggests that the primary surgical repair of Truncus Arteriosus, by normalizing patient hemodynamics, can inherently improve the function of an associated quadricuspid valve. This could potentially reduce the necessity for direct, complex, and high-risk surgical interventions on the valve leaflets during the initial repair, leading to fewer re-operations, reduced morbidity, and improved long-term prognosis for these vulnerable neonatal patients.

Limitations

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract, but as a single-patient study based on computational modeling, generalizability and the inherent simplifications of in-silico models are common limitations not detailed here.

Future Directions

The successful reproduction of in-vivo outcomes with this modeling approach suggests a strong potential for its expansion to larger patient cohorts. This could allow for personalized predictive modeling to identify patients who would benefit from TA repair alone versus those who might still require direct valve intervention, thereby optimizing surgical strategies and patient selection.

Abstract

Truncus arteriosus (TA) is a rare and severe congenital heart disease. Quadricuspid valve morphology occurs in 25% of all TA patients and is linked to regurgitation and increased risk of re-operation. It remains unclear how hemodynamic changes after TA repair alter valve performance. This study simulated pre- and postoperative conditions in a neonatal TA patient to investigate valve performance without direct intervention. We hypothesize that valve performance before and after truncal repair can be predicted in-silico, matching in-vivo imaging and identifying mechanisms how hemodynamic changes after repair will reduce valve regurgitation without direct intervention. Pre- and postoperative CT images of a neonatal patient with quadricuspid valve were segmented. Free edge length and geometric height from the patient's echocardiogram were used to model the valve. For the preoperative condition, ventricular pressures were set equal modeling an unrestricted ventricular septal defect. Systemic and pulmonary resistances were tuned based on the patient's Qp:Qs ratio. For the postoperative condition, boundary conditions were modified to mimic patient-specific hemodynamics after TA repair. The preoperative simulation confirmed mild valve regurgitation seen in-vivo. Interaction between asymmetric flow and surrounding vessel resulted in asymmetric opening and closing. Poor central coaptation led to a central regurgitant jet toward the septum. Altered postoperative hemodynamics improved coaptation and eliminated regurgitation, as seen in-vivo. This modeling approach reproduced in-vivo pre- and postoperative valve performance and identified mechanisms improving coaptation after TA repair. TA repair led to elimination of regurgitation due to enhanced central coaptation. Thus, altered postoperative hemodynamic conditions after TA repair may improve valve performance without direct leaflet intervention.
