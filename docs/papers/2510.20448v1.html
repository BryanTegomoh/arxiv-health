MolBridge: Atom-Level Joint Graph Refinement for Robust Drug-Drug Interaction Event Prediction

arXiv ID: 2510.20448v1
Published: 2025-10-23
Authors: Xuan Lin, Aocheng Ding, Tengfei Ma, Hua Liang, Zhe Quan
Categories: cs.LG, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

MolBridge is a novel atom-level joint graph refinement framework designed to predict drug-drug interaction (DDI) events with high accuracy and robustness. It addresses the limitations of existing methods by explicitly modeling atom-level cross-molecular interactions within a joint graph and utilizing a structure consistency module to overcome over-smoothing challenges. This approach leads to superior performance across diverse molecular complexities, including long-tail and inductive scenarios, offering enhanced mechanistic interpretability.

Medical Relevance

Accurate DDI prediction is critical for patient safety, preventing adverse drug events, and optimizing therapeutic outcomes, especially given the risks associated with complex molecular structures and enzyme-mediated competition. This research offers a more precise, molecular-level understanding and prediction capability for these interactions.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the development of a novel atom-level joint graph refinement framework (MolBridge) for predicting adverse drug-drug interactions (DDIs). This can be used to improve patient safety by identifying potential harmful drug combinations, accelerate drug development by screening for DDI risks, and enhance clinical decision support by providing more accurate DDI predictions for clinicians.

Key Points

  • Existing DDI prediction models often rely on isolated drug representations and fail to explicitly model fine-grained, atom-level cross-molecular interactions, limiting their effectiveness for diverse molecular complexities and DDI type distributions.
  • MolBridge constructs a 'joint graph' that integrates the atomic structures of drug pairs, enabling direct and explicit modeling of inter-drug associations at an atom level.
  • A central methodological innovation is the 'structure consistency module' which iteratively refines node features within the joint graph while preserving global structural context, specifically designed to prevent information loss from over-smoothing in long-range atomic dependency modeling.
  • This joint design allows MolBridge to effectively learn both local (atom-level) and global (molecular-level) interaction patterns, yielding robust representations for both frequent and rare (long-tail) DDI types.
  • Extensive experiments on two benchmark datasets demonstrate that MolBridge consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, achieving superior performance across long-tail and inductive DDI prediction scenarios.
  • The fine-grained graph refinement approach of MolBridge significantly improves the accuracy, robustness, and mechanistic interpretability of DDI event prediction.
  • The work contributes to Web Mining and Content Analysis by developing advanced graph-based methods for mining and analyzing complex drug-drug interaction networks.

Methodology

MolBridge constructs a joint graph from the atomic structures of drug pairs, enabling direct modeling of inter-drug associations. It employs a graph refinement framework that integrates a 'structure consistency module,' which iteratively refines atom-level node features within the joint graph. This module is specifically designed to mitigate over-smoothing while maintaining global structural context, thereby facilitating the learning of both local atomic interactions and broader molecular patterns for DDI prediction.

Key Findings

MolBridge consistently outperforms state-of-the-art baselines on two benchmark datasets, demonstrating superior accuracy and robustness. This improved performance is particularly evident in predicting rare (long-tail) DDI types and handling inductive scenarios (new drug combinations). The framework also enhances the mechanistic interpretability of DDI event predictions by modeling interactions at a fine-grained atomic level.

Clinical Impact

This research has the potential to significantly improve patient safety by enabling more accurate and early prediction of adverse DDIs, thereby reducing medication errors and complications. It could accelerate drug discovery and development by identifying potential interactions earlier in the pipeline, leading to more effective and safer drug candidates. Clinicians could benefit from enhanced tools for personalized medication management, particularly for patients on polypharmacy, by providing deeper insights into the specific molecular mechanisms of drug interactions.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations of MolBridge itself. It rather highlights how MolBridge's structure consistency module *overcomes* a key challenge in joint graph settings, namely the potential loss of information caused by over-smoothing when modeling long-range atomic dependencies. Thus, the work presents MolBridge as a solution to existing limitations in DDI prediction, rather than having specific noted limitations of its own within the scope of this abstract.

Future Directions

While not explicitly detailing future improvements for MolBridge itself, the abstract states that the work contributes to Web Mining and Content Analysis by developing graph-based methods for mining and analyzing drug-drug interaction networks. This suggests a future direction in applying these advanced graph-based methodologies to broader network analysis tasks, potentially in web content or other complex biological and chemical networks beyond DDI prediction.
