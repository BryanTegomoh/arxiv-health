Population-scale testing can suppress the spread of infectious disease

Summary

This paper proposes a robust intervention for infectious disease control based on repeated, population-scale testing and isolation of infected individuals. Through rigorous analysis and simulations, the authors demonstrate that a sufficient testing rate can reduce the effective reproduction number (R_e) below 1.0, leading to rapid epidemic collapse, independent of strong assumptions about test accuracy or compliance. This strategy offers a rapid response to emerging pathogens, complementing traditional pharmaceutical interventions.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a crucial non-pharmaceutical strategy for rapid control of emerging infectious diseases, particularly when vaccines or specific drugs are unavailable or in development. It offers a scalable and robust public health framework for epidemic suppression, directly impacting pandemic preparedness and response.

Key Points

  • The proposed intervention centers on universal, repeated testing of every individual for the disease, followed by isolation of those testing positive.
  • At a sufficient rate of testing, the effective reproduction number (R_e) is reduced below 1.0, causing the epidemic to rapidly collapse.
  • The model is robust and does not rely on strong or unrealistic assumptions regarding test accuracy, isolation compliance, population structure, or specific epidemiological parameters.
  • The required test frequency to suppress an epidemic is monotonic and near-linear with respect to R0, the infectious period, and the fraction of susceptible individuals, provided results are obtained rapidly.
  • The testing regime is effective against both early-phase and established epidemics, and its benefits are additive to other interventions such as contact tracing and social distancing.
  • Even suboptimal or partial implementation of testing provides benefit, reducing infections and improving public health and economic conditions.
  • Optimal performance is achieved with concurrent testing regimes (e.g., mass testing campaigns), which outperform random sampling, ideally utilizing rapid, immediate-result tests.

Methodology

The conclusions are derived from rigorous mathematical analysis and simulations of appropriate epidemiological models, which assess the impact of various testing frequencies and strategies on disease transmission dynamics.

Key Findings

The study demonstrates that population-scale, repeated testing combined with isolation can effectively reduce the basic reproduction number below 1.0, leading to epidemic suppression. This approach is robust to varying test accuracy and compliance levels, is effective at any stage of an epidemic, and its benefits are additive to other public health measures. The required test frequency correlates near-linearly with key epidemiological parameters like R0 and infectious period.

Clinical Impact

This research provides a strong scientific basis for implementing widespread, repeated diagnostic testing programs as a primary tool for infectious disease control. It informs public health policy on resource allocation for testing infrastructure, emphasizing the importance of rapid result turnaround and optimal testing design (e.g., concurrent screening) to achieve epidemic suppression, thereby reducing morbidity, mortality, and economic disruption.

Limitations

Practical implementation relies on achieving and sustaining high rates of population-wide testing, ensuring rapid delivery of test results, and maintaining sufficient compliance with isolation measures. While the model is robust to imperfections in these areas, the feasibility and logistical challenges of scaling such an intervention, as well as the economic costs associated with achieving the necessary frequency and speed, are implicitly the major real-world constraints.

Future Directions

The paper suggests that the ideal implementation would involve the development and widespread availability of mass-produced, disposable home tests that provide immediate results. This would facilitate the optimal performance of concurrent testing regimes, thereby making the strategy more feasible and effective for rapid disease control.

Abstract

Major advances in public health have resulted from disease prevention. However, prevention of a new infectious disease by vaccination or pharmaceuticals is made difficult by the slow process of vaccine and drug development. We propose an additional intervention that allows rapid control of emerging infectious diseases, and can also be used to eradicate diseases that rely almost exclusively on human-to-human transmission. The intervention is based on (1) testing every individual for the disease, (2) repeatedly, and (3) isolation of infected individuals. We show here that at a sufficient rate of testing, the reproduction number is reduced below 1.0 and the epidemic will rapidly collapse. The approach does not rely on strong or unrealistic assumptions about test accuracy, isolation compliance, population structure or epidemiological parameters, and its success can be monitored in real time by following the test positivity rate. In addition to the compliance rate and false negatives, the required rate of testing depends on the design of the testing regime, with concurrent testing outperforming random sampling. Provided that results are obtained rapidly, the test frequency required to suppress an epidemic is monotonic and near-linear with respect to R0, the infectious period, and the fraction of susceptible individuals. The testing regime is effective against both early phase and established epidemics, and additive to other interventions (e.g. contact tracing and social distancing). It is also robust to failure: any rate of testing reduces the number of infections, improving both public health and economic conditions. These conclusions are based on rigorous analysis and simulations of appropriate epidemiological models. A mass-produced, disposable test that could be used at home would be ideal, due to the optimal performance of concurrent tests that return immediate results.
