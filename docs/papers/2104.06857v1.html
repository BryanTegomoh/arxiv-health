Population-scale testing can suppress the spread of infectious disease

arXiv ID: 2104.06857v1
Published: 2021-04-14
Authors: Jussi Taipale, Ioannis Kontoyiannis, Sten Linnarsson
Categories: q-bio.PE, math.PR

Summary

This paper proposes a strategy for rapidly controlling and potentially eradicating human-to-human transmitted infectious diseases through repeated, population-scale testing and isolation of infected individuals. It demonstrates, via rigorous analysis and simulations, that a sufficient testing rate can reduce the basic reproduction number below 1.0, leading to epidemic collapse, regardless of strong assumptions about test accuracy or compliance.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a crucial, rapid-response public health strategy for managing new infectious disease outbreaks, particularly when specific pharmaceutical interventions or vaccines are unavailable, offering a pathway to prevent widespread morbidity and mortality.

Key Points

  • The core intervention involves frequent, population-wide testing of all individuals combined with rapid isolation of confirmed cases.
  • At a sufficient rate of testing, this strategy reduces the effective reproduction number (R) below 1.0, resulting in a rapid collapse of the epidemic.
  • The approach is highly robust, not relying on strong or unrealistic assumptions regarding test accuracy, isolation compliance rates, population structure, or specific epidemiological parameters.
  • The required testing rate is influenced by the design of the testing regime (concurrent testing outperforms random sampling), compliance, false negatives, and critically, the speed of result acquisition.
  • The necessary test frequency to suppress an epidemic is monotonic and near-linear with respect to R0, the infectious period, and the fraction of susceptible individuals.
  • This intervention is effective against both early-phase and established epidemics and is additive to other public health measures like contact tracing and social distancing.
  • Even at suboptimal rates, testing still reduces the number of infections, providing both public health and economic benefits.

Methodology

The conclusions are derived from rigorous mathematical analysis and simulations of appropriate epidemiological models.

Key Findings

A repeated, population-scale testing and isolation strategy can effectively reduce the reproduction number (R) below 1.0, leading to a rapid collapse of an epidemic. The required testing frequency is predictable based on R0, infectious period, and the susceptible population, and the method is robust to imperfections in test accuracy or compliance.

Clinical Impact

This intervention offers a practical, real-time monitored framework for health authorities to rapidly suppress or eradicate infectious disease outbreaks through structured, frequent testing and isolation, providing a critical tool in scenarios where traditional medical interventions are lacking. It provides a means to directly impact R in a predictable manner.

Limitations

While robust to many imperfect conditions, the optimal performance and required testing frequency critically depend on the rapid obtainment of test results. The practical, large-scale implementation of concurrent, repeated testing for every individual would necessitate the development and availability of mass-produced, potentially at-home, rapid diagnostic tests.

Future Directions

The paper implicitly suggests that the development and widespread availability of mass-produced, disposable tests capable of returning immediate results (ideally for home use) would be crucial to maximize the efficacy and feasibility of this strategy, particularly for implementing optimal concurrent testing regimes.

Abstract

Major advances in public health have resulted from disease prevention. However, prevention of a new infectious disease by vaccination or pharmaceuticals is made difficult by the slow process of vaccine and drug development. We propose an additional intervention that allows rapid control of emerging infectious diseases, and can also be used to eradicate diseases that rely almost exclusively on human-to-human transmission. The intervention is based on (1) testing every individual for the disease, (2) repeatedly, and (3) isolation of infected individuals. We show here that at a sufficient rate of testing, the reproduction number is reduced below 1.0 and the epidemic will rapidly collapse. The approach does not rely on strong or unrealistic assumptions about test accuracy, isolation compliance, population structure or epidemiological parameters, and its success can be monitored in real time by following the test positivity rate. In addition to the compliance rate and false negatives, the required rate of testing depends on the design of the testing regime, with concurrent testing outperforming random sampling. Provided that results are obtained rapidly, the test frequency required to suppress an epidemic is monotonic and near-linear with respect to R0, the infectious period, and the fraction of susceptible individuals. The testing regime is effective against both early phase and established epidemics, and additive to other interventions (e.g. contact tracing and social distancing). It is also robust to failure: any rate of testing reduces the number of infections, improving both public health and economic conditions. These conclusions are based on rigorous analysis and simulations of appropriate epidemiological models. A mass-produced, disposable test that could be used at home would be ideal, due to the optimal performance of concurrent tests that return immediate results.
