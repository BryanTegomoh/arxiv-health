Population-scale testing can suppress the spread of infectious disease

arXiv ID: 2104.06857v1
Published: 2021-04-14
Authors: Jussi Taipale, Ioannis Kontoyiannis, Sten Linnarsson
Categories: q-bio.PE, math.PR
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper proposes a novel population-scale intervention for the rapid control and potential eradication of infectious diseases, relying on repeated, universal testing of all individuals combined with isolation of infected persons. Through rigorous analysis and simulations, it demonstrates that a sufficient rate of such testing can reduce the effective reproduction number (R) below 1.0, leading to rapid epidemic collapse, irrespective of strong assumptions about test accuracy or compliance.

Medical Relevance

This intervention offers a rapid, non-pharmaceutical strategy for controlling novel infectious diseases, mitigating the challenges of slow vaccine and drug development. It provides a robust, measurable framework for suppressing or eradicating human-to-human transmitted pathogens, complementing existing public health measures and offering immediate benefits to both public health and economic stability during an outbreak.

Key Points

  • The core intervention involves three components: (1) testing every individual for the disease, (2) repeatedly, and (3) isolation of infected individuals.
  • At a sufficient rate of testing, the effective reproduction number (R) is reduced below 1.0, leading to a rapid collapse of the epidemic.
  • The approach is robust and does not rely on strong assumptions about test accuracy, isolation compliance, population structure, or specific epidemiological parameters.
  • Success of the intervention can be monitored in real-time by tracking the test positivity rate.
  • The required test frequency is monotonic and near-linear with R0, the infectious period, and the fraction of susceptible individuals, provided that test results are obtained rapidly.
  • Different testing regimes have varying efficacy, with concurrent testing significantly outperforming random sampling.
  • The strategy is effective against both early and established epidemics, is additive to other interventions (e.g., contact tracing, social distancing), and any rate of testing, even if suboptimal, reduces infections, offering public health and economic benefits.

Methodology

The conclusions are based on rigorous analysis and simulations of appropriate epidemiological models. This involves mathematical modeling to assess the impact of various testing frequencies, test designs (concurrent vs. random sampling), and epidemiological parameters on the disease's reproduction number and overall spread.

Clinical Impact

This strategy provides a practical framework for rapidly deploying a non-pharmaceutical intervention during novel infectious disease outbreaks, potentially preventing healthcare system overload and reducing morbidity and mortality. It offers a quantifiable method to control transmission, significantly enhance the efficacy of existing measures like contact tracing, and improve overall public health and economic stability during an epidemic. It empowers public health authorities with a powerful tool for rapid response.

However, it implicitly highlights factors critical for optimal performance, such as the need for rapid test results and the influence of isolation compliance and false negative rates on the required testing frequency. Achieving 'population-scale' and 'concurrent' testing with immediate results represents a significant logistical and technological challenge for practical implementation.

Abstract

Major advances in public health have resulted from disease prevention. However, prevention of a new infectious disease by vaccination or pharmaceuticals is made difficult by the slow process of vaccine and drug development. We propose an additional intervention that allows rapid control of emerging infectious diseases, and can also be used to eradicate diseases that rely almost exclusively on human-to-human transmission. The intervention is based on (1) testing every individual for the disease, (2) repeatedly, and (3) isolation of infected individuals. We show here that at a sufficient rate of testing, the reproduction number is reduced below 1.0 and the epidemic will rapidly collapse. The approach does not rely on strong or unrealistic assumptions about test accuracy, isolation compliance, population structure or epidemiological parameters, and its success can be monitored in real time by following the test positivity rate. In addition to the compliance rate and false negatives, the required rate of testing depends on the design of the testing regime, with concurrent testing outperforming random sampling. Provided that results are obtained rapidly, the test frequency required to suppress an epidemic is monotonic and near-linear with respect to R0, the infectious period, and the fraction of susceptible individuals. The testing regime is effective against both early phase and established epidemics, and additive to other interventions (e.g. contact tracing and social distancing). It is also robust to failure: any rate of testing reduces the number of infections, improving both public health and economic conditions. These conclusions are based on rigorous analysis and simulations of appropriate epidemiological models. A mass-produced, disposable test that could be used at home would be ideal, due to the optimal performance of concurrent tests that return immediate results.
