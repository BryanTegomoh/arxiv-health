Profiling of a network behind an infectious disease outbreak

arXiv ID: 0905.3582v5
Published: 2009-05-21
Authors: Yoshiharu Maeno
Categories: cs.AI, q-bio.PE
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a novel method to reverse-engineer the underlying network topology and transmission parameters driving infectious disease outbreaks. By employing stochastic differential equations within a meta-population framework and maximal likelihood estimation on early growth phase data, it can uncover the effectively decisive network structure and epidemiological parameters. The method's efficacy was demonstrated using both synthetic datasets and real-world data from the SARS outbreak.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a powerful computational tool to rapidly understand the dynamics and critical pathways of an emerging infectious disease. Such insights are invaluable for public health officials to implement timely, targeted, and effective interventions to control outbreaks and mitigate their societal and health impact.

AI Health Application

The research develops an AI-driven method (solving an inverse problem using stochastic differential equations and maximal likelihood estimation) to identify the effective transmission network topology and parameters of an infectious disease outbreak. This AI application provides a tool to better understand how diseases spread, predict their trajectory, and potentially inform public health interventions and strategies during early growth phases of outbreaks.

Key Points

  • Presents an inverse problem solution to identify the hidden network topology and transmission parameters of infectious outbreaks.
  • Models disease spread using stochastic differential equations (SDEs) within combined epidemiological compartment and meta-population network models.
  • Utilizes maximal likelihood estimation (MLE) based on probability density functions (PDFs) derived from SDEs for parameter and topology inference.
  • Specifically designed to operate on data collected during the *early growth phase* of an outbreak.
  • Validated against both computationally synthesized datasets and the real-world WHO dataset from the SARS outbreak.
  • Aims to reveal the "effectively decisive topology" crucial for understanding the stochastic propagation of diseases over heterogeneous networks.
  • Addresses the challenges posed by stochasticity and spatial heterogeneity in disease spread modeling.

Methodology

The method integrates epidemiological compartment models (e.g., SIR/SEIR) within a meta-population network framework, with population dynamics described by stochastic differential equations (SDEs). Probability density functions derived from these SDEs are then utilized for maximal likelihood estimation (MLE) to infer the effectively decisive network topology and transmission parameters from observed data, specifically focusing on the early growth phase of an outbreak.

Key Findings

The paper introduces a validated method capable of effectively solving the inverse problem of inferring the hidden network topology and transmission parameters governing stochastic infectious disease outbreaks. This capability was successfully demonstrated against both computationally synthesized datasets and real-world WHO data from the SARS epidemic, indicating its potential for practical application.

Clinical Impact

This method offers the potential for earlier identification of critical transmission routes, high-risk populations, and key epidemiological parameters during an outbreak. This can guide rapid, data-driven allocation of resources for testing, contact tracing, isolation strategies, vaccination campaigns, and targeted non-pharmaceutical interventions (NPIs), thereby improving outbreak response, patient management, and public health outcomes.

Limitations

The method is specifically designed and tested to operate effectively on data collected during the *early growth phase* of an infectious disease outbreak. Its applicability or performance in later phases or with incomplete data beyond this initial period is not discussed.

Abstract

Stochasticity and spatial heterogeneity are of great interest recently in studying the spread of an infectious disease. The presented method solves an inverse problem to discover the effectively decisive topology of a heterogeneous network and reveal the transmission parameters which govern the stochastic spreads over the network from a dataset on an infectious disease outbreak in the early growth phase. Populations in a combination of epidemiological compartment models and a meta-population network model are described by stochastic differential equations. Probability density functions are derived from the equations and used for the maximal likelihood estimation of the topology and parameters. The method is tested with computationally synthesized datasets and the WHO dataset on SARS outbreak.
