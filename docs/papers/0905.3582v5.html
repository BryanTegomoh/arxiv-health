Profiling of a network behind an infectious disease outbreak

arXiv ID: 0905.3582v5
Published: 2009-05-21
Authors: Yoshiharu Maeno
Categories: cs.AI, q-bio.PE
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00

Summary

This paper introduces a novel method to solve an inverse problem, identifying the effectively decisive network topology and transmission parameters governing stochastic infectious disease spread. It leverages early growth phase outbreak data by integrating stochastic differential equations with epidemiological compartment and meta-population network models, using maximal likelihood estimation for inference. The method was validated with both synthetic and real-world SARS outbreak data.

Medical Relevance

This method provides critical, data-driven insights into the underlying transmission pathways and parameters of infectious diseases, which is essential for understanding, predicting, and ultimately controlling outbreaks during their crucial initial stages.

AI Health Application

The research applies AI and advanced computational methods (e.g., solving inverse problems, maximal likelihood estimation, stochastic differential equations) to analyze the underlying network structure and transmission dynamics of infectious disease outbreaks. This enables better understanding, prediction, and potentially control strategies for public health interventions, thus representing an AI application in epidemiology and disease surveillance.

Key Points

  • Addresses the challenges of stochasticity and spatial heterogeneity in understanding infectious disease spread.
  • Proposes an inverse problem approach to infer critical network topology and reveal governing transmission parameters from outbreak data.
  • Utilizes a multi-component modeling framework: stochastic differential equations describe populations within epidemiological compartment models (e.g., SIR/SEIR) and a meta-population network model.
  • Employs probability density functions, derived from the stochastic differential equations, for maximal likelihood estimation of network structure and parameters.
  • The method is specifically designed for and applied to datasets from the early growth phase of an infectious disease outbreak.
  • Validation of the method's efficacy was performed using computationally synthesized datasets and the real-world WHO dataset on the SARS outbreak.

Methodology

The paper presents a method to solve an inverse problem using maximal likelihood estimation (MLE). It describes populations within a meta-population network model and epidemiological compartment models (e.g., SIR, SEIR) via stochastic differential equations (SDEs). Probability density functions (PDFs) are derived from these SDEs, which are then utilized for MLE to estimate the effectively decisive network topology and the associated transmission parameters from observed outbreak data.

Key Findings

The proposed method successfully solves the inverse problem, accurately discovering the effectively decisive network topology and revealing the transmission parameters that govern stochastic spreads. Its efficacy was demonstrated through successful application to both computationally synthesized datasets and the real-world WHO SARS outbreak dataset.

Clinical Impact

This research can significantly improve public health responses by enabling earlier and more precise identification of critical transmission pathways and high-risk populations during an outbreak's initial growth phase. This allows for targeted, efficient allocation of resources and interventions (e.g., vaccination, contact tracing, isolation strategies) to contain the spread more effectively and mitigate its impact on public health.

Limitations

The method is specifically designed for and tested with datasets from the *early growth phase* of an infectious disease outbreak, suggesting its direct applicability might be limited to this initial stage. Its effectiveness relies heavily on the quality and availability of detailed early outbreak data.

Future Directions

The abstract does not explicitly state future research directions. However, potential future work could include extending the method's applicability to later stages of an outbreak, incorporating diverse data types (e.g., genomic, mobility data), and evaluating its generalizability across a broader spectrum of infectious diseases.

Abstract

Stochasticity and spatial heterogeneity are of great interest recently in studying the spread of an infectious disease. The presented method solves an inverse problem to discover the effectively decisive topology of a heterogeneous network and reveal the transmission parameters which govern the stochastic spreads over the network from a dataset on an infectious disease outbreak in the early growth phase. Populations in a combination of epidemiological compartment models and a meta-population network model are described by stochastic differential equations. Probability density functions are derived from the equations and used for the maximal likelihood estimation of the topology and parameters. The method is tested with computationally synthesized datasets and the WHO dataset on SARS outbreak.
