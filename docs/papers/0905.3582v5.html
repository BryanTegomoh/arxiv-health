Profiling of a network behind an infectious disease outbreak

arXiv ID: 0905.3582v5
Published: 2009-05-21
Authors: Yoshiharu Maeno
Categories: cs.AI, q-bio.PE
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper presents a novel method for an inverse problem, aiming to uncover the effectively decisive network topology and key transmission parameters governing an infectious disease outbreak. By analyzing data from the early growth phase, it employs stochastic differential equations to model populations within a combined epidemiological compartment and meta-population network framework. The approach leverages probability density functions and maximal likelihood estimation, validated with both computationally synthesized datasets and real-world WHO SARS outbreak data.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a powerful computational tool to rapidly decipher the complex spread mechanisms of infectious diseases. By identifying critical transmission pathways and parameters early, it can significantly inform public health interventions and resource allocation strategies during nascent outbreaks, potentially saving lives and mitigating larger epidemics.

AI Health Application

This research applies AI/computational intelligence techniques (solving inverse problems, maximal likelihood estimation, stochastic differential equations as a modeling framework) to analyze complex network dynamics of infectious disease outbreaks. It aims to infer crucial epidemiological parameters and network structures from outbreak data, which is a direct application of AI for disease modeling, prediction, and informing public health interventions and biosecurity measures.

Key Points

  • Addresses the challenge of inferring underlying spread dynamics (topology and parameters) amidst stochasticity and spatial heterogeneity in infectious disease outbreaks.
  • Proposes an inverse problem solution to discover the effectively decisive topology of a heterogeneous network and reveal stochastic transmission parameters.
  • The methodology is specifically designed to work with data collected during the critical early growth phase of an outbreak.
  • Models population dynamics using stochastic differential equations (SDEs), integrating epidemiological compartment models with a meta-population network framework.
  • Employs probability density functions (PDFs), derived from the SDEs, for maximal likelihood estimation (MLE) to infer both network topology and transmission parameters.
  • Validation of the method's efficacy was performed using computationally synthesized datasets and applied to the real-world WHO dataset on the SARS outbreak.

Methodology

The method solves an inverse problem by describing populations through stochastic differential equations (SDEs). These SDEs integrate epidemiological compartment models (e.g., SIR, SEIR) with a meta-population network model to represent spatial heterogeneity. Probability density functions (PDFs) are derived from these SDEs, which are then used as the basis for maximal likelihood estimation (MLE) to infer both the network's decisive topology and the associated transmission parameters from observed outbreak data.

Key Findings

The presented method is capable of effectively discovering the decisive topology of a heterogeneous network and revealing the governing stochastic transmission parameters from infectious disease outbreak data. Its practical efficacy and robustness were demonstrated through successful testing with both computationally synthesized datasets and the real-world WHO dataset from the SARS outbreak.

Clinical Impact

This method could enable public health authorities to gain a rapid, data-driven understanding of an emerging pathogen's spread dynamics. This can lead to more targeted and efficient non-pharmaceutical interventions (e.g., contact tracing, travel restrictions), optimized resource deployment (e.g., hospital beds, vaccine distribution), and more accurate forecasting of outbreak trajectories during the critical early stages.

Limitations

The method is specifically designed and validated for datasets pertaining to the "early growth phase" of an infectious disease outbreak, which might limit its applicability or accuracy in analyzing data from later, more complex epidemic phases or endemic situations.

Future Directions

The abstract does not explicitly state future research directions. However, potential future work could involve extending the method to later epidemic phases, incorporating more complex population behaviors or intervention strategies, or applying it to a wider range of historical and ongoing outbreaks.

Abstract

Stochasticity and spatial heterogeneity are of great interest recently in studying the spread of an infectious disease. The presented method solves an inverse problem to discover the effectively decisive topology of a heterogeneous network and reveal the transmission parameters which govern the stochastic spreads over the network from a dataset on an infectious disease outbreak in the early growth phase. Populations in a combination of epidemiological compartment models and a meta-population network model are described by stochastic differential equations. Probability density functions are derived from the equations and used for the maximal likelihood estimation of the topology and parameters. The method is tested with computationally synthesized datasets and the WHO dataset on SARS outbreak.
