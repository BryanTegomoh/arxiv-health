Development and pilot evaluation of a virtual reality simulator for HDR prostate brachytherapy

Summary

This paper details the development and pilot evaluation of a virtual reality (VR) simulator designed for High Dose Rate (HDR) prostate brachytherapy. The simulator, comprising patient preparation and template-guided needle insertion modules, significantly increased self-reported confidence among oncology staff and trainees across various procedural and equipment-related knowledge domains immediately after training.

Medical Relevance

This VR simulator provides a safe, reproducible, and accessible platform for training medical professionals in complex HDR prostate brachytherapy, which is crucial for improving procedural competency and patient safety in a high-risk oncology procedure without exposing actual patients to learning-related errors.

Key Points

  • A virtual reality simulator was developed using Unreal Engine for HDR prostate brachytherapy training, featuring two distinct modules: patient preparation and template-guided needle insertion.
  • The evaluation involved oncology staff and trainees (N=11 for patient prep, N=27 for needle insertion) from two Canadian centers.
  • Pre- and post-surveys assessed self-reported confidence across four domains: recalling steps, explaining steps, identifying equipment, and explaining equipment function.
  • For the patient preparation module, statistically significant increases in confidence were observed across all four domains (p-values ranging from 0.002 to 0.023).
  • Similarly, for the needle insertion module, highly significant increases in confidence were found for all four domains (all p-values < 0.001).
  • The simulator proved feasible for deployment and was immediately associated with higher self-reported confidence in key procedural areas.
  • The findings are intended to inform the design and implementation of future curriculum for brachytherapy training.

Methodology

The study involved developing two VR modules (patient preparation and template-guided needle insertion) using Unreal Engine. A pilot evaluation was conducted at two sites with oncology staff and trainees. Participants completed pre- and post-intervention surveys to assess self-reported confidence in recalling/explaining procedural steps and identifying/explaining equipment function. Paired Wilcoxon signed rank tests were utilized to compare before and after confidence scores for each module.

Clinical Impact

This VR simulator provides a practical and innovative training tool that can enhance the foundational knowledge and confidence of oncology staff and trainees in performing HDR prostate brachytherapy. By enabling risk-free practice of complex procedures, it has the potential to improve the quality of training, reduce the learning curve, and ultimately contribute to safer and more effective treatment delivery for prostate cancer patients.

Limitations

The study was a pilot evaluation, and the assessment relied on self-reported confidence, which is a subjective measure. The sample sizes were relatively small (N=11 for patient preparation, N=27 for needle insertion), and the evaluation focused on immediate gains, not assessing long-term retention of confidence or objective skill transfer to clinical practice.

Future Directions

The findings can directly inform future curriculum design and implementation work in medical education for brachytherapy. Subsequent research could investigate the simulator's impact on objective skill performance, long-term knowledge retention, and ultimately, patient outcomes.

Abstract

Purpose: To develop a virtual reality simulator for high dose rate prostate brachytherapy and to test whether participation is associated with immediate gains in self-reported confidence across predefined procedural domains in two cohorts. Methods: Two modules were developed and implemented using Unreal Engine: patient preparation and template guided needle insertion. Oncology staff and trainees completed pre and post surveys that assessed confidence for recalling steps, explaining steps, identifying equipment, and explaining equipment function. Studies were conducted at the Hands On Brachytherapy Workshop (HOWBT) in London, Ontario, and at Sunnybrook Odette Cancer Centre in Toronto, Ontario. Paired Wilcoxon signed rank tests with two-sided p values compared before and after scores within each module. Results: Patient preparation (N=11) confidence increased for recalling steps (W=65, p=0.002), explaining steps (W=51, p = 0.023), identifying equipment (W=65, p=0.002), and explaining equipment function (W=60, p=0.0078). Needle insertion (N=27) confidence increased for recalling steps (W=292, p<0.001), explaining steps (W=347, p<0.001), identifying equipment (W=355, p<0.001), and explaining equipment function (W=354, p<0.001). Conclusion: The simulator was feasible to deploy and was associated with higher self-reported confidence across key domains immediately after training. Findings may inform future curriculum design and implementation work.
