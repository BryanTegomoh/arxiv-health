Summary

This paper details the development and pilot evaluation of a virtual reality (VR) simulator designed for high dose rate (HDR) prostate brachytherapy. Medical Relevance

This research addresses a critical need in medical training for complex procedures like HDR prostate brachytherapy by providing a safe, repeatable, and immersive learning environment, potentially improving trainee proficiency and ultimately patient safety and outcomes.

Key Points

  • A VR simulator was developed for HDR prostate brachytherapy, featuring two modules: patient preparation and template-guided needle insertion.
  • The simulator was built using Unreal Engine, leveraging modern game development technology for realistic rendering and interaction.
  • Participants included oncology staff and trainees, evaluated at two distinct clinical/educational sites in Ontario.
  • Pre- and post-training surveys assessed self-reported confidence across four domains: recalling steps, explaining steps, identifying equipment, and explaining equipment function.
  • For the patient preparation module (N=11), all four confidence domains showed significant increases (p-values ranging from 0.002 to 0.023).
  • For the needle insertion module (N=27), all four confidence domains also demonstrated highly significant increases (p<0.001 for all).
  • The study concluded that the VR simulator is feasible for deployment and effectively boosts immediate self-reported confidence, suggesting its utility in medical education.

Methodology

Two VR modules, patient preparation and template guided needle insertion, were developed using Unreal Engine. Oncology staff and trainees participated in a pilot evaluation conducted at the Hands On Brachytherapy Workshop and Sunnybrook Odette Cancer Centre. Self-reported confidence was measured using pre and post-training surveys for recalling steps, explaining steps, identifying equipment, and explaining equipment function. Statistical analysis involved paired Wilcoxon signed rank tests with two-sided p-values to compare before and after scores within each module.

Key Findings

Significant immediate gains in self-reported confidence were observed across all predefined procedural domains after participation in both VR modules. For patient preparation (N=11), confidence increased significantly for recalling steps (W=65, p=0.002), explaining steps (W=51, p=0.023), identifying equipment (W=65, p=0.002), and explaining equipment function (W=60, p=0.0078). For needle insertion (N=27), even stronger increases were found across all domains (W=292-355, all p<0.001).

Clinical Impact

The development and successful pilot evaluation of this VR simulator offer a valuable tool for training radiation oncology professionals in HDR prostate brachytherapy. By enhancing confidence and potentially procedural knowledge in a low-risk virtual environment, it could shorten the learning curve, improve the quality of patient care by better-prepared staff, and standardize training protocols for a technically demanding procedure.

Limitations

The study's primary limitation is its reliance on self-reported confidence as an outcome measure, rather than objective skill assessment or performance metrics. It was a pilot evaluation with relatively small sample sizes (N=11 and N=27), and immediate gains in confidence do not necessarily correlate with long-term retention or actual procedural competence in a clinical setting.

Future Directions

The findings are intended to inform future curriculum design and implementation work. Future research should include objective skill assessment, larger-scale studies, evaluation of long-term knowledge retention, and assessment of the simulator's impact on clinical performance and patient outcomes.

Abstract

Purpose: To develop a virtual reality simulator for high dose rate prostate brachytherapy and to test whether participation is associated with immediate gains in self-reported confidence across predefined procedural domains in two cohorts. Methods: Two modules were developed and implemented using Unreal Engine: patient preparation and template guided needle insertion. Oncology staff and trainees completed pre and post surveys that assessed confidence for recalling steps, explaining steps, identifying equipment, and explaining equipment function. Studies were conducted at the Hands On Brachytherapy Workshop (HOWBT) in London, Ontario, and at Sunnybrook Odette Cancer Centre in Toronto, Ontario. Paired Wilcoxon signed rank tests with two-sided p values compared before and after scores within each module. Results: Patient preparation (N=11) confidence increased for recalling steps (W=65, p=0.002), explaining steps (W=51, p = 0.023), identifying equipment (W=65, p=0.002), and explaining equipment function (W=60, p=0.0078). Needle insertion (N=27) confidence increased for recalling steps (W=292, p<0.001), explaining steps (W=347, p<0.001), identifying equipment (W=355, p<0.001), and explaining equipment function (W=354, p<0.001). Conclusion: The simulator was feasible to deploy and was associated with higher self-reported confidence across key domains immediately after training. Findings may inform future curriculum design and implementation work.
