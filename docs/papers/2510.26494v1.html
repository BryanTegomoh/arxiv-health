Simulating and Experimenting with Social Media Mobilization Using LLM Agents

arXiv ID: 2510.26494v1
Published: 2025-10-30
Authors: Sadegh Shirani, Mohsen Bayati
Categories: cs.SI, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.80 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This research paper introduces an agent-based simulation framework that uses heterogeneous LLM agents, realistic demographics, and authentic social network topology to model political mobilization on social media. The framework successfully reproduces qualitative patterns of mobilization observed in landmark field experiments, particularly demonstrating stronger effects from social messaging and measurable peer spillovers. It offers a controlled, reproducible environment for exploring counterfactual designs in social influence research.

Medical Relevance

This simulation framework, while applied to political mobilization, is highly relevant to medicine and health by providing a robust methodology to understand how health-related information, public health messages, or even misinformation spreads through social networks and influences health behaviors. It can model the dynamics of peer influence crucial for vaccine uptake, lifestyle changes, and adoption of new medical advice at scale.

AI Health Application

The LLM agent-based simulation framework developed here can be applied to model and predict the spread of health-related information (including misinformation) on social media, understand peer influence on health behaviors (e.g., vaccine uptake, adherence to medical advice), and optimize the design and targeting of public health interventions or health campaigns through digital networks.

Key Points

  • Develops an agent-based simulation framework for social media mobilization leveraging LLM agents.
  • Integrates real U.S. Census demographic distributions and authentic Twitter network topology for realistic simulation environments.
  • Utilizes heterogeneous LLM agents (GPT-4.1, Mini, Nano) to represent varying levels of political sophistication among simulated individuals.
  • Replicates informational and social mobilization treatments from a 61-million-person Facebook experiment.
  • Reproduces qualitative patterns of mobilization, including stronger effects under social message treatments and measurable peer spillovers.
  • Provides a controlled and reproducible environment for testing counterfactual designs and sensitivity analyses in social mobilization.
  • Serves as a bridge between high-validity field experiments and flexible computational modeling for understanding large-scale social influence.

Methodology

The study employs an agent-based simulation framework. It assigns demographic attributes from U.S. Census data, political stances, and one of three LLM variants (GPT-4.1, Mini, Nano) representing political sophistication to each agent. These agents interact over a realistic Twitter network topology, receiving personalized feeds. The framework dynamically updates agents' engagement and voting intentions, replicating informational and social mobilization treatments of a prior field experiment.

Key Findings

The simulation successfully reproduced qualitative patterns observed in large-scale field experiments, including more pronounced mobilization effects when messages leveraged social connections (social message treatments) compared to purely informational ones. Furthermore, the model detected measurable peer spillover effects, indicating that the influence extends beyond directly targeted individuals to their social contacts. This demonstrates the framework's ability to realistically model complex social influence dynamics.

Clinical Impact

This framework has significant potential to inform public health strategies. It could be adapted to simulate the spread of health information, model the effectiveness of different public health campaigns (e.g., vaccine promotion, disease prevention, healthy lifestyle encouragement) by testing various messaging strategies (informational vs. social influence) and their impact on health behaviors. It could also help identify mechanisms for rapid spread of health misinformation and aid in developing targeted interventions to mitigate its impact, thereby optimizing resource allocation for health communication efforts.

Limitations

While the abstract highlights the framework's strengths in reproducibility and controlled experimentation, an inherent limitation is its reliance on a simulated environment. The qualitative reproduction of patterns, while valuable, may not perfectly capture the nuanced quantitative effects or all unforeseen complexities of real-world human behavior in diverse health contexts. The fidelity of LLM agents to accurately model human health decision-making and susceptibility to influence requires further validation in health-specific applications.

Future Directions

Future research could adapt this framework to model specific health behaviors (e.g., vaccine hesitancy, chronic disease management adherence, mental health seeking behavior), integrating health-specific agent attributes like health literacy or trust in medical institutions. It could also be used to proactively test counterfactual public health interventions, identify optimal social network pathways for health message dissemination, and conduct sensitivity analyses on factors influencing health outcomes on social media platforms.

Abstract

Online social networks have transformed the ways in which political mobilization messages are disseminated, raising new questions about how peer influence operates at scale. Building on the landmark 61-million-person Facebook experiment \citep{bond201261}, we develop an agent-based simulation framework that integrates real U.S. Census demographic distributions, authentic Twitter network topology, and heterogeneous large language model (LLM) agents to examine the effect of mobilization messages on voter turnout. Each simulated agent is assigned demographic attributes, a personal political stance, and an LLM variant (\texttt{GPT-4.1}, \texttt{GPT-4.1-Mini}, or \texttt{GPT-4.1-Nano}) reflecting its political sophistication. Agents interact over realistic social network structures, receiving personalized feeds and dynamically updating their engagement behaviors and voting intentions. Experimental conditions replicate the informational and social mobilization treatments of the original Facebook study. Across scenarios, the simulator reproduces qualitative patterns observed in field experiments, including stronger mobilization effects under social message treatments and measurable peer spillovers. Our framework provides a controlled, reproducible environment for testing counterfactual designs and sensitivity analyses in political mobilization research, offering a bridge between high-validity field experiments and flexible computational modeling.\footnote{Code and data available at https://github.com/CausalMP/LLM-SocioPol}
