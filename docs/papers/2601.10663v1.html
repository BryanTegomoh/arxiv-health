Sporadic Creutzfeldt Jakob disease presenting with cerebral atrophy following traumatic brain injury mimicking hydrocephalus a case report and literature review

Authors: Chun Zeng, Dezhu Gao, Liang Wu

Summary

This case report details a patient diagnosed with sporadic Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (sCJD) who initially presented with cognitive deficits, gait instability, and incontinence, 8 months after a traumatic brain injury (TBI). The initial CT imaging showed cerebral ventricle dilation, misleadingly mimicking hydrocephalus, until further specialized investigations confirmed sCJD as the underlying cause of cerebral atrophy.

Medical Relevance

This case improves clinicians' understanding of sCJD's varied and potentially misleading presentations, especially in the context of prior TBI and atypical imaging. It underscores the critical need for advanced diagnostic testing to correctly identify rare, rapidly progressive neurological conditions and prevent misdiagnosis.

Key Points

  • Sporadic Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (sCJD) is a rapidly progressive and ultimately fatal neurodegenerative disorder, typically resulting in death within one year.
  • The presented case involved a patient who developed significant cognitive deficits, gait instability, and urinary/fecal incontinence 8 months following a traumatic brain injury (TBI).
  • Initial CT imaging revealed cerebral ventricle dilation, which could easily be misinterpreted as hydrocephalus, despite the underlying pathology being cerebral atrophy.
  • A definitive diagnosis of sCJD was subsequently established through a comprehensive diagnostic battery, including elevated 14-3-3 and tau proteins in the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), characteristic MRI findings, and EEG patterns.
  • The patient's clinical symptoms rapidly deteriorated, a hallmark of sCJD, necessitating a return to local hospital for conservative palliative care due to the lack of effective treatment.
  • The report emphasizes the critical importance of meticulous differential diagnosis to distinguish rare neurodegenerative disorders like sCJD from more common conditions, particularly when presented with atypical imaging findings (e.g., atrophy mimicking hydrocephalus).
  • This case highlights an unusual presentation where sCJD symptoms and significant cerebral atrophy became evident following a TBI, challenging initial diagnostic assumptions.

Methodology

The study primarily utilizes a case report methodology, detailing the clinical progression, diagnostic workup, and outcome of a single patient. This is complemented by a literature review. Diagnostic methods included clinical assessment, CT imaging, MRI, EEG, and cerebrospinal fluid (CSF) analysis for 14-3-3 and tau proteins.

Key Findings

Sporadic CJD can manifest with cerebral atrophy mimicking hydrocephalus, particularly after traumatic brain injury, which can complicate initial diagnosis. Comprehensive diagnostic evaluation, involving specific CSF biomarkers (14-3-3, tau), advanced neuroimaging (MRI), and electroencephalography (EEG), is crucial for accurate confirmation of sCJD in such atypical presentations. The patient's rapid clinical deterioration further supported the sCJD diagnosis.

Clinical Impact

This case report enhances clinical awareness among neurologists, neurosurgeons, and radiologists regarding the diverse and challenging presentations of sCJD, especially when a history of TBI is present or when initial imaging suggests common conditions like hydrocephalus. It reinforces the necessity of specialized diagnostic investigations to achieve an accurate diagnosis, enabling appropriate patient management, counseling, and avoiding unnecessary interventions for misdiagnosed conditions.

Limitations

As a single case report, the generalizability of these findings is inherently limited. The abstract does not provide detailed discussion on the potential mechanistic link between traumatic brain injury and the manifestation or acceleration of sCJD in this specific patient, nor does it address the prevalence of such atypical presentations.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated as future research, the paper implicitly highlights the ongoing need for a deeper understanding of the varied clinical and radiological spectrum of rare neurodegenerative diseases. Future work could investigate the potential role of TBI as a modifying factor or trigger in neurodegenerative processes like sCJD, and further characterize atypical imaging presentations to improve early and accurate diagnosis.

Abstract

Introduction Sporadic Creutzfeldt Jakob disease sCJD is a rapidly progressive neurodegenerative disease without effective treatment that usually results in death within one year. The recently applied methods have improved the accuracy of the disease diagnosis and the specific radiological findings provide the necessary information for differential diagnosis. Research question The research is aimed to provide a different perspective on the development of CJD and associated literature review. Materials and methods The study presents a case who presented cognitive deficits, gait instability, and urinary and fecal incontinence suffered from traumatic brain injury eight months ago before admission with cerebral ventricle dilation on CT images. Furthermore, studies describe relevant cases are also included. Results The patients symptoms got deteriorated. Further examinations, including 14-3-3 and tau proteins in the cerebrospinal fluid CSF, MRI, and EEG, confirmed the patients diagnosis of sCJD. He returned to the local hospital for the conservative treatment without effective medical intervention. Conclusion This case illustrates the diagnostic process of CJD and underscores the importance of distinguishing rare disorders from common conditions to achieve a comprehensive understanding of the disease.
