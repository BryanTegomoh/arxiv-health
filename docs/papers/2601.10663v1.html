Sporadic Creutzfeldt Jakob disease presenting with cerebral atrophy following traumatic brain injury mimicking hydrocephalus a case report and literature review

arXiv ID: 2601.10663v1
Published: 2026-01-15
Authors: Chun Zeng, Dezhu Gao, Liang Wu
Categories: q-bio.NC
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper presents a case of sporadic Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (sCJD) in a patient who initially exhibited symptoms and imaging findings (cerebral ventricle dilation post-TBI) that mimicked hydrocephalus. The study details the diagnostic process, which eventually confirmed sCJD through specific CSF biomarkers (14-3-3 and tau proteins), characteristic MRI findings, and EEG abnormalities. It underscores the critical importance of a comprehensive differential diagnosis to distinguish rare neurodegenerative diseases from more common conditions.

Medical Relevance

This case report is highly relevant for clinicians, especially neurologists and neurosurgeons, as it illustrates an atypical presentation of sCJD that can mimic hydrocephalus. It emphasizes the critical need for a high index of suspicion and comprehensive neurological workup to accurately differentiate rapidly progressive neurodegenerative disorders from more common conditions, ensuring correct diagnosis and appropriate patient counseling despite the lack of effective treatment for sCJD.

Key Points

  • A patient presented with cognitive deficits, gait instability, and urinary/fecal incontinence, eight months after experiencing a traumatic brain injury (TBI).
  • Initial CT imaging revealed cerebral ventricle dilation, leading to a potential misinterpretation or suspicion of hydrocephalus.
  • The patient's neurological symptoms rapidly deteriorated, necessitating further advanced diagnostic evaluations.
  • The diagnosis of sporadic Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (sCJD) was ultimately confirmed by the detection of elevated 14-3-3 and tau proteins in the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF), along with characteristic MRI abnormalities and EEG patterns.
  • This case highlights the diagnostic challenge of sCJD, particularly when its presentation, especially post-TBI and with ventricle dilation, can mimic more common conditions like hydrocephalus.
  • The study implicitly includes a literature review to provide a broader context and different perspective on CJD development.
  • sCJD remains a rapidly progressive and fatal neurodegenerative disease for which no effective medical treatment currently exists, necessitating conservative patient management.

Methodology

The study employs a case report methodology, detailing the clinical course, diagnostic challenges, and confirmatory tests for a single patient diagnosed with sporadic Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease. This clinical narrative is augmented by a literature review to contextualize the case and provide insights into CJD development.

Key Findings

The primary key finding is the confirmed diagnosis of sporadic Creutzfeldt-Jakob disease (sCJD) in a patient whose initial presentation, including cerebral ventricle dilation following traumatic brain injury, mimicked hydrocephalus. The definitive diagnosis was achieved through specific CSF analysis showing elevated 14-3-3 and tau proteins, characteristic MRI signal changes, and supportive EEG abnormalities.

Clinical Impact

The clinical impact lies in enhancing diagnostic vigilance among healthcare professionals for rare neurodegenerative diseases like sCJD, especially in complex presentations that may initially suggest more common conditions. It reinforces the importance of utilizing specific diagnostic tools (CSF biomarkers, advanced MRI, EEG) for accurate differential diagnosis, leading to timely and correct patient management, even when effective curative treatments are unavailable.

Abstract

Introduction Sporadic Creutzfeldt Jakob disease sCJD is a rapidly progressive neurodegenerative disease without effective treatment that usually results in death within one year. The recently applied methods have improved the accuracy of the disease diagnosis and the specific radiological findings provide the necessary information for differential diagnosis. Research question The research is aimed to provide a different perspective on the development of CJD and associated literature review. Materials and methods The study presents a case who presented cognitive deficits, gait instability, and urinary and fecal incontinence suffered from traumatic brain injury eight months ago before admission with cerebral ventricle dilation on CT images. Furthermore, studies describe relevant cases are also included. Results The patients symptoms got deteriorated. Further examinations, including 14-3-3 and tau proteins in the cerebrospinal fluid CSF, MRI, and EEG, confirmed the patients diagnosis of sCJD. He returned to the local hospital for the conservative treatment without effective medical intervention. Conclusion This case illustrates the diagnostic process of CJD and underscores the importance of distinguishing rare disorders from common conditions to achieve a comprehensive understanding of the disease.
