Learning temporal embeddings from electronic health records of chronic kidney disease patients

arXiv ID: 2601.18675v1
Published: 2026-01-26
Authors: Aditya Kumar, Mario A. Cypko, Oliver Amft
Categories: cs.LG, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper investigates the ability of temporal embedding models, specifically recurrent neural networks, to learn clinically meaningful and task-agnostic representations from longitudinal electronic health records of chronic kidney disease (CKD) patients. It demonstrates that the Time-aware LSTM (T-LSTM) produces superior, more structured embeddings for CKD stage classification. Furthermore, learning embeddings as an intermediate step consistently outperforms direct end-to-end prediction for in-ICU mortality, indicating a more effective approach for clinical predictive tasks.

Medical Relevance

This work is crucial for developing transparent and generalizable AI models in medicine, enabling a deeper understanding of disease dynamics from patient data. By providing better representations for chronic conditions like CKD, it can improve diagnosis, risk stratification, and personalized treatment planning, ultimately enhancing patient care and outcomes.

AI Health Application

The AI application involves developing and evaluating temporal embedding models (using LSTMs and their variants) to extract clinically meaningful representations from longitudinal Electronic Health Records (EHRs). These representations are then applied to improve performance in critical clinical tasks such as classifying CKD stages and predicting in-ICU mortality, aiming to support model-guided medicine and enhance clinical prediction capabilities.

Key Points

  • The research aims to determine if temporal embedding models can learn clinically meaningful and transparent representations from longitudinal EHRs without sacrificing predictive performance, and how architectural choices influence embedding quality.
  • Three recurrent neural network architectures were compared: vanilla LSTM, attention-augmented LSTM, and Time-aware LSTM (T-LSTM), focusing on chronic kidney disease (CKD) patients using the MIMIC-IV dataset.
  • Models were trained both as embedding generators and as direct end-to-end predictors to evaluate the impact of the learning paradigm on performance and representation quality.
  • T-LSTM significantly outperformed other architectures in producing structured embeddings, achieving a lower Davies-Bouldin Index (9.91) and higher CKD stage classification accuracy (0.74) compared to vanilla LSTM (DBI=15.85, accuracy=0.63) and attention-augmented LSTM (DBI=20.72, accuracy=0.67).
  • For in-ICU mortality prediction, models that learned embeddings as an intermediate step consistently outperformed direct end-to-end predictors, improving accuracy from 0.72-0.75 to 0.82-0.83.
  • The findings suggest that learning robust, task-agnostic embeddings as an intermediate step is a more effective strategy than direct end-to-end learning for clinical prediction tasks based on temporal EHR data.

Methodology

The study utilized the MIMIC-IV dataset, specifically focusing on patients diagnosed with Chronic Kidney Disease. It employed a comparative analysis of three recurrent neural network architectures: a vanilla Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), an attention-augmented LSTM, and a Time-aware LSTM (T-LSTM). Each model was trained in two configurations: as an embedding model (generating intermediate representations) and as a direct end-to-end predictor. Embedding quality was assessed using CKD stage clustering (quantified by Davies-Bouldin Index and classification accuracy) and the models' performance on an in-ICU mortality prediction task.

Key Findings

The T-LSTM architecture produced more structured and clinically meaningful embeddings, achieving a lower Davies-Bouldin Index (9.91) and higher CKD stage classification accuracy (0.74) compared to the vanilla LSTM (DBI=15.85, accuracy=0.63) and attention-augmented LSTM (DBI=20.72, accuracy=0.67). Importantly, for in-ICU mortality prediction, models trained to learn embeddings as an intermediate step consistently outperformed direct end-to-end predictors, improving accuracy from a range of 0.72-0.75 to 0.82-0.83.

Clinical Impact

The ability of T-LSTM to create structured and clinically meaningful representations from EHRs could significantly enhance the development of explainable and generalizable AI tools for chronic disease management and critical care. Improved CKD staging and more accurate mortality prediction can lead to earlier and more precise clinical interventions, better resource allocation in ICUs, and more personalized treatment plans, ultimately optimizing patient care and potentially reducing adverse outcomes.

Abstract

We investigate whether temporal embedding models trained on longitudinal electronic health records can learn clinically meaningful representations without compromising predictive performance, and how architectural choices affect embedding quality. Model-guided medicine requires representations that capture disease dynamics while remaining transparent and task agnostic, whereas most clinical prediction models are optimised for a single task. Representation learning facilitates learning embeddings that generalise across downstream tasks, and recurrent architectures are well-suited for modelling temporal structure in observational clinical data. Using the MIMIC-IV dataset, we study patients with chronic kidney disease (CKD) and compare three recurrent architectures: a vanilla LSTM, an attention-augmented LSTM, and a time-aware LSTM (T-LSTM). All models are trained both as embedding models and as direct end-to-end predictors. Embedding quality is evaluated via CKD stage clustering and in-ICU mortality prediction. The T-LSTM produces more structured embeddings, achieving a lower Davies-Bouldin Index (DBI = 9.91) and higher CKD stage classification accuracy (0.74) than the vanilla LSTM (DBI = 15.85, accuracy = 0.63) and attention-augmented LSTM (DBI = 20.72, accuracy = 0.67). For in-ICU mortality prediction, embedding models consistently outperform end-to-end predictors, improving accuracy from 0.72-0.75 to 0.82-0.83, which indicates that learning embeddings as an intermediate step is more effective than direct end-to-end learning.
