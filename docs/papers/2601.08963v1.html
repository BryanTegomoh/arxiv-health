Breaking the Bottlenecks: Scalable Diffusion Models for 3D Molecular Generation

Summary

This paper provides a principled reinterpretation of the Directly Denoising Diffusion Model (DDDM) for 3D molecular generation using the Reverse Transition Kernel (RTK) framework, establishing theoretical clarity for its efficient deterministic denoising. This unification addresses key bottlenecks in traditional diffusion models, enabling faster convergence, higher structural fidelity, and enhanced chemical validity in generating novel molecular structures. The framework ensures numerical stability and preserves molecular symmetries, making 3D molecular generation more scalable and reliable.

This research significantly accelerates and improves the reliability of generating novel 3D molecular structures, which is a foundational and often rate-limiting step in early-stage drug discovery, lead optimization, and the design of new therapeutic compounds.

Key Points

  • Traditional diffusion models for 3D molecular generation are constrained by long sampling trajectories, stochastic variance in the reverse process, and limited structural awareness in denoising dynamics.
  • The Directly Denoising Diffusion Model (DDDM) mitigates these inefficiencies through deterministic denoising steps, but its theoretical underpinnings lacked clarity.
  • This work reinterprets DDDM within the principled Reverse Transition Kernel (RTK) framework, unifying deterministic and stochastic diffusion processes under a shared probabilistic formalism.
  • The RTK perspective elucidates that the DDDM reverse process, expressed as an approximate kernel operator, implicitly optimizes a structured transport map between noisy and clean molecular samples, explaining its inference efficiency.
  • The RTK view resolves several bottlenecks by ensuring numerical stability via well-conditioned reverse kernels, improving sample consistency by eliminating stochastic variance, and enabling scalable, SE(3)-equivariant denoisers that preserve molecular symmetry.
  • Empirically, RTK-guided deterministic denoising demonstrates faster convergence and achieves higher structural fidelity compared to stochastic diffusion models.
  • The method successfully preserves chemical validity across the GEOM-DRUGS dataset, indicating its robustness and practical utility in generating chemically sound molecular structures.

Abstract

Diffusion models have emerged as a powerful class of generative models for molecular design, capable of capturing complex structural distributions and achieving high fidelity in 3D molecule generation. However, their widespread use remains constrained by long sampling trajectories, stochastic variance in the reverse process, and limited structural awareness in denoising dynamics. The Directly Denoising Diffusion Model (DDDM) mitigates these inefficiencies by replacing stochastic reverse MCMC updates with deterministic denoising step, substantially reducing inference time. Yet, the theoretical underpinnings of such deterministic updates have remained opaque. In this work, we provide a principled reinterpretation of DDDM through the lens of the Reverse Transition Kernel (RTK) framework by Huang et al. 2024, unifying deterministic and stochastic diffusion under a shared probabilistic formalism. By expressing the DDDM reverse process as an approximate kernel operator, we show that the direct denoising process implicitly optimizes a structured transport map between noisy and clean samples. This perspective elucidates why deterministic denoising achieves efficient inference. Beyond theoretical clarity, this reframing resolves several long-standing bottlenecks in molecular diffusion. The RTK view ensures numerical stability by enforcing well-conditioned reverse kernels, improves sample consistency by eliminating stochastic variance, and enables scalable and symmetry-preserving denoisers that respect SE(3) equivariance. Empirically, we demonstrate that RTK-guided deterministic denoising achieves faster convergence and higher structural fidelity than stochastic diffusion models, while preserving chemical validity across GEOM-DRUGS dataset. Code, models, and datasets are publicly available in our project repository.
