Systematic Evaluation of Preprocessing Techniques for Accurate Image Registration in Digital Pathology

Summary

This study systematically evaluated various preprocessing techniques, particularly color transformations, to enhance accurate image registration between hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) and non-linear multimodal images in digital pathology. The research found that CycleGAN color transformation consistently achieved the lowest registration errors, significantly improving spatial alignment compared to other methods. These findings underscore the critical role of effective preprocessing in enabling reliable multi-modal image analysis for advanced applications in digital pathology.

Medical Relevance

Accurate image registration is fundamental in digital pathology for integrating information from various stains and modalities, enabling precise biomarker analysis, tissue reconstruction, and comprehensive diagnostic assessment. This research improves the reliability of such integration, which is critical for advancing personalized medicine and pathology research.

AI Health Application

This research applies AI (specifically, deep learning via CycleGAN) and other computational image processing techniques to preprocess and register medical images in digital pathology. This improves the accuracy of spatial alignment between different pathological stains or imaging modalities, which is critical for automated analysis, quantitative biomarker assessment, and integrated multi-modal pathological diagnoses. This contributes to enhancing the efficiency and reliability of AI-driven diagnostic tools in pathology.

  • **Dataset:** Utilized 20 tissue sample pairs, each comprising H&E stained images and non-linear multimodal images.
  • **Preprocessing Methods:** Investigated various color transformations (CycleGAN, Macenko, Reinhard, Vahadane), alongside inversion, contrast adjustment, intensity normalization, and denoising.

  • **Key Finding - CycleGAN Superiority:** CycleGAN color transformation consistently demonstrated the lowest registration errors across both scenarios (original and inverted multimodal images) compared to Macenko, Reinhard, and Vahadane.

Methodology

The study utilized a dataset of 20 tissue sample pairs (H&E and non-linear multimodal images). Each pair underwent various preprocessing steps including color transformations (CycleGAN, Macenko, Reinhard, Vahadane), inversion, contrast adjustment, intensity normalization, and denoising. All images were registered using the VALIS method, which performs rigid registration followed by two-step non-rigid registration on both low and high-resolution images. Registration performance was quantitatively evaluated using the relative Target Registration Error (rTRE), summarized as median of median rTRE (MMrTRE) and average of median rTRE (AMrTRE), and qualitatively through a custom point-based evaluation with ten manually selected key points. Two scenarios (original vs. inverted multimodal images) were analyzed.

Key Findings

The most significant finding was that CycleGAN color transformation consistently achieved the lowest registration errors across both scenarios tested (original and inverted multimodal images). In contrast, other investigated color transformation methods (Macenko, Reinhard, Vahadane) resulted in higher registration errors. This highlights that applying an effective color transformation, specifically CycleGAN, as a preprocessing step critically improves the spatial alignment accuracy between different imaging modalities in digital pathology.

This enhancement supports more precise disease characterization, personalized treatment strategies, and robust development of AI-driven diagnostic tools in digital pathology.

Abstract

Image registration refers to the process of spatially aligning two or more images by mapping them into a common coordinate system, so that corresponding anatomical or tissue structures are matched across images. In digital pathology, registration enables direct comparison and integration of information from different stains or imaging modalities, sup-porting applications such as biomarker analysis and tissue reconstruction. Accurate registration of images from different modalities is an essential step in digital pathology. In this study, we investigated how various color transformation techniques affect image registration between hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) stained images and non-linear multimodal images. We used a dataset of 20 tissue sample pairs, with each pair undergoing several preprocessing steps, including different color transformation (CycleGAN, Macenko, Reinhard, Vahadane), inversion, contrast adjustment, intensity normalization, and denoising. All images were registered using the VALIS registration method, which first applies rigid registration and then performs non-rigid registration in two steps on both low and high-resolution images. Registration performance was evaluated using the relative Target Registration Error (rTRE). We reported the median of median rTRE values (MMrTRE) and the average of median rTRE values (AMrTRE) for each method. In addition, we performed a custom point-based evaluation using ten manually selected key points. Registration was done separately for two scenarios, using either the original or inverted multimodal images. In both scenarios, CycleGAN color transformation achieved the lowest registration errors, while the other methods showed higher errors. These findings show that applying color transformation before registration improves alignment between images from different modalities and supports more reliable analysis in digital pathology.
