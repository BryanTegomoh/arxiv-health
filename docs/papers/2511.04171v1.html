Systematic Evaluation of Preprocessing Techniques for Accurate Image Registration in Digital Pathology

Summary

This study systematically evaluated various preprocessing techniques, specifically color transformations, to improve the accuracy of image registration between Hematoxylin and Eosin (H&E) stained and non-linear multimodal images in digital pathology. The research found that applying CycleGAN color transformation significantly reduced registration errors compared to other methods, thereby enabling more reliable integration and analysis of multi-modal pathological data. These findings underscore the importance of optimal preprocessing for accurate multi-modal alignment in computational pathology.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medical and health fields as it directly addresses a critical technical challenge in digital pathology: accurately aligning images from diverse sources. Improved registration enables pathologists and researchers to precisely compare and integrate spatial information, which is fundamental for advanced biomarker analysis, detailed tissue reconstruction, and developing more robust diagnostic and prognostic tools.

Methodology

The study utilized a dataset of 20 tissue sample pairs, each comprising an H&E stained image and a non-linear multimodal image. These pairs underwent several preprocessing steps, including different color transformation techniques (CycleGAN, Macenko, Reinhard, Vahadane), inversion, contrast adjustment, intensity normalization, and denoising. Image registration was performed using the VALIS method, which applies rigid registration followed by a two-step non-rigid registration on both low and high-resolution images. Registration performance was quantitatively assessed using the relative Target Registration Error (rTRE), reported as the median of median rTRE (MMrTRE) and average of median rTRE (AMrTRE), in addition to a custom point-based evaluation involving ten manually selected key points. The assessment was performed under two distinct scenarios: using original multimodal images and using inverted multimodal images.

Key Findings

The primary finding is that among all evaluated preprocessing techniques, CycleGAN color transformation consistently yielded the lowest registration errors, as quantified by rTRE, in both scenarios tested (original and inverted multimodal images). This indicates a significant advantage of CycleGAN in improving the spatial alignment between H&E and non-linear multimodal images compared to other methods such as Macenko, Reinhard, and Vahadane, which exhibited higher registration errors.

The clinical impact of this research is substantial, as it provides a practical method to enhance the accuracy of multi-modal image registration in digital pathology. This improved accuracy will lead to more precise co-localization of biomarkers, more reliable quantitative analysis of tissue morphology across different stains and modalities, and better integration of diverse diagnostic information. Ultimately, this can support more informed clinical decision-making, facilitate the development of more accurate diagnostic algorithms, and advance personalized treatment strategies in various medical fields, particularly in oncology and histopathology.

Abstract

Image registration refers to the process of spatially aligning two or more images by mapping them into a common coordinate system, so that corresponding anatomical or tissue structures are matched across images. In digital pathology, registration enables direct comparison and integration of information from different stains or imaging modalities, sup-porting applications such as biomarker analysis and tissue reconstruction. Accurate registration of images from different modalities is an essential step in digital pathology. In this study, we investigated how various color transformation techniques affect image registration between hematoxylin and eosin (H&E) stained images and non-linear multimodal images. We used a dataset of 20 tissue sample pairs, with each pair undergoing several preprocessing steps, including different color transformation (CycleGAN, Macenko, Reinhard, Vahadane), inversion, contrast adjustment, intensity normalization, and denoising. All images were registered using the VALIS registration method, which first applies rigid registration and then performs non-rigid registration in two steps on both low and high-resolution images. Registration performance was evaluated using the relative Target Registration Error (rTRE). We reported the median of median rTRE values (MMrTRE) and the average of median rTRE values (AMrTRE) for each method. In addition, we performed a custom point-based evaluation using ten manually selected key points. Registration was done separately for two scenarios, using either the original or inverted multimodal images. In both scenarios, CycleGAN color transformation achieved the lowest registration errors, while the other methods showed higher errors. These findings show that applying color transformation before registration improves alignment between images from different modalities and supports more reliable analysis in digital pathology.
