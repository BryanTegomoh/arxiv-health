Learning Subgroups with Maximum Treatment Effects without Causal Heuristics

arXiv ID: 2511.20189v1
Published: 2025-11-25
Authors: Lincen Yang, Zhong Li, Matthijs van Leeuwen, Saber Salehkaleybar
Categories: cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a novel approach for identifying patient subgroups that exhibit the maximum average treatment effect, a critical challenge in precision medicine and other domains. By directly formulating the problem within the Structural Causal Model (SCM) framework and assuming a partition-based data-generating model, the authors demonstrate that optimal subgroup discovery can be recast as a standard supervised learning task. Their method, instantiated with CART and avoiding ad-hoc causal heuristics, significantly outperforms existing baselines in accurately identifying target subgroups on synthetic and semi-synthetic datasets.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to precision medicine as it provides a more robust and accurate computational method for identifying specific patient cohorts most likely to benefit from a particular treatment, facilitating targeted therapies and improving patient outcomes.

AI Health Application

This research develops an AI/machine learning methodology for learning patient subgroups that exhibit the maximum average treatment effect. This application is crucial for precision medicine, where AI can be used to analyze patient data (genomic, clinical, lifestyle) to identify specific groups that are most likely to respond positively to a particular drug or intervention. This enables targeted therapies, improving efficacy and reducing adverse effects, thereby enhancing clinical decision support systems.

Methodology

The authors reformulate the problem of optimal subgroup discovery within the Structural Causal Model (SCM) framework. They theoretically show that, under the assumption of a partition-based data-generating model, this complex causal inference problem simplifies into a standard supervised learning task (either regression or classification) focused on recovering the data-generating models. They instantiate this general approach using the CART (Classification and Regression Trees) algorithm to learn the subgroups. The method's performance is empirically validated against various baseline approaches on a large collection of synthetic and semi-synthetic datasets.

Key Findings

The primary finding is that the proposed SCM-based approach, which re-frames subgroup discovery as a standard supervised learning problem and inherently avoids ad-hoc 'causal' heuristics, significantly and more accurately identifies subgroups with maximum treatment effects across diverse synthetic and semi-synthetic datasets, outperforming a wide range of existing baseline methods.

Clinical Impact

This method has significant potential to enhance clinical decision-making by enabling more precise patient stratification in clinical trials and in real-world settings. It could lead to the development of highly targeted therapies, minimizing ineffective treatments and adverse side effects, thereby improving overall treatment efficacy and patient quality of life in areas like oncology, cardiology, and rare diseases.

Limitations

The abstract implicitly notes a limitation through its core assumption: the method's theoretical reduction relies on "the assumption of a partition-based model." This implies that its applicability or performance might be constrained in scenarios where the true underlying data-generating process deviates significantly from a partition-based structure.

Future Directions

The abstract does not explicitly state future research directions. However, potential future work could involve investigating the method's robustness to violations of the partition-based model assumption, extending the framework to more complex data-generating processes, or exploring other advanced partition-based supervised learning algorithms beyond CART.

Abstract

Discovering subgroups with the maximum average treatment effect is crucial for targeted decision making in domains such as precision medicine, public policy, and education. While most prior work is formulated in the potential outcome framework, the corresponding structural causal model (SCM) for this task has been largely overlooked. In practice, two approaches dominate. The first estimates pointwise conditional treatment effects and then fits a tree on those estimates, effectively turning subgroup estimation into the harder problem of accurate pointwise estimation. The second constructs decision trees or rule sets with ad-hoc 'causal' heuristics, typically without rigorous justification for why a given heuristic may be used or whether such heuristics are necessary at all. We address these issues by studying the problem directly under the SCM framework. Under the assumption of a partition-based model, we show that optimal subgroup discovery reduces to recovering the data-generating models and hence a standard supervised learning problem (regression or classification). This allows us to adopt any partition-based methods to learn the subgroup from data. We instantiate the approach with CART, arguably one of the most widely used tree-based methods, to learn the subgroup with maximum treatment effect. Finally, on a large collection of synthetic and semi-synthetic datasets, we compare our method against a wide range of baselines and find that our approach, which avoids such causal heuristics, more accurately identifies subgroups with maximum treatment effect. Our source code is available at https://github.com/ylincen/causal-subgroup.
