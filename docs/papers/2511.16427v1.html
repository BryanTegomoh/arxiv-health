Generative Modeling of Clinical Time Series via Latent Stochastic Differential Equations

Summary

This paper introduces a generative modeling framework based on latent neural stochastic differential equations (SDEs) to overcome challenges in clinical time series data, such as irregular sampling, complex latent physiology, and inherent uncertainties. The framework effectively models non-linear interactions and stochasticity, demonstrating superior accuracy and uncertainty estimation compared to baseline models in individual treatment effect estimation and physiological forecasting tasks.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a powerful tool for analyzing complex clinical time series data, enabling more precise and uncertainty-aware predictions vital for understanding patient trajectories, personalizing treatment strategies, and supporting critical medical decision-making in dynamic clinical settings.

AI Health Application

The research develops a generative AI model based on latent neural stochastic differential equations to analyze complex clinical time series data. This AI application is designed to predict disease progression, forecast physiological signals in ICU patients, and estimate individual treatment effects (e.g., for lung cancer), ultimately enhancing medical understanding and supporting clinical decision-making with uncertainty-aware predictions.

Methodology

The authors propose a generative modeling framework that conceptualizes clinical time series as discrete-time, partial observations of an underlying controlled stochastic dynamical system. This framework utilizes latent neural Stochastic Differential Equations (SDEs) to model the complex latent dynamics, incorporating modality-dependent emission models. State estimation and parameter learning are performed efficiently through variational inference, allowing the model to probabilistically learn from irregularly sampled data.

Key Findings

The research found that the proposed latent neural SDE framework successfully addresses key challenges in clinical time series data by naturally handling irregular sampling, learning complex non-linear interactions, and robustly capturing stochasticity from both disease progression and measurement noise. It significantly outperformed ordinary differential equation (ODE) and long short-term memory (LSTM) baseline models across two critical tasks: individual treatment effect estimation in a simulated PKPD lung cancer model and probabilistic forecasting of physiological signals in real ICU data, demonstrating superior accuracy and more reliable uncertainty quantification.

Clinical Impact

This framework has the potential to significantly enhance clinical decision-making by providing precise, uncertainty-aware predictions for individual patients. It could lead to more personalized and optimized treatment strategies, improved risk stratification, and better management of critical care patients through advanced forecasting of physiological parameters and a deeper understanding of disease progression.

Abstract

Clinical time series data from electronic health records and medical registries offer unprecedented opportunities to understand patient trajectories and inform medical decision-making. However, leveraging such data presents significant challenges due to irregular sampling, complex latent physiology, and inherent uncertainties in both measurements and disease progression. To address these challenges, we propose a generative modeling framework based on latent neural stochastic differential equations (SDEs) that views clinical time series as discrete-time partial observations of an underlying controlled stochastic dynamical system. Our approach models latent dynamics via neural SDEs with modality-dependent emission models, while performing state estimation and parameter learning through variational inference. This formulation naturally handles irregularly sampled observations, learns complex non-linear interactions, and captures the stochasticity of disease progression and measurement noise within a unified scalable probabilistic framework. We validate the framework on two complementary tasks: (i) individual treatment effect estimation using a simulated pharmacokinetic-pharmacodynamic (PKPD) model of lung cancer, and (ii) probabilistic forecasting of physiological signals using real-world intensive care unit (ICU) data from 12,000 patients. Results show that our framework outperforms ordinary differential equation and long short-term memory baseline models in accuracy and uncertainty estimation. These results highlight its potential for enabling precise, uncertainty-aware predictions to support clinical decision-making.
