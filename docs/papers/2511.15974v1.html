KRAL: Knowledge and Reasoning Augmented Learning for LLM-assisted Clinical Antimicrobial Therapy

arXiv ID: 2511.15974v1
Published: 2025-11-20
Authors: Zhe Li, Yehan Qiu, Yujie Chen, Xiang Zhou
Categories: cs.AI
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces KRAL (Knowledge and Reasoning Augmented Learning), a novel paradigm designed to overcome inherent limitations of LLMs in complex clinical antimicrobial therapy, such as knowledge gaps, privacy concerns, high costs, and limited reasoning. KRAL achieves this by distilling teacher-model reasoning, employing semi-supervised data augmentation, and utilizing agentic reinforcement learning. It significantly outperforms traditional RAG and SFT methods in both knowledge question-answering and reasoning capabilities at a fraction of the training cost, enabling safe and effective local LLM deployment in clinical decision support.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing the practical application of AI in medicine, particularly for complex and dynamic clinical decision support like antimicrobial stewardship. By enhancing LLM safety, accuracy, and efficiency while preserving data privacy, KRAL provides a scalable solution for integrating advanced AI into daily clinical practice, potentially improving patient outcomes and resource utilization.

AI Health Application

This research develops KRAL, an AI framework designed to enhance local LLMs for clinical decision support, specifically in antimicrobial therapy and diagnostics. It aims to overcome limitations of LLMs in medicine (knowledge gaps, privacy, reasoning) to provide low-cost, high-safety tools for complex medical decision-making.

Key Points

  • Addresses critical LLM limitations (knowledge gaps, data privacy, high deployment costs, limited reasoning) for high-stakes clinical decision-making, specifically in antimicrobial therapy.
  • Proposes KRAL, a low-cost, scalable, and privacy-preserving paradigm that leverages teacher-model reasoning for knowledge and reasoning distillation via answer-to-question reverse generation.
  • Utilizes heuristic learning for semi-supervised data augmentation, drastically reducing manual annotation requirements by approximately 80%.
  • Employs agentic reinforcement learning to jointly enhance medical knowledge and reasoning capabilities while optimizing computational and memory efficiency.
  • Achieved significant improvements in knowledge question-answering (Accuracy@1 on MEDQA increased by 1.8% vs. SFT and 3.6% vs. RAG) and reasoning capability (Pass@1 on PUMCH Antimicrobial increased by 27% vs. SFT and 27.2% vs. RAG).
  • Demonstrates high cost-efficiency, achieving its performance at approximately 20% of the long-term training costs associated with Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT).
  • Features a hierarchical evaluation employing diverse teacher-model proxies to reduce assessment costs and a modular interface for seamless system updates, facilitating practical implementation.

Methodology

KRAL integrates several innovative techniques: teacher-model reasoning distillation using answer-to-question reverse generation, heuristic learning for semi-supervised data augmentation (reducing manual annotation by ~80%), and agentic reinforcement learning to co-optimize medical knowledge and reasoning. Its evaluation involves a hierarchical system employing diverse teacher-model proxies to lower assessment costs and features a modular design for system updates.

Key Findings

KRAL significantly outperformed both RAG and SFT. It boosted Accuracy@1 on the MEDQA benchmark by 1.8% (vs. SFT) and 3.6% (vs. RAG) for knowledge question-answering. For reasoning, it improved Pass@1 on the PUMCH Antimicrobial benchmark by 27% (vs. SFT) and 27.2% (vs. RAG). Critically, these enhancements were achieved at approximately 20% of the long-term training costs incurred by SFT.

Clinical Impact

KRAL enables the deployment of highly capable, privacy-preserving, and cost-efficient local LLMs for critical medical decision support, specifically in antimicrobial therapy. This can lead to more precise and personalized treatment plans, reduce the cognitive load on clinicians, enhance antimicrobial stewardship, and potentially improve patient safety and outcomes by providing timely, data-driven insights in complex clinical scenarios.

Abstract

Clinical antimicrobial therapy requires the dynamic integration of pathogen profiles, host factors, pharmacological properties of antimicrobials, and the severity of infection.This complexity imposes fundamental limitations on the applicability of Large Language Models (LLMs) in high-stakes clinical decision-making including knowledge gaps, data privacy concerns, high deployment costs, and limited reasoning capabilities. To address these challenges, we propose KRAL (Knowledge and Reasoning Augmented Learning), a low-cost, scalable, privacy-preserving paradigm that leverages teacher-model reasoning to automatically distill knowledge and reasoning trajectories via answer-to-question reverse generation, employs heuristic learning for semi-supervised data augmentation (reducing manual annotation requirements by approximately 80%), and utilizes agentic reinforcement learning to jointly enhance medical knowledge and reasoning while optimizing computational and memory efficiency. A hierarchical evaluation employing diverse teacher-model proxies reduces assessment costs, while modular interface design facilitates seamless system updates. Experimental results demonstrate that KRAL significantly outperforms traditional Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) and Supervised Fine-Tuning (SFT) methods. It improves knowledge question-answering capability (Accuracy@1 on the external open-source benchmark MEDQA increased by 1.8% vs. SFT and 3.6% vs. RAG) and reasoning capability (Pass@1 on the external benchmark PUMCH Antimicrobial increased by 27% vs. SFT and 27.2% vs. RAG), achieved at ~20% of SFT's long-term training costs. This establishes KRAL as an effective solution for enhancing local LLMs' clinical diagnostic capabilities, enabling low-cost, high-safety deployment in complex medical decision support.
