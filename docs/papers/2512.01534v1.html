Deep Unsupervised Anomaly Detection in Brain Imaging: Large-Scale Benchmarking and Bias Analysis

arXiv ID: 2512.01534v1
Published: 2025-12-01
Authors: Alexander Frotscher, Christian F. Baumgartner, Thomas Wolfers
Categories: cs.CV, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper presents a large-scale, multi-center benchmark for deep unsupervised anomaly detection in brain MRI, evaluating various algorithms across diverse healthy and clinical cohorts. It reveals significant variability in performance, identifies systematic biases related to scanners, lesion characteristics, age, and sex, and concludes that algorithmic limitations, rather than data availability, currently hinder progress.

Medical Relevance

This research is critical for advancing the clinical utility of unsupervised anomaly detection in brain imaging, as it identifies significant challenges and biases that must be addressed for these methods to reliably detect pathological deviations in diverse patient populations without requiring labor-intensive lesion annotations.

AI Health Application

Deep unsupervised anomaly detection for identifying pathological deviations (e.g., brain lesions) in MRI scans, aiding in early diagnosis of neurological conditions, improving efficiency of radiological assessments, and addressing challenges for clinical translation of AI in neuroradiology by benchmarking performance and analyzing biases related to patient demographics and imaging hardware.

Key Points

  • A large-scale, multi-center benchmark was established for deep unsupervised anomaly detection in brain MRI, utilizing nearly 6,000 healthy scans for training and over 3,400 scans from healthy and diverse clinical cohorts for testing.
  • Algorithm performance for Dice-based lesion segmentation varied substantially (0.03 to 0.65), indicating a lack of consistent reliability across different methods.
  • Reconstruction-based methods, especially diffusion-inspired approaches, achieved the strongest lesion segmentation, while feature-based methods demonstrated greater robustness under distributional shifts.
  • Systematic biases were widespread, including scanner-related effects, increased failure to detect small and low-contrast lesions, and variations in false positives based on age and sex.
  • Increasing the quantity of healthy training data yielded only modest performance gains, suggesting that algorithmic deficiencies, rather than data scarcity, are the primary limitation.
  • The study emphasizes priorities for clinical translation: image native pretraining, principled deviation measures, fairness-aware modeling, and robust domain adaptation.

Methodology

The study involved a large-scale, multi-center benchmark of deep unsupervised anomaly detection algorithms. It used a training cohort of 2,976 T1-weighted and 2,972 T2-weighted scans from healthy individuals (ages 6-89) across six scanners. A validation set of 92 scans was used for hyperparameter tuning and unbiased threshold estimation. Testing was performed on 2,221 T1w and 1,262 T2w scans from healthy datasets and diverse clinical cohorts. Performance was quantified using Dice-based segmentation, and robustness was assessed by evaluating the impact of different scanners, lesion types/sizes, and demographics (age, sex).

Key Findings

Significant variability in Dice-based segmentation performance (0.03-0.65) was observed across algorithms. Reconstruction-based methods, particularly diffusion-inspired ones, excelled in lesion segmentation, while feature-based methods showed better robustness to distributional shifts. However, systematic biases were common, including scanner-related effects, higher rates of missing small/low-contrast lesions, and age- and sex-dependent false positives. Furthermore, increasing healthy training data provided only modest improvements, suggesting algorithmic limitations are more critical than data availability.

Clinical Impact

The findings highlight the urgent need for developing more robust, fair, and less biased unsupervised anomaly detection algorithms for brain imaging. Addressing the identified systematic biases (e.g., scanner effects, demographic variations, lesion characteristics) is paramount for ensuring equitable and accurate diagnosis and monitoring of neurological conditions in real-world clinical settings, ultimately facilitating the safe and effective translation of AI into neuroradiology.

Limitations

The current unsupervised anomaly detection frameworks are limited by inherent algorithmic shortcomings rather than data availability. They are susceptible to systematic biases, including scanner-related effects, poor detection of small and low-contrast lesions, and variations in false positives based on patient age and sex, which hinder their generalizability and clinical reliability.

Future Directions

Priorities for future research and clinical translation include developing methods for image native pretraining, establishing principled deviation measures, incorporating fairness-aware modeling to mitigate biases, and creating robust domain adaptation techniques to improve generalizability across different clinical settings and scanner types.

Abstract

Deep unsupervised anomaly detection in brain magnetic resonance imaging offers a promising route to identify pathological deviations without requiring lesion-specific annotations. Yet, fragmented evaluations, heterogeneous datasets, and inconsistent metrics have hindered progress toward clinical translation. Here, we present a large-scale, multi-center benchmark of deep unsupervised anomaly detection for brain imaging. The training cohort comprised 2,976 T1 and 2,972 T2-weighted scans from healthy individuals across six scanners, with ages ranging from 6 to 89 years. Validation used 92 scans to tune hyperparameters and estimate unbiased thresholds. Testing encompassed 2,221 T1w and 1,262 T2w scans spanning healthy datasets and diverse clinical cohorts. Across all algorithms, the Dice-based segmentation performance varied between 0.03 and 0.65, indicating substantial variability. To assess robustness, we systematically evaluated the impact of different scanners, lesion types and sizes, as well as demographics (age, sex). Reconstruction-based methods, particularly diffusion-inspired approaches, achieved the strongest lesion segmentation performance, while feature-based methods showed greater robustness under distributional shifts. However, systematic biases, such as scanner-related effects, were observed for the majority of algorithms, including that small and low-contrast lesions were missed more often, and that false positives varied with age and sex. Increasing healthy training data yields only modest gains, underscoring that current unsupervised anomaly detection frameworks are limited algorithmically rather than by data availability. Our benchmark establishes a transparent foundation for future research and highlights priorities for clinical translation, including image native pretraining, principled deviation measures, fairness-aware modeling, and robust domain adaptation.
