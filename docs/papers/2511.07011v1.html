Multilingual Lexical Feature Analysis of Spoken Language for Predicting Major Depression Symptom Severity

Published: 2025-11-10

Summary

This study explored the potential of multilingual lexical features in mobile-captured spoken language for objectively assessing Major Depressive Disorder (MDD) symptom severity. While some interpretable lexical associations were found in English and Dutch speech, the overall predictive power for MDD symptom severity across all languages tested was near chance level, indicating the need for further methodological advancements.

Medical Relevance

This research addresses the critical need for more objective, frequent, and accessible methods to monitor Major Depressive Disorder symptom severity and detect relapse between clinical appointments, potentially leading to earlier intervention and improved patient outcomes through mobile health technologies.

AI Health Application

The research explores the application of machine learning (ML) models to analyze multilingual lexical features from spoken language to predict Major Depressive Disorder symptom severity. The goal is to develop an AI-powered tool for objective and continuous monitoring of mental health conditions, potentially aiding in earlier detection of relapse and more regular assessment of patient status between clinical appointments.

Key Points

  • The research aimed to develop an objective, regular assessment method for MDD symptom severity and relapse detection using spoken language from mobile devices.
  • Longitudinal speech data from 5,836 recordings of 586 participants in the UK, Netherlands, and Spain (RADAR-MDD study), alongside PHQ-8 assessments, were analyzed.
  • Linear mixed-effects modelling was used to identify interpretable lexical features associated with MDD symptom severity.
  • In English data, MDD symptom severity correlated with 7 features, including lexical diversity and absolutist language. In Dutch, associations were found with words per sentence and positive word frequency, but no associations were observed in Spanish recordings.
  • Four regressor Machine Learning (ML) models, utilizing both interpretable lexical features and high-dimensional vector embeddings, showed near chance-level prediction performance across all languages.
  • Key limitations included smaller sample sizes for non-English speech, potential influence of elicitation prompts, and a lack of advanced Natural Language Processing (NLP) tools for non-English languages.
  • The study concludes that future research requires larger, multilingual samples, improved data collection protocols, and ML models capable of accounting for intra- and inter-individual language variations.

Methodology

The study conducted an initial exploratory analysis using longitudinal speech data and PHQ-8 assessments from 5,836 recordings across 586 participants from the RADAR-MDD study in the UK, Netherlands, and Spain. Interpretable lexical features associated with MDD symptom severity were identified using linear mixed-effects modelling. Subsequently, the prediction performance of four regressor machine learning models was tested using both these interpretable lexical features and high-dimensional vector embeddings.

Key Findings

In English data, MDD symptom severity was associated with seven specific features, including measures of lexical diversity and the use of absolutist language. For Dutch data, associations were observed with words per sentence and the frequency of positive words. Notably, no significant associations between lexical features and MDD symptom severity were found in recordings collected in Spain. Overall, the predictive power of both interpretable lexical features and high-dimensional vector embeddings, when tested with four different regressor ML models, was consistently found to be near chance level across all languages.

Clinical Impact

While the current predictive power is limited, successful advancement of this research could pave the way for novel digital biomarkers in mental health. This would enable continuous, objective, and remote monitoring of MDD symptom trajectories and relapse risk, potentially facilitating proactive clinical decision-making, personalized treatment adjustments, and more timely interventions, ultimately enhancing the management of major depression.

Limitations

The study acknowledged several limitations, including the use of smaller sample sizes for non-English speech data, which could affect the generalizability of findings in those languages. Methodological choices, such as the specific elicitation prompt used during speech collection, may have also constrained the observable effect sizes. Furthermore, the lack of robust Natural Language Processing (NLP) tools for languages other than English significantly restricted the choice and depth of lexical features that could be analyzed.

Future Directions

To fully understand the clinical utility of lexical markers, future research should prioritize collecting larger samples across a greater variety of languages and implementing improved data elicitation protocols. Crucially, there is a need to develop and apply advanced machine learning models specifically designed to account for the complex within-individual variability and between-individual differences observed in language patterns of individuals with MDD.

Abstract

Background: Captured between clinical appointments using mobile devices, spoken language has potential for objective, more regular assessment of symptom severity and earlier detection of relapse in major depressive disorder. However, research to date has largely been in non-clinical cross-sectional samples of written language using complex machine learning (ML) approaches with limited interpretability. Methods: We describe an initial exploratory analysis of longitudinal speech data and PHQ-8 assessments from 5,836 recordings of 586 participants in the UK, Netherlands, and Spain, collected in the RADAR-MDD study. We sought to identify interpretable lexical features associated with MDD symptom severity with linear mixed-effects modelling. Interpretable features and high-dimensional vector embeddings were also used to test the prediction performance of four regressor ML models. Results: In English data, MDD symptom severity was associated with 7 features including lexical diversity measures and absolutist language. In Dutch, associations were observed with words per sentence and positive word frequency; no associations were observed in recordings collected in Spain. The predictive power of lexical features and vector embeddings was near chance level across all languages. Limitations: Smaller samples in non-English speech and methodological choices, such as the elicitation prompt, may have also limited the effect sizes observable. A lack of NLP tools in languages other than English restricted our feature choice. Conclusion: To understand the value of lexical markers in clinical research and practice, further research is needed in larger samples across several languages using improved protocols, and ML models that account for within- and between-individual variations in language.
