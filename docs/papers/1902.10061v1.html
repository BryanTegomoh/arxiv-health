Supervised learning improves disease outbreak detection

arXiv ID: 1902.10061v1
Published: 2019-02-06
Authors: Benedikt Zacher, Irina Czogiel
Categories: stat.AP, cs.LG, stat.ML
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

Zacher and Czogiel introduce the first supervised learning approach for infectious disease outbreak detection using Hidden Markov Models (HMMs). This novel method leverages routinely collected public health surveillance data to improve detection accuracy. The model significantly reduces false positive rates (up to 50%) while maintaining comparable sensitivity, thereby enhancing the efficiency and reliability of outbreak detection systems.

Medical Relevance

The early and accurate detection of infectious disease outbreaks is paramount for public health, enabling timely interventions to control spread, minimize morbidity and mortality, and protect population health. This research directly improves the reliability and efficiency of these critical surveillance activities.

AI Health Application

The paper describes the application of supervised learning (a machine learning technique) and hidden Markov models for the early detection of infectious disease outbreaks within public health surveillance systems. This is a direct medical AI application aimed at improving public health outcomes and biosecurity.

Key Points

  • Developed the first supervised learning approach specifically for disease outbreak detection.
  • The proposed methodology is based on Hidden Markov Models (HMMs), a probabilistic graphical model suitable for sequential data.
  • Leverages routinely collected infectious disease data from established public health surveillance systems for training and detection.
  • Evaluated using real-world epidemiological data for *Salmonella* and *Campylobacter*, complemented by simulations.
  • Achieved a substantial reduction in the false positive rate (up to 50%) compared to a state-of-the-art statistical approach.
  • Maintained the same level of sensitivity as the benchmark state-of-the-art method, ensuring no compromise on detection capability.
  • Positions supervised machine learning as a crucial advancement for improving the performance and resource allocation of public health surveillance systems.

Methodology

The authors developed a novel supervised learning approach, specifically a Hidden Markov Model (HMM), for disease outbreak detection. This method learns from historical, routinely collected infectious disease surveillance data to identify patterns indicative of outbreaks, moving beyond traditional statistical aberration detection. The model's performance was evaluated using real-world *Salmonella* and *Campylobacter* data, as well as synthetic simulations, and benchmarked against a state-of-the-art statistical method.

Key Findings

The primary finding is that the proposed supervised HMM model significantly outperforms a state-of-the-art statistical outbreak detection method by reducing the false positive rate by up to 50%. Critically, this improvement in specificity (fewer false alarms) was achieved while retaining the same level of sensitivity (ability to detect true outbreaks), demonstrating a more efficient and accurate detection capability.

Clinical Impact

This research has a direct and significant clinical and practical impact on public health surveillance. By substantially reducing false positive alerts, public health agencies can allocate their limited resources more effectively to investigate genuine outbreaks, avoiding unnecessary investigations and associated costs. This leads to quicker, more targeted responses to real public health threats, ultimately improving disease control, patient outcomes, and overall population health protection.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations of the proposed supervised learning approach. However, it implicitly addresses limitations of existing state-of-the-art methods, such as their higher false positive rates, which this new model aims to overcome.

Future Directions

The authors envision their supervised learning approach as a significant advancement that will pave the way for further development of machine learning applications in disease outbreak detection. This suggests future research will focus on exploring and refining other machine learning techniques for public health surveillance systems to continuously enhance their capabilities.

Abstract

The early detection of infectious disease outbreaks is a crucial task to protect population health. To this end, public health surveillance systems have been established to systematically collect and analyse infectious disease data. A variety of statistical tools are available, which detect potential outbreaks as abberations from an expected endemic level using these data. Here, we develop the first supervised learning approach based on hidden Markov models for disease outbreak detection, which leverages data that is routinely collected within a public health surveillance system. We evaluate our model using real Salmonella and Campylobacter data, as well as simulations. In comparison to a state-of-the-art approach, which is applied in multiple European countries including Germany, our proposed model reduces the false positive rate by up to 50% while retaining the same sensitivity. We see our supervised learning approach as a significant step to further develop machine learning applications for disease outbreak detection, which will be instrumental to improve public health surveillance systems.
