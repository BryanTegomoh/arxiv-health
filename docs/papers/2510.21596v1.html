Automated interictal epileptic spike detection from simple and noisy annotations in MEG data

Summary

This paper introduces two deep learning models, a feature-based Artificial Neural Network (ANN) and a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), for automated interictal epileptic spike detection in Magnetoencephalography (MEG) data. Designed to operate effectively even with simple, noisy, and single-expert annotations common in clinical practice, these models significantly outperform a state-of-the-art method on a holdout test set. The research highlights the robustness of these architectures and proposes an interactive machine learning strategy to improve annotation quality and accelerate the overall diagnostic process in drug-resistant epilepsy.

Medical Relevance

Accurate localization of interictal epileptic spikes from MEG data is critical for identifying the epileptogenic zone in drug-resistant epilepsy patients undergoing presurgical evaluation. This automated detection method can significantly improve the efficiency, consistency, and reliability of this process, potentially leading to better patient outcomes by facilitating more precise surgical planning.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the automated detection of interictal epileptic spikes from MEG data using deep learning models (ANN and CNN). This serves as a clinical decision support tool for neurologists and epileptologists, aiming to improve the accuracy and efficiency of diagnosing epilepsy and localizing the epileptogenic zone for presurgical planning. It also employs an interactive machine learning strategy to improve data annotation quality, which is crucial for training robust medical AI models in clinical settings.

Key Points

  • Addresses the challenge of manual interictal epileptic spike detection in MEG, which is fastidious, error-prone, and suffers from moderate interrater agreement, along with the unsuitability of current automated methods for clinical practice.
  • Proposes two deep learning architectures, a feature-based ANN and a CNN, specifically adapted for spike detection using simple, temporal, and single-expert annotations.
  • Models were trained on MEG data from 59 patients and evaluated on a holdout test set of 10 patients, comparing performance against a state-of-the-art baseline.
  • Both proposed models achieved superior performance (CNN F1-score=0.46, ANN F1-score=0.44) compared to the state-of-the-art model for classifying short time windows of MEG signal.
  • An interactive machine learning strategy was employed, demonstrating the models' robustness to noisy annotations and their capability to iteratively improve data annotation quality.
  • The study emphasizes the robustness of these relatively simple deep learning architectures in handling complex and imperfectly annotated real-world clinical data.
  • The approach offers significant potential for faster data annotation and provides efficient tools for automated interictal spike detection, crucial for presurgical epilepsy evaluation.

Methodology

The study developed two deep learning models: a feature-based Artificial Neural Network (ANN) and a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN). These models were trained on a database of MEG recordings from 59 patients, utilizing only simple, temporal, and single-expert annotations of interictal epileptic spikes. The models were designed to classify short time windows of MEG signal. Performance was evaluated against a state-of-the-art model on a holdout test set of 10 patients, primarily using F1-scores as the metric. An interactive machine learning strategy was also implemented to assess and improve the quality of data annotations.

Key Findings

The proposed CNN (F1-score=0.46) and ANN (F1-score=0.44) models significantly outperformed a state-of-the-art baseline model when tested on 10 holdout patients for classifying interictal epileptic spikes. The interactive machine learning strategy successfully demonstrated the robustness of these models to noisy annotations and their ability to facilitate iterative improvements in annotation quality. The research further highlighted that models with simple architectures can achieve robust performance when analyzing complex and imperfectly annotated real-world MEG data.

Clinical Impact

This automated interictal epileptic spike detection method holds substantial clinical impact by addressing the current laborious and error-prone manual process. It offers the potential for significantly faster and more consistent detection, which can accelerate presurgical evaluation in drug-resistant epilepsy, reduce the workload on highly skilled clinicians, and ultimately improve the accuracy of epileptogenic zone localization. The interactive machine learning strategy additionally promises to expedite and enhance the quality of data annotation in clinical settings.

Limitations

While the paper addresses the limitations of existing methods, an implicit limitation of the presented models themselves, despite outperforming the baseline, could be the F1-scores (below 0.5), which might indicate room for improvement towards standalone clinical diagnostic reliability. The abstract also does not fully detail the diversity or specific characteristics of the 59-patient dataset, which could impact the generalizability of the findings.

Future Directions

The authors suggest that the interactive machine learning strategy offers significant potential for faster and improved data annotation, implying further development and integration into clinical annotation workflows. The models are presented as 'useful and efficient tools,' indicating a trajectory towards their continued refinement, validation, and eventual deployment for automated interictal spike detection in clinical practice to support epilepsy diagnosis and surgical planning.

Abstract

In drug-resistant epilepsy, presurgical evaluation of epilepsy can be considered. Magnetoencephalography (MEG) has been shown to be an effective exam to inform the localization of the epileptogenic zone through the localization of interictal epileptic spikes. Manual detection of these pathological biomarkers remains a fastidious and error-prone task due to the high dimensionality of MEG recordings, and interrater agreement has been reported to be only moderate. Current automated methods are unsuitable for clinical practice, either requiring extensively annotated data or lacking robustness on non-typical data. In this work, we demonstrate that deep learning models can be used for detecting interictal spikes in MEG recordings, even when only temporal and single-expert annotations are available, which represents real-world clinical practice. We propose two model architectures: a feature-based artificial neural network (ANN) and a convolutional neural network (CNN), trained on a database of 59 patients, and evaluated against a state-of-the-art model to classify short time windows of signal. In addition, we employ an interactive machine learning strategy to iteratively improve our data annotation quality using intermediary model outputs. Both proposed models outperform the state-of-the-art model (F1-scores: CNN=0.46, ANN=0.44) when tested on 10 holdout test patients. The interactive machine learning strategy demonstrates that our models are robust to noisy annotations. Overall, results highlight the robustness of models with simple architectures when analyzing complex and imperfectly annotated data. Our method of interactive machine learning offers great potential for faster data annotation, while our models represent useful and efficient tools for automated interictal spikes detection.
