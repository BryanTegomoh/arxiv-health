Boosting Predictive Performance on Tabular Data through Data Augmentation with Latent-Space Flow-Based Diffusion

arXiv ID: 2511.16571v1
Published: 2025-11-20
Authors: Md. Tawfique Ihsan, Md. Rakibul Hasan Rafi, Ahmed Shoyeb Raihan, Imtiaz Ahmed, Abdullahil Azeem
Categories: cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00

Summary

This paper introduces novel latent-space, tree-driven diffusion methods (PCAForest, EmbedForest, AttentionForest) for minority oversampling in tabular data, addressing challenges of severe class imbalance, tabular heterogeneity, and privacy concerns. These models leverage conditional flow matching with gradient-boosted trees in compact latent spaces, demonstrating an efficient and privacy-aware approach to high-fidelity data augmentation. AttentionForest achieves superior minority recall, while PCAForest and EmbedForest offer favorable accuracy-efficiency trade-offs.

Methodology

The paper proposes a family of latent-space, tree-driven diffusion methods for minority class oversampling in tabular data. These methods utilize conditional flow matching, where Gradient-Boosted Trees (GBTs) learn the vector field to guide the diffusion process. The models operate in compact latent spaces to maintain tabular structure and improve computational efficiency. Three specific variants are introduced: PCAForest (using linear PCA for embedding), EmbedForest (using a learned nonlinear embedding), and AttentionForest (using an attention-augmented learned embedding). Each variant couples its GBT-based flow with a decoder component to map the generated latent samples back to the original feature space for evaluation and use.

Key Findings

AttentionForest achieved the best average minority recall across diverse datasets, while also maintaining competitive precision, calibration, and distributional similarity. PCAForest and EmbedForest demonstrated comparable utility but with significantly faster generation times, highlighting favorable accuracy-efficiency trade-offs. Privacy, evaluated using nearest-neighbor distance ratio and distance-to-closest-record, was found to be comparable to or better than the ForestDiffusion baseline. Ablation studies revealed that smaller latent embeddings tend to improve minority recall, whereas aggressive learning rates negatively impact model stability during training.

Abstract

Severe class imbalance is common in real-world tabular learning, where rare but important minority classes are essential for reliable prediction. Existing generative oversampling methods such as GANs, VAEs, and diffusion models can improve minority-class performance, but they often struggle with tabular heterogeneity, training stability, and privacy concerns. We propose a family of latent-space, tree-driven diffusion methods for minority oversampling that use conditional flow matching with gradient-boosted trees as the vector-field learner. The models operate in compact latent spaces to preserve tabular structure and reduce computation. We introduce three variants: PCAForest, which uses linear PCA embedding; EmbedForest, which uses a learned nonlinear embedding; and AttentionForest, which uses an attention-augmented embedding. Each method couples a GBT-based flow with a decoder back to the original feature space. Across 11 datasets from healthcare, finance, and manufacturing, AttentionForest achieves the best average minority recall while maintaining competitive precision, calibration, and distributional similarity. PCAForest and EmbedForest reach similar utility with much faster generation, offering favorable accuracy-efficiency trade-offs. Privacy evaluated with nearest-neighbor distance ratio and distance-to-closest-record is comparable to or better than the ForestDiffusion baseline. Ablation studies show that smaller embeddings tend to improve minority recall, while aggressive learning rates harm stability. Overall, latent-space, tree-driven diffusion provides an efficient and privacy-aware approach to high-fidelity tabular data augmentation under severe class imbalance.
