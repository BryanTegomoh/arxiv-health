Enhancing Social Robots through Resilient AI

arXiv ID: 2510.21469v1
Published: 2025-10-24
Authors: Domenico Palmisano, Giuseppe Palestra, Berardina Nadja De Carolis
Categories: cs.RO, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper highlights the critical necessity of resilience in social robots, particularly as Artificial Intelligence (AI) becomes more integrated into sensitive domains like healthcare. It posits that resilient AI, characterized by its ability to maintain essential functions under adverse conditions, is fundamental for fostering trust in robots, an indispensable element when interacting with elderly populations who often demonstrate low trust in such systems.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly pertinent to medicine and health by addressing the foundational requirement for trustworthy AI in social robots, crucial for their effective deployment in elder care and broader healthcare settings. Ensuring resilience directly impacts patient safety, acceptance, and the ultimate success of human-robot interaction in medical contexts.

AI Health Application

The research focuses on developing resilient AI for social robots to be deployed in healthcare settings, specifically for elderly individuals. This application aims to enhance trust, ensure continuous operation even under adverse conditions, and maintain essential functionalities for care, companionship, or assistance roles in elder care, thereby directly impacting health outcomes and quality of life for this vulnerable population.

Key Points

  • AI is increasingly integrated into sensitive sectors including healthcare, education, and daily life, demanding high reliability.
  • Resilience and robustness are identified as crucial characteristics for AI systems operating in these sensitive environments.
  • The paper establishes resilience as a foundational attribute for social robots, directly impacting their efficacy and acceptance.
  • Resilience is presented as a key mechanism for building and ensuring trust in social robots.
  • Trust in social robots is deemed essential, particularly when operating with elderly people, a demographic historically exhibiting low trust in these technologies.
  • Resilience is defined as the capacity to operate and sustain core operational capabilities even when facing adverse, stressful, degraded, or weakened conditions.

Methodology

Based on the abstract, the methodology appears to be conceptual and theoretical, presenting an argument for the fundamental nature of resilience in social robots and defining its importance, particularly in healthcare contexts. The paper 'shows how resilience is a fundamental characteristic,' suggesting a framework-building or persuasive reasoning approach rather than empirical experimentation.

Key Findings

The central finding is the assertion that resilience is a fundamental and non-negotiable characteristic for social robots. This resilience directly underpins the ability of these robots to engender and maintain trust, which is identified as an essential prerequisite for their successful deployment, especially in high-stakes environments like elder care where trust levels are currently a significant barrier.

Clinical Impact

The clinical impact lies in guiding the design and development of future healthcare robots. By emphasizing resilient AI, this work encourages the creation of more dependable, robust, and trustworthy social robots. This could lead to improved patient acceptance and compliance, safer care delivery for the elderly, and more effective assistive tools for healthcare professionals, ultimately enhancing the quality and accessibility of care.

Limitations

The abstract itself does not explicitly state limitations of the presented research. However, it implicitly highlights a broader challenge: the low trust of elderly people in robotic systems. The abstract does not provide details on specific architectural implementations for resilient AI, empirical validation of its impact on trust, or a comparison with alternative approaches.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated in the abstract, implied future directions would involve developing concrete, resilient AI architectures for social robots, conducting empirical studies to quantify the impact of resilience on trust and acceptance in diverse patient populations (especially the elderly), and exploring specific applications and ethical considerations of resilient social robots in various healthcare scenarios.

Abstract

As artificial intelligence continues to advance and becomes more integrated into sensitive areas like healthcare, education, and everyday life, it's crucial for these systems to be both resilient and robust. This paper shows how resilience is a fundamental characteristic of social robots, which, through it, ensure trust in the robot itself-an essential element especially when operating in contexts with elderly people, who often have low trust in these systems. Resilience is therefore the ability to operate under adverse or stressful conditions, even when degraded or weakened, while maintaining essential operational capabilities.
