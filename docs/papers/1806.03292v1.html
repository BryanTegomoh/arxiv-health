Global stability of epidemic models with imperfect vaccination and quarantine on scale-free networks

arXiv ID: 1806.03292v1
Published: 2018-06-07
Authors: Shanshan Chen, Michael Small, Xinchu Fu
Categories: q-bio.PE, math.CA, nlin.AO
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00

Summary

This paper introduces and rigorously analyzes two novel epidemic models (SVIQR and SVIQS) on scale-free networks, incorporating variable population size, degree-related imperfect vaccination, and quarantine. It investigates their basic reproduction number, global stability of equilibria, and bifurcation behaviors using diverse mathematical techniques and validates them with stochastic simulations, providing insights for public health planning.

Medical Relevance

This research provides advanced mathematical frameworks to evaluate the effectiveness and predict the outcomes of public health interventions like vaccination and quarantine in populations with heterogeneous contact patterns, directly informing strategies to reduce the spread of infectious diseases.

AI Health Application

The paper uses mathematical modeling and stochastic network simulations to understand disease spread. While it does not develop a specific AI application, the models and insights derived are directly applicable and foundational for developing AI systems in health, such as predictive analytics for outbreak forecasting, optimizing intervention strategies, resource allocation for vaccination campaigns, or informing AI-driven decision support tools for public health policy.

Key Points

  • Introduction and mathematical formulation of SVIQR (Susceptible-Vaccinated-Infected-Quarantined-Recovered) and SVIQS (Susceptible-Vaccinated-Infected-Quarantined-Susceptible) epidemic models on scale-free networks.
  • Models incorporate key public health interventions: variable population size, degree-related imperfect vaccination, and quarantine of symptomatic individuals.
  • Development of mathematical methods to analyze model dynamics, including calculation of the basic reproduction number (R0) and global stability analysis of disease-free and endemic equilibria.
  • For the SVIQR model (with permanent natural immunity), a forward bifurcation is demonstrated, and both the disease-free and a unique endemic equilibrium are proven to be globally asymptotically stable using suitable Lyapunov functions.
  • For the SVIQS model (without permanent natural immunity), conditions for the occurrence of multiple endemic equilibria are derived, and it is shown that the system cannot undergo a backward bifurcation under certain conditions.
  • The SVIQS model's disease-free equilibrium is globally asymptotically stable, disease persistence is proven, and the endemic equilibrium is shown to be globally attractive using monotone iterative techniques.
  • The quantitative agreement between the deterministic mean-field approach and stochastic network simulations validates the models' analytical findings.

Methodology

The study employs mathematical modeling to formulate SVIQR and SVIQS epidemic models within a mean-field approach on scale-free networks. Analytical techniques include calculating the basic reproduction number (R0), performing bifurcation analysis (forward and backward), and rigorously proving global stability of disease-free and endemic equilibria using Lyapunov functions and monotone iterative techniques. Stochastic network simulations are utilized for quantitative validation of the deterministic analytical results.

Key Findings

The SVIQR model exhibits a forward bifurcation, with both its disease-free and unique endemic equilibria proven globally asymptotically stable. The SVIQS model, under specific conditions, allows for multiple endemic equilibria and avoids backward bifurcation, demonstrating global asymptotic stability of its disease-free equilibrium, disease persistence, and global attractiveness of its endemic equilibrium. The dynamics of both models are critically dependent on the basic reproduction number, and all analytical findings are robustly supported by stochastic simulations.

Clinical Impact

These models offer a robust tool for public health officials and policymakers to predict disease dynamics and optimize intervention strategies. They can guide decisions on optimal vaccination coverage, efficacy requirements, and implementation timing, as well as the stringency and duration of quarantine measures, particularly in populations exhibiting complex contact networks, thereby enhancing preparedness and response to infectious disease outbreaks.

Abstract

Public health services are constantly searching for new ways to reduce the spread of infectious diseases, such as public vaccination of asymptomatic individuals, quarantine (isolation) and treatment of symptomatic individuals. Epidemic models have a long history of assisting in public health planning and policy making. In this paper, we introduce epidemic models including variable population size, degree-related imperfect vaccination and quarantine on scale-free networks. More specifically, the models are formulated both on the population with and without permanent natural immunity to infection, which corresponds respectively to the susceptible-vaccinated-infected-quarantined-recovered (SVIQR) model and the susceptible-vaccinated-infected-quarantined (SVIQS) model. We develop different mathematical methods and techniques to study the dynamics of two models, including the basic reproduction number, the global stability of disease-free and endemic equilibria. For the SVIQR model, we show that the system exhibits a forward bifurcation. Meanwhile, the disease-free and unique endemic equilibria are shown to be globally asymptotically stable by constructing suitable Lyapunov functions. For the SVIQS model, conditions ensuring the occurrence of multiple endemic equilibria are derived. Under certain conditions, this system cannot undergo a backward bifurcation. The global asymptotical stability of disease-free equilibrium, and the persistence of the disease are proved. The endemic equilibrium is shown to be globally attractive by using monotone iterative technique. Finally, stochastic network simulations yield quantitative agreement with the deterministic mean-field approach.
