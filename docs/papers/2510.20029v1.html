BrainPuzzle: Hybrid Physics and Data-Driven Reconstruction for Transcranial Ultrasound Tomography

arXiv ID: 2510.20029v1
Published: 2025-10-22
Authors: Shengyu Chen, Shihang Feng, Yi Luo, Xiaowei Jia, Youzuo Lin
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

BrainPuzzle introduces a novel hybrid physics- and data-driven framework to achieve accurate speed-of-sound (SoS) map reconstruction for transcranial ultrasound tomography, addressing limitations of traditional methods. It combines reverse time migration for robust structural fragment generation with a transformer-based super-resolution network to fuse these fragments from sparse, multi-angle acquisitions. Experiments on synthetic datasets demonstrate its superior SoS reconstruction accuracy and image completeness, overcoming challenges posed by skull aberrations and sparse data.

Medical Relevance

Accurate speed-of-sound mapping of brain tissues using ultrasound is critical for non-invasive diagnosis and monitoring of various neurological conditions, offering a portable, safer, and potentially more accessible alternative to other neuroimaging modalities like MRI or CT, especially for point-of-care applications.

AI Health Application

The research applies a hybrid AI approach (combining machine learning with physical modeling) to improve the accuracy, completeness, and clinical feasibility of transcranial ultrasound tomography for brain imaging. Specifically, it uses a transformer-based super-resolution encoder-decoder with a graph-based attention unit to fuse migration fragments from multi-angle acquisitions, compensating for sparse data and skull-induced signal degradation, which is critical for quantitative brain diagnostics.

Key Points

  • Addresses the significant challenges in quantitative transcranial ultrasound, including large sound speed differences between skull and brain, signal attenuation, mode conversion, phase aberration, and difficulties in coupling large probes.
  • Critiques existing methods: physics-based Full-Waveform Inversion (FWI) is limited by weak signals and incomplete spatial coverage, while purely data-driven methods can lead to quantitatively biased SoS maps due to poor modeling of complex wave propagation through bone.
  • Proposes BrainPuzzle, a two-stage hybrid framework. Stage 1 uses Reverse Time Migration (RTM) on multi-angle acquisitions to produce structural "migration fragments" that preserve details even under low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR).
  • Stage 2 employs a transformer-based super-resolution encoder-decoder, augmented with a Graph-based Attention Unit (GAU), to fuse the RTM fragments into a coherent and quantitatively accurate SoS image.
  • Incorporates a partial-array acquisition strategy using a movable, low-count transducer set, enhancing clinical feasibility and coupling, with the hybrid algorithm compensating for the resulting missing aperture.
  • Demonstrates superior SoS reconstruction accuracy and image completeness compared to existing approaches through extensive experiments on two synthetic datasets.
  • Aims to advance quantitative ultrasound brain imaging by providing a more robust and accurate method for mapping brain tissue properties.

Methodology

BrainPuzzle employs a two-stage hybrid approach. Stage 1 utilizes Reverse Time Migration (time-reversal acoustics) applied to multi-angle ultrasound acquisitions to generate structural migration fragments. Stage 2 consists of a transformer-based super-resolution encoder-decoder, integrated with a Graph-based Attention Unit (GAU), which fuses these fragments into a quantitative speed-of-sound (SoS) map. Data acquisition uses a partial-array strategy with a movable, low-count transducer set.

Key Findings

The BrainPuzzle framework achieves superior accuracy and completeness in reconstructing speed-of-sound maps of the brain on synthetic datasets. It effectively overcomes the challenges of skull-induced aberrations, low signal-to-noise ratios, and sparse data acquisition that limit traditional and purely data-driven methods, producing quantitatively accurate SoS images.

Clinical Impact

This research has the potential to significantly advance quantitative ultrasound brain imaging, making it a more viable and reliable tool for clinical applications. It could enable more accurate, portable, and less invasive diagnostic or monitoring capabilities for neurological conditions, potentially improving patient access to neuroimaging and facilitating point-of-care diagnostics.

Limitations

The current validation of BrainPuzzle is limited to synthetic datasets. Its performance and generalizability remain to be demonstrated with experimental phantom data and, more critically, in vivo human studies. The computational complexity of combining RTM and transformer models could also be a factor for real-time clinical deployment.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated, future research directions would logically involve validating the BrainPuzzle framework through rigorous experimental studies using physical phantoms and, ultimately, human in vivo trials to assess its clinical efficacy and robustness. Further optimization for computational efficiency and real-time application would also be beneficial.

Abstract

Ultrasound brain imaging remains challenging due to the large difference in sound speed between the skull and brain tissues and the difficulty of coupling large probes to the skull. This work aims to achieve quantitative transcranial ultrasound by reconstructing an accurate speed-of-sound (SoS) map of the brain. Traditional physics-based full-waveform inversion (FWI) is limited by weak signals caused by skull-induced attenuation, mode conversion, and phase aberration, as well as incomplete spatial coverage since full-aperture arrays are clinically impractical. In contrast, purely data-driven methods that learn directly from raw ultrasound data often fail to model the complex nonlinear and nonlocal wave propagation through bone, leading to anatomically plausible but quantitatively biased SoS maps under low signal-to-noise and sparse-aperture conditions. To address these issues, we propose BrainPuzzle, a hybrid two-stage framework that combines physical modeling with machine learning. In the first stage, reverse time migration (time-reversal acoustics) is applied to multi-angle acquisitions to produce migration fragments that preserve structural details even under low SNR. In the second stage, a transformer-based super-resolution encoder-decoder with a graph-based attention unit (GAU) fuses these fragments into a coherent and quantitatively accurate SoS image. A partial-array acquisition strategy using a movable low-count transducer set improves feasibility and coupling, while the hybrid algorithm compensates for the missing aperture. Experiments on two synthetic datasets show that BrainPuzzle achieves superior SoS reconstruction accuracy and image completeness, demonstrating its potential for advancing quantitative ultrasound brain imaging.
