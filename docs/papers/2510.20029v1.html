BrainPuzzle: Hybrid Physics and Data-Driven Reconstruction for Transcranial Ultrasound Tomography

Published: 2025-10-22
Authors: Shengyu Chen, Shihang Feng, Yi Luo, Xiaowei Jia, Youzuo Lin

Summary

BrainPuzzle addresses the significant challenges of transcranial ultrasound tomography (TUT) by proposing a novel hybrid physics- and data-driven framework. It overcomes the limitations of both traditional physics-based full-waveform inversion and purely data-driven methods, achieving superior accuracy and completeness in speed-of-sound (SoS) map reconstruction for brain imaging, even under low signal-to-noise and sparse-aperture conditions.

Medical Relevance

Accurate speed-of-sound mapping of brain tissues through the skull is crucial for non-invasive, radiation-free diagnostic imaging, enabling improved detection and monitoring of various neurological conditions or brain injuries.

AI Health Application

The research applies a hybrid AI approach, integrating traditional physics-based modeling (reverse time migration) with machine learning techniques (a transformer-based super-resolution encoder-decoder with a graph-based attention unit). This AI framework is used to reconstruct accurate and complete speed-of-sound maps of the brain from transcranial ultrasound data. This application aims to enhance the diagnostic quality and clinical feasibility of ultrasound brain imaging by improving signal processing, compensating for sparse data, and addressing challenges like skull-induced attenuation and phase aberration, thereby providing more reliable quantitative information for medical diagnosis.

Key Points

  • Aims to achieve quantitative transcranial ultrasound by reconstructing accurate speed-of-sound (SoS) maps of the brain, despite skull-induced artifacts.
  • Identifies limitations in existing methods: physics-based FWI suffers from weak signals and incomplete spatial coverage, while purely data-driven methods struggle with complex wave propagation through bone.
  • Proposes BrainPuzzle, a two-stage hybrid framework combining physical modeling (reverse time migration) with machine learning (transformer-based super-resolution).
  • Stage 1 uses reverse time migration (time-reversal acoustics) on multi-angle acquisitions to generate 'migration fragments' that preserve structural details.
  • Stage 2 employs a transformer-based super-resolution encoder-decoder, enhanced with a graph-based attention unit (GAU), to fuse these fragments into a coherent and quantitatively accurate SoS image.
  • Utilizes a practical partial-array acquisition strategy with movable, low-count transducers to improve clinical feasibility and coupling, with the algorithm compensating for missing aperture.
  • Demonstrates superior SoS reconstruction accuracy and image completeness compared to existing methods through experiments on two synthetic datasets.

Methodology

BrainPuzzle is a hybrid two-stage framework. The first stage employs reverse time migration (RTM) on multi-angle ultrasound acquisitions to produce initial 'migration fragments' that capture structural details. The second stage then utilizes a transformer-based super-resolution encoder-decoder network, equipped with a graph-based attention unit (GAU), to fuse these fragments and reconstruct a coherent, quantitatively accurate speed-of-sound (SoS) map. This is designed to work with a partial-array acquisition strategy using a movable, low-count transducer set.

Key Findings

The BrainPuzzle framework achieved superior accuracy and completeness in reconstructing speed-of-sound (SoS) maps for brain imaging. It effectively mitigated challenges from skull-induced signal degradation and sparse data acquisition, outperforming traditional and purely data-driven methods on synthetic datasets.

Clinical Impact

This research offers a significant step towards practical and quantitative transcranial ultrasound brain imaging. By overcoming current technical hurdles, it has the potential to enable more accessible, safer (non-ionizing radiation), and potentially portable or real-time brain diagnostics and monitoring in various clinical settings.

Limitations

The primary limitation explicitly stated is that the effectiveness of BrainPuzzle was demonstrated through experiments on "two synthetic datasets." This implies that validation on real-world ex vivo or in vivo human data is still needed to confirm its clinical applicability.

Future Directions

While not explicitly detailed as a 'future directions' section, the paper's demonstration on synthetic data and its 'potential for advancing quantitative ultrasound brain imaging' strongly imply that future work will involve validating the BrainPuzzle framework on real human anatomical models and, eventually, in clinical settings (ex vivo and in vivo studies).

Abstract

Ultrasound brain imaging remains challenging due to the large difference in sound speed between the skull and brain tissues and the difficulty of coupling large probes to the skull. This work aims to achieve quantitative transcranial ultrasound by reconstructing an accurate speed-of-sound (SoS) map of the brain. Traditional physics-based full-waveform inversion (FWI) is limited by weak signals caused by skull-induced attenuation, mode conversion, and phase aberration, as well as incomplete spatial coverage since full-aperture arrays are clinically impractical. In contrast, purely data-driven methods that learn directly from raw ultrasound data often fail to model the complex nonlinear and nonlocal wave propagation through bone, leading to anatomically plausible but quantitatively biased SoS maps under low signal-to-noise and sparse-aperture conditions. To address these issues, we propose BrainPuzzle, a hybrid two-stage framework that combines physical modeling with machine learning. In the first stage, reverse time migration (time-reversal acoustics) is applied to multi-angle acquisitions to produce migration fragments that preserve structural details even under low SNR. In the second stage, a transformer-based super-resolution encoder-decoder with a graph-based attention unit (GAU) fuses these fragments into a coherent and quantitatively accurate SoS image. A partial-array acquisition strategy using a movable low-count transducer set improves feasibility and coupling, while the hybrid algorithm compensates for the missing aperture. Experiments on two synthetic datasets show that BrainPuzzle achieves superior SoS reconstruction accuracy and image completeness, demonstrating its potential for advancing quantitative ultrasound brain imaging.
