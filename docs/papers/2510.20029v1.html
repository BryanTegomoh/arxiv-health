BrainPuzzle: Hybrid Physics and Data-Driven Reconstruction for Transcranial Ultrasound Tomography

arXiv ID: 2510.20029v1
Published: 2025-10-22
Authors: Shengyu Chen, Shihang Feng, Yi Luo, Xiaowei Jia, Youzuo Lin
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces BrainPuzzle, a novel hybrid two-stage framework combining physics-based modeling and machine learning to achieve quantitative transcranial ultrasound tomography. It addresses the significant challenges of skull-induced signal degradation and impractical full-aperture arrays by utilizing reverse time migration to generate robust structural fragments, which are then fused into an accurate speed-of-sound (SoS) map by a transformer-based super-resolution network. Experiments on synthetic datasets demonstrate BrainPuzzle's superior SoS reconstruction accuracy and image completeness, promising advancements in non-invasive brain imaging.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and quantitative transcranial ultrasound imaging could provide a non-invasive, radiation-free, and potentially portable diagnostic and monitoring tool for various neurological conditions, offering an alternative or complement to current imaging modalities like CT and MRI, especially in resource-limited settings or for continuous monitoring.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the 'BrainPuzzle' framework, a hybrid two-stage approach that uses machine learning (specifically, a transformer-based super-resolution encoder-decoder with a graph-based attention unit) to fuse migration fragments into a coherent and quantitatively accurate speed-of-sound image of the brain. This directly applies AI/ML to enhance the accuracy and completeness of transcranial ultrasound brain imaging, improving diagnostic capabilities despite challenging conditions like low signal-to-noise and sparse-aperture acquisitions.

Key Points

  • Transcranial ultrasound brain imaging is challenging due to the skull's large sound speed difference, high attenuation, mode conversion, and phase aberration, along with the difficulty of clinically coupling large, full-aperture probes.
  • Traditional physics-based Full-Waveform Inversion (FWI) is limited by weak signals and incomplete spatial coverage, while purely data-driven methods often produce quantitatively biased SoS maps under low SNR and sparse-aperture conditions.
  • BrainPuzzle proposes a hybrid two-stage framework: Stage 1 applies Reverse Time Migration (RTM) to multi-angle acquisitions to produce robust 'migration fragments' preserving structural details even under low SNR.
  • Stage 2 utilizes a transformer-based super-resolution encoder-decoder, enhanced with a graph-based attention unit (GAU), to fuse these fragments into a coherent and quantitatively accurate SoS image.
  • The framework supports a practical partial-array acquisition strategy using a movable, low-count transducer set, improving clinical feasibility and coupling, with the hybrid algorithm compensating for the missing aperture.
  • Evaluation on two synthetic datasets demonstrated BrainPuzzle's superior accuracy in SoS reconstruction and improved image completeness, outperforming existing methods.
  • The method aims to overcome the limitations of both purely physics-based and purely data-driven approaches, offering a more robust and quantitative solution for transcranial ultrasound.

Methodology

BrainPuzzle employs a two-stage hybrid approach. The first stage uses a physics-based method, Reverse Time Migration (RTM), applied to multi-angle ultrasound acquisitions to generate initial 'migration fragments' that robustly capture structural details even with low signal-to-noise ratios. The second stage then leverages a data-driven machine learning model: a transformer-based super-resolution encoder-decoder network, featuring a graph-based attention unit (GAU), which is tasked with fusing these fragments into a coherent, complete, and quantitatively accurate speed-of-sound (SoS) map. This methodology is designed to be compatible with a practical partial-array transducer acquisition strategy.

Key Findings

The BrainPuzzle framework achieved superior accuracy in reconstructing speed-of-sound (SoS) maps and demonstrated enhanced image completeness when tested on two distinct synthetic datasets. This indicates its effectiveness in producing quantitatively accurate brain SoS images despite the challenges posed by the skull and sparse data acquisition, surpassing the performance of traditional physics-based or purely data-driven methods.

Clinical Impact

This research holds significant potential for advancing non-invasive brain imaging, offering a safer (radiation-free) and potentially more accessible and portable method for diagnosing and monitoring neurological conditions such as stroke, cerebral edema, hydrocephalus, or traumatic brain injury. Its ability to work with partial arrays could lead to more practical and cost-effective ultrasound devices for point-of-care diagnostics, reducing reliance on less accessible or more expensive imaging modalities like MRI or CT in certain clinical scenarios.

Limitations

The abstract highlights several limitations in existing methods: traditional physics-based FWI struggles with weak signals, mode conversion, phase aberration, and incomplete spatial coverage due to impractical full-aperture arrays. Purely data-driven methods are noted to fail in accurately modeling complex nonlinear wave propagation through bone, leading to quantitatively biased SoS maps under low SNR and sparse-aperture conditions. The current validation of BrainPuzzle is exclusively on synthetic datasets, indicating a need for future experimental and clinical validation.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated as 'future directions,' the paper's conclusion that BrainPuzzle 'demonstrates its potential for advancing quantitative ultrasound brain imaging' strongly implies the necessity for further research. This would logically include validating the framework with experimental phantom data, ex-vivo human skull samples, and eventually in-vivo studies to translate its capabilities from synthetic environments to clinical applications.

Abstract

Ultrasound brain imaging remains challenging due to the large difference in sound speed between the skull and brain tissues and the difficulty of coupling large probes to the skull. This work aims to achieve quantitative transcranial ultrasound by reconstructing an accurate speed-of-sound (SoS) map of the brain. Traditional physics-based full-waveform inversion (FWI) is limited by weak signals caused by skull-induced attenuation, mode conversion, and phase aberration, as well as incomplete spatial coverage since full-aperture arrays are clinically impractical. In contrast, purely data-driven methods that learn directly from raw ultrasound data often fail to model the complex nonlinear and nonlocal wave propagation through bone, leading to anatomically plausible but quantitatively biased SoS maps under low signal-to-noise and sparse-aperture conditions. To address these issues, we propose BrainPuzzle, a hybrid two-stage framework that combines physical modeling with machine learning. In the first stage, reverse time migration (time-reversal acoustics) is applied to multi-angle acquisitions to produce migration fragments that preserve structural details even under low SNR. In the second stage, a transformer-based super-resolution encoder-decoder with a graph-based attention unit (GAU) fuses these fragments into a coherent and quantitatively accurate SoS image. A partial-array acquisition strategy using a movable low-count transducer set improves feasibility and coupling, while the hybrid algorithm compensates for the missing aperture. Experiments on two synthetic datasets show that BrainPuzzle achieves superior SoS reconstruction accuracy and image completeness, demonstrating its potential for advancing quantitative ultrasound brain imaging.
