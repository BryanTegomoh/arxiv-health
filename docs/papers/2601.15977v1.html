Predicting Healthcare System Visitation Flow by Integrating Hospital Attributes and Population Socioeconomics with Human Mobility Data

Published: 2026-01-22
Authors: Binbin Lin, Lei Zou, Hao Tian, Heng Cai, Yifan Yang, Bing Zhou
Categories: cs.LG, cs.SI
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00

Summary

This study integrates hospital attributes, population socioeconomics, and human mobility data to predict healthcare visitation flows and analyze their influencing factors. It developed and evaluated five predictive models, identifying Deep Gravity as the top performer, and revealed how hospital characteristics and socioeconomic factors differentially impact visitation patterns based on travel distance and specific demographic groups.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a data-driven framework for understanding and predicting complex healthcare utilization patterns, offering crucial insights for optimizing resource allocation, strategic planning of health services, and addressing health disparities within healthcare systems.

AI Health Application

The AI/ML models (Naive Regression, Gradient Boosting, Multilayer Perceptrons, Deep Gravity, Heterogeneous Graph Neural Networks) are applied to predict and simulate healthcare system visitation flows. This application can inform hospital operations management (e.g., staffing, bed allocation), optimize resource distribution across a healthcare network, identify underserved populations, and support public health policy decisions by understanding how socioeconomic factors and hospital attributes influence patient access and demand.

Key Points

  • Addresses a gap by integrating hospital capacities, occupancy rates, reputation, popularity, population socioeconomics (SES), and spatial mobility data to predict healthcare visitation flows.
  • Utilized four years of SafeGraph mobility data and Google Maps Reviews user experience data from Houston, Texas, for model training and simulation.
  • Five predictive models were employed: Naive Regression, Gradient Boosting, Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs), Deep Gravity, and Heterogeneous Graph Neural Networks (HGNN), with Deep Gravity demonstrating superior performance.
  • Shapley additive explanation (SHAP) analysis and Partial Dependence Plot (PDP) method were used for interpreting factor importance and combined impacts.
  • Identified that hospital capacities, ICU occupancy rates, ratings, and popularity significantly influence visitation, with their effects varying based on travel distance (convenience for short-distance, ratings for long-distance).
  • Revealed SES-specific visitation patterns: White-majority areas showed lower sensitivity to hospital ratings for short-distance visits, while Asian populations and those with higher education prioritized ratings.
  • Highlighted potential healthcare disparities, as areas with higher proportions of Hispanic, Black, under-18, and over-65 populations exhibited more frequent hospital visits, possibly indicating greater healthcare needs or limited alternative access.

Methodology

The study integrated four years of SafeGraph mobility data and Google Maps Reviews with hospital attributes (capacities, ICU occupancy, ratings, popularity) and population socioeconomic status (SES) for Houston, Texas. Five machine learning models ‚Äî Naive Regression, Gradient Boosting, Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs), Deep Gravity, and Heterogeneous Graph Neural Networks (HGNN) ‚Äî were trained and applied for flow prediction. Model performance was evaluated, and the Shapley additive explanation (SHAP) analysis and Partial Dependence Plot (PDP) method were used to interpret the combined impacts of various factors.

Key Findings

Deep Gravity outperformed other predictive models in simulating visitation flows. Hospital capacities, ICU occupancy rates, ratings, and popularity are significant drivers of visitation, with their influence varying by travel distance (convenience for short-distance visits vs. ratings for long-distance visits). Socioeconomic status further shapes these patterns: White-majority areas showed lower sensitivity to hospital ratings for short-distance visits, while Asian populations and those with higher education levels prioritized hospital ratings. Areas with higher proportions of Hispanic, Black, under-18, and over-65 populations demonstrated more frequent hospital visits, indicating potential greater healthcare needs or limited access to alternative services.

Clinical Impact

This research can enable healthcare systems and public health officials to forecast patient demand more accurately, optimize hospital bed and staff allocation, and design targeted interventions for communities with specific healthcare needs or access barriers. By understanding how different demographic groups prioritize healthcare facility attributes, providers can tailor outreach and service delivery to improve patient engagement, reduce disparities, and enhance overall population health management, particularly in urban planning and crisis preparedness.

Abstract

Healthcare visitation patterns are influenced by a complex interplay of hospital attributes, population socioeconomics, and spatial factors. However, existing research often adopts a fragmented approach, examining these determinants in isolation. This study addresses this gap by integrating hospital capacities, occupancy rates, reputation, and popularity with population SES and spatial mobility patterns to predict visitation flows and analyze influencing factors. Utilizing four years of SafeGraph mobility data and user experience data from Google Maps Reviews, five flow prediction models, Naive Regression, Gradient Boosting, Multilayer Perceptrons (MLPs), Deep Gravity, and Heterogeneous Graph Neural Networks (HGNN),were trained and applied to simulate visitation flows in Houston, Texas, U.S. The Shapley additive explanation (SHAP) analysis and the Partial Dependence Plot (PDP) method were employed to examine the combined impacts of different factors on visitation patterns. The findings reveal that Deep Gravity outperformed other models. Hospital capacities, ICU occupancy rates, ratings, and popularity significantly influence visitation patterns, with their effects varying across different travel distances. Short-distance visits are primarily driven by convenience, whereas long-distance visits are influenced by hospital ratings. White-majority areas exhibited lower sensitivity to hospital ratings for short-distance visits, while Asian populations and those with higher education levels prioritized hospital rating in their visitation decisions. SES further influence these patterns, as areas with higher proportions of Hispanic, Black, under-18, and over-65 populations tend to have more frequent hospital visits, potentially reflecting greater healthcare needs or limited access to alternative medical services.
