Summary

SIGMMA proposes a novel multi-modal contrastive alignment framework to learn hierarchical representations of histopathology images and spatial transcriptome profiles across multiple scales, overcoming the limitations of single-scale approaches. By integrating multi-scale contrastive alignment and graph-based modeling of cell interactions, the framework significantly improves gene-expression prediction and cross-modal retrieval tasks, demonstrating better capture of cross-modal correspondences.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing our understanding of diseases by comprehensively integrating tissue morphology (histopathology) with molecular activity (spatial transcriptomics) at a hierarchical, multi-scale level. By enabling a more nuanced and coherent alignment of these modalities, it can uncover deeper biological insights into disease mechanisms, progression, and patient-specific variations.

The proposed AI framework, Sigmma, can lead to AI-powered diagnostic tools for pathology, enabling more accurate and nuanced analysis of tissue samples by integrating visual and molecular information. It can aid in biomarker discovery by identifying novel correlations between tissue morphology and gene expression patterns, supporting precision medicine initiatives. Furthermore, it provides a computational tool for deeper understanding of disease pathogenesis by modeling cell-cell interactions and tissue microenvironments at multiple scales, which is crucial for therapeutic development and personalized treatment strategies.

Methodology

SIGMMA utilizes a multi-modal contrastive alignment framework. It learns hierarchical representations by processing histopathology images and spatial transcriptomic profiles across various scales. A core component is multi-scale contrastive alignment, which ensures coherence between representations at different scales and modalities. Additionally, it models cell interactions as a graph, integrating inter- and intra-subgraph relationships to capture spatial organization and cellular communication.

Key Findings

SIGMMA learns representations that significantly better capture cross-modal correspondences between histopathology images and spatial transcriptomic data. It achieves an average improvement of 9.78% in gene-expression prediction tasks and a substantial average improvement of 26.93% in cross-modal retrieval tasks. The framework also demonstrates the ability to learn meaningful multi-tissue organization in downstream biological analyses.

Abstract

Recent advances in computational pathology have leveraged vision-language models to learn joint representations of Hematoxylin and Eosin (HE) images with spatial transcriptomic (ST) profiles. However, existing approaches typically align HE tiles with their corresponding ST profiles at a single scale, overlooking fine-grained cellular structures and their spatial organization. To address this, we propose Sigmma, a multi-modal contrastive alignment framework for learning hierarchical representations of HE images and spatial transcriptome profiles across multiple scales. Sigmma introduces multi-scale contrastive alignment, ensuring that representations learned at different scales remain coherent across modalities. Furthermore, by representing cell interactions as a graph and integrating inter- and intra-subgraph relationships, our approach effectively captures cell-cell interactions, ranging from fine to coarse, within the tissue microenvironment. We demonstrate that Sigmm learns representations that better capture cross-modal correspondences, leading to an improvement of avg. 9.78\% in the gene-expression prediction task and avg. 26.93\% in the cross-modal retrieval task across datasets. We further show that it learns meaningful multi-tissue organization in downstream analyses.
