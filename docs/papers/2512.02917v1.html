Summary

This study validates PETfectior, a deep learning denoising solution, for generating high-quality PET images from half-counting statistics, aiming to reduce patient radiation exposure and costs. It demonstrates that images processed with PETfectior from reduced acquisition data maintain excellent lesion detectability, quantitative SUVmax accuracy, and subjective image quality comparable to standard-of-care scans.

Medical Relevance

This technology offers a significant advance in nuclear medicine by enabling reduced patient radiation exposure and lower radiopharmaceutical costs, while maintaining or even improving diagnostic image quality and quantitative accuracy in PET scans, particularly for oncology.

AI Health Application

This paper presents 'PETfectior,' an artificial intelligence-based deep learning denoising solution for Positron Emission Tomography (PET) scans. Its application is to enhance the signal-to-noise ratio of low-count PET images, enabling the generation of high-quality diagnostic images from scans with reduced radioactivity and/or acquisition time. This directly impacts health by minimizing patient radiation exposure and potentially reducing healthcare costs, while maintaining or improving the accuracy of cancer detection and quantitative measurements (SUVmax) crucial for diagnosis and treatment monitoring.

Key Points

  • PETfectior is an artificial intelligence-based software designed to enhance signal-to-noise ratio, producing high-quality PET images from low-count-rate data.
  • The study prospectively included 258 18F-FDG PET/CT patients, comparing standard-of-care (100%) scans with half-counting-statistics scans processed by PETfectior (50%+PETfectior).
  • PETfectior-processed images achieved a high sensitivity of 99.9% for lesion detection across 1649 lesions, with only one false positive, indicating robust diagnostic reliability at reduced acquisition parameters.
  • Quantitative SUVmax measurements from 50%+PETfectior images showed strong agreement with standard 100% images, with a bias of -1.01% and 95% limits of agreement within 12.5%.
  • Subjective image quality of 50%+PETfectior images was consistently rated by experienced physicians as equal to or better than the standard-of-care scans.
  • The technology safely allows for a 50% reduction in PET acquisition time or administered radiopharmaceutical dose without compromising lesion detectability, quantitative accuracy, or perceived image quality.

Methodology

A prospective clinical validation study involving 258 18F-FDG PET/CT patients was conducted. Standard-of-care (100%) scans were acquired and reconstructed according to EARL standards 2. Half-counting-statistics versions (50%) were then generated from list-mode data and subsequently processed with the PETfectior software. Lesion detectability (manual or automatic segmentation), SUVmax quantitative concordance, and subjective image quality (visually assessed by two experienced physicians) were evaluated by comparing the 50%+PETfectior images against the 100% reference.

Key Findings

Across 198 studies, 1649 lesions were detected. The 50%+PETfectior images demonstrated 99.9% sensitivity for lesion detection with only 1 false positive. SUVmax measurements in 100% and 50%+PETfectior images agreed within 12.5% (95% limits of agreement), showing a minimal bias of -1.01%. Subjective image quality of the PETfectior-processed images was rated equal to or better than the standard-of-care images.

Clinical Impact

PETfectior has the potential to significantly impact clinical practice by enabling a substantial reduction (50%) in patient radiation dose and radiopharmaceutical costs associated with PET/CT scans. This can be achieved without compromising diagnostic confidence, lesion detectability, or quantitative accuracy, thereby making PET imaging safer, more accessible, and more cost-effective for cancer diagnosis and staging.

Limitations

The abstract describes this as an 'initial clinical validation,' suggesting that while promising, further larger-scale studies, validation across diverse PET scanner models and reconstruction algorithms, and investigations into specific challenging lesion types or anatomical regions may be necessary to fully establish its widespread applicability.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated, the success of this initial validation implies future research could include multi-center trials, evaluation in broader and more diverse patient populations, long-term impact analysis on patient outcomes, and exploration of even greater dose or acquisition time reductions while maintaining diagnostic integrity.

Abstract

Background: Diagnostic PET image quality depends on the administered activity and acquisition time. However, minimizing these variables is desirable to reduce patient radiation exposure and radiopharmaceutical costs. PETfectior is an artificial intelligence-based software that processes PET scans and increases signal-to-noise ratio, obtaining high-quality images from low-count-rate images. We perform an initial clinical validation of PETfectior on images acquired with half of the counting statistics required to meet the most recent EANM quantitative standards for 18F-FDG PET, evaluating lesions detectability, quantitative performance and image quality. Materials and methods: 258 patients referred for 18F-FDG PET/CT were prospectively included. The standard-of-care scans (100% scans) were acquired and reconstructed according to EARL standards 2. Half-counting-statistics versions were generated from list-mode data and processed with PETfecftior (50%+PETfectior scans). All oncologic lesions were segmented on both PET/CT versions, manually or automatically, and lesions detectability was evaluated. The SUVmax of the lesions was measured and the quantitative concordance of 50%+PETfectior and 100% images was evaluated. Subjective image quality was visually assessed by two experienced physicians. Results: 1649 lesions were detected in a total of 198 studies. The 50%+PETfectior images showed high sensitivity for lesion detection (99.9%) and only 1 false positive was detected. The SUVmax measured in 100% and 50%+PETfectior images agreed within 12.5% (95% limits of agreement), with a bias of -1.01%. Image quality of the 50%+PETfectior images was rated equal to or better than the standard-of-care images. Conclusion: PETfectior can safely be used in clinical practice at half counting statistics, with high sensitivity and specificity, low quantitative bias and high subjective image quality.
