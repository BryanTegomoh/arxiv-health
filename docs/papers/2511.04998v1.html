BiPETE: A Bi-Positional Embedding Transformer Encoder for Risk Assessment of Alcohol and Substance Use Disorder with Electronic Health Records

arXiv ID: 2511.04998v1
Published: 2025-11-07
Authors: Daniel S. Lee, Mayra S. Haedo-Cruz, Chen Jiang, Oshin Miranda, LiRong Wang
Categories: cs.LG, cs.AI, q-bio.QM
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces BiPETE, a novel Transformer Encoder model designed for single-disease risk prediction from Electronic Health Records (EHRs). BiPETE uniquely integrates dual positional embeddings (rotary for relative timing and sinusoidal for visit order) to effectively model temporal dependencies in irregular EHR data. The model demonstrates significant performance improvements in predicting Alcohol and Substance Use Disorder (ASUD) risk, while offering interpretable insights into key clinical risk and protective factors.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a powerful, interpretable tool for early identification of individuals at high risk for alcohol and substance use disorders using existing EHR data. Such proactive risk assessment can facilitate timely clinical interventions, personalized prevention strategies, and targeted resource allocation, ultimately improving patient outcomes.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the development of BiPETE, a Transformer-based deep learning model, to predict the risk of Alcohol and Substance Use Disorder (ASUD) in patients using their Electronic Health Records (EHRs). It's designed to identify individuals at high risk, particularly those with existing mental health conditions like depression and PTSD. Furthermore, the model's interpretability feature identifies specific clinical markers (e.g., inflammatory, hematologic, metabolic markers, medications, comorbidities) that contribute to ASUD risk, providing valuable insights for clinicians to understand and potentially mitigate these risks, thus supporting personalized early intervention and risk management strategies.

Key Points

  • Proposes BiPETE, a Bi-Positional Embedding Transformer Encoder, specifically engineered for single-disease risk prediction using EHRs.
  • Addresses the challenge of modeling temporal dependencies in EHRs by integrating rotary positional embeddings (encoding relative visit timing) and sinusoidal embeddings (preserving visit order).
  • Trained directly on EHR data from two mental health cohorts (depressive disorder and PTSD) without large-scale pretraining, to predict the risk of ASUD.
  • Achieves substantial performance improvements over baseline models, boosting the Area Under the Precision-Recall Curve (AUPRC) by 34% in the depression cohort and 50% in the PTSD cohort.
  • An ablation study empirically confirms the effectiveness and necessity of the dual positional encoding strategy.
  • Utilizes the Integrated Gradients method for model interpretability, identifying key clinical features such as abnormal inflammatory, hematologic, and metabolic markers, specific medications, and comorbidities associated with ASUD risk or protection.
  • Presents a practical and interpretable framework for disease risk prediction that can deepen the understanding of risk assessment and inform mitigation strategies.

Methodology

The study proposes BiPETE, a Transformer Encoder model that incorporates both rotary positional embeddings to capture relative visit timing and sinusoidal embeddings to preserve visit order. This model was trained on EHR data from patients with depressive disorder and PTSD to predict the risk of ASUD, notably without relying on large-scale pretraining. Performance was evaluated using AUPRC. An ablation study was conducted to validate the contribution of the dual positional encoding. The Integrated Gradients method was applied to interpret model predictions and identify influential clinical features.

Key Findings

BiPETE significantly outperformed baseline models in ASUD risk prediction, yielding AUPRC improvements of 34% in the depression cohort and 50% in the PTSD cohort. The dual positional encoding strategy was confirmed to be critical for this performance. The interpretability analysis identified key clinical features associated with ASUD risk or protection, including abnormal inflammatory, hematologic, and metabolic markers, as well as specific medications and comorbidities.

Clinical Impact

The BiPETE framework offers an interpretable and high-performing solution for identifying individuals at risk for ASUD, allowing clinicians to implement early screening and preventative measures. The identified risk and protective factors can guide personalized clinical decision-making, enabling targeted interventions and more effective management strategies in mental health and primary care settings. This can lead to improved patient care pathways and resource utilization.

Abstract

Transformer-based deep learning models have shown promise for disease risk prediction using electronic health records(EHRs), but modeling temporal dependencies remains a key challenge due to irregular visit intervals and lack of uniform structure. We propose a Bi-Positional Embedding Transformer Encoder or BiPETE for single-disease prediction, which integrates rotary positional embeddings to encode relative visit timing and sinusoidal embeddings to preserve visit order. Without relying on large-scale pretraining, BiPETE is trained on EHR data from two mental health cohorts-depressive disorder and post-traumatic stress disorder (PTSD)-to predict the risk of alcohol and substance use disorders (ASUD). BiPETE outperforms baseline models, improving the area under the precision-recall curve (AUPRC) by 34% and 50% in the depression and PTSD cohorts, respectively. An ablation study further confirms the effectiveness of the dual positional encoding strategy. We apply the Integrated Gradients method to interpret model predictions, identifying key clinical features associated with ASUD risk and protection, such as abnormal inflammatory, hematologic, and metabolic markers, as well as specific medications and comorbidities. Overall, these key clinical features identified by the attribution methods contribute to a deeper understanding of the risk assessment process and offer valuable clues for mitigating potential risks. In summary, our study presents a practical and interpretable framework for disease risk prediction using EHR data, which can achieve strong performance.
