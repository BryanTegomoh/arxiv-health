Recognition of Abnormal Events in Surveillance Videos using Weakly Supervised Dual-Encoder Models

arXiv ID: 2511.13276v1
Published: 2025-11-17
Authors: Noam Tsfaty, Avishai Weizman, Liav Cohen, Moshe Tshuva, Yehudit Aperstein
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 0.80 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a weakly supervised dual-encoder model designed for detecting rare and diverse abnormal events within surveillance videos. The framework effectively combines convolutional and transformer representations using a top-k pooling mechanism, achieving a notable performance of 90.7% Area Under the Curve (AUC) on the UCF-Crime dataset.

Medical Relevance

This technology holds significant potential for enhancing patient safety and monitoring in healthcare settings by automating the detection of critical abnormal events, such as falls, seizures, or agitated behavior, in real-time surveillance videos.

AI Health Application

The AI application would involve automated video analytics systems deployed in healthcare environments to continuously monitor surveillance feeds. These systems could detect critical abnormal events such as patient falls, aggressive behavior among patients or towards staff, unauthorized entry into restricted areas (e.g., pharmacies, operating rooms, high-security labs for biosecurity), patient wandering or elopement, and other security or safety incidents, providing real-time alerts to healthcare staff or security personnel.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical challenge of detecting infrequent and varied abnormal events in surveillance video footage.
  • Utilizes a weakly supervised learning paradigm, relying solely on video-level labels rather than fine-grained annotations.
  • Proposes a novel dual-backbone framework that integrates two distinct types of neural network architectures.
  • Combines the strengths of convolutional neural networks (CNNs) for spatial feature extraction with transformers for temporal dependencies.
  • Employs top-k pooling to aggregate relevant features, likely highlighting salient abnormal segments within videos.
  • Achieved a high anomaly detection performance, quantified by 90.7% Area Under the Curve (AUC).
  • The model's efficacy was demonstrated and validated using the UCF-Crime dataset, a standard benchmark for crime and anomaly detection.

Methodology

The methodology centers on a weakly supervised dual-backbone framework. This framework integrates feature representations derived from both convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and transformer models. These combined representations are then processed through a top-k pooling mechanism to effectively identify and localize abnormal events within surveillance video streams.

Key Findings

The primary finding is the successful development of a dual-encoder model that achieves a high level of performance in recognizing abnormal events, evidenced by a 90.7% Area Under the Curve (AUC) score on the UCF-Crime dataset.

Clinical Impact

This research could lead to the development of automated monitoring systems in clinical environments (e.g., hospitals, nursing homes, assisted living facilities) that provide early warnings for patient distress, falls, or other critical abnormal behaviors. This capability could reduce caregiver workload, improve response times to emergencies, and proactively enhance the safety and well-being of vulnerable patients.

Abstract

We address the challenge of detecting rare and diverse anomalies in surveillance videos using only video-level supervision. Our dual-backbone framework combines convolutional and transformer representations through top-k pooling, achieving 90.7% area under the curve (AUC) on the UCF-Crime dataset.
