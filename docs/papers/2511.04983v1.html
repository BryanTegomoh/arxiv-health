Predicting Cognitive Assessment Scores in Older Adults with Cognitive Impairment Using Wearable Sensors

arXiv ID: 2511.04983v1
Published: 2025-11-07
Authors: Assma Habadi, Milos Zefran, Lijuan Yin, Woojin Song, Maria Caceres, Elise Hu, Naoko Muramatsu
Categories: q-bio.NC, cs.LG, eess.SP

Summary

This paper presents an AI-driven methodology to predict cognitive assessment scores in older adults with mild cognitive impairment or mild dementia using continuous physiological data from wearable sensors. By applying supervised learning to features extracted from blood volume pulse, skin conductance, temperature, and movement, the study successfully demonstrated accurate tracking of working memory, processing speed, and attention, significantly outperforming traditional predictors.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a valuable non-invasive, continuous, and potentially remote alternative to current time-consuming and disruptive cognitive screening tools. It offers a pathway for earlier detection of cognitive changes and more personalized, timely interventions in older adults with cognitive impairment.

AI Health Application

The AI application involves using supervised learning and feature engineering techniques on physiological data from wearable sensors (blood volume pulse, skin conductance, temperature, movement) to accurately predict scores on established cognitive tests (working memory, processing speed, attention) in older adults with cognitive impairment or mild dementia. This enables continuous, non-invasive tracking of specific cognitive functions, supporting remote assessments and informing clinical interventions.

Key Points

  • The study utilized the Empatica EmbracePlus wearable device to continuously monitor blood volume pulse, skin conductance, temperature, and movement in 23 older adults.
  • Participants underwent assessments using three NIH Toolbox Cognitive Battery tests, targeting working memory, processing speed, and attention.
  • Feature extraction from physiological signals was performed using wavelet-based and segmentation methods, followed by supervised learning algorithms for prediction.
  • The predictive models showed strong performance, achieving Spearman's œÅ of 0.73-0.82 and mean absolute errors of 0.14-0.16, significantly surpassing a naive mean predictor.
  • Specific physiological signal combinations were found to be optimally predictive for different cognitive functions (e.g., heart-related signals + movement + temperature for working memory; movement + skin conductance for processing speed; heart + skin conductance for attention).
  • The research highlights AI's capability to derive robust insights and predictions even from a relatively small data sample.
  • The findings support the potential for non-invasive, continuous cognitive function tracking, enabling remote assessments and facilitating clinical interventions.

Methodology

The study involved 23 older adults who completed three NIH Toolbox Cognitive Battery tests assessing working memory, processing speed, and attention. Concurrently, physiological signals (blood volume pulse, skin conductance, temperature, and movement) were recorded using the Empatica EmbracePlus wearable device. Statistical features were extracted from these signals via wavelet-based and segmentation techniques. Supervised learning models were then applied to predict cognitive assessment scores, with validation rigorously performed through cross-validation, hold-out testing, and bootstrapping.

Key Findings

The supervised learning models demonstrated strong predictive accuracy for cognitive assessment scores, yielding Spearman's œÅ values between 0.73 and 0.82 and mean absolute errors ranging from 0.14 to 0.16, significantly outperforming a naive mean predictor. Crucially, the study identified distinct physiological signatures for different cognitive domains: working memory was best predicted by heart-related signals combined with movement and temperature; processing speed by movement paired with skin conductance; and attention by heart signals in tandem with skin conductance.

Clinical Impact

This approach offers a paradigm shift towards continuous, non-invasive, and objective monitoring of specific cognitive functions in older adults, mitigating the limitations of episodic, subjective assessments. It could significantly enhance remote patient monitoring, facilitate earlier identification of cognitive decline or treatment efficacy, and enable more precise and timely clinical interventions for individuals with mild cognitive impairment or mild dementia.

Limitations

The study was conducted with a relatively small sample size (23 participants). However, the methodology explicitly demonstrated the ability to leverage AI effectively to achieve robust results even under this constraint, suggesting a potential mitigation of typical sample size limitations.

Future Directions

The abstract does not explicitly mention specific future research directions, focusing instead on the immediate implications and potential of the demonstrated approach.

Abstract

Background and Objectives: This paper focuses on using AI to assess the cognitive function of older adults with mild cognitive impairment or mild dementia using physiological data provided by a wearable device. Cognitive screening tools are disruptive, time-consuming, and only capture brief snapshots of activity. Wearable sensors offer an attractive alternative by continuously monitoring physiological signals. This study investigated whether physiological data can accurately predict scores on established cognitive tests. Research Design and Methods: We recorded physiological signals from 23 older adults completing three NIH Toolbox Cognitive Battery tests, which assess working memory, processing speed, and attention. The Empatica EmbracePlus, a wearable device, measured blood volume pulse, skin conductance, temperature, and movement. Statistical features were extracted using wavelet-based and segmentation methods. We then applied supervised learning and validated predictions via cross-validation, hold-out testing, and bootstrapping. Results: Our models showed strong performance with Spearman's \rho of 0.73-0.82 and mean absolute errors of 0.14-0.16, significantly outperforming a naive mean predictor. Sensor roles varied: heart-related signals combined with movement and temperature best predicted working memory, movement paired with skin conductance was most informative for processing speed, and heart in tandem with skin conductance worked best for attention. Discussion and Implications: These findings suggest that wearable sensors paired with AI tools such as supervised learning and feature engineering can noninvasively track specific cognitive functions in older adults, enabling continuous monitoring. Our study demonstrates how AI can be leveraged when the data sample is small. This approach may support remote assessments and facilitate clinical interventions.
