Synthetic Data Reveals Generalization Gaps in Correlated Multiple Instance Learning

arXiv ID: 2510.25759v1
Published: 2025-10-29
Authors: Ethan Harvey, Dennis Johan Loevlie, Michael C. Hughes
Categories: cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This research paper identifies a significant generalization gap in Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) methods, particularly when contextual relationships between instances are crucial for classification, as often occurs in medical imaging. By designing a synthetic task where adjacent instance features are essential for accurate prediction and comparing MIL performance against an optimal Bayes estimator, the authors demonstrate that both conventional and newer correlated MIL approaches struggle to achieve optimal generalization, even with substantial training data.

Medical Relevance

This research directly impacts medical imaging, where MIL is frequently applied to classify high-resolution 2D images from patches or 3D volumes from slices. The identified generalization gap suggests that current MIL models may overlook vital diagnostic contextual information, potentially affecting the accuracy and reliability of automated medical diagnoses.

AI Health Application

This research aims to improve the underlying AI models (Multiple Instance Learning) used for classifying medical images. Enhanced MIL can lead to more accurate and robust AI systems for tasks such as automated disease detection (e.g., tumor identification from biopsies or lesions from radiology scans), image-based diagnosis, and prognosis in various medical specialties. By addressing generalization gaps and contextual relationships, it contributes to developing more reliable medical AI applications.

Key Points

  • Traditional Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) methods often disregard contextual relationships between instances (e.g., adjacent image patches or 3D slices), which can be critical for accurate medical imaging classification.
  • A novel synthetic classification task was developed where accounting for adjacent instance features is a prerequisite for optimal prediction accuracy.
  • The task's design allows for the calculation of an optimal Bayes estimator in closed-form, providing a theoretical upper bound for performance.
  • Off-the-shelf MIL approaches exhibit significant limitations and a large performance gap compared to this optimal Bayes estimator.
  • Even newer 'correlated MIL' methods, designed to consider context, empirically struggle to generalize optimally, failing to match the Bayes estimator's performance despite being trained on tens of thousands of instances.
  • The study quantitatively demonstrates a 'generalization gap' in correlated MIL methods, highlighting their inability to fully exploit crucial contextual information required for complex classification tasks.

Methodology

The researchers designed a synthetic classification task specifically requiring the integration of adjacent instance features for accurate prediction. They established an optimal Bayes estimator for this task, available in closed-form, to serve as a ground truth performance benchmark. Performance of both conventional off-the-shelf MIL methods and newer correlated MIL methods was then quantified and compared against this Bayes optimum.

Key Findings

The primary findings reveal that both standard and advanced correlated MIL methods exhibit a substantial generalization gap when faced with tasks where contextual instance relationships are critical. These models consistently underperform compared to the theoretical Bayes optimum, even after extensive training, indicating an inherent limitation in their ability to effectively learn and utilize essential contextual information.

Clinical Impact

In clinical settings, this generalization gap could lead to suboptimal performance in AI-powered diagnostic tools for medical imaging. If MIL models cannot effectively incorporate contextual information (e.g., the appearance of cells around a tumor, or slices adjacent to a lesion), they might miss subtle but critical diagnostic cues, potentially resulting in misdiagnoses, delayed treatment, or a lack of trust in automated systems.

Limitations

The study's primary limitation, while intentional for analytical rigor, is its reliance on synthetic data. While this allows for precise control and comparison against a Bayes optimum, it means the exact quantitative generalization gap might differ in real-world, noisy, and complex medical imaging datasets. The paper itself highlights the struggle of newer correlated MIL methods *even* with tens of thousands of instances, suggesting a fundamental architectural limitation rather than merely a data volume issue.

Future Directions

Implicitly, the research points towards the need for developing novel MIL architectures that are explicitly designed to capture and effectively utilize complex contextual and relational information between instances. Future work should focus on methods that can bridge this observed generalization gap, potentially through graph neural networks, attention mechanisms explicitly modeled for spatial context, or other sequence-aware deep learning approaches tailored for MIL problems in medical imaging.

Abstract

Multiple instance learning (MIL) is often used in medical imaging to classify high-resolution 2D images by processing patches or classify 3D volumes by processing slices. However, conventional MIL approaches treat instances separately, ignoring contextual relationships such as the appearance of nearby patches or slices that can be essential in real applications. We design a synthetic classification task where accounting for adjacent instance features is crucial for accurate prediction. We demonstrate the limitations of off-the-shelf MIL approaches by quantifying their performance compared to the optimal Bayes estimator for this task, which is available in closed-form. We empirically show that newer correlated MIL methods still struggle to generalize as well as possible when trained from scratch on tens of thousands of instances.
