Synthetic Data Reveals Generalization Gaps in Correlated Multiple Instance Learning

arXiv ID: 2510.25759v1
Published: 2025-10-29
Authors: Ethan Harvey, Dennis Johan Loevlie, Michael C. Hughes
Categories: cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper investigates the limitations of Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) methods in medical imaging tasks where contextual relationships between instances (e.g., adjacent patches or slices) are crucial. By designing a synthetic classification task where context is paramount, the authors demonstrate significant generalization gaps, showing that both conventional and newer correlated MIL approaches struggle to perform optimally compared to a Bayes estimator.

Medical Relevance

In medical imaging, classifying high-resolution images or volumes often relies on MIL, but ignoring spatial or volumetric context between instances (patches/slices) can lead to diagnostic errors, making robust, context-aware MIL essential for reliable AI-assisted clinical decision-making.

AI Health Application

The research aims to improve the performance and generalization capabilities of Multiple Instance Learning (MIL) models, which are critical for AI-driven classification tasks in medical imaging. Specifically, it addresses the challenge of incorporating contextual information from adjacent patches or slices in medical images, which is essential for more accurate and reliable diagnoses in areas like digital pathology or 3D medical volume analysis.

Key Points

  • Conventional MIL approaches often ignore critical contextual relationships between instances, such as the appearance of nearby image patches or slices, which are essential in medical imaging.
  • A novel synthetic classification task was designed where accounting for adjacent instance features is a prerequisite for accurate prediction, allowing for a controlled evaluation.
  • The performance of off-the-shelf MIL methods was quantified against an optimal Bayes estimator, available in closed-form for this synthetic task, revealing their inherent limitations.
  • Even newer 'correlated MIL' methods, intended to address contextual dependencies, empirically failed to generalize as effectively as possible when trained from scratch.
  • The study identified significant generalization gaps in existing MIL methods, even with tens of thousands of instances for training, suggesting an inability to fully leverage contextual information.
  • The research underscores the necessity for MIL models that can robustly incorporate and exploit contextual features to improve accuracy and reliability in complex medical imaging scenarios.

Methodology

The study involved designing a synthetic classification task where accurate prediction was contingent upon leveraging contextual relationships between instances. It quantitatively evaluated the performance of both conventional and newer correlated MIL methods by comparing them against a closed-form optimal Bayes estimator, which served as a theoretical upper bound for performance.

Key Findings

Conventional MIL approaches demonstrate significant performance limitations when contextual relationships are crucial. More importantly, even advanced 'correlated MIL' methods struggle to generalize optimally from scratch on tasks requiring contextual understanding, even with large training datasets (tens of thousands of instances), indicating persistent challenges in robustly learning and exploiting instance correlations.

Clinical Impact

The findings imply that current MIL-based AI tools in medical imaging might be prone to errors or suboptimal performance in clinical settings where crucial diagnostic cues are often contextual. Developing MIL systems that genuinely account for correlated instances is vital to improve diagnostic accuracy, reduce misdiagnosis rates, and enhance trust in AI for tasks like lesion detection, disease staging, and prognosis in radiology and pathology.

Limitations

The primary limitation highlighted is the inherent struggle of existing conventional and even newer correlated MIL methods to generalize effectively on tasks requiring contextual understanding. While the study uses a *synthetic* task, it is designed to expose fundamental challenges relevant to real-world correlated medical data, rather than being a limitation of the study's design itself.

Future Directions

Future research should focus on developing novel MIL architectures and training strategies that are inherently capable of modeling and effectively exploiting contextual and correlational relationships between instances. This is crucial for improving generalization capabilities and achieving optimal performance in real-world medical imaging applications where such correlations are paramount for accurate diagnosis.

Abstract

Multiple instance learning (MIL) is often used in medical imaging to classify high-resolution 2D images by processing patches or classify 3D volumes by processing slices. However, conventional MIL approaches treat instances separately, ignoring contextual relationships such as the appearance of nearby patches or slices that can be essential in real applications. We design a synthetic classification task where accounting for adjacent instance features is crucial for accurate prediction. We demonstrate the limitations of off-the-shelf MIL approaches by quantifying their performance compared to the optimal Bayes estimator for this task, which is available in closed-form. We empirically show that newer correlated MIL methods still struggle to generalize as well as possible when trained from scratch on tens of thousands of instances.
