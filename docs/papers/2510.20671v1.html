Summary

This paper introduces GRACE, a novel graph neural network (GNN) framework designed to predict the appropriate locus of care for addiction patients, a critical clinical decision often hindered by resource scarcity and data class imbalance. GRACE formalizes this as a structured learning problem, employing extensive feature engineering and a new unbiased meta-graph approach to address class imbalance. Experimental results on real-world data show a significant improvement of 11-35% in the F1 score for the minority class over existing baselines.

Medical Relevance

Accurately matching addiction patients to the most appropriate level of care is crucial for optimizing treatment effectiveness, preventing relapse, and ensuring the efficient allocation of scarce specialized healthcare resources, directly impacting patient recovery and healthcare system sustainability.

AI Health Application

The paper describes an AI application that uses a Graph Neural Network (GRACE) to predict the appropriate 'locus of care' for addiction patients. This acts as an automated clinical decision support tool, helping healthcare providers determine the optimal treatment setting (e.g., inpatient, outpatient) for individuals, thereby improving treatment outcomes and efficient allocation of scarce healthcare resources in addiction treatment.

Methodology

The core methodology centers on a novel Graph Neural Network (GNN) framework, GRACE, which reframes locus of care prediction as a structured learning problem. This involves extensive feature engineering to capture relevant patient and contextual information. A key innovative aspect is the development of a new approach to construct an unbiased meta-graph. This meta-graph is specifically designed to mitigate the severe class imbalance issues prevalent in real-world addiction datasets, allowing the GNN to learn robustly across all care categories, particularly for less represented patient groups.

Key Findings

The GRACE framework significantly improved prediction performance, especially for underrepresented patient populations. On real-world datasets, it achieved an improvement of 11-35% in the F1 score for the minority class when compared against competitive baseline models, indicating its effectiveness in handling challenging imbalanced data and accurately identifying optimal care pathways.

This could lead to optimized allocation of scarce resources (e.g., inpatient beds, specialized staff), improved patient outcomes through better care matching, reduced clinician burnout, and more equitable care by enhancing prediction accuracy for historically underserved or less common patient profiles.

Abstract

Determining the appropriate locus of care for addiction patients is one of the most critical clinical decisions that affects patient treatment outcomes and effective use of resources. With a lack of sufficient specialized treatment resources, such as inpatient beds or staff, there is an unmet need to develop an automated framework for the same. Current decision-making approaches suffer from severe class imbalances in addiction datasets. To address this limitation, we propose a novel graph neural network (GRACE) framework that formalizes locus of care prediction as a structured learning problem. Further, we perform extensive feature engineering and propose a new approach of obtaining an unbiased meta-graph to train a GNN to overcome the class imbalance problem. Experimental results in real-world data show an improvement of 11-35% in terms of the F1 score of the minority class over competitive baselines.
