Large-scale Long-tailed Disease Diagnosis on Radiology Images

Published: 2023-12-26
Authors: Qiaoyu Zheng, Weike Zhao, Chaoyi Wu, Xiaoman Zhang, Lisong Dai, Hengyu Guan, Yuehua Li, Ya Zhang, Yanfeng Wang, Weidi Xie

Summary

This paper introduces RadDiag, a foundational transformer-based AI model designed for comprehensive 2D and 3D radiology diagnosis across diverse modalities and anatomies. To address data scarcity and privacy, RadDiag was trained on RP3D-DiagDS, a novel dataset of 40,936 cases covering 5,568 disorders, curated from high-quality, clinician-reviewed public online images. The model achieved 95.14% AUC internally and demonstrated state-of-the-art zero-shot and fine-tuned performance on external hospital datasets, highlighting the immense value of public medical data for developing generalist AI in healthcare.

Medical Relevance

This work is highly relevant to medicine by addressing the critical need for robust, generalist AI diagnostic systems in radiology, leveraging publicly available data to circumvent traditional barriers of data scarcity and privacy. Such a system can greatly enhance the accuracy and efficiency of clinical diagnostics across a broad spectrum of diseases.

AI Health Application

The paper presents RadDiag, a foundational AI model utilizing transformer-based fusion for large-scale, long-tailed disease diagnosis across 2D and 3D radiology images (various modalities and anatomies). It is designed to enhance clinical diagnostics by providing comprehensive disease diagnosis, with potential for zero-shot application or fine-tuning on external hospital datasets, thus supporting the development of a generalist AI for healthcare.

Key Points

  • RadDiag is introduced as a foundational AI model for generalist radiology diagnosis, utilizing a transformer-based fusion module to process both 2D and 3D inputs across various modalities and anatomical regions.
  • A novel, large-scale dataset named RP3D-DiagDS was created, comprising 40,936 cases with 195,010 scans covering 5,568 distinct disorders (930 unique ICD-10-CM codes), sourced from high-quality, clinician-reviewed radiological images available online.
  • The model demonstrated strong internal performance, achieving an impressive 95.14% Area Under the Curve (AUC) on internal evaluation, further enhanced by a knowledge-enhancement strategy.
  • RadDiag exhibits robust generalizability, successfully applying its diagnostic capabilities zero-shot or with fine-tuning to external diagnosis datasets obtained from various hospitals, achieving state-of-the-art results.
  • The research underscores the significant potential and value of publicly shared medical data on the internet as a tremendous resource for building advanced, generalist AI systems for healthcare applications.

Methodology

RadDiag employs a transformer-based fusion module designed to process multi-modal (2D/3D) radiological inputs across various anatomies. The RP3D-DiagDS dataset was meticulously curated from high-quality, clinician-reviewed radiological images and their corresponding diagnosis labels found online. Model performance was evaluated using AUC on an internal test set, integrating a knowledge-enhancement strategy. External validation was performed by applying RadDiag in a zero-shot manner or after fine-tuning on diverse hospital-sourced datasets.

Key Findings

RadDiag achieved 95.14% AUC on internal evaluation with knowledge enhancement. It demonstrated state-of-the-art performance when applied zero-shot or fine-tuned on external hospital datasets. The study conclusively showed that publicly shared medical data on the internet is a valuable resource for developing generalist AI in healthcare.

Clinical Impact

This research has the potential to significantly improve clinical diagnostics by providing a highly accurate and generalizable AI system for interpreting radiology images, particularly for a wide range of diseases, including those in the long-tail. By overcoming data acquisition challenges through innovative data sourcing, RadDiag can facilitate the widespread adoption of AI in diagnostic workflows, leading to more efficient, consistent, and potentially earlier disease detection in diverse clinical settings.

Abstract

Developing a generalist radiology diagnosis system can greatly enhance clinical diagnostics. In this paper, we introduce RadDiag, a foundational model supporting 2D and 3D inputs across various modalities and anatomies, using a transformer-based fusion module for comprehensive disease diagnosis. Due to patient privacy concerns and the lack of large-scale radiology diagnosis datasets, we utilize high-quality, clinician-reviewed radiological images available online with diagnosis labels. Our dataset, RP3D-DiagDS, contains 40,936 cases with 195,010 scans covering 5,568 disorders (930 unique ICD-10-CM codes). Experimentally, our RadDiag achieves 95.14% AUC on internal evaluation with the knowledge-enhancement strategy. Additionally, RadDiag can be zero-shot applied or fine-tuned to external diagnosis datasets sourced from various hospitals, demonstrating state-of-the-art results. In conclusion, we show that publicly shared medical data on the Internet is a tremendous and valuable resource that can potentially support building a generalist AI for healthcare.
