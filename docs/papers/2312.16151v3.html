Large-scale Long-tailed Disease Diagnosis on Radiology Images

Summary

This paper introduces RadDiag, a foundational model for generalist radiology diagnosis capable of processing 2D and 3D images across diverse modalities and anatomies using a transformer-based fusion module. To address the scarcity of large-scale datasets, the authors compiled RP3D-DiagDS, a novel dataset of 40,936 cases covering 5,568 disorders from high-quality, clinician-reviewed online radiology images. RadDiag demonstrated strong performance, achieving 95.14% AUC internally and state-of-the-art results when applied zero-shot or fine-tuned on external hospital datasets.

Medical Relevance

Developing a generalist radiology diagnosis system like RadDiag can significantly improve clinical diagnostics by providing comprehensive, AI-assisted interpretation across a wide spectrum of diseases and imaging types, potentially reducing diagnostic errors and improving efficiency in diverse healthcare settings.

AI Health Application

The paper presents RadDiag, a foundational AI model designed to perform comprehensive disease diagnosis from 2D and 3D radiology images across various modalities and anatomies. This AI system aims to enhance clinical diagnostics by assisting healthcare professionals in identifying a wide range of disorders, potentially serving as a generalist diagnostic tool in healthcare settings.

  • It employs a transformer-based fusion module for comprehensive disease diagnosis, indicating a robust architecture for integrating diverse imaging information.
  • A novel dataset, RP3D-DiagDS, was constructed from high-quality, clinician-reviewed online radiological images due to patient privacy and dataset availability challenges.
  • RP3D-DiagDS is extensive, comprising 40,936 cases with 195,010 scans, covering 5,568 disorders (930 unique ICD-10-CM codes), specifically addressing 'long-tailed disease diagnosis'.
  • RadDiag achieved an impressive 95.14% AUC on internal evaluation, utilizing a knowledge-enhancement strategy.

Methodology

The study developed RadDiag, a transformer-based foundational model designed for 2D and 3D radiology image inputs across multiple modalities and anatomies. To train this model, a large-scale dataset named RP3D-DiagDS was curated by leveraging high-quality, clinician-reviewed radiological images and associated diagnosis labels available online. The model's performance was evaluated internally using AUC, incorporating a knowledge-enhancement strategy, and externally through zero-shot application and fine-tuning on diagnosis datasets sourced from various hospitals.

Key Findings

RadDiag achieved an internal evaluation AUC of 95.14% with a knowledge-enhancement strategy. Furthermore, it demonstrated state-of-the-art performance when applied zero-shot or fine-tuned on external diagnosis datasets from diverse hospital sources. The study concluded that publicly available online medical data is a valuable and untapped resource for building generalist AI in healthcare.

Clinical Impact

This research offers a pathway towards creating highly capable AI systems that can assist radiologists and clinicians in diagnosing a vast range of diseases from various medical images. Such a generalist model could lead to more accurate and faster diagnoses, particularly for long-tailed or rare conditions, improve diagnostic consistency across different hospitals, and potentially make expert-level diagnostic support more accessible globally.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations of the RadDiag model or the RP3D-DiagDS dataset. However, the reliance on publicly shared online data, while resourceful, may inherently introduce biases or lack of representativeness compared to centrally managed, diverse institutional datasets, though the abstract implies their selection process addressed quality.

Future Directions

The paper suggests that publicly shared medical data on the Internet holds immense potential to support the future development of generalist AI models for healthcare, implying continued research into leveraging such resources and expanding the capabilities of generalist diagnostic AI.

Abstract

Developing a generalist radiology diagnosis system can greatly enhance clinical diagnostics. In this paper, we introduce RadDiag, a foundational model supporting 2D and 3D inputs across various modalities and anatomies, using a transformer-based fusion module for comprehensive disease diagnosis. Due to patient privacy concerns and the lack of large-scale radiology diagnosis datasets, we utilize high-quality, clinician-reviewed radiological images available online with diagnosis labels. Our dataset, RP3D-DiagDS, contains 40,936 cases with 195,010 scans covering 5,568 disorders (930 unique ICD-10-CM codes). Experimentally, our RadDiag achieves 95.14% AUC on internal evaluation with the knowledge-enhancement strategy. Additionally, RadDiag can be zero-shot applied or fine-tuned to external diagnosis datasets sourced from various hospitals, demonstrating state-of-the-art results. In conclusion, we show that publicly shared medical data on the Internet is a tremendous and valuable resource that can potentially support building a generalist AI for healthcare.
