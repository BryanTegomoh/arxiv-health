Summary

The paper introduces SlotSPE, a novel slot-based framework leveraging slot attention and factorial coding to effectively model structural prognostic events from multimodal cancer data (histology images and gene profiles). By compressing complex inputs into compact, distinctive slots, SlotSPE significantly enhances intra- and inter-modal interaction modeling, leading to superior survival prediction, robustness to missing data, and improved interpretability across ten cancer benchmarks.

Medical Relevance

This research significantly advances the field of cancer prognostics by providing a more accurate, robust, and interpretable method for predicting patient survival based on complex multimodal data, which can lead to more precise risk stratification and personalized treatment planning.

It aims to assist clinicians in providing more precise prognoses for cancer patients by integrating and analyzing complex data from histology images and gene profiles, thereby enhancing personalized treatment planning and patient management.

Methodology

SlotSPE employs a slot-based architectural design that utilizes 'slot attention' mechanisms to transform high-dimensional, multimodal inputs (histology images and gene profiles) into compact, modality-specific sets of 'slots'. These slots are designed to capture distinct, high-level structural prognostic events, drawing inspiration from the principle of factorial coding. The framework subsequently models intra- and inter-modal interactions based on these learned slot representations, and crucially, allows for the integration of biological priors to enhance prognostic relevance.

Key Findings

SlotSPE achieved superior performance in cancer survival prediction, outperforming existing methods in 8 out of 10 cancer benchmarks with an overall accuracy improvement of 2.9%. A significant finding is the model's robustness to missing genomic data, which is critical for real-world clinical applicability. Furthermore, it delivers markedly enhanced interpretability by enabling the decomposition of predictions into identifiable structural prognostic events.

Clinical Impact

The enhanced accuracy, robustness, and interpretability offered by SlotSPE could significantly impact clinical practice by providing more reliable patient survival predictions, aiding oncologists in personalized treatment selection, and improving risk assessment. Its robustness to incomplete data makes it highly valuable in diverse clinical settings. The structured event decomposition also offers clinicians insights into the specific biological factors (e.g., spatial patterns, pathway co-activations) driving a patient's prognosis, potentially leading to new diagnostic markers or therapeutic targets.

Abstract

The integration of histology images and gene profiles has shown great promise for improving survival prediction in cancer. However, current approaches often struggle to model intra- and inter-modal interactions efficiently and effectively due to the high dimensionality and complexity of the inputs. A major challenge is capturing critical prognostic events that, though few, underlie the complexity of the observed inputs and largely determine patient outcomes. These events, manifested as high-level structural signals such as spatial histologic patterns or pathway co-activations, are typically sparse, patient-specific, and unannotated, making them inherently difficult to uncover. To address this, we propose SlotSPE, a slot-based framework for structural prognostic event modeling. Specifically, inspired by the principle of factorial coding, we compress each patient's multimodal inputs into compact, modality-specific sets of mutually distinctive slots using slot attention. By leveraging these slot representations as encodings for prognostic events, our framework enables both efficient and effective modeling of complex intra- and inter-modal interactions, while also facilitating seamless incorporation of biological priors that enhance prognostic relevance. Extensive experiments on ten cancer benchmarks show that SlotSPE outperforms existing methods in 8 out of 10 cohorts, achieving an overall improvement of 2.9%. It remains robust under missing genomic data and delivers markedly improved interpretability through structured event decomposition.
