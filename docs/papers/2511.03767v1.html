Phenotype discovery of traumatic brain injury segmentations from heterogeneous multi-site data

Summary

This research addresses the intrinsic heterogeneity of traumatic brain injury (TBI) by identifying shared structural injury patterns from a large, multi-site neuroimaging dataset. Utilizing T1-weighted MRI data from the FITBIR repository, the study harmonized images, segmented 132 brain regions, and identified significant volume differences in 37 regions between TBI patients and controls. Subsequent analysis revealed three distinct phenotype clusters of injury, providing objective imaging-based characterizations of TBI.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant for medicine as it offers a data-driven approach to classify TBI into distinct structural phenotypes, moving beyond generalized outcome measures. This improved characterization can lead to more precise diagnostics, targeted treatments, and personalized prognostic assessments for TBI patients.

Methodology

The study utilized T1-weighted MRI data from the Federal Interagency Traumatic Brain Injury Research (FITBIR) repository. Data underwent harmonization to a local dataset, followed by automated segmentation into 132 regions of interest (ROIs). After quality assurance and outlier removal, ROI volumes were calculated and converted to z-scores relative to control group means and standard deviations. A multivariate linear regression was applied to regress out sex, age, and total brain volume. Significant volumetric differences between TBI and control groups were identified using independent t-tests with False Discovery Rate (FDR) correction. Finally, Independent Component Analysis (ICA) was performed on the component loadings of TBI subjects, followed by clustering, to discover distinct injury phenotypes.

Key Findings

The study identified significant volumetric differences in 37 out of 132 brain ROIs between TBI subjects and controls. These differences were then clustered into three principal 'phenotypes' or shared pathways of injury: 1) structures including the brainstem, occipital pole, and regions posterior to the orbit; 2) subcortical gray matter and insular cortex; and 3) cerebral and cerebellar white matter. These distinct patterns highlight specific neuroanatomical vulnerabilities and injury profiles within the heterogeneous TBI population.

Clinical Impact

The discovery of distinct TBI phenotypes based on structural imaging data holds significant clinical impact. It provides objective imaging biomarkers that can aid in subclassifying TBI patients, potentially improving diagnostic accuracy, guiding the selection of more specific and effective treatments, and enabling better prediction of recovery trajectories. This moves towards a precision medicine approach for TBI management, facilitating personalized rehabilitation strategies based on the identified injury patterns.

Abstract

Traumatic brain injury (TBI) is intrinsically heterogeneous, and typical clinical outcome measures like the Glasgow Coma Scale complicate this diversity. The large variability in severity and patient outcomes render it difficult to link structural damage to functional deficits. The Federal Interagency Traumatic Brain Injury Research (FITBIR) repository contains large-scale multi-site magnetic resonance imaging data of varying resolutions and acquisition parameters (25 shared studies with 7,693 sessions that have age, sex and TBI status defined - 5,811 TBI and 1,882 controls). To reveal shared pathways of injury of TBI through imaging, we analyzed T1-weighted images from these sessions by first harmonizing to a local dataset and segmenting 132 regions of interest (ROIs) in the brain. After running quality assurance, calculating the volumes of the ROIs, and removing outliers, we calculated the z-scores of volumes for all participants relative to the mean and standard deviation of the controls. We regressed out sex, age, and total brain volume with a multivariate linear regression, and we found significant differences in 37 ROIs between subjects with TBI and controls (p < 0.05 with independent t-tests with false discovery rate correction). We found that differences originated in 1) the brainstem, occipital pole and structures posterior to the orbit, 2) subcortical gray matter and insular cortex, and 3) cerebral and cerebellar white matter using independent component analysis and clustering the component loadings of those with TBI.
