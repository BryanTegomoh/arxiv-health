Phenotype discovery of traumatic brain injury segmentations from heterogeneous multi-site data

Summary

This paper addresses the significant heterogeneity of Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI) by analyzing T1-weighted MRI data from the large-scale, multi-site FITBIR repository. Through a rigorous methodological pipeline including data harmonization, segmentation of 132 brain regions, and statistical analysis, the study identified significant volumetric differences in 37 ROIs between TBI patients and controls. Critically, these differences clustered into three distinct injury patterns (phenotypes) affecting specific brain regions, providing a data-driven approach to characterize TBI's structural impact.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing our understanding of TBI beyond simplistic classifications, providing a data-driven foundation for identifying distinct biological subtypes. Uncovering specific structural injury phenotypes can lead to more precise diagnostics, improved prognostication, and the development of targeted, personalized therapeutic and rehabilitation strategies for TBI patients, moving towards precision medicine.

AI Health Application

The research applies computational methods such as image harmonization, segmentation of regions of interest (ROIs), quality assurance, statistical analysis (z-scores, multivariate linear regression, t-tests with FDR correction), independent component analysis (ICA), and clustering to large-scale medical imaging data (MRI). These techniques are central to medical AI for automated image analysis, biomarker identification, phenotype discovery, and classifying disease subtypes in neurological conditions like TBI. The goal of 'phenotype discovery' heavily relies on data-driven, often AI-powered, pattern recognition from complex medical datasets.

Key Points

  • The study leverages heterogeneous multi-site MRI data from the Federal Interagency Traumatic Brain Injury Research (FITBIR) repository, including 7,693 sessions (5,811 TBI, 1,882 controls).
  • T1-weighted images were harmonized to a local dataset and segmented into 132 Regions of Interest (ROIs) to quantify brain volumes.
  • Methodology included quality assurance, outlier removal, z-score normalization of ROI volumes relative to controls, and multivariate linear regression to control for sex, age, and total brain volume.
  • Significant volumetric differences were identified in 37 ROIs between TBI subjects and controls, confirmed by independent t-tests with false discovery rate (FDR) correction (p < 0.05).
  • Independent Component Analysis (ICA) and subsequent clustering within the TBI group revealed three distinct anatomical patterns (phenotypes) of injury.
  • These injury phenotypes localized to: 1) the brainstem, occipital pole, and structures posterior to the orbit; 2) subcortical gray matter and insular cortex; and 3) cerebral and cerebellar white matter.
  • The research demonstrates the feasibility of discovering shared structural injury pathways in TBI using large, diverse neuroimaging datasets, despite intrinsic data variability.

Methodology

The study analyzed T1-weighted MRI data from 7,693 sessions within the FITBIR repository. Data were first harmonized to a local dataset to address multi-site heterogeneity. Subsequently, 132 brain regions of interest (ROIs) were segmented, and their volumes calculated after quality assurance and outlier removal. ROI volumes were then z-scored relative to the control group's mean and standard deviation. Covariates including sex, age, and total brain volume were regressed out using multivariate linear regression. Group differences between TBI and controls were assessed with independent t-tests, corrected for multiple comparisons using False Discovery Rate (FDR). Finally, Independent Component Analysis (ICA) followed by clustering of component loadings was applied to the TBI group to identify shared injury phenotypes.

Key Findings

The study identified significant volumetric differences in 37 specific brain ROIs between individuals with TBI and controls. Furthermore, three distinct structural injury phenotypes were discovered within the TBI population through ICA and clustering. These phenotypes primarily involved 1) posterior brain structures (brainstem, occipital pole, and structures posterior to the orbit), 2) subcortical gray matter and the insular cortex, and 3) cerebral and cerebellar white matter.

Clinical Impact

Identifying distinct TBI phenotypes based on structural brain damage has significant clinical implications. It enables more accurate patient stratification, potentially leading to improved diagnostic criteria and more precise prognostication than current broad TBI classifications. This work could guide the development of tailored interventions, facilitate more effective recruitment for clinical trials based on specific injury patterns, and inform personalized rehabilitation plans, thereby optimizing TBI management and improving patient outcomes.

Abstract

Traumatic brain injury (TBI) is intrinsically heterogeneous, and typical clinical outcome measures like the Glasgow Coma Scale complicate this diversity. The large variability in severity and patient outcomes render it difficult to link structural damage to functional deficits. The Federal Interagency Traumatic Brain Injury Research (FITBIR) repository contains large-scale multi-site magnetic resonance imaging data of varying resolutions and acquisition parameters (25 shared studies with 7,693 sessions that have age, sex and TBI status defined - 5,811 TBI and 1,882 controls). To reveal shared pathways of injury of TBI through imaging, we analyzed T1-weighted images from these sessions by first harmonizing to a local dataset and segmenting 132 regions of interest (ROIs) in the brain. After running quality assurance, calculating the volumes of the ROIs, and removing outliers, we calculated the z-scores of volumes for all participants relative to the mean and standard deviation of the controls. We regressed out sex, age, and total brain volume with a multivariate linear regression, and we found significant differences in 37 ROIs between subjects with TBI and controls (p < 0.05 with independent t-tests with false discovery rate correction). We found that differences originated in 1) the brainstem, occipital pole and structures posterior to the orbit, 2) subcortical gray matter and insular cortex, and 3) cerebral and cerebellar white matter using independent component analysis and clustering the component loadings of those with TBI.
