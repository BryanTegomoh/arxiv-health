Phenotype discovery of traumatic brain injury segmentations from heterogeneous multi-site data

Summary

This study addressed the intrinsic heterogeneity of Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI) by analyzing a large-scale, multi-site MRI dataset from the FITBIR repository to discover shared structural injury phenotypes. By harmonizing T1-weighted images, segmenting 132 brain regions, and controlling for confounding factors, the researchers identified significant volumetric differences in 37 ROIs between TBI patients and controls, clustering into three distinct injury patterns encompassing the brainstem, subcortical gray matter, insular cortex, and cerebral/cerebellar white matter.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a robust, data-driven approach to identifying consistent patterns of structural brain damage in TBI, which is critical for overcoming diagnostic challenges posed by its heterogeneity. Such phenotype discovery can pave the way for more accurate stratification of TBI patients and the development of targeted, personalized therapeutic strategies.

AI Health Application

The phenotype discovery and identification of specific brain regions affected by TBI using quantitative methods (segmentation, independent component analysis, clustering) provide a strong foundation for medical AI applications. This research can enable the development of AI models for automated TBI diagnosis, patient stratification into distinct TBI subtypes based on imaging biomarkers, prediction of clinical outcomes, and personalized treatment guidance. The analysis of heterogeneous multi-site data and the emphasis on discovering shared pathways of injury are key areas where AI and machine learning excel.

Key Points

  • Analyzed a massive multi-site dataset from the FITBIR repository, including 7,693 MRI sessions (5,811 TBI, 1,882 controls), effectively tackling TBI's inherent heterogeneity.
  • Employed rigorous data harmonization techniques to standardize T1-weighted images despite varying resolutions and acquisition parameters across multiple sites.
  • Quantified structural brain changes by segmenting 132 regions of interest (ROIs) and calculating their volumes, providing granular anatomical data.
  • Identified 37 ROIs with statistically significant volumetric differences between TBI subjects and controls (p < 0.05, False Discovery Rate corrected), after normalizing volumes via z-scores relative to controls.
  • Utilized multivariate linear regression to regress out confounding factors such as sex, age, and total brain volume, enhancing the specificity of the findings.
  • Discovered three distinct patterns of injury (phenotypes) within the TBI population through Independent Component Analysis and clustering: (1) brainstem, occipital pole, and structures posterior to the orbit; (2) subcortical gray matter and insular cortex; and (3) cerebral and cerebellar white matter.

Methodology

The study utilized T1-weighted MRI data from the FITBIR repository. Initial steps involved harmonizing data to a local dataset, followed by segmenting 132 brain ROIs. After quality assurance and outlier removal, ROI volumes were calculated, z-scored relative to controls, and then subjected to multivariate linear regression to account for sex, age, and total brain volume. Significant differences between TBI and control groups were determined using independent t-tests with False Discovery Rate (FDR) correction. Independent Component Analysis (ICA) and subsequent clustering of component loadings were applied to the TBI group to identify distinct injury patterns.

Key Findings

Significant volumetric differences were found in 37 specific brain regions between individuals with TBI and controls. These differences were clustered into three primary injury patterns: 1) regions comprising the brainstem, occipital pole, and structures posterior to the orbit; 2) areas within the subcortical gray matter and insular cortex; and 3) the white matter of both the cerebrum and cerebellum.

Clinical Impact

The identification of specific TBI injury phenotypes can lead to the development of more precise neuroimaging biomarkers for diagnosis and prognosis. This could enable clinicians to stratify TBI patients based on their distinct structural damage patterns, facilitating the implementation of tailored rehabilitation programs and the targeted development of novel therapeutic interventions for specific TBI subtypes.

Abstract

Traumatic brain injury (TBI) is intrinsically heterogeneous, and typical clinical outcome measures like the Glasgow Coma Scale complicate this diversity. The large variability in severity and patient outcomes render it difficult to link structural damage to functional deficits. The Federal Interagency Traumatic Brain Injury Research (FITBIR) repository contains large-scale multi-site magnetic resonance imaging data of varying resolutions and acquisition parameters (25 shared studies with 7,693 sessions that have age, sex and TBI status defined - 5,811 TBI and 1,882 controls). To reveal shared pathways of injury of TBI through imaging, we analyzed T1-weighted images from these sessions by first harmonizing to a local dataset and segmenting 132 regions of interest (ROIs) in the brain. After running quality assurance, calculating the volumes of the ROIs, and removing outliers, we calculated the z-scores of volumes for all participants relative to the mean and standard deviation of the controls. We regressed out sex, age, and total brain volume with a multivariate linear regression, and we found significant differences in 37 ROIs between subjects with TBI and controls (p < 0.05 with independent t-tests with false discovery rate correction). We found that differences originated in 1) the brainstem, occipital pole and structures posterior to the orbit, 2) subcortical gray matter and insular cortex, and 3) cerebral and cerebellar white matter using independent component analysis and clustering the component loadings of those with TBI.
