Conveying Imagistic Thinking in TCM Translation: A Prompt Engineering and LLM-Based Evaluation Framework

arXiv ID: 2511.23059v1
Published: 2025-11-28
Authors: Jiatong Han
Categories: cs.CL, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.85 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This study addresses the challenge of accurately conveying imagistic thinking, crucial to Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) theory, in English translations, as existing literal renderings hinder clinical application. It introduces a human-in-the-loop (HITL) framework employing prompt engineering with DeepSeek V3.1 to translate fundamental passages from the Huangdi Neijing, focusing on metaphor and metonymy. An LLM-based evaluation, utilizing ChatGPT 5 Pro and Gemini 2.5 Pro simulating real-world readers, found that prompt-adjusted LLM translations consistently outperformed human and baseline LLM translations across five cognitive dimensions.

Medical Relevance

Accurate translation of TCM's imagistic conceptual networks is crucial for clinicians and students to correctly interpret and apply its diagnostic and therapeutic principles, thereby enhancing the efficacy and safety of TCM interventions globally. This work directly addresses a fundamental barrier to understanding and integrating TCM into broader medical practice.

AI Health Application

The study applies AI (specifically LLMs like DeepSeek V3.1, ChatGPT 5 Pro, and Gemini 2.5 Pro) and prompt engineering to translate complex, concept-dense medical texts from Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM). The goal is to improve the accuracy and conceptual clarity of these translations to facilitate the understanding and application of TCM principles in clinical practice. AI is also used to simulate real-world readers to evaluate the cognitive effectiveness of these translations.

Key Points

  • TCM theory is built on imagistic thinking (metaphor/metonymy), which existing literal English translations fail to adequately convey, impeding clinical application.
  • A human-in-the-loop (HITL) framework was developed, guiding DeepSeek V3.1 via prompt engineering to identify and translate imagistic elements in selected Huangdi Neijing passages.
  • Evaluation involved ChatGPT 5 Pro and Gemini 2.5 Pro simulating three types of real-world readers to score human, baseline LLM, and prompt-adjusted LLM translations.
  • Translations were assessed across five cognitive dimensions, with subsequent qualitative analysis through structured interviews and Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis (IPA).
  • Prompt-adjusted LLM translations demonstrated superior performance across all five cognitive dimensions, showing high cross-model and cross-role consistency.
  • Interview themes revealed insights into differences between human and machine translations, effective strategies for metaphor and metonymy transfer, and readers' cognitive preferences.
  • The study proposes a cognitive, efficient, and replicable HITL methodological pathway for translating ancient, concept-dense medical texts like those in TCM.

Methodology

A human-in-the-loop (HITL) framework was used. DeepSeek V3.1 was guided by prompt-based cognitive scaffolding to identify and translate metaphor and metonymy from four fundamental Huangdi Neijing passages. For evaluation, ChatGPT 5 Pro and Gemini 2.5 Pro were prompted to simulate three real-world reader types, scoring human, baseline LLM, and prompt-adjusted LLM translations across five cognitive dimensions. This quantitative scoring was supplemented by structured interviews and Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis (IPA).

Key Findings

The prompt-adjusted LLM translations consistently achieved the best performance across all five cognitive dimensions during evaluation. This superior performance exhibited high consistency across both evaluator models (ChatGPT and Gemini) and simulated reader roles. Qualitative analysis from interviews highlighted key differences between human and machine translations, effective strategies for the nuanced transfer of metaphor and metonymy, and specific cognitive preferences readers hold when engaging with complex TCM concepts.

Clinical Impact

By providing a robust method for conveying the imagistic thinking inherent in TCM, this research can lead to more accurate and clinically actionable translations of foundational TCM texts. This improved understanding directly benefits TCM practitioners, students, and researchers by enabling a deeper comprehension of diagnostic logic and therapeutic principles, potentially enhancing treatment efficacy and patient safety in clinical practice worldwide.

Abstract

Traditional Chinese Medicine theory is built on imagistic thinking, in which medical principles and diagnostic and therapeutic logic are structured through metaphor and metonymy. However, existing English translations largely rely on literal rendering, making it difficult for target-language readers to reconstruct the underlying conceptual networks and apply them in clinical practice. This study adopted a human-in-the-loop framework and selected four passages from the medical canon Huangdi Neijing that are fundamental in theory. Through prompt-based cognitive scaffolding, DeepSeek V3.1 was guided to identify metaphor and metonymy in the source text and convey the theory in translation. In the evaluation stage, ChatGPT 5 Pro and Gemini 2.5 Pro were instructed by prompts to simulate three types of real-world readers. Human translations, baseline model translations, and prompt-adjusted translations were scored by the simulated readers across five cognitive dimensions, followed by structured interviews and Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis. Results show that the prompt-adjusted LLM translations perform best across all five dimensions, with high cross-model and cross-role consistency. The interview themes reveal differences between human and machine translation, effective strategies for metaphor and metonymy transfer, and readers' cognitive preferences. This study provides a cognitive, efficient and replicable HITL methodological pathway for translation of ancient, concept-dense texts like TCM.
