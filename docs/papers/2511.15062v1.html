Interpretable temporal fusion network of multi- and multi-class arrhythmia classification

arXiv ID: 2511.15062v1
Published: 2025-11-19
Authors: Yun Kwan Kim
Categories: cs.LG
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper proposes an interpretable temporal fusion network designed to overcome challenges in arrhythmia classification from electrocardiograms (ECGs) due to varying arrhythmia lengths and onset times. The framework integrates local and global information extraction with attention-based fusion, achieving high accuracy in multi- and multi-class arrhythmia detection, classification, and precise timing of episodes on MIT-BIH databases.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and precisely timed detection of arrhythmias from ECGs is crucial for cardiologists in diagnosis, risk stratification, and developing effective, personalized treatment plans. This research enhances CDSS capabilities, potentially leading to earlier intervention and improved patient outcomes.

AI Health Application

Development of an AI model utilizing interpretable temporal fusion networks for automated, accurate, and precise detection and multi-class classification of cardiac arrhythmias from ECG data, serving as a clinical decision support system to assist cardiologists in diagnosis and subsequent treatment planning.

Key Points

  • Addresses a critical challenge in clinical decision support systems (CDSSs) for arrhythmia: the variable lengths and onset times of arrhythmia episodes on ECGs, which prior methods often overlook.
  • Proposes a novel framework comprising (i) local and global feature extraction and (ii) local-global information fusion with an attention mechanism to process ECG data within a constrained input length effectively.
  • Aims to not only classify arrhythmias but also precisely identify the onset, ending points, and duration of arrhythmia episodes.
  • Evaluated on 10-class and 4-class arrhythmia detection using the MIT-BIH Arrhythmia Database (MITDB) and the MIT-BIH Atrial Fibrillation Database (AFDB).
  • Achieved high F1-scores: on MITDB (Duration: 96.45%, Episode: 82.05%, Dice score: 96.31%) and on AFDB (Duration: 97.57%, Episode: 98.31%, Dice score: 97.45%), demonstrating statistically superior performance compared to benchmark models.
  • Demonstrated strong generalization capabilities through cross-database testing (MITDB-trained model on AFDB, and MIT-BIH malignant ventricular arrhythmia database-trained model on MITDB), outperforming state-of-the-art methods.
  • The method effectively captures both local and global ECG dynamics without significant information loss, leading to more accurate detection and precise temporal localization of arrhythmias.

Methodology

The proposed framework utilizes a temporal fusion network that first performs (i) local and global feature extraction from ECG signals. This is followed by (ii) local-global information fusion incorporating an attention mechanism to effectively integrate features and capture temporal dynamics, enabling classification and precise temporal localization of arrhythmias within constrained input lengths.

Key Findings

The model achieved F1-scores of 96.45% (Duration), 82.05% (Episode), and 96.31% (Dice score) on the MITDB, and 97.57% (Duration), 98.31% (Episode), and 97.45% (Dice score) on the AFDB, for 10-class and 4-class arrhythmia detection. These results were statistically superior to benchmark models, and cross-database generalization tests also showed superior performance compared to state-of-the-art models, indicating robust and accurate performance in detecting and precisely timing arrhythmia episodes.

Clinical Impact

This method enables cardiologists to detect and classify various arrhythmias with higher accuracy and precisely determine their onset, ending points, and duration. This enhanced temporal precision can significantly improve diagnostic confidence, facilitate more timely and targeted therapeutic interventions, and ultimately lead to the development of more accurate and effective patient-specific treatment plans.

Limitations

Abstract

Clinical decision support systems (CDSSs) have been widely utilized to support the decisions made by cardiologists when detecting and classifying arrhythmia from electrocardiograms. However, forming a CDSS for the arrhythmia classification task is challenging due to the varying lengths of arrhythmias. Although the onset time of arrhythmia varies, previously developed methods have not considered such conditions. Thus, we propose a framework that consists of (i) local and global extraction and (ii) local-global information fusion with attention to enable arrhythmia detection and classification within a constrained input length. The framework's performance was evaluated in terms of 10-class and 4-class arrhythmia detection, focusing on identifying the onset and ending point of arrhythmia episodes and their duration using the MIT-BIH arrhythmia database (MITDB) and the MIT-BIH atrial fibrillation database (AFDB). Duration, episode, and Dice score performances resulted in overall F1-scores of 96.45%, 82.05%, and 96.31% on the MITDB and 97.57%, 98.31%, and 97.45% on the AFDB, respectively. The results demonstrated statistically superior performance compared to those of the benchmark models. To assess the generalization capability of the proposed method, an MITDB-trained model and MIT-BIH malignant ventricular arrhythmia database-trained model were tested AFDB and MITDB, respectively. Superior performance was attained compared with that of a state-of-the-art model. The proposed method effectively captures both local and global information and dynamics without significant information loss. Consequently, arrhythmias can be detected with greater accuracy, and their occurrence times can be precisely determined, enabling the clinical field to develop more accurate treatment plans based on the proposed method.
