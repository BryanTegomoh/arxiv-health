Modeling COVID-19 Transmission using IDSIM, an Epidemiological-Modelling Desktop App with Multi-Level Immunization Capabilities

Published: 2021-12-31
Authors: Eleodor Nichita, Mary-Anne Pietrusiak, Fangli Xie, Peter Schwanke, Anjali Pandya

Summary

This paper introduces IDSIM, a native Windows desktop application developed by Ontario Tech University and the Durham Region Health Department, designed for in-house epidemiological modeling of infectious diseases, particularly COVID-19. The app utilizes a multi-stratified compartmental model to simulate various virus variants, vaccination levels, and public health measures. Key findings include the necessity of both natural and vaccination-induced immunity for Delta variant herd immunity, the impact of waning immunity on achieving it, and predictions for an Omicron wave's significant challenges and peak timing based on booster dose administration.

Medical Relevance

This research provides local public health units with an essential in-house tool for real-time epidemiological forecasting and scenario planning, directly supporting evidence-based decision-making for resource allocation, vaccination strategies, and implementation of public health interventions during pandemics.

Key Points

  • IDSIM, a native Windows desktop app, was developed to provide in-house epidemiological modeling capabilities for local public health units.
  • The app implements a multi-stratified compartmental epidemiological model capable of simulating multiple virus variants, different levels of vaccination, and public health measures (e.g., physical distancing, contact tracing, testing).
  • Simulations investigated factors such as vaccination coverage, vaccine effectiveness, waning immunity, the advent of the Omicron variant, and the impact of booster doses on COVID-19 transmission.
  • For the Delta variant, achieving herd immunity was found to require both natural immunity and vaccination-induced immunity.
  • Waning vaccine-induced immunity was shown to prolong the time necessary to reach herd immunity.
  • An Omicron variant-driven wave is predicted to pose significant public health challenges, with infections peaking in approximately two to three months, a timeline dependent on the rate of booster dose administration, assuming no additional public health measures are implemented.
  • The app aims to reduce infections and severe cases by informing public health strategies.

Methodology

The study involved the development of IDSIM, a native Windows desktop application, which is an implementation of a multi-stratified compartmental epidemiological model. Simulations were performed using this app to investigate the effects of various epidemiological factors on COVID-19 transmission within the Durham Region, Canada, including different vaccination scenarios, immunity waning, and variant dynamics (Delta, Omicron).

Key Findings

For the Delta variant, achieving herd immunity necessitates a combination of natural immunity acquired from infection and vaccination-induced immunity. Waning vaccine-induced immunity significantly extends the timeframe required to reach herd immunity. Furthermore, without additional public health measures, an Omicron variant wave is projected to cause significant public health challenges, with predicted infection peaks occurring within two to three months, a duration influenced by the rate of booster dose administration.

Clinical Impact

The IDSIM app offers public health departments a powerful, localized forecasting tool to proactively model disease transmission and potential outcomes. This enables strategic planning for vaccination campaigns, optimal allocation of healthcare resources (e.g., hospital beds, staffing), and timely implementation or adjustment of non-pharmaceutical interventions (e.g., masking, physical distancing), ultimately aiming to mitigate infection rates and severe disease burden.

Abstract

The COVID-19 pandemic has placed unprecedented demands on local public health units in Ontario, Canada, one of which was the need for in-house epidemiological-modelling capabilities. To address this need, Ontario Tech University and the Durham Region Health Department developed a native Windows desktop app that performs epidemiological modelling of infectious diseases. The app is an implementation of a multi-stratified compartmental epidemiological model that can accommodate multiple virus variants and levels of vaccination, as well as public health measures such as physical distancing, contact tracing followed by quarantine, and testing followed by isolation. This article presents the epidemiological model and epidemiological-simulation results obtained using the developed app. The simulations investigate the effects of different factors on COVID-19 transmission in Durham Region, including vaccination coverage, vaccine effectiveness, waning of vaccine-induced immunity, advent of the Omicron variant and effect of COVID-19 booster vaccines in reducing the number of infections and severe cases. Results indicate that, for the Delta variant, natural immunity, in addition to vaccination-induced immunity, is necessary to achieve herd immunity and that waning of vaccine-induced immunity lengthens the time necessary to reach herd immunity. In the absence of additional public health measures, a wave driven by the Omicron variant is predicted to pose significant public health challenges with infections predicted to peak in approximately two to three months, depending on the rate of administration of booster doses.
