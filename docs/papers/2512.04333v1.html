Summary

RGE-GCN is a novel framework combining Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) for classification with a recursive gene elimination strategy for early cancer detection from RNA-seq data. It effectively identifies a compact set of interpretable and predictive gene biomarkers by outperforming standard tools in accuracy and F1-scores, with selected genes aligning with known cancer pathways.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for improving cancer patient survival rates by developing a robust, data-driven method for early cancer detection and biomarker discovery from RNA-seq data, overcoming limitations of conventional approaches.

AI Health Application

This research applies Graph Convolutional Networks, a type of Artificial Intelligence, to analyze RNA-seq data for the purpose of early cancer detection and the identification of predictive and interpretable genetic biomarkers. This directly aids in medical diagnostics and personalized treatment strategies.

Key Points

  • Addresses the challenge of high-dimensional RNA-seq data and complex gene relationships for reliable early cancer biomarker identification.
  • Introduces RGE-GCN, a unified pipeline that integrates feature selection (recursive gene elimination) and classification using Graph Convolutional Networks.
  • Methodology involves building gene expression graphs, classifying cancer vs. normal samples with GCNs, and utilizing Integrated Gradients to score gene importance.
  • Recursively prunes less relevant genes to converge on a compact, highly predictive, and biologically interpretable set of biomarkers.
  • Demonstrated superior performance (higher accuracy and F1-scores) compared to traditional differential expression methods (DESeq2, edgeR, limma-voom) on synthetic and real-world RNA-seq cohorts.
  • Validated on lung, kidney, and cervical cancer datasets, showing consistency across diverse cancer types.
  • Identified genes were biologically significant, aligning with well-known cancer pathways such as PI3K-AKT, MAPK, SUMOylation, and immune regulation.

Methodology

RGE-GCN constructs a graph representing gene expression profiles. A Graph Convolutional Network (GCN) is then used for binary classification (cancer vs. normal). Gene importance is assessed via Integrated Gradients. Through an iterative, recursive process, less informative genes are progressively eliminated, leading to a refined and compact set of highly predictive biomarkers.

Key Findings

RGE-GCN consistently achieved significantly higher accuracy and F1-scores across synthetic and real-world RNA-seq datasets (lung, kidney, cervical cancers) compared to established differential expression tools like DESeq2, edgeR, and limma-voom. Crucially, the genes selected by RGE-GCN were biologically relevant, demonstrating alignment with critical cancer pathways including PI3K-AKT, MAPK, SUMOylation, and immune regulation.

Clinical Impact

This framework offers a promising, generalizable approach for developing advanced diagnostic tools for early cancer detection. By identifying interpretable and robust gene biomarkers, RGE-GCN could facilitate earlier intervention, improved patient stratification, and contribute to personalized treatment strategies, ultimately enhancing clinical outcomes and survival rates in oncology.

Abstract

Early detection of cancer plays a key role in improving survival rates, but identifying reliable biomarkers from RNA-seq data is still a major challenge. The data are high-dimensional, and conventional statistical methods often fail to capture the complex relationships between genes. In this study, we introduce RGE-GCN (Recursive Gene Elimination with Graph Convolutional Networks), a framework that combines feature selection and classification in a single pipeline. Our approach builds a graph from gene expression profiles, uses a Graph Convolutional Network to classify cancer versus normal samples, and applies Integrated Gradients to highlight the most informative genes. By recursively removing less relevant genes, the model converges to a compact set of biomarkers that are both interpretable and predictive. We evaluated RGE-GCN on synthetic data as well as real-world RNA-seq cohorts of lung, kidney, and cervical cancers. Across all datasets, the method consistently achieved higher accuracy and F1-scores than standard tools such as DESeq2, edgeR, and limma-voom. Importantly, the selected genes aligned with well-known cancer pathways including PI3K-AKT, MAPK, SUMOylation, and immune regulation. These results suggest that RGE-GCN shows promise as a generalizable approach for RNA-seq based early cancer detection and biomarker discovery (https://rce-gcn.streamlit.app/ ).
