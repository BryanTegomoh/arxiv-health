Simulating the impact of perception bias on social contact surveys for infectious disease modelling

Summary

This paper investigates how perception biases in social contact surveys, specifically concerning the estimation of others' age and race, impact the accuracy of derived contact patterns and subsequent infectious disease model projections. The study found that such biases significantly reduce the accuracy of contact patterns, leading to a systematic underestimation of cumulative disease incidence in older individuals (65+) and racial minority populations when these biased patterns are used in transmission models.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for enhancing the accuracy of infectious disease modeling and improving public health preparedness. Misleading model projections due to survey biases can result in ineffective interventions and misallocation of resources, potentially exacerbating health disparities in vulnerable populations.

Methodology

The study employed a simulation approach, creating a synthetic contact network representing the population of New Mexico. It then simulated contact surveys within this network, introducing perception biases specifically affecting the estimation of contact age and race. The accuracy of the derived contact patterns was assessed, and these potentially biased patterns were subsequently incorporated into a Susceptible-Infectious-Recovered (SIR) compartmental model to project cumulative disease incidence.

Key Findings

Perception biases in estimating a contact's age and race significantly reduced the accuracy of contact patterns derived from simulated surveys. When these biased patterns were applied to an SIR transmission model, they consistently led to an underestimation of cumulative incidence, particularly among individuals aged 65 and older, and among those identifying as races other than White.

Clinical Impact

The findings highlight a critical challenge in public health data collection and modeling. Inaccurate contact data due to perception biases can lead to flawed infectious disease models, resulting in misguided public health policies, inadequate targeting of interventions, and a failure to address the actual disease burden in older and racial minority populations, potentially widening health disparities.

Limitations

Based on the abstract, a primary limitation is the reliance on a synthetic contact network rather than real-world observational data, which, while useful for controlled simulation, may not fully capture the complex nuances of human social interactions and the myriad factors influencing perception biases in actual surveys.

Abstract

Social contact patterns are a key input to many infectious disease models. Contact surveys, where participants are asked to provide information on their recent close and casual contacts with others, are one of the standard methods to measure contact patterns in a population. Surveys that require detailed sociodemographic descriptions of contacts allow for the specification of fine-grained contact rates between subpopulations in models. However, perception biases affecting a surveyed person's ability to estimate sociodemographic attributes (e.g., age, race, socioeconomic status) of others could affect contact rates derived from survey data. Here, we simulate contact surveys using a synthetic contact network of New Mexico to investigate the impact of these biases on survey accuracy and infectious disease model projections. We found that perception biases affecting the estimation of another individual's age and race substantially decreased the accuracy of the derived contact patterns. Using these biased patterns in a Susceptible-Infectious-Recovered compartmental model lead to an underestimation of cumulative incidence among older people (65+ years) and individuals identifying as races other than White. Our study shows that perception biases can impact contact patterns estimated from surveys in ways that systematically underestimate disease burden in minority populations when used in transmission models.
