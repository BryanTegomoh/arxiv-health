Simulating the impact of perception bias on social contact surveys for infectious disease modelling

Summary

This study simulates contact surveys to investigate how perception biases, specifically in estimating age and race of contacts, affect the accuracy of derived contact patterns used in infectious disease models. It found that these biases substantially decrease accuracy, leading to a systematic underestimation of cumulative incidence among older people and non-White individuals in Susceptible-Infectious-Recovered (SIR) models.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for enhancing the accuracy and equity of infectious disease modeling. Inaccurate contact data due to perception biases can lead to misinformed public health policies and an underestimation of disease burden in vulnerable populations, thus hindering effective interventions.

Key Points

  • Social contact patterns, a critical input for infectious disease models, are commonly derived from contact surveys where participants report recent contacts.
  • The accuracy of contact rates derived from surveys can be compromised by perception biases in estimating sociodemographic attributes (e.g., age, race) of contacts.
  • The study simulated contact surveys on a synthetic contact network representing New Mexico to quantify the impact of such biases.
  • Perception biases affecting the estimation of another individual's age and race significantly decreased the accuracy of the contact patterns derived from these simulated surveys.
  • When these biased contact patterns were incorporated into a Susceptible-Infectious-Recovered (SIR) compartmental model, it resulted in an underestimation of cumulative incidence.
  • Specifically, the cumulative incidence was underestimated among older people (65+ years) and individuals identifying as races other than White.
  • The research concludes that perception biases in contact surveys can systematically underestimate disease burden in minority populations when used in transmission models, potentially leading to health disparities.

Methodology

The study employed a simulation-based approach. It constructed a synthetic contact network for New Mexico and then simulated contact surveys within this network. During these simulated surveys, perception biases were introduced when estimating the age and race of contacts. The accuracy of the derived contact patterns was assessed, and subsequently, these patterns were fed into a Susceptible-Infectious-Recovered (SIR) compartmental model to project infectious disease outcomes.

Key Findings

1. Perception biases in estimating the age and race of contacts substantially reduce the accuracy of contact patterns derived from surveys. 2. Using these biased contact patterns in an SIR model leads to a significant underestimation of cumulative infectious disease incidence. 3. This underestimation disproportionately affects older individuals (65+ years) and non-White populations. 4. The study demonstrates a systematic underestimation of disease burden in minority populations when survey-derived contact data with perception biases are used in transmission models.

Clinical Impact

This research has direct implications for public health practice and clinical epidemiology. It indicates that current infectious disease models relying on contact survey data might systematically underrepresent disease spread and impact in specific demographic groups, particularly older adults and racial minorities. This could lead to misallocation of healthcare resources, insufficient targeting of vaccination campaigns or public health interventions, and an exacerbation of health inequities. It underscores the need for methodological improvements in contact data collection or post-collection data adjustment to correct for perception biases and ensure more equitable and effective disease control strategies.

Abstract

Social contact patterns are a key input to many infectious disease models. Contact surveys, where participants are asked to provide information on their recent close and casual contacts with others, are one of the standard methods to measure contact patterns in a population. Surveys that require detailed sociodemographic descriptions of contacts allow for the specification of fine-grained contact rates between subpopulations in models. However, perception biases affecting a surveyed person's ability to estimate sociodemographic attributes (e.g., age, race, socioeconomic status) of others could affect contact rates derived from survey data. Here, we simulate contact surveys using a synthetic contact network of New Mexico to investigate the impact of these biases on survey accuracy and infectious disease model projections. We found that perception biases affecting the estimation of another individual's age and race substantially decreased the accuracy of the derived contact patterns. Using these biased patterns in a Susceptible-Infectious-Recovered compartmental model lead to an underestimation of cumulative incidence among older people (65+ years) and individuals identifying as races other than White. Our study shows that perception biases can impact contact patterns estimated from surveys in ways that systematically underestimate disease burden in minority populations when used in transmission models.
