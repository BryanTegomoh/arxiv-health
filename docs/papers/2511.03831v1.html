Higher-Order Causal Structure Learning with Additive Models

arXiv ID: 2511.03831v1
Published: 2025-11-05
Authors: James Enouen, Yujia Zheng, Ignavier Ng, Yan Liu, Kun Zhang
Categories: cs.LG, math.ST, stat.ML, stat.TH

View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper extends the Causal Additive Model (CAM) to incorporate higher-order interactions in causal structure learning, representing these complex relationships using directed acyclic hypergraphs (hyper DAGs). It introduces the theoretical framework, identifiability results for these novel structures, and an extended greedy CAM algorithm, demonstrating that learning such complex structures can lead to improved empirical performance in synthetic experiments.

Medical Relevance

Understanding complex disease etiologies, drug-drug interactions, and multi-gene regulatory networks often requires discerning higher-order causal relationships. This work provides a theoretical and algorithmic framework to uncover such intricate mechanisms, moving beyond simple pairwise causal links, which is crucial for precision medicine and systems biology.

AI Health Application

This method could be applied in medical AI to: 1) Identify complex gene-gene or gene-environment interactions contributing to disease susceptibility, progression, or treatment response. 2) Discover higher-order drug-drug or drug-target interactions, leading to better drug combinations, personalized medicine strategies, and prediction of adverse effects. 3) Unravel complex causal pathways in multi-factorial diseases, aiding in diagnosis, prognosis, and therapeutic target identification. 4) Model intricate biological networks relevant to biosecurity, such as understanding pathogen virulence factors or host immune responses through interaction analysis.

Key Points

  • Addresses the lack of explicit treatment for higher-order interactions in causal discovery, which are prevalent in real-world processes.
  • Extends the Causal Additive Model (CAM) to additive models capable of learning and representing higher-order causal interactions.
  • Introduces the directed acyclic hypergraph (hyper DAG) as a novel representational structure for these higher-order causal mechanisms, extending the traditional DAG.
  • Provides necessary theoretical definitions, tools, and identifiability results for hyper DAGs, including an extension of typical Markov equivalence classes.
  • Argues that learning these more complex hypergraph structures can empirically lead to better causal insights, despite the increased complexity.
  • Notes that simpler CAM-like assumptions correspond to easier-to-learn hyper DAGs and better finite sample complexity, implicitly highlighting challenges for truly higher-order learning.
  • Develops an extension of the greedy CAM algorithm capable of searching the more complex hyper DAG space and demonstrates its effectiveness in synthetic experiments.

Methodology

The methodology involves developing a novel theoretical framework by introducing directed acyclic hypergraphs (hyper DAGs) to represent higher-order causal interactions. It includes defining necessary mathematical tools and establishing identifiability conditions for these structures, extending existing concepts like Markov equivalence classes. Furthermore, the authors adapt and extend the greedy CAM algorithm to navigate the complex search space of hyper DAGs, validating its empirical performance through synthetic experiments.

Key Findings

The paper introduces a principled way to incorporate higher-order interactions into causal structure learning using hyper DAGs. It establishes theoretical identifiability for these structures and demonstrates that an extended greedy CAM algorithm can effectively learn them. A key insight is that while more complex, accounting for higher-order interactions can lead to empirically better causal models, although simpler structures might offer better finite sample complexity.

Clinical Impact

This research could significantly advance our ability to model complex biological systems, allowing for the discovery of previously hidden synergistic or antagonistic effects among multiple genes, proteins, drugs, or environmental factors. This could lead to the identification of novel therapeutic targets, more accurate prognoses, personalized treatment regimens that account for complex patient profiles, and a deeper understanding of multi-factorial diseases like cancer, diabetes, or autoimmune disorders.

Limitations

The explicit empirical validation is limited to synthetic experiments, meaning its performance on real-world, high-dimensional biological or clinical data remains to be fully demonstrated. The abstract also implicitly suggests that while complex structures may yield better empirical results, they might come with challenges regarding finite sample complexity compared to simpler models.

Future Directions

Although not explicitly stated as 'future directions,' the paper's conclusion regarding empirical usefulness in synthetic experiments strongly implies the next step is to apply and validate the extended greedy CAM algorithm and the hyper DAG framework on diverse real-world medical and biological datasets to assess its practical utility and scalability.

Abstract

Causal structure learning has long been the central task of inferring causal insights from data. Despite the abundance of real-world processes exhibiting higher-order mechanisms, however, an explicit treatment of interactions in causal discovery has received little attention. In this work, we focus on extending the causal additive model (CAM) to additive models with higher-order interactions. This second level of modularity we introduce to the structure learning problem is most easily represented by a directed acyclic hypergraph which extends the DAG. We introduce the necessary definitions and theoretical tools to handle the novel structure we introduce and then provide identifiability results for the hyper DAG, extending the typical Markov equivalence classes. We next provide insights into why learning the more complex hypergraph structure may actually lead to better empirical results. In particular, more restrictive assumptions like CAM correspond to easier-to-learn hyper DAGs and better finite sample complexity. We finally develop an extension of the greedy CAM algorithm which can handle the more complex hyper DAG search space and demonstrate its empirical usefulness in synthetic experiments.
