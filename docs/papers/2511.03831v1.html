Higher-Order Causal Structure Learning with Additive Models

arXiv ID: 2511.03831v1
Published: 2025-11-05
Authors: James Enouen, Yujia Zheng, Ignavier Ng, Yan Liu, Kun Zhang
Categories: cs.LG, math.ST, stat.ML, stat.TH

Summary

This paper addresses the gap in causal structure learning by explicitly treating higher-order interactions, extending the Causal Additive Model (CAM) to additive models with these complex mechanisms. It introduces a novel Directed Acyclic Hypergraph (Hyper DAG) representation and provides theoretical tools, identifiability results, and an extended greedy algorithm for learning these structures, demonstrating its empirical utility in synthetic experiments.

Medical Relevance

Many biological and disease processes involve intricate, multi-factor interactions (e.g., gene-environment interactions, drug synergies). This methodology provides a powerful framework to explicitly discover and model these higher-order causal mechanisms, which is critical for understanding disease etiology, identifying complex therapeutic targets, and advancing personalized medicine.

AI Health Application

Medical AI applications could leverage this research to develop more sophisticated and interpretable models for: 1) identifying complex causal pathways in disease development by uncovering higher-order interactions between genetic, environmental, and lifestyle factors; 2) predicting drug efficacy and potential adverse events by modeling intricate drug-drug or drug-gene interactions; 3) personalizing treatment strategies by understanding how multiple patient-specific factors (e.g., biomarkers, comorbidities) interact to influence therapeutic outcomes; 4) improving diagnostic accuracy by modeling complex relationships between various diagnostic indicators; and 5) enhancing explainable AI in healthcare by providing a deeper, mechanistic understanding of how factors collectively cause health outcomes, moving beyond simple correlations.

Key Points

  • Addresses the significant challenge of explicitly incorporating higher-order interactions into causal structure learning, which are prevalent in real-world systems.
  • Proposes an extension of the Causal Additive Model (CAM) to additive models capable of representing and learning these higher-order causal interactions.
  • Introduces the Directed Acyclic Hypergraph (Hyper DAG) as a novel graphical representation to model these complex interaction structures, extending the traditional DAG framework.
  • Provides foundational theoretical definitions, tools, and identifiability results for the Hyper DAG, thereby expanding the understanding of Markov equivalence classes in this context.
  • Argues that learning more complex hypergraph structures, despite their intricacy, can lead to superior empirical results, with simpler models like CAM corresponding to easier-to-learn Hyper DAGs with better finite sample complexity.
  • Develops an adapted greedy CAM algorithm specifically designed to navigate and discover causal structures within the complex search space of Hyper DAGs.
  • Demonstrates the empirical usefulness and effectiveness of the proposed methodology and algorithm through controlled synthetic experiments.

Methodology

The methodology involves a theoretical extension of the Causal Additive Model (CAM) to incorporate higher-order interactions, represented by a novel Directed Acyclic Hypergraph (Hyper DAG). This includes establishing mathematical definitions, developing theoretical tools, and deriving identifiability results for the Hyper DAG. An extended version of the greedy CAM algorithm is then developed to search this complex Hyper DAG space, and its effectiveness is validated through synthetic experiments.

Key Findings

The paper establishes a robust theoretical framework for causal structure learning with higher-order interactions using Hyper DAGs, including novel identifiability conditions. It provides a principled justification that, despite increased complexity, learning these higher-order structures can lead to better empirical causal insights. Furthermore, it delivers a practical algorithmic solution (an extended greedy CAM algorithm) capable of navigating this new structural space, validated through synthetic experiments.

Clinical Impact

By uncovering previously hidden higher-order causal interactions, this research has the potential to profoundly impact clinical practice. It could lead to the identification of novel, synergistic drug targets, more accurate prediction models for complex diseases based on multiple interacting risk factors, and the development of highly personalized treatment regimens that account for the intricate interplay of biological pathways and patient characteristics.

Limitations

The primary limitation noted is that the empirical usefulness of the proposed method is currently demonstrated in synthetic experiments, indicating a need for extensive validation on real-world, potentially noisy and high-dimensional medical and biological datasets. The discussion also implies that while more complex hypergraph structures can yield better results, they may present increased challenges in terms of finite sample complexity compared to simpler models like CAM.

Abstract

Causal structure learning has long been the central task of inferring causal insights from data. Despite the abundance of real-world processes exhibiting higher-order mechanisms, however, an explicit treatment of interactions in causal discovery has received little attention. In this work, we focus on extending the causal additive model (CAM) to additive models with higher-order interactions. This second level of modularity we introduce to the structure learning problem is most easily represented by a directed acyclic hypergraph which extends the DAG. We introduce the necessary definitions and theoretical tools to handle the novel structure we introduce and then provide identifiability results for the hyper DAG, extending the typical Markov equivalence classes. We next provide insights into why learning the more complex hypergraph structure may actually lead to better empirical results. In particular, more restrictive assumptions like CAM correspond to easier-to-learn hyper DAGs and better finite sample complexity. We finally develop an extension of the greedy CAM algorithm which can handle the more complex hyper DAG search space and demonstrate its empirical usefulness in synthetic experiments.
