Alzheimers Disease Progression Prediction Based on Manifold Mapping of Irregularly Sampled Longitudinal Data

Published: 2025-11-25
Authors: Xin Hong, Ying Shi, Yinhao Li, Yen-Wei Chen
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces R-TNAG, a novel framework designed to predict Alzheimer's disease (AD) progression from irregularly sampled longitudinal structural MRI (sMRI) data. By projecting sMRI features into a Riemannian manifold and employing a Time-aware Neural ODE alongside an Attention-based Riemannian Gated Recurrent Unit, the model effectively captures the intrinsic geometry and continuous evolution of disease. Experimental results demonstrate that R-TNAG consistently outperforms state-of-the-art methods in both disease status prediction and cognitive score regression, showing robust performance across varying data conditions.

Medical Relevance

This research significantly enhances the accuracy and robustness of Alzheimer's disease progression prediction using real-world, often irregular, longitudinal imaging data, which is vital for enabling earlier and more personalized interventions, improving patient stratification for clinical trials, and optimizing long-term care planning.

AI Health Application

The AI model is designed to predict the progression of Alzheimer's Disease (AD) and individual cognitive decline based on longitudinal sMRI data. This application can assist clinicians in: 1) early and more accurate diagnosis or staging of AD, 2) predicting future disease trajectory for better patient management and personalized treatment planning, 3) monitoring treatment efficacy, and 4) facilitating clinical trial design by identifying patients at higher risk of progression.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical challenge of modeling AD progression from irregularly sampled longitudinal sMRI data, a common issue in clinical settings.
  • Proposes the R-TNAG framework, comprising a Riemannian manifold mapping, a Time-aware Neural Ordinary Differential Equation (TNODE), and an Attention-based Riemannian Gated Recurrent Unit (ARGRU).
  • Utilizes Riemannian manifold mapping to project high-dimensional sMRI features into a non-Euclidean space, preserving the intrinsic continuity and nonlinear geometric structure of disease progression.
  • Employs TNODE to model the continuous evolution of latent disease states between discrete, irregularly observed time points, enhancing temporal consistency.
  • Integrates ARGRU to adaptively weigh and integrate historical and current information, specifically designed to manage and leverage data from irregular observation intervals.
  • Achieves superior performance compared to state-of-the-art models in two key predictive tasks: AD disease status classification and cognitive score regression.
  • Demonstrates robust and stable performance across varying sequence lengths and missing data rates, with strong temporal generalizability and confirmed applicability through cross-dataset validation.

Methodology

The proposed R-TNAG framework first extracts features from high-dimensional sMRI data and maps them into a Riemannian manifold space to capture the intrinsic, non-linear geometric structure of disease progression. On this manifold representation, a Time-aware Neural Ordinary Differential Equation (TNODE) is utilized to model the continuous evolution of latent disease states between irregularly sampled observations. Simultaneously, an Attention-based Riemannian Gated Recurrent Unit (ARGRU) is employed to adaptively integrate historical and current information, specifically designed to handle and leverage data despite irregular observation intervals. This joint design aims to provide robust AD trajectory prediction with improved temporal consistency.

Key Findings

The R-TNAG method consistently outperformed existing state-of-the-art models in both AD disease status prediction (classification) and cognitive score regression. Ablation studies confirmed the individual contributions and complementary roles of each module (Riemannian manifold mapping, TNODE, and ARGRU) in enhancing overall predictive accuracy. Furthermore, the model exhibited strong temporal generalizability, maintaining stable performance across varying sequence lengths and different rates of missing data. Its robustness and applicability in diverse clinical scenarios were further validated through cross-dataset experimentation.

Abstract

The uncertainty of clinical examinations frequently leads to irregular observation intervals in longitudinal imaging data, posing challenges for modeling disease progression.Most existing imaging-based disease prediction models operate in Euclidean space, which assumes a flat representation of data and fails to fully capture the intrinsic continuity and nonlinear geometric structure of irregularly sampled longitudinal images. To address the challenge of modeling Alzheimers disease (AD) progression from irregularly sampled longitudinal structural Magnetic Resonance Imaging (sMRI) data, we propose a Riemannian manifold mapping, a Time-aware manifold Neural ordinary differential equation, and an Attention-based riemannian Gated recurrent unit (R-TNAG) framework. Our approach first projects features extracted from high-dimensional sMRI into a manifold space to preserve the intrinsic geometry of disease progression. On this representation, a time-aware Neural Ordinary Differential Equation (TNODE) models the continuous evolution of latent states between observations, while an Attention-based Riemannian Gated Recurrent Unit (ARGRU) adaptively integrates historical and current information to handle irregular intervals. This joint design improves temporal consistency and yields robust AD trajectory prediction under irregular sampling.Experimental results demonstrate that the proposed method consistently outperforms state-of-the-art models in both disease status prediction and cognitive score regression. Ablation studies verify the contributions of each module, highlighting their complementary roles in enhancing predictive accuracy. Moreover, the model exhibits stable performance across varying sequence lengths and missing data rates, indicating strong temporal generalizability. Cross-dataset validation further confirms its robustness and applicability in diverse clinical settings.
