Transformer-Based Scalable Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning for Networked Systems with Long-Range Interactions

Published: 2025-11-17
Authors: Vidur Sinha, Muhammed Ustaomeroglu, Guannan Qu

Summary

This paper introduces STACCA (Shared Transformer Actor-Critic with Counterfactual Advantage), a novel transformer-based Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) framework addressing key limitations in network control. STACCA effectively captures long-range dependencies and achieves generalizability across diverse network topologies, utilizing a Graph Transformer Actor-Critic architecture and an improved credit assignment mechanism. Evaluated on epidemic containment and rumor spreading, it demonstrates superior performance, network generalization, and scalability.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a powerful, scalable, and generalizable framework for modeling and controlling complex dynamic processes within networked systems, offering direct applicability to critical public health challenges. It can enhance our ability to predict, contain, and manage health crises like infectious disease outbreaks more effectively.

AI Health Application

The research proposes a transformer-based multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) framework designed to manage and control complex networked systems with long-range interactions. Its direct application to 'epidemic containment' means this AI can be used for public health interventions, such as optimizing resource allocation, deploying control measures (e.g., quarantines, vaccine distribution, contact tracing efforts), or advising policy decisions during disease outbreaks to minimize spread and impact. It falls under the umbrella of AI for public health and biosecurity response.

Key Points

  • Addresses two major limitations of existing MARL: inability to capture long-range dependencies (e.g., cascading failures, epidemic outbreaks) and lack of generalizability across network topologies.
  • Introduces STACCA, a unified transformer-based MARL framework leveraging Graph Transformers for both actor and critic components.
  • Employs a centralized Graph Transformer Critic to model long-range interactions and provide system-level feedback, crucial for complex network dynamics.
  • Utilizes a shared Graph Transformer Actor that learns a generalizable policy capable of adapting to diverse network structures without requiring retraining.
  • Integrates a novel counterfactual advantage estimator compatible with state-value critic estimates to improve credit assignment during training, enhancing learning efficiency and accuracy.
  • Validated on critical network control tasks, specifically epidemic containment and rumor spreading, demonstrating improved performance over existing methods.
  • Achieves significant advancements in network generalization and scalability, highlighting the potential of transformer-based MARL for large-scale, dynamic networked systems.

Methodology

The authors developed STACCA, a transformer-based Multi-Agent Reinforcement Learning (MARL) framework. It employs a centralized Graph Transformer Critic to model long-range dependencies and provide system-level feedback, coupled with a shared Graph Transformer Actor for learning generalizable policies across varied network topologies. A novel counterfactual advantage estimator is integrated to improve credit assignment during the training process, enhancing its compatibility with state-value critic estimates.

Key Findings

STACCA demonstrates significantly improved performance, superior network generalization across diverse topologies, and enhanced scalability when applied to complex networked systems. These benefits were empirically validated through its successful application in epidemic containment and rumor-spreading network control tasks, proving its capability to manage long-range interactions and adapt to new network structures.

Clinical Impact

The STACCA framework could enable the development of highly adaptive and predictive control strategies for public health interventions. This includes optimizing resource allocation during infectious disease outbreaks, designing robust vaccination or mitigation strategies in dynamic populations, and informing real-time decision-making to contain epidemics, ultimately leading to more effective disease management and better public health outcomes.

Limitations

Existing MARL methods typically rely on assumptions of local agent interaction decay, which limits their capacity to capture crucial long-range dependencies such as those seen in cascading power failures or large-scale epidemic outbreaks. Furthermore, most conventional approaches lack generalizability across different network topologies, necessitating extensive retraining when applied to new or evolving network graphs.

Future Directions

The results highlight the significant potential for transformer-based MARL architectures to achieve scalable and generalizable control in a broader range of large-scale networked systems. Future work could involve extending STACCA's application to other critical healthcare and public health network challenges, further exploring its robustness in dynamic and evolving network environments.

Abstract

Multi-agent reinforcement learning (MARL) has shown promise for large-scale network control, yet existing methods face two major limitations. First, they typically rely on assumptions leading to decay properties of local agent interactions, limiting their ability to capture long-range dependencies such as cascading power failures or epidemic outbreaks. Second, most approaches lack generalizability across network topologies, requiring retraining when applied to new graphs. We introduce STACCA (Shared Transformer Actor-Critic with Counterfactual Advantage), a unified transformer-based MARL framework that addresses both challenges. STACCA employs a centralized Graph Transformer Critic to model long-range dependencies and provide system-level feedback, while its shared Graph Transformer Actor learns a generalizable policy capable of adapting across diverse network structures. Further, to improve credit assignment during training, STACCA integrates a novel counterfactual advantage estimator that is compatible with state-value critic estimates. We evaluate STACCA on epidemic containment and rumor-spreading network control tasks, demonstrating improved performance, network generalization, and scalability. These results highlight the potential of transformer-based MARL architectures to achieve scalable and generalizable control in large-scale networked systems.
