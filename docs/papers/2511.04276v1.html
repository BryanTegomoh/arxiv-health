Summary

This study employs a predator-prey mathematical framework to model the interactions between *Aedes* vectors and dengue pathogens, demonstrating how vector competence (vc) is a primary determinant of within-vector infection dynamics and endemic disease persistence. It reveals a fundamental biological trade-off in tropical/subtropical environments where high vector competence cannot be offset by enhanced innate immune capacity, leading to inherent instability in dengue transmission dynamics.

Medical Relevance

This research provides crucial insights into the fundamental biological mechanisms governing dengue persistence and transmission instability, which is vital for developing more effective public health interventions and targeted vector control strategies.

This research, while a mathematical modeling study and not using AI itself, provides critical biological and epidemiological insights (e.g., vector competence dynamics, conditions for endemic persistence, evolutionary constraints) that can inform and enhance AI and machine learning models for: 1) Predicting dengue outbreaks and identifying high-risk areas. 2) Optimizing resource allocation for vector control strategies. 3) Developing early warning systems for dengue epidemics. 4) Simulating the impact of different intervention strategies and climate change on disease spread.

  • Vector competence (vc) is identified as a critical parameter driving within-vector infection dynamics and the endemic persistence of dengue.
  • The model demonstrates that endemic dengue conditions emerge naturally from these biological interactions, and their global stability is mathematically proven.
  • A fundamental biological trade-off is observed: vectors can evolve increased transmission potential (high vc) but are constrained in their ability to enhance innate immune capacity, especially under tropical/subtropical environmental pressures.

  • The inability to concurrently enhance immune capacity alongside transmission potential limits the vector's evolutionary response to pathogen-driven selection.

Methodology

Key Findings

Dengue's endemic persistence arises naturally from the biological interplay between *Aedes* vectors and the dengue pathogen, largely driven by vector competence. A critical finding is a biological trade-off: under environmental pressures, vectors can increase their transmission potential (vector competence) but cannot concomitantly enhance their innate immune system to offset this, leading to inherent instability in disease transmission.

Understanding this trade-off could lead to more nuanced approaches to disease surveillance, outbreak prediction, and the development of novel interventions that account for the vector's biological limitations.

Abstract

Dengue continues to pose a major global threat, infecting nearly 390 million people annually. Recognizing the pivotal role of vector competence (vc), recent research focuses on mosquito parameters to inform transmission modeling and vector control strategies.This study models interactions between Aedes vectors and dengue pathogens, highlighting vc as a key driver of within vector infection dynamics and endemic persistence. Using a predator prey framework, we show that endemic conditions emerge naturally from the biological interplay between the vectors strategies to pathogen pressure and we prove global stability of such conditions. Our results reveal that under tropical and subtropical environmental pressures, the innate immune system of vectors cannot offset high vc during endemic outbreaks, highlighting a fundamental biological trade off, vectors can evolve increased transmission potential but cannot enhance immune capacity. This constraint defines the limits of their evolutionary response to pathogen driven selection and drives instability in disease transmission dynamics.
