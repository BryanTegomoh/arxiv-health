Summary

This study employs a predator-prey mathematical framework to model Aedes mosquito-dengue virus interactions, identifying vector competence (vc) as a critical driver of disease persistence. It demonstrates that while endemic conditions are globally stable, the vector's innate immune system is overwhelmed by high vc in tropical/subtropical environments, revealing an evolutionary trade-off that limits immune enhancement and drives transmission instability.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a fundamental understanding of dengue's endemic persistence and the biological limits of its vector, offering crucial insights for developing more effective, biologically-informed vector control strategies and refining epidemiological transmission models to improve public health interventions.

The study utilized a mathematical modeling approach, specifically employing a predator-prey framework, to simulate the dynamic interactions between Aedes vectors and dengue pathogens. The methodology also included analytical proofs to establish the global stability of the identified endemic conditions.

Key Findings

Endemic dengue conditions naturally arise from vector-pathogen interactions and are globally stable. However, under the high pathogen pressure characteristic of tropical/subtropical settings, the high vector competence (vc) of Aedes mosquitoes overwhelms their innate immune systems. This reveals a fundamental biological trade-off where vectors can evolve increased transmission potential but cannot comparably enhance immune capacity, ultimately driving instability in disease transmission dynamics.

Clinical Impact

The findings offer a deeper mechanistic understanding of dengue transmission, which can inform the development of more targeted and effective vector control strategies by focusing on factors influencing vector competence. This can also lead to more accurate predictive models for dengue outbreaks, improving public health planning, resource allocation, and timely interventions.

Abstract

Dengue continues to pose a major global threat, infecting nearly 390 million people annually. Recognizing the pivotal role of vector competence (vc), recent research focuses on mosquito parameters to inform transmission modeling and vector control strategies.This study models interactions between Aedes vectors and dengue pathogens, highlighting vc as a key driver of within vector infection dynamics and endemic persistence. Using a predator prey framework, we show that endemic conditions emerge naturally from the biological interplay between the vectors strategies to pathogen pressure and we prove global stability of such conditions. Our results reveal that under tropical and subtropical environmental pressures, the innate immune system of vectors cannot offset high vc during endemic outbreaks, highlighting a fundamental biological trade off, vectors can evolve increased transmission potential but cannot enhance immune capacity. This constraint defines the limits of their evolutionary response to pathogen driven selection and drives instability in disease transmission dynamics.
