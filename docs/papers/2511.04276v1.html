Summary

This study models dengue transmission using a predator-prey framework, demonstrating that endemic conditions naturally arise from vector-pathogen interactions and are globally stable. It identifies vector competence (vc) as a key driver of disease persistence, revealing a fundamental biological trade-off where vectors cannot enhance immune capacity to offset high vc in endemic tropical/subtropical environments, leading to unstable transmission dynamics.

Medical Relevance

This research deepens the understanding of the underlying biological and evolutionary mechanisms driving dengue endemicity and transmission instability. These insights are crucial for developing more effective, biologically-informed vector control strategies and public health interventions against this persistent global health threat.

  • Vector competence (vc) is identified as a critical determinant driving within-vector infection dynamics and the endemic persistence of dengue.
  • The model demonstrates that endemic conditions for dengue naturally emerge from the biological interplay between vector strategies and pathogen pressure, and these conditions are globally stable.

Methodology

The study utilized a mathematical modeling approach, specifically a predator-prey framework, to simulate the dynamic interactions between Aedes vectors and dengue pathogens. This framework was used to analyze within-vector infection dynamics, determine the emergence of endemic conditions, and prove the global stability of such conditions.

Key Findings

The study found that vector competence (vc) is a key driver of within-vector infection dynamics and endemic dengue persistence. Endemic conditions emerge naturally from vector-pathogen interactions and exhibit global stability. Critically, under tropical/subtropical conditions, the vector's innate immune system cannot offset high vc during outbreaks, revealing a biological trade-off: vectors can evolve increased transmission potential but not enhanced immune capacity. This constraint limits evolutionary responses and drives instability in transmission dynamics.

Clinical Impact

By elucidating the fundamental biological trade-offs and mechanisms governing dengue persistence and instability, this research offers critical insights for designing more targeted and effective vector control strategies. Understanding the limits of vector immune evolution suggests that interventions focused on modulating vector competence or disrupting this specific trade-off could be more impactful than broad-spectrum approaches, potentially leading to more stable disease control and reduced endemic outbreaks.

Abstract

Dengue continues to pose a major global threat, infecting nearly 390 million people annually. Recognizing the pivotal role of vector competence (vc), recent research focuses on mosquito parameters to inform transmission modeling and vector control strategies.This study models interactions between Aedes vectors and dengue pathogens, highlighting vc as a key driver of within vector infection dynamics and endemic persistence. Using a predator prey framework, we show that endemic conditions emerge naturally from the biological interplay between the vectors strategies to pathogen pressure and we prove global stability of such conditions. Our results reveal that under tropical and subtropical environmental pressures, the innate immune system of vectors cannot offset high vc during endemic outbreaks, highlighting a fundamental biological trade off, vectors can evolve increased transmission potential but cannot enhance immune capacity. This constraint defines the limits of their evolutionary response to pathogen driven selection and drives instability in disease transmission dynamics.
