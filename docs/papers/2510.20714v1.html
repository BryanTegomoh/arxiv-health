Optimizing Clinical Fall Risk Prediction: A Data-Driven Integration of EHR Variables with the Johns Hopkins Fall Risk Assessment Tool

Published: 2025-10-23
Authors: Fardin Ganjkhanloo, Emmett Springer, Erik H. Hoyer, Daniel L. Young, Kimia Ghobadi

Summary

This study optimized clinical fall risk prediction by integrating Electronic Health Record (EHR) variables with the Johns Hopkins Fall Risk Assessment Tool (JHFRAT) using a data-driven constrained score optimization (CSO) model. The CSO model significantly improved predictive performance (AUC-ROC=0.91) compared to the standard JHFRAT (AUC-ROC=0.86), offering a robust and interpretable method for enhancing inpatient fall prevention.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to patient safety and quality improvement in hospital settings, as it offers an enhanced, data-driven approach to accurately identify patients at high risk of falls, thereby enabling more targeted and effective fall prevention interventions and resource allocation.

AI Health Application

The paper applies machine learning models, specifically constrained score optimization (CSO) and XGBoost, to electronic health record (EHR) variables and existing clinical assessment data (JHFRAT) to optimize and improve the accuracy of inpatient fall risk prediction. This is a direct application of AI to enhance a medical assessment tool, support clinical decision-making, and improve patient safety protocols within healthcare settings.

Key Points

  • The study aimed to improve fall risk prediction beyond the JHFRAT by incorporating additional clinically relevant EHR variables.
  • A retrospective analysis was conducted on 54,209 inpatient admissions from three Johns Hopkins Health System hospitals (March 2022 - October 2023).
  • Constrained Score Optimization (CSO) models were used for risk prediction, prioritizing interpretability and clinical knowledge integration.
  • The CSO model demonstrated a significant improvement in predictive performance (AUC-ROC=0.91) compared to the current JHFRAT (AUC-ROC=0.86).
  • Surprisingly, the CSO model's performance was similar with and without the inclusion of additional EHR variables.
  • While a black-box XGBoost model achieved a higher AUC-ROC (0.94), the CSO model showed greater robustness to variations in risk labeling.
  • The findings provide a robust, evidence-based foundation for health systems to enhance inpatient fall prevention protocols and patient safety through data-driven optimization.

Methodology

A retrospective analysis of 54,209 inpatient admissions was performed. Constrained Score Optimization (CSO) models, designed to incorporate clinical knowledge and maintain interpretability, were developed using JHFRAT assessment data and additional EHR variables. The performance of the CSO models was compared against the standard JHFRAT and a benchmark XGBoost (black-box) model using AUC-ROC.

Key Findings

The CSO model significantly improved fall risk prediction (AUC-ROC=0.91) over the current JHFRAT (AUC-ROC=0.86). Interestingly, the additional EHR variables did not substantially improve the CSO model's performance. Although XGBoost achieved a higher AUC-ROC (0.94), the CSO model demonstrated superior robustness to variations in risk labeling, balancing predictive power with interpretability and stability.

Clinical Impact

The enhanced fall risk prediction model offers health systems a more accurate and robust tool to identify high-risk patients. This can lead to more effective and targeted fall prevention strategies, better allocation of healthcare resources, and ultimately, a reduction in inpatient falls, thereby improving patient safety and healthcare outcomes.

Limitations

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract, however, the study is limited to data from Johns Hopkins Health System hospitals, which may affect generalizability to other healthcare systems. The abstract also notes a trade-off where the CSO model (knowledge-based) has lower raw predictive power (AUC-ROC) compared to a black-box model (XGBoost) but offers higher robustness and interpretability.

Future Directions

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract beyond stating the approach provides a robust foundation for health systems to enhance fall prevention protocols. Future work could involve external validation in diverse healthcare settings and prospective studies to evaluate the real-world impact of implementing this optimized tool.

Abstract

In this study we aim to better align fall risk prediction from the Johns Hopkins Fall Risk Assessment Tool (JHFRAT) with additional clinically meaningful measures via a data-driven modelling approach. We conducted a retrospective analysis of 54,209 inpatient admissions from three Johns Hopkins Health System hospitals between March 2022 and October 2023. A total of 20,208 admissions were included as high fall risk encounters, and 13,941 were included as low fall risk encounters. To incorporate clinical knowledge and maintain interpretability, we employed constrained score optimization (CSO) models on JHFRAT assessment data and additional electronic health record (EHR) variables. The model demonstrated significant improvements in predictive performance over the current JHFRAT (CSO AUC-ROC=0.91, JHFRAT AUC-ROC=0.86). The constrained score optimization models performed similarly with and without the EHR variables. Although the benchmark black-box model (XGBoost), improves upon the performance metrics of the knowledge-based constrained logistic regression (AUC-ROC=0.94), the CSO demonstrates more robustness to variations in risk labelling. This evidence-based approach provides a robust foundation for health systems to systematically enhance inpatient fall prevention protocols and patient safety using data-driven optimization techniques, contributing to improved risk assessment and resource allocation in healthcare settings.
