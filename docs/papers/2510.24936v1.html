Summary

This paper introduces IBIS, a novel hybrid architecture integrating Inception-BiLSTM with a Support Vector Machine (SVM), specifically designed to overcome the pervasive problem of overfitting in Wi-Fi sensing for Human Activity Recognition (HAR). By applying this robust approach to Doppler-derived data, IBIS achieves nearly 99% accuracy in movement recognition, demonstrating significantly improved model generalization and robust classification boundaries. The solution offers a low-cost, non-intrusive method with high potential for applications in healthcare.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a highly accurate and robust method for non-intrusive human activity and movement recognition using standard Wi-Fi signals, which is critically important for healthcare applications like continuous patient monitoring, fall detection, rehabilitation assessment, and ambient assisted living without the need for wearable or intrusive sensors.

AI Health Application

The research presents a novel AI (machine learning) architecture, IBIS, designed for highly accurate and robust human activity/movement recognition using Wi-Fi sensing. This AI application can be used to non-intrusively monitor patients in homes or healthcare facilities, enabling tasks such as fall detection for the elderly, tracking recovery post-surgery, assessing mobility for rehabilitation, and general behavioral analysis for health management.

The Inception-BiLSTM component likely handles the feature extraction and temporal sequence learning from Doppler-derived Wi-Fi sensing data, while the SVM acts as a final classification layer, aiming to create more robust decision boundaries and mitigate overfitting.

Abstract

The increasing interest in Wi-Fi sensing stems from its potential to capture environmental data in a low-cost, non-intrusive way, making it ideal for applications like healthcare, space occupancy analysis, and gesture-based IoT control. However, a major limitation in this field is the common problem of overfitting, where models perform well on training data but fail to generalize to new data. To overcome this, we introduce a novel hybrid architecture that integrates Inception-BiLSTM with a Support Vector Machine (SVM), which we refer to as IBIS. Our IBIS approach is uniquely engineered to improve model generalization and create more robust classification boundaries. By applying this method to Doppler-derived data, we achieve a movement recognition accuracy of nearly 99%. Comprehensive performance metrics and confusion matrices confirm the significant effectiveness of our proposed solution.
