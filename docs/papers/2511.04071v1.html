Left Atrial Segmentation with nnU-Net Using MRI

Summary

This paper presents the application of the nnU-Net deep learning framework for accurate left atrial (LA) segmentation from cardiac MRI scans, a critical step for atrial fibrillation (AF) ablation guidance and biophysical cardiac modeling. The self-configuring nnU-Net model achieved a high mean Dice similarity coefficient of 93.5 on the Left Atrial Segmentation Challenge 2013 dataset, demonstrating superior performance and robust generalization compared to traditional methods.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to cardiology and electrophysiology by offering an automated, precise method for left atrial segmentation, which directly enhances pre-procedural planning for atrial fibrillation ablation and improves the accuracy of cardiac biophysical models crucial for understanding heart disease.

AI Health Application

The AI application described is the automated and precise segmentation of the left atrium from cardiac MRI images using a deep learning framework (nnU-Net). This automation aims to support and improve clinical workflows by reducing the time and observer dependency of manual segmentation. Specifically, it directly aids in guiding atrial fibrillation ablation procedures by providing accurate anatomical models of the left atrium and can contribute to the development of biophysical cardiac models for research and treatment planning.

Methodology

The study applied the nnU-Net framework, a self-configuring deep learning architecture, to perform left atrial segmentation. The nnU-Net automatically configured its preprocessing steps, convolutional network architecture, and training pipeline based on the input data. The model was trained and evaluated on the Left Atrial Segmentation Challenge 2013 dataset, comprising thirty cardiac MRI scans with corresponding expert-annotated ground truth masks. Performance was quantitatively assessed using the Dice similarity coefficient (DSC).

Key Findings

The nnU-Net model achieved a mean Dice similarity coefficient of 93.5, demonstrating excellent agreement with expert manual annotations. This performance significantly surpassed several traditional segmentation methods. The network proved robust, generalizing well across diverse left atrial morphologies, image contrasts, and overall image quality, successfully delineating both the atrial body and its associated proximal pulmonary veins.

Clinical Impact

This automated and highly accurate LA segmentation method has significant potential to revolutionize clinical workflows for atrial fibrillation (AF) ablation. It can drastically reduce the time and observer variability associated with manual delineation, enabling more precise and standardized pre-operative mapping. This improved guidance could lead to more effective ablation procedures, better patient outcomes, and facilitate large-scale research studies and advanced biophysical cardiac model development.

Abstract

Accurate segmentation of the left atrium (LA) from cardiac MRI is critical for guiding atrial fibrillation (AF) ablation and constructing biophysical cardiac models. Manual delineation is time-consuming, observer-dependent, and impractical for large-scale or time-sensitive clinical workflows. Deep learning methods, particularly convolutional architectures, have recently demonstrated superior performance in medical image segmentation tasks. In this study, we applied the nnU-Net framework, an automated, self-configuring deep learning segmentation architecture, to the Left Atrial Segmentation Challenge 2013 dataset. The dataset consists of thirty MRI scans with corresponding expert-annotated masks. The nnU-Net model automatically adapted its preprocessing, network configuration, and training pipeline to the characteristics of the MRI data. Model performance was quantitatively evaluated using the Dice similarity coefficient (DSC), and qualitative results were compared against expert segmentations. The proposed nnUNet model achieved a mean Dice score of 93.5, demonstrating high overlap with expert annotations and outperforming several traditional segmentation approaches reported in previous studies. The network exhibited robust generalization across variations in left atrial shape, contrast, and image quality, accurately delineating both the atrial body and proximal pulmonary veins.
