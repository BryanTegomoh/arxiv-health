Tackling Tuberculosis: A Comparative Dive into Machine Learning for Tuberculosis Detection

Published: 2025-12-02
Authors: Daanish Hindustani, Sanober Hindustani, Preston Nguyen
Categories: cs.CV, cs.AI

Summary

This study investigates the efficacy of machine learning models, specifically a pretrained ResNet-50 and a general SqueezeNet, for tuberculosis (TB) detection using chest X-ray images. It demonstrates that SqueezeNet significantly outperforms ResNet-50, highlighting deep learning's potential to provide more efficient and accessible TB diagnostics compared to traditional methods. The research suggests crucial implications for early TB identification and treatment, particularly in resource-limited settings.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medicine and public health by offering a pathway to overcome the inefficiency and accessibility barriers of traditional TB diagnosis, potentially leading to earlier intervention, reduced transmission, and improved patient outcomes globally, especially in vulnerable populations.

AI Health Application

The AI application is an automated diagnostic tool for tuberculosis detection from chest X-ray images, utilizing deep learning (ResNet-50, SqueezeNet) to assist or potentially expedite the identification of TB, especially in areas with limited medical resources.

Key Points

  • Traditional TB diagnostic methods (sputum smear microscopy, culture) are inefficient, especially in resource-limited areas, prompting the need for advanced solutions.
  • The study utilized a Kaggle dataset comprising 4,200 chest X-ray images for model training and evaluation.
  • Preprocessing steps included data splitting, augmentation, and resizing to optimize training efficiency for the deep learning models.
  • SqueezeNet achieved superior performance with 89% accuracy, 98% precision, 80% recall, and an 87% F1 score, alongside a 32% loss.
  • ResNet-50 showed comparatively lower performance, achieving 73% accuracy, 88% precision, 52% recall, and a 65% F1 score, with a 54% loss.
  • The research emphasizes machine learning's potential for early TB diagnosis and treatment initiation, particularly through integration into mobile devices for regions lacking adequate resources.
  • Despite promising results, the continuous development of faster, smaller, and more accurate TB detection models is deemed crucial for global TB eradication efforts.

Methodology

The study employed a comparative approach, applying a pretrained ResNet-50 model and a general SqueezeNet model to a Kaggle dataset of 4,200 chest X-ray images for TB detection. Data preprocessing involved splitting, augmentation, and resizing. Model performance was rigorously evaluated using standard metrics: accuracy, precision, recall, F1 score, and a confusion matrix.

Key Findings

SqueezeNet significantly outperformed ResNet-50, achieving an accuracy of 89%, precision of 98%, recall of 80%, F1 score of 87%, and a loss of 32%. In contrast, ResNet-50 showed an accuracy of 73%, precision of 88%, recall of 52%, F1 score of 65%, and a higher loss of 54%.

Clinical Impact

The findings suggest that machine learning models, particularly SqueezeNet, could serve as a valuable tool for rapid and accurate TB diagnosis from chest X-rays. This can facilitate earlier treatment initiation, reduce disease progression, and improve public health outcomes, especially in resource-limited settings where these models could be deployed on mobile devices, bridging critical diagnostic gaps.

Limitations

The paper implicitly notes the current limitations by stating the need for 'continued development of faster, smaller, and more accurate TB detection models,' indicating that existing models, while promising, may not yet meet all practical deployment requirements in terms of speed, computational footprint, and diagnostic precision.

Future Directions

Future research should focus on the continued development of machine learning models that are faster, smaller (for mobile device integration), and possess even higher accuracy to enhance global efforts in combating tuberculosis.

Abstract

This study explores the application of machine learning models, specifically a pretrained ResNet-50 model and a general SqueezeNet model, in diagnosing tuberculosis (TB) using chest X-ray images. TB, a persistent infectious disease affecting humanity for millennia, poses challenges in diagnosis, especially in resource-limited settings. Traditional methods, such as sputum smear microscopy and culture, are inefficient, prompting the exploration of advanced technologies like deep learning and computer vision. The study utilized a dataset from Kaggle, consisting of 4,200 chest X-rays, to develop and compare the performance of the two machine learning models. Preprocessing involved data splitting, augmentation, and resizing to enhance training efficiency. Evaluation metrics, including accuracy, precision, recall, and confusion matrix, were employed to assess model performance. Results showcase that the SqueezeNet achieved a loss of 32%, accuracy of 89%, precision of 98%, recall of 80%, and an F1 score of 87%. In contrast, the ResNet-50 model exhibited a loss of 54%, accuracy of 73%, precision of 88%, recall of 52%, and an F1 score of 65%. This study emphasizes the potential of machine learning in TB detection and possible implications for early identification and treatment initiation. The possibility of integrating such models into mobile devices expands their utility in areas lacking TB detection resources. However, despite promising results, the need for continued development of faster, smaller, and more accurate TB detection models remains crucial in contributing to the global efforts in combating TB.
