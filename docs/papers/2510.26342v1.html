Linear Causal Discovery with Interventional Constraints

Summary

This paper introduces 'interventional constraints,' a novel concept in causal discovery that leverages high-level causal knowledge as inequality constraints on total causal effects between variable pairs. This approach aims to refine linear causal models by ensuring learned relationships are consistent with established scientific findings, thereby improving model accuracy and facilitating the discovery of new, otherwise costly, causal connections. The method proposes a metric for total causal effects and utilizes a two-stage constrained optimization to integrate these constraints.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for medicine and health because it provides a method to integrate known biological mechanisms into causal models, ensuring that scientific discoveries are consistent with established facts and accelerating the development of new treatments by identifying accurate causal pathways in complex biological systems.

In health, it can be applied to: (1) Identify and refine causal pathways in biological systems related to disease progression, allowing for better understanding of disease mechanisms. (2) Accelerate drug discovery by pinpointing potential drug targets and predicting the effects of interventions. (3) Analyze complex biological data (e.g., omics data) to uncover novel causal relationships between genes, proteins, and health outcomes. (4) Develop more explainable AI models for healthcare by ensuring consistency with established medical and biological knowledge.

Methodology

The authors propose a novel approach for linear causal discovery by introducing 'interventional constraints,' which are high-level causal knowledge encoded as inequality constraints on total causal effects between variable pairs. This involves defining a specific metric to quantify these total causal effects within linear causal models. The causal discovery problem is then framed as a constrained optimization task, which is efficiently solved using a tailored two-stage constrained optimization method. This methodology fundamentally differs from causal discovery methods that rely on interventional data by using qualitative knowledge about effect directions rather than direct experimental perturbations.

Key Findings

Integrating interventional constraints significantly improves the accuracy of learned causal models and ensures their consistency with established scientific and biological findings, thereby enhancing model explainability and trustworthiness. The method also demonstrates the ability to facilitate the discovery of novel causal relationships that would otherwise be costly or time-consuming to identify experimentally, as validated on real-world datasets like the Sachs dataset.

This methodology has the potential to profoundly impact clinical research and practice by enabling the development of more robust and biologically plausible models for disease mechanisms and drug action. It can accelerate drug discovery by identifying more precise therapeutic targets and causal pathways, leading to the design of more effective and safer treatments. Abstract

Incorporating causal knowledge and mechanisms is essential for refining causal models and improving downstream tasks such as designing new treatments. In this paper, we introduce a novel concept in causal discovery, termed interventional constraints, which differs fundamentally from interventional data. While interventional data require direct perturbations of variables, interventional constraints encode high-level causal knowledge in the form of inequality constraints on causal effects. For instance, in the Sachs dataset (Sachs et al.\ 2005), Akt has been shown to be activated by PIP3, meaning PIP3 exerts a positive causal effect on Akt. Existing causal discovery methods allow enforcing structural constraints (for example, requiring a causal path from PIP3 to Akt), but they may still produce incorrect causal conclusions such as learning that "PIP3 inhibits Akt". Interventional constraints bridge this gap by explicitly constraining the total causal effect between variable pairs, ensuring learned models respect known causal influences. To formalize interventional constraints, we propose a metric to quantify total causal effects for linear causal models and formulate the problem as a constrained optimization task, solved using a two-stage constrained optimization method. We evaluate our approach on real-world datasets and demonstrate that integrating interventional constraints not only improves model accuracy and ensures consistency with established findings, making models more explainable, but also facilitates the discovery of new causal relationships that would otherwise be costly to identify.
