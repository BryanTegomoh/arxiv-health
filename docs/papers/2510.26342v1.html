Summary

This paper introduces "interventional constraints" into linear causal discovery, a novel concept encoding high-level causal knowledge as inequality constraints on total causal effects between variables. By explicitly enforcing known causal influences (e.g., "PIP3 positively activates Akt"), this method significantly improves model accuracy and consistency with established findings, thereby accelerating the discovery of new causal relationships crucial for applications like designing new treatments.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a more robust and accurate method for identifying causal relationships in complex biological systems, which is fundamental for understanding disease mechanisms, pinpointing therapeutic targets, and ultimately designing effective new treatments and interventions in medicine and health.

AI Health Application

The research develops an AI-driven causal discovery method (Linear Causal Discovery with Interventional Constraints) to build more accurate and explainable causal models for biological systems. This can be applied in health to understand complex biological pathways involved in diseases, identify novel drug targets, refine existing treatment strategies, and potentially guide the development of new diagnostic or therapeutic approaches by leveraging known biological mechanisms and discovering new ones.

Key Points

  • Introduces "interventional constraints" as a new form of causal knowledge, distinct from interventional data, which are inequality constraints on total causal effects.
  • Addresses the limitation of existing causal discovery methods that can enforce structural constraints but fail to ensure the correct sign or magnitude of causal effects.
  • Proposes a specific metric to quantify total causal effects within the framework of linear causal models.
  • Formulates the causal discovery problem as a constrained optimization task, integrating these interventional constraints to guide the learning process.
  • Employs a two-stage constrained optimization method to solve the newly formulated problem.
  • Demonstrates improved model accuracy on real-world datasets and ensures consistency with established biological findings, leading to more explainable causal models.
  • Facilitates the discovery of novel causal relationships that would otherwise be costly or difficult to identify through traditional experimental methods.

Methodology

The authors propose formalizing "interventional constraints" as inequality constraints on the total causal effects between variable pairs within linear causal models. They introduce a metric to quantify these total causal effects and then formulate the causal discovery problem as a constrained optimization task. This task is subsequently solved using a custom two-stage constrained optimization method.

Key Findings

The integration of interventional constraints significantly enhances the accuracy of learned causal models. It ensures that the discovered models are consistent with known causal influences, thereby improving their explainability and reliability. Furthermore, this approach proves effective in uncovering new causal relationships that would be prohibitively expensive or time-consuming to identify through conventional experimental means.

This can lead to the development of more effective and safer drugs, inform personalized medicine strategies, and provide deeper insights into disease mechanisms that guide clinical diagnostics and interventions.

Abstract

Incorporating causal knowledge and mechanisms is essential for refining causal models and improving downstream tasks such as designing new treatments. In this paper, we introduce a novel concept in causal discovery, termed interventional constraints, which differs fundamentally from interventional data. While interventional data require direct perturbations of variables, interventional constraints encode high-level causal knowledge in the form of inequality constraints on causal effects. For instance, in the Sachs dataset (Sachs et al.\ 2005), Akt has been shown to be activated by PIP3, meaning PIP3 exerts a positive causal effect on Akt. Existing causal discovery methods allow enforcing structural constraints (for example, requiring a causal path from PIP3 to Akt), but they may still produce incorrect causal conclusions such as learning that "PIP3 inhibits Akt". Interventional constraints bridge this gap by explicitly constraining the total causal effect between variable pairs, ensuring learned models respect known causal influences. To formalize interventional constraints, we propose a metric to quantify total causal effects for linear causal models and formulate the problem as a constrained optimization task, solved using a two-stage constrained optimization method. We evaluate our approach on real-world datasets and demonstrate that integrating interventional constraints not only improves model accuracy and ensures consistency with established findings, making models more explainable, but also facilitates the discovery of new causal relationships that would otherwise be costly to identify.
