Orchestrator Multi-Agent Clinical Decision Support System for Secondary Headache Diagnosis in Primary Care

arXiv ID: 2512.04207v1
Published: 2025-12-03
Authors: Xizhi Wu, Nelly Estefanie Garduno-Rapp, Justin F Rousseau, Mounika Thakkallapally, Hang Zhang, Yuelyu Ji, Shyam Visweswaran, Yifan Peng, Yanshan Wang
Categories: cs.AI

Summary

This paper introduces an LLM-based multi-agent clinical decision support system utilizing an orchestrator-specialist architecture to improve the diagnosis of secondary headaches from free-text clinical vignettes in primary care. The system decomposes diagnosis into seven specialized agents, producing evidence-grounded rationales, and demonstrated superior F1 scores, especially when combined with guideline-based prompting (GPrompt), outperforming single-LLM baselines across various open-source models.

Medical Relevance

Secondary headaches require prompt and specialized care due to potential devastating consequences, yet their identification is challenging in primary care. This system offers a critical tool to assist primary care clinicians in accurately and timely identifying these high-risk patients, potentially preventing serious morbidity and mortality.

AI Health Application

The paper describes a Large Language Model (LLM)-based multi-agent clinical decision support system (CDSS) for secondary headache diagnosis. This AI application aims to assist primary care clinicians in interpreting free-text clinical vignettes to identify 'red flag' features and determine the need for urgent evaluation, thereby improving diagnostic accuracy and patient care in a critical medical domain.

Methodology

An LLM-based multi-agent system was developed with an orchestrator-specialist architecture, where a central orchestrator manages task decomposition and routes to seven domain-specialized agents. The system was evaluated using 90 expert-validated secondary headache cases, comparing its F1 scores against a single-LLM baseline across five open-source LLMs (Qwen-30B, GPT-OSS-20B, Qwen-14B, Qwen-8B, Llama-3.1-8B) and two prompting strategies (QPrompt and GPrompt).

Key Findings

The orchestrated multi-agent system, especially when combined with clinical practice guideline-based prompting (GPrompt), consistently achieved the highest F1 scores for secondary headache diagnosis. This architectural approach demonstrated significant improvements in accuracy over single-LLM baselines, with particularly pronounced gains observed in smaller language models, highlighting the effectiveness of structured multi-agent reasoning.

Clinical Impact

This system has the potential to significantly improve the diagnostic accuracy of secondary headaches in primary care settings, enabling earlier recognition and appropriate referral for specialized care. By providing explicit and interpretable rationales, it fosters trust and facilitates clinician understanding, reducing under-recognition and inappropriate care, ultimately leading to better patient outcomes and reduced healthcare burden from untreated serious conditions.

Abstract

Unlike most primary headaches, secondary headaches need specialized care and can have devastating consequences if not treated promptly. Clinical guidelines highlight several 'red flag' features, such as thunderclap onset, meningismus, papilledema, focal neurologic deficits, signs of temporal arteritis, systemic illness, and the 'worst headache of their life' presentation. Despite these guidelines, determining which patients require urgent evaluation remains challenging in primary care settings. Clinicians often work with limited time, incomplete information, and diverse symptom presentations, which can lead to under-recognition and inappropriate care. We present a large language model (LLM)-based multi-agent clinical decision support system built on an orchestrator-specialist architecture, designed to perform explicit and interpretable secondary headache diagnosis from free-text clinical vignettes. The multi-agent system decomposes diagnosis into seven domain-specialized agents, each producing a structured and evidence-grounded rationale, while a central orchestrator performs task decomposition and coordinates agent routing. We evaluated the multi-agent system using 90 expert-validated secondary headache cases and compared its performance with a single-LLM baseline across two prompting strategies: question-based prompting (QPrompt) and clinical practice guideline-based prompting (GPrompt). We tested five open-source LLMs (Qwen-30B, GPT-OSS-20B, Qwen-14B, Qwen-8B, and Llama-3.1-8B), and found that the orchestrated multi-agent system with GPrompt consistently achieved the highest F1 scores, with larger gains in smaller models. These findings demonstrate that structured multi-agent reasoning improves accuracy beyond prompt engineering alone and offers a transparent, clinically aligned approach for explainable decision support in secondary headache diagnosis.
