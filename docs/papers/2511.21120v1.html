Learning Cell-Aware Hierarchical Multi-Modal Representations for Robust Molecular Modeling

arXiv ID: 2511.21120v1
Published: 2025-11-26
Authors: Mengran Li, Zelin Zang, Wenbin Xing, Junzhou Chen, Ronghui Zhang, Jiebo Luo, Stan Z. Li

Summary

This paper introduces CHMR (Cell-aware Hierarchical Multi-modal Representations), a novel framework for robust molecular property prediction that addresses limitations in existing methods by jointly modeling local-global dependencies between molecules and cellular responses. CHMR uniquely captures latent biological hierarchies using a tree-structured vector quantization module, leading to significant performance improvements (3.6% for classification, 17.2% for regression) over state-of-the-art baselines across 728 tasks.

Medical Relevance

This research is fundamentally important for drug discovery and development, as accurately predicting how chemical perturbations propagate through biological systems helps understand drug mechanisms, predict efficacy, and identify potential toxicities, thereby streamlining the path to new therapeutics.

AI Health Application

This paper presents CHMR, an AI/ML framework that learns robust, multi-modal molecular representations by integrating chemical structures with cellular responses (like morphology and gene expression). This framework can be applied to predict drug effects, toxicity, and mechanisms of action, thereby accelerating drug discovery, improving drug safety, and enabling more effective therapeutic interventions. It provides a foundational tool for 'integrative biomedical modeling' using AI.

Key Points

  • Addresses critical limitations in existing molecular modeling, namely the neglect of cellular responses, modality incompleteness in biological data, and insufficient modeling of hierarchical dependencies.
  • Proposes CHMR, a framework that integrates information from chemical structures with cellular responses (e.g., morphology, gene expression).
  • Introduces a novel tree-structured vector quantization (VQ) module to explicitly capture and model latent biological hierarchies across molecular, cellular, and genomic levels.
  • Jointly models local-global dependencies between molecules and cellular responses, leading to more robust and biologically grounded representations.
  • Evaluated on nine public benchmarks encompassing 728 diverse tasks, demonstrating broad applicability and superior performance.
  • Achieves average performance improvements of 3.6% on classification tasks and a substantial 17.2% on regression tasks compared to state-of-the-art baselines.
  • Positions CHMR as a generalizable framework for integrative biomedical modeling, highlighting the advantages of hierarchy-aware, multimodal learning.

Methodology

CHMR is a deep learning framework designed to integrate diverse biological data. It models molecules and cellular responses (like morphology and gene expression) through a multi-modal approach, capturing local-global dependencies. A core innovation is a novel tree-structured vector quantization (VQ) module that explicitly learns and represents latent biological hierarchies spanning molecular, cellular, and genomic levels, thereby providing structured, biologically grounded representations for robust prediction tasks.

Key Findings

The CHMR framework significantly outperforms state-of-the-art baselines across a wide array of 728 tasks on nine public benchmarks. Specifically, it demonstrated an average improvement of 3.6% in classification performance and a notable 17.2% improvement in regression tasks, underscoring its superior accuracy and robustness in molecular property prediction.

Clinical Impact

By providing more accurate and biologically grounded predictions of molecular properties and drug effects, CHMR can accelerate the drug discovery process, reduce costly late-stage failures, and facilitate the development of more effective and safer medicines. It could enable precision medicine approaches by allowing for better prediction of individual patient responses to drugs based on a holistic understanding of molecular and cellular interactions.

Limitations

The abstract primarily highlights two key limitations of *existing* cell-aware approaches that CHMR aims to overcome: (1) modality incompleteness in external biological data, and (2) insufficient modeling of hierarchical dependencies across molecular, cellular, and genomic levels. The abstract does not explicitly state any inherent limitations of the CHMR framework itself.

Future Directions

The paper suggests that CHMR offers "a generalizable framework for integrative biomedical modeling." This implies future research could involve applying CHMR to a broader spectrum of complex biological problems, integrating an even wider range of multi-omics data, and potentially extending its hierarchical modeling capabilities to capture more intricate biological relationships for comprehensive biomedical understanding.

Abstract

Understanding how chemical perturbations propagate through biological systems is essential for robust molecular property prediction. While most existing methods focus on chemical structures alone, recent advances highlight the crucial role of cellular responses such as morphology and gene expression in shaping drug effects. However, current cell-aware approaches face two key limitations: (1) modality incompleteness in external biological data, and (2) insufficient modeling of hierarchical dependencies across molecular, cellular, and genomic levels. We propose CHMR (Cell-aware Hierarchical Multi-modal Representations), a robust framework that jointly models local-global dependencies between molecules and cellular responses and captures latent biological hierarchies via a novel tree-structured vector quantization module. Evaluated on nine public benchmarks spanning 728 tasks, CHMR outperforms state-of-the-art baselines, yielding average improvements of 3.6% on classification and 17.2% on regression tasks. These results demonstrate the advantage of hierarchy-aware, multimodal learning for reliable and biologically grounded molecular representations, offering a generalizable framework for integrative biomedical modeling. The code is in https://github.com/limengran98/CHMR.
