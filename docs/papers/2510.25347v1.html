3D CT-Based Coronary Calcium Assessment: A Feature-Driven Machine Learning Framework

Summary

This study presents a radiomics-based machine learning framework for 3D CT-based coronary calcium assessment, utilizing pseudo-labeling to overcome the scarcity of expert annotations. It demonstrates that radiomics features, coupled with traditional classifiers, significantly outperform deep learning features derived from pre-trained foundation models (CT-FM, RadImageNet) for classifying zero versus non-zero coronary artery calcium in non-contrast CCTA scans, achieving 84% accuracy.

Medical Relevance

Coronary artery calcium (CAC) scoring is crucial for early detection and risk stratification of coronary artery disease (CAD). This research offers a more accessible and scalable method for automated CAC assessment, potentially improving patient care by facilitating earlier and more widespread CAD risk evaluation without extensive manual annotation effort.

AI Health Application

The AI application is an automated, machine learning-based framework for 3D CT-based coronary calcium assessment. It aims to streamline and enhance the accuracy of CAC scoring from non-contrast CCTA scans, assisting in the early detection and risk stratification of coronary artery disease, while also addressing the challenge of limited expert annotations in medical AI development through techniques like pseudo-labeling.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical challenge of limited annotated data for training machine learning models in coronary artery calcium (CAC) scoring.
  • Proposes a novel radiomics-based pipeline that leverages pseudo-labeling to generate training labels, thus eliminating the need for expert-defined segmentations.
  • Compares the performance of traditional radiomics features against deep learning features extracted from pre-trained foundation models (CT-FM, RadImageNet).
  • Evaluates the framework on a clinical CCTA dataset of 182 patients, classifying individuals into zero versus non-zero calcium scores.
  • Radiomics-based models achieved significantly higher performance (84% accuracy, p<0.05) compared to CNN-derived embeddings from foundation models.
  • Investigates the impact of training on non-contrast datasets versus combined contrast and non-contrast datasets, with consistent testing on non-contrast scans.

Methodology

The study employs a radiomics-based pipeline that addresses data scarcity by generating training labels through pseudo-labeling, obviating the need for expert-defined segmentations. Image features are extracted using two distinct approaches: traditional radiomics feature extraction and deep learning features derived from pre-trained foundation models (CT-FM, RadImageNet). These features are then utilized with traditional classifiers to classify non-contrast CCTA scans into zero versus non-zero calcium score groups. The methodology was evaluated on a clinical dataset of 182 patients and included an investigation into the impact of training data composition.

Key Findings

Radiomics-based models significantly outperformed CNN-derived embeddings from pre-trained foundation models (CT-FM, RadImageNet), achieving an 84% accuracy (p<0.05) in classifying zero versus non-zero coronary artery calcium. This superior performance was accomplished without the necessity of expert annotations for segmentation, leveraging pseudo-labeling instead. The study also explored the effects of training on purely non-contrast datasets versus combined contrast and non-contrast datasets.

Clinical Impact

This framework offers a practical and scalable solution for automated coronary artery calcium (CAC) assessment, particularly in clinical settings where expert annotation resources are limited. By enabling efficient and accurate identification of patients with non-zero CAC, it can significantly enhance early detection and risk stratification of coronary artery disease (CAD), potentially leading to timelier interventions and improved patient outcomes.

Limitations

The primary reliance on pseudo-labeling, while addressing data scarcity, inherently introduces a potential for label inaccuracies compared to true expert-defined segmentations. The evaluation was performed on a clinical CCTA dataset of 182 patients, which, while valuable, may be considered a moderate size for comprehensive generalization across diverse patient populations.

Abstract

Coronary artery calcium (CAC) scoring plays a crucial role in the early detection and risk stratification of coronary artery disease (CAD). In this study, we focus on non-contrast coronary computed tomography angiography (CCTA) scans, which are commonly used for early calcification detection in clinical settings. To address the challenge of limited annotated data, we propose a radiomics-based pipeline that leverages pseudo-labeling to generate training labels, thereby eliminating the need for expert-defined segmentations. Additionally, we explore the use of pretrained foundation models, specifically CT-FM and RadImageNet, to extract image features, which are then used with traditional classifiers. We compare the performance of these deep learning features with that of radiomics features. Evaluation is conducted on a clinical CCTA dataset comprising 182 patients, where individuals are classified into two groups: zero versus non-zero calcium scores. We further investigate the impact of training on non-contrast datasets versus combined contrast and non-contrast datasets, with testing performed only on non contrast scans. Results show that radiomics-based models significantly outperform CNN-derived embeddings from foundation models (achieving 84% accuracy and p<0.05), despite the unavailability of expert annotations.
