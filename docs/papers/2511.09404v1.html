Spatio-Temporal Graph Unlearning

Authors: Qiming Guo, Wenbo Sun, Wenlu Wang

Summary

This paper introduces CallosumNet, a novel spatio-temporal graph unlearning framework designed to efficiently remove specific data from complex dynamic models while complying with stringent privacy regulations like GDPR. Inspired by the brain's corpus callosum, CallosumNet uses a divide-and-conquer approach with Enhanced Subgraph Construction and Global Ganglion Bridging to achieve complete data unlearning with minimal accuracy loss, significantly outperforming existing methods.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for healthcare AI, where spatio-temporal graphs are used for patient monitoring and disease progression modeling. It enables efficient and compliant removal of sensitive patient data from predictive models, directly addressing GDPR/CCPA requirements without the prohibitive cost of full model retraining.

AI Health Application

This research develops a foundational method for efficient spatio-temporal graph unlearning, which is directly applicable to AI systems in healthcare. It enables these AI models, which process dynamic and interconnected health data (e.g., patient sensor data, electronic health record trends, disease spread models), to comply with stringent data privacy regulations by efficiently removing unauthorized or requested data without requiring full model retraining. This is crucial for building responsible and compliant medical AI applications.

Key Points

  • Spatio-temporal graphs (STGs) are critical for modeling dynamic processes like healthcare monitoring, but their global information diffusion makes unlearning specific data points (e.g., patient records) challenging and costly.
  • Existing unlearning methods, primarily for static graphs or localized data, are inefficient for STGs, often requiring nearly full model retraining to erase a single node's information.
  • CallosumNet proposes a novel divide-and-conquer framework, drawing inspiration from the corpus callosum, to address the need for effective STG unlearning.
  • It incorporates two core techniques: (1) Enhanced Subgraph Construction (ESC), which adaptively creates localized subgraphs using 'biologically-inspired virtual ganglions'; and (2) Global Ganglion Bridging (GGB), which reconstructs global STG dependencies from these subgraphs.
  • The framework enables complete unlearning of unauthorized data from STG models, ensuring compliance with privacy regulations such as GDPR and CCPA.
  • Empirical results across four real-world datasets demonstrate CallosumNet achieves unlearning with only 1%-2% relative Mean Absolute Error (MAE) loss compared to a fully retrained ('gold') model, significantly outperforming state-of-the-art baselines.
  • Ablation studies confirm the individual effectiveness and contribution of both ESC and GGB components to CallosumNet's performance.

Methodology

CallosumNet utilizes a divide-and-conquer framework for spatio-temporal graph unlearning. It employs Enhanced Subgraph Construction (ESC) to adaptively build localized subgraphs using biologically-inspired virtual ganglions. Subsequently, Global Ganglion Bridging (GGB) is used to reconstruct global spatio-temporal dependencies by bridging these localized subgraphs, effectively restoring the full graph representation for accurate model training after unlearning. Performance is evaluated using relative MAE loss against a gold standard model on diverse real-world datasets.

Key Findings

CallosumNet achieves complete unlearning of specific node data from spatio-temporal graph models. It maintains high model accuracy post-unlearning, incurring only 1%-2% relative MAE loss compared to a fully retrained model. The framework significantly surpasses existing state-of-the-art baselines in both unlearning efficacy and accuracy preservation. Both the ESC and GGB techniques are independently validated as effective components.

Clinical Impact

This technology can profoundly impact clinical practice by enabling healthcare organizations to comply with stringent data privacy regulations (e.g., GDPR, CCPA) when using AI models on dynamic patient data. It facilitates the removal of erroneous, outdated, or unauthorized patient information from predictive models without incurring massive computational costs for full retraining, thereby improving data governance, accelerating model updates, and fostering patient trust in AI-driven healthcare solutions.

Abstract

Spatio-temporal graphs are widely used in modeling complex dynamic processes such as traffic forecasting, molecular dynamics, and healthcare monitoring. Recently, stringent privacy regulations such as GDPR and CCPA have introduced significant new challenges for existing spatio-temporal graph models, requiring complete unlearning of unauthorized data. Since each node in a spatio-temporal graph diffuses information globally across both spatial and temporal dimensions, existing unlearning methods primarily designed for static graphs and localized data removal cannot efficiently erase a single node without incurring costs nearly equivalent to full model retraining. Therefore, an effective approach for complete spatio-temporal graph unlearning is a pressing need. To address this, we propose CallosumNet, a divide-and-conquer spatio-temporal graph unlearning framework inspired by the corpus callosum structure that facilitates communication between the brain's two hemispheres. CallosumNet incorporates two novel techniques: (1) Enhanced Subgraph Construction (ESC), which adaptively constructs multiple localized subgraphs based on several factors, including biologically-inspired virtual ganglions; and (2) Global Ganglion Bridging (GGB), which reconstructs global spatio-temporal dependencies from these localized subgraphs, effectively restoring the full graph representation. Empirical results on four diverse real-world datasets show that CallosumNet achieves complete unlearning with only 1%-2% relative MAE loss compared to the gold model, significantly outperforming state-of-the-art baselines. Ablation studies verify the effectiveness of both proposed techniques.
