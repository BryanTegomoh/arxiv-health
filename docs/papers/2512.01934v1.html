Physical ID-Transfer Attacks against Multi-Object Tracking via Adversarial Trajectory

arXiv ID: 2512.01934v1
Published: 2025-12-01
Authors: Chenyi Wang, Yanmao Man, Raymond Muller, Ming Li, Z. Berkay Celik, Ryan Gerdes, Jonathan Petit
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 0.85 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces AdvTraj, the first online and physical ID-manipulation attack targeting Multi-Object Tracking (MOT) systems. By employing adversarial trajectories, AdvTraj successfully transfers an attacker's ID to a targeted object, achieving 100% success in simulations and high transferability across state-of-the-art MOT algorithms. This research reveals critical vulnerabilities in the object association phase of current MOT systems, paving the way for enhanced robustness.

Medical Relevance

While a computer vision paper, its findings are critically relevant to medical and health domains utilizing Multi-Object Tracking (MOT) for patient monitoring, robotic assistance, or autonomous healthcare logistics. ID-transfer attacks could lead to severe consequences such as misidentification of patients, staff, or medical equipment, potentially resulting in incorrect treatment, medication errors, or compromised patient safety and privacy.

AI Health Application

The paper directly contributes to understanding and enhancing the robustness and security of AI-powered Multi-Object Tracking systems deployed in healthcare. This is critical for the safe and reliable application of computer vision AI in areas such as patient safety monitoring, hospital operational efficiency, security surveillance, and the navigation of autonomous medical devices, ensuring that these systems cannot be easily deceived by physical maneuvers.

Key Points

  • The research addresses an under-explored area: online and physical ID-manipulation attacks against Multi-Object Tracking (MOT) systems, specifically targeting the object association phase.
  • It introduces AdvTraj, a novel attack method that uses adversarial trajectories to transfer an attacker's ID to a targeted object, without directly attacking the underlying object detection module.
  • AdvTraj is designed to be an online and physical attack, in contrast to previous digital, model-specific, or offline attacks that primarily hijacked individual trackers or manipulated detector IDs.
  • Simulation results in CARLA demonstrate a 100% success rate for white-box attacks against the SORT algorithm across various scenarios.
  • The attack exhibits high transferability, achieving up to 93% success rate against other state-of-the-art MOT algorithms, attributed to their common design principles in object association.
  • The study characterizes the patterns of effective adversarial trajectories and proposes two universal adversarial maneuvers that can be performed by a human walker or driver in real-world scenarios.
  • The findings highlight significant weaknesses in the object association phase of contemporary MOT systems, providing critical insights for future research into system robustness.

Methodology

The study developed AdvTraj, an online and physical ID-manipulation attack, and evaluated its effectiveness through simulations conducted in CARLA. It primarily focused on white-box attacks against the SORT algorithm to establish its feasibility and then assessed its transferability against various state-of-the-art MOT algorithms. The methodology also involved characterizing effective adversarial trajectory patterns and devising two universal physical maneuvers that could implement the attack in practical settings.

Key Findings

AdvTraj achieved a 100% ID-transfer success rate in white-box attacks against the SORT algorithm within simulated environments. Crucially, it demonstrated high transferability, with up to a 93% success rate against diverse state-of-the-art Multi-Object Tracking (MOT) algorithms, indicating a fundamental and systemic vulnerability in the object association phase common across these systems.

Clinical Impact

The demonstrated vulnerabilities in Multi-Object Tracking (MOT) systems could have significant clinical impact by compromising the integrity and reliability of tracking in healthcare settings. Misidentification of patients, healthcare staff, or critical medical equipment could lead to severe clinical errors, wrong interventions, medication errors, and breaches of patient privacy and safety, underscoring the necessity for robust security measures in integrated healthcare tracking systems.

Limitations

The primary evaluation of AdvTraj was conducted in simulated environments (CARLA), which may not fully capture the complexities, environmental noise, and unpredictable variables inherent in real-world physical scenarios. While the abstract emphasizes 'physical ID-transfer,' the experimental details provided are largely simulation-based.

Future Directions

The paper implicitly suggests future research directions focused on enhancing the robustness of Multi-Object Tracking (MOT) systems, particularly by strengthening the object association phase to mitigate vulnerabilities exploited by adversarial trajectories. This includes developing defense mechanisms against such online and physical ID-transfer attacks.

Abstract

Multi-Object Tracking (MOT) is a critical task in computer vision, with applications ranging from surveillance systems to autonomous driving. However, threats to MOT algorithms have yet been widely studied. In particular, incorrect association between the tracked objects and their assigned IDs can lead to severe consequences, such as wrong trajectory predictions. Previous attacks against MOT either focused on hijacking the trackers of individual objects, or manipulating the tracker IDs in MOT by attacking the integrated object detection (OD) module in the digital domain, which are model-specific, non-robust, and only able to affect specific samples in offline datasets. In this paper, we present AdvTraj, the first online and physical ID-manipulation attack against tracking-by-detection MOT, in which an attacker uses adversarial trajectories to transfer its ID to a targeted object to confuse the tracking system, without attacking OD. Our simulation results in CARLA show that AdvTraj can fool ID assignments with 100% success rate in various scenarios for white-box attacks against SORT, which also have high attack transferability (up to 93% attack success rate) against state-of-the-art (SOTA) MOT algorithms due to their common design principles. We characterize the patterns of trajectories generated by AdvTraj and propose two universal adversarial maneuvers that can be performed by a human walker/driver in daily scenarios. Our work reveals under-explored weaknesses in the object association phase of SOTA MOT systems, and provides insights into enhancing the robustness of such systems.
