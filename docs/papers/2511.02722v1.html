Association-sensory spatiotemporal hierarchy and functional gradient-regularised recurrent neural network with implications for schizophrenia

Summary

This study investigates the sensory-to-association (AS) cortical hierarchy in schizophrenia using fMRI data and computational modeling. It reveals that schizophrenia patients exhibit a compressed AS hierarchy, indicating reduced functional differentiation and attenuated neural timescales, which mechanistically contributes to destabilized neural computations as evidenced by less stable fixed points in gradient-regularized recurrent neural networks.

Medical Relevance

This research offers a mechanistic account of how altered large-scale cortical organization, specifically a compressed sensory-to-association hierarchy, underlies cognitive dysfunction in schizophrenia by destabilizing neural computations, potentially informing future diagnostic biomarkers or targeted therapeutic interventions.

AI Health Application

Key Points

  • Schizophrenia compresses the sensory-to-association (AS) cortical hierarchy, signifying reduced functional differentiation within the neocortex.
  • Neural timescales are attenuated in schizophrenia: the longer time constants typically found in specialized, locally cohesive brain regions are flattened.
  • A novel recurrent neural network (RNN) framework, regularized by subject-specific AS gradients, was developed to model computational efficiency.
  • RNNs endowed with greater AS gradient spread demonstrated more efficient learning, plateaued at lower task loss, and maintained stronger alignment to the prescribed hierarchical geometry during working memory tasks.
  • Fixed point linearisation of these RNNs showed that high-range networks settled into more stable neural states during memory delay, characterized by lower energy and smaller maximal Jacobian eigenvalues.
  • The research provides a mechanistic link between large-scale cortical architecture (gradient de-differentiation) and fixed point stability, suggesting destabilized neural computations in schizophrenia, supported by both empirical timescale flattening and model-based evidence.

Methodology

The study utilized a large fMRI dataset (N=355) to extract individual sensory-to-association (AS) gradients via spectral analysis of brain connectivity, quantifying hierarchical specialization by gradient spread. Neural timescales were modeled using the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process. Computationally, subject-specific recurrent neural networks (RNNs) were regularized by these gradients and trained on working memory tasks. Fixed point linearisation was applied to analyze neural states during memory delay, evaluating stability through energy and maximal Jacobian eigenvalues.

Key Findings

Schizophrenia significantly compresses the AS hierarchy, leading to reduced functional differentiation. Specialized brain regions, typically exhibiting longer neural time constants, show attenuated (flattened) dynamics in schizophrenia. Computationally, RNNs with greater AS gradient spread learned more efficiently, achieved lower task loss, and maintained stronger alignment to hierarchical geometry. Fixed point analysis of these high-range RNNs revealed more stable neural states during memory delay, evidenced by lower energy and smaller maximal Jacobian eigenvalues. This links cortical de-differentiation to destabilized neural computations in schizophrenia.

Clinical Impact

This study provides a mechanistic framework for understanding cognitive deficits in schizophrenia, suggesting that impaired cortical functional differentiation leads to computational instability. This deepened understanding could contribute to the development of novel neuroimaging biomarkers for schizophrenia, assist in stratifying patients based on brain architectural features, and potentially inspire new computational or neurofeedback-based therapeutic strategies aimed at restoring cortical hierarchical organization and computational stability.

Abstract

The human neocortex is functionally organised at its highest level along a continuous sensory-to-association (AS) hierarchy. This study characterises the AS hierarchy of patients with schizophrenia in a comparison with controls. Using a large fMRI dataset (N=355), we extracted individual AS gradients via spectral analysis of brain connectivity, quantified hierarchical specialisation by gradient spread, and related this spread with connectivity geometry. We found that schizophrenia compresses the AS hierarchy indicating reduced functional differentiation. By modelling neural timescale with the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process, we observed that the most specialised, locally cohesive regions at the gradient extremes exhibit dynamics with a longer time constant, an effect that is attenuated in schizophrenia. To study computation, we used the gradients to regularise subject-specific recurrent neural networks (RNNs) trained on working memory tasks. Networks endowed with greater gradient spread learned more efficiently, plateaued at lower task loss, and maintained stronger alignment to the prescribed AS hierarchical geometry. Fixed point linearisation showed that high-range networks settled into more stable neural states during memory delay, evidenced by lower energy and smaller maximal Jacobian eigenvalues. This gradient-regularised RNN framework therefore links large-scale cortical architecture with fixed point stability, providing a mechanistic account of how gradient de-differentiation could destabilise neural computations in schizophrenia, convergently supported by empirical timescale flattening and model-based evidence of less stable fixed points.
