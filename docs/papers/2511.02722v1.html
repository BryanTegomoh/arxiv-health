Association-sensory spatiotemporal hierarchy and functional gradient-regularised recurrent neural network with implications for schizophrenia

arXiv ID: 2511.02722v1
Published: 2025-11-04
Authors: Subati Abulikemu, Puria Radmard, Michail Mamalakis, John Suckling
Categories: q-bio.NC, cs.LG

Summary

This study investigated the sensory-to-association (AS) cortical hierarchy in schizophrenia patients compared to controls, revealing a compressed hierarchy and reduced functional differentiation in patients. Using fMRI, neural timescale modeling, and gradient-regularized recurrent neural networks (RNNs), the research demonstrated that this de-differentiation leads to flattened neural timescales and less stable neural computations, offering a mechanistic explanation for cognitive deficits in schizophrenia.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a mechanistic understanding of how altered large-scale brain architecture, specifically a compressed AS hierarchy, contributes to cognitive deficits and computational instability in schizophrenia. This deepened insight could inform the development of novel diagnostic biomarkers, therapeutic targets, or personalized computational interventions for the disorder.

AI Health Application

The research applies recurrent neural networks (RNNs) to model brain function and dysfunction associated with schizophrenia. The RNN framework is used to link large-scale cortical architecture (AS hierarchy) with neural computational stability, offering a mechanistic account of how observed brain changes in schizophrenia could destabilize neural computations. This represents an application of AI for understanding disease mechanisms, which is foundational for developing future diagnostic tools, prognostic indicators, and targeted therapies for mental health conditions.

Key Points

  • Schizophrenia is associated with a compressed sensory-to-association (AS) cortical hierarchy, indicating reduced functional differentiation in patients compared to controls.
  • Individual AS gradients were extracted from a large fMRI dataset (N=355) via spectral analysis of brain connectivity, with hierarchical specialisation quantified by gradient spread.
  • Modeling neural timescales with the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process showed that the most specialised, locally cohesive regions in controls exhibit longer time constants, an effect significantly attenuated in schizophrenia.
  • Gradient-regularized recurrent neural networks (RNNs) trained on working memory tasks demonstrated that networks with greater AS gradient spread (higher differentiation) learned more efficiently, achieved lower task loss, and maintained stronger alignment to the prescribed hierarchical geometry.
  • Fixed point linearisation of these RNNs revealed that high-range networks settled into more stable neural states during memory delay, evidenced by lower energy and smaller maximal Jacobian eigenvalues.

Methodology

The study utilized a large fMRI dataset (N=355) to extract individual sensory-to-association (AS) gradients through spectral analysis of brain connectivity. Hierarchical specialisation was quantified by gradient spread. Neural timescales were modeled using the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process. To study computation, subject-specific recurrent neural networks (RNNs) were regularized by these gradients and trained on working memory tasks. Computational stability was analyzed through fixed point linearisation of the RNNs, assessing energy and maximal Jacobian eigenvalues of neural states.

Key Findings

Schizophrenia patients exhibited a compressed AS cortical hierarchy, indicating reduced functional differentiation. Specialized brain regions in healthy controls demonstrated longer neural time constants, an effect attenuated in schizophrenia. Gradient-regularized RNNs with greater hierarchical spread learned more efficiently, showed lower task loss, and better aligned with the AS geometry. Fixed point analysis of these RNNs revealed that higher gradient spread led to more stable neural states during memory delay, characterized by lower energy and smaller maximal Jacobian eigenvalues. This suggests a direct link between cortical de-differentiation and computational instability in schizophrenia.

Clinical Impact

This research enhances our understanding of the neuropathophysiology of schizophrenia, particularly how abnormalities in large-scale cortical organization affect cognitive function. It opens avenues for developing fMRI-based biomarkers to diagnose or monitor schizophrenia progression based on AS gradient characteristics or neural timescale alterations. Furthermore, the mechanistic framework could guide the development of computational models for personalized treatment strategies, potentially identifying targets for cognitive remediation interventions aimed at restoring functional differentiation and computational stability.

Abstract

The human neocortex is functionally organised at its highest level along a continuous sensory-to-association (AS) hierarchy. This study characterises the AS hierarchy of patients with schizophrenia in a comparison with controls. Using a large fMRI dataset (N=355), we extracted individual AS gradients via spectral analysis of brain connectivity, quantified hierarchical specialisation by gradient spread, and related this spread with connectivity geometry. We found that schizophrenia compresses the AS hierarchy indicating reduced functional differentiation. By modelling neural timescale with the Ornstein-Uhlenbeck process, we observed that the most specialised, locally cohesive regions at the gradient extremes exhibit dynamics with a longer time constant, an effect that is attenuated in schizophrenia. To study computation, we used the gradients to regularise subject-specific recurrent neural networks (RNNs) trained on working memory tasks. Networks endowed with greater gradient spread learned more efficiently, plateaued at lower task loss, and maintained stronger alignment to the prescribed AS hierarchical geometry. Fixed point linearisation showed that high-range networks settled into more stable neural states during memory delay, evidenced by lower energy and smaller maximal Jacobian eigenvalues. This gradient-regularised RNN framework therefore links large-scale cortical architecture with fixed point stability, providing a mechanistic account of how gradient de-differentiation could destabilise neural computations in schizophrenia, convergently supported by empirical timescale flattening and model-based evidence of less stable fixed points.
