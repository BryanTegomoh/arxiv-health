Adapting Neural Audio Codecs to EEG

Summary

This paper demonstrates that pretrained neural audio codecs, specifically the state-of-the-art DAC, can be effectively adapted for EEG compression despite inherent modality differences. By preprocessing raw EEG data to match the codec's input constraints and fine-tuning on EEG, stable and high-fidelity reconstructions are achieved. The approach, including a multi-channel extension (DAC-MC), is shown to preserve clinically relevant information on medical EEG datasets.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a novel and efficient method for compressing Electroencephalography (EEG) data, which is crucial for reducing storage demands, facilitating faster data transfer for remote analysis, and potentially enabling real-time processing in neurological diagnostics and monitoring.

AI Health Application

The paper describes an AI application for efficient compression of medical sensor data (EEG). This can facilitate the storage, transmission, and processing of large volumes of EEG data, which is crucial for remote diagnostics, telemedicine, and building large-scale datasets for training other medical AI models for the diagnosis and monitoring of neurological disorders like epilepsy and other brain abnormalities. By preserving 'clinically relevant information,' this work supports the development of robust AI systems for clinical decision support and analysis.

Methodology

The methodology involves adapting a state-of-the-art neural audio codec (DAC) by preprocessing raw EEG signals to conform to its stride-based framing input. The audio-pretrained encoder-decoder components of DAC are directly reused and then fine-tuned on EEG specific data. Compression-quality trade-offs are investigated by adjusting parameters such as residual codebook depth, codebook size, and input sampling rate. For multi-channel EEG, a novel extension named DAC-MC is introduced, which incorporates attention-based cross-channel aggregation and channel-specific decoding while maintaining the audio-pretrained initialization. Evaluation is performed on clinically relevant datasets, TUH Abnormal and Epilepsy, using spectrogram-based reconstruction loss and downstream classification accuracy as key metrics.

Key Findings

The study's key findings are that pretrained neural audio codecs can be successfully adapted for stable and high-fidelity EEG reconstruction, with significant improvements from fine-tuning on EEG data. The proposed multi-channel extension, DAC-MC, effectively captures spatial dependencies inherent in multi-electrode EEG. Crucially, the adapted compression methods are demonstrated to preserve clinically relevant information, as evidenced by robust performance in reconstruction quality and downstream diagnostic classification tasks.

Abstract

EEG and audio are inherently distinct modalities, differing in sampling rate, channel structure, and scale. Yet, we show that pretrained neural audio codecs can serve as effective starting points for EEG compression, provided that the data are preprocessed to be suitable to the codec's input constraints. Using DAC, a state-of-the-art neural audio codec as our base, we demonstrate that raw EEG can be mapped into the codec's stride-based framing, enabling direct reuse of the audio-pretrained encoder-decoder. Even without modification, this setup yields stable EEG reconstructions, and fine-tuning on EEG data further improves fidelity and generalization compared to training from scratch. We systematically explore compression-quality trade-offs by varying residual codebook depth, codebook (vocabulary) size, and input sampling rate. To capture spatial dependencies across electrodes, we propose DAC-MC, a multi-channel extension with attention-based cross-channel aggregation and channel-specific decoding, while retaining the audio-pretrained initialization. Evaluations on the TUH Abnormal and Epilepsy datasets show that the adapted codecs preserve clinically relevant information, as reflected in spectrogram-based reconstruction loss and downstream classification accuracy.
