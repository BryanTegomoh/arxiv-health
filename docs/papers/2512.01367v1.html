A Fine Evaluation Method for Cube Copying Test for Early Detection of Alzheimer's Disease

Published: 2025-12-01
Authors: Xinyu Jiang, Cuiyun Gao, Wenda Huang, Yiyang Jiang, Binwen Luo, Yuxin Jiang, Mengting Wang, Haoran Wen, Yang Zhao, Xuemei Chen, Songqun Huang
Categories: cs.LG

Summary

This paper introduces a novel fine evaluation method for the Cube Copying Test (CCT) to improve early detection of Alzheimer's Disease (AD) by addressing the limitations of traditional binary scoring. Utilizing dynamic handwriting analysis and a BiLSTM-Attention neural network, the method achieves 86.69% accuracy in classifying cognitive impairment, revealing nuanced correlations between drawing ability scores, age, and education levels. This provides a more objective and comprehensive tool for early screening and personalized intervention for visual spatial cognitive impairment.

Medical Relevance

This method offers a more nuanced and accurate tool for the early detection of visual spatial cognitive impairment, a key early clinical manifestation of Alzheimer's Disease. By overcoming the limitations of traditional binary scoring, it can enable earlier and more precise identification of individuals at risk, leading to more timely diagnoses and tailored interventions.

AI Health Application

The study develops an AI model (BiLSTM-Attention network) to extract and analyze dynamic handwriting features from the Cube Copying Test. This AI application aims to provide a more objective and accurate method for early screening and detection of Alzheimer's Disease and Mild Cognitive Impairment, thereby supporting personalized intervention strategies for cognitive impairment.

Key Points

  • Addresses the limitations of the MoCA's CCT '0/1' binary scoring method, which leads to bias in evaluating visual spatial cognitive ability, particularly for elders with less formal education.
  • Proposes a 'fine evaluation method' for the CCT based on dynamic handwriting feature extraction using independently developed Cogni-CareV3.0 software.
  • The methodology involves extracting spatial and motion features from segmented dynamic handwriting, normalizing unequal-dimensional feature matrices, and employing a BiLSTM-Attention model for classification.
  • Achieved a classification accuracy of 86.69%, demonstrating significant superiority compared to existing similar studies.
  • Revealed significant regularity in cube drawing ability scores when comparing MCI patients and healthy controls, and also across different age groups and education levels.
  • Identified a negative correlation between scores for all cognitive tasks (including cube drawing) and age, and a significant positive correlation with levels of education.
  • Aims to provide a more objective and comprehensive evaluation method for early screening and personalized intervention for visual spatial cognitive impairment associated with AD.

Methodology

The study utilized Cogni-CareV3.0 software to collect dynamic handwriting data during the Cube Copying Test. It then extracted spatial and motion features from segmented dynamic handwriting, normalized these unequal-dimensional feature matrices, and classified the data using a bidirectional long short-term memory network model combined with an attention mechanism (BiLSTM-Attention).

Key Findings

The proposed dynamic handwriting-based CCT evaluation method achieved a high classification accuracy of 86.69%, outperforming comparable studies. It demonstrated that cube drawing ability scores exhibit significant regularity across MCI patients, healthy controls, age, and education levels. Crucially, scores for cognitive tasks, including cube drawing, were found to be negatively correlated with age and positively correlated with education levels.

Clinical Impact

This fine evaluation method offers a practical advancement for early AD screening by providing a more objective and detailed assessment of visual spatial cognitive function, circumventing the bias of traditional binary scoring methods. It could lead to earlier, more accurate identification of individuals with incipient cognitive impairment, facilitating personalized intervention strategies and improving the monitoring of disease progression in clinical settings.

Abstract

Background: Impairment of visual spatial cognitive function is the most common early clinical manifestation of Alzheimer's Disease (AD). When the Montreal Cognitive Assessment (MoCA) uses the "0/1" binary method ("pass/fail") to evaluate the visual spatial cognitive ability represented by the Cube Copying Test(CCT), the elder with less formal education generally score 0 point, resulting in serious bias in the evaluation results. Therefore, this study proposes a fine evaluation method for CCT based on dynamic handwriting feature extraction of DH-SCSM-BLA. method : The Cogni-CareV3.0 software independently developed by our team was used to collect dynamic handwriting data of CCT. Then, the spatial and motion features of segmented dynamic handwriting were extracted, and feature matrix with unequal dimensions were normalized. Finally, a bidirectional long short-term memory network model combined with attention mechanism (BiLSTM-Attention) was adopted for classification. Result: The experimental results showed that: The proposed method has significant superiority compared to similar studies, with a classification accuracy of 86.69%. The distribution of cube drawing ability scores has significant regularity for three aspects such as MCI patients and healthy control group, age, and levels of education. It was also found that score for each cognitive task including cube drawing ability score is negatively correlated with age. Score for each cognitive task including cube drawing ability score, but positively correlated with levels of education significantly. Conclusion: This study provides a relatively objective and comprehensive evaluation method for early screening and personalized intervention of visual spatial cognitive impairment.
