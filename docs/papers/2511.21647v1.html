Fast 3D Ultrasound Localization Microscopy via Projection-based Processing Framework

arXiv ID: 2511.21647v1
Published: 2025-11-26
Authors: Jingke Zhang, Jingyi Yin, U-Wai Lok, Lijie Huang, Ryan M. DeRuiter, Tao Wu, Kaipeng Ji, Yanzhe Zhao, James D. Krier, Xiang-yang Zhu, Lilach O. Lerman, Chengwu Huang, Shigao Chen

View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a novel projection-based processing framework for 3D Ultrasound Localization Microscopy (ULM) that dramatically reduces computational demands, enabling fast vascular reconstruction. By reformulating all pipeline steps into efficient 2D operations, the framework achieves near real-time processing speeds in an *in vivo* pig kidney model, maintaining image quality comparable to conventional 3D methods. This breakthrough offers the potential for real-time feedback during scanning, promising to improve diagnostic reliability and accelerate clinical workflows.

Medical Relevance

Real-time, high-resolution 3D visualization of microvasculature is crucial for diagnosing various diseases, guiding interventions, and monitoring treatment responses. This innovation addresses the primary technical hurdle hindering the widespread clinical adoption of 3D ULM, making it a viable tool for immediate diagnostic feedback.

AI Health Application

While the paper describes an optimized computational processing framework rather than an AI algorithm itself, its advancements enable crucial capabilities for future medical AI applications. Specifically, achieving real-time processing, improved robustness, and reduced operator dependence for a high-resolution imaging modality like 3D ULM creates a fertile ground for AI algorithms. AI can then be applied to this real-time, high-quality data for automated diagnosis, quantitative analysis of vascular structures, disease progression monitoring, and enhanced procedural guidance, making the overall workflow more intelligent and efficient.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical bottleneck of 3D ULM: exponential growth in voxel count leading to heavy computational demands and extensive post-processing time.
  • Proposes a projection-based processing framework that reformulates all 3D ULM pipeline steps (beamforming, clutter filtering, motion estimation, microbubble separation, localization) into computationally efficient 2D operations.
  • Validated using an RCA-based 3D *in vivo* pig kidney ULM study, demonstrating its effectiveness in a biological setting.
  • Achieves rapid reconstruction, processing a 0.75-s ensemble (400 Hz, 25x27.4x27.4 mm3 volume) in just 0.52 seconds (70% of acquisition time) on a single RTX A6000 Ada GPU.
  • Maintains high ULM image quality, quantitatively comparable to conventional 3D processing, with a structural similarity index (SSIM) of 0.93 for density maps.
  • Demonstrates excellent quantitative agreement in blood flow velocity, achieving a voxel-wise velocity agreement with a slope of 0.93 and R2 = 0.88 relative to conventional 3D results.
  • Presents the first demonstration of the potential for real-time feedback during 3D ULM scanning, paving the way for improved robustness, reduced operator dependence, and accelerated clinical workflows.

Methodology

The study reformulated the entire 3D ULM pipeline‚Äîincluding beamforming, clutter filtering, motion estimation, microbubble separation, and localization‚Äîinto a series of computationally efficient 2D operations using a projection-based framework. This was implemented and tested on RCA-based 3D *in vivo* pig kidney ULM data. The processing speed was evaluated on a single RTX A6000 Ada GPU, and ULM image quality and quantitative velocity agreement were compared against conventional 3D processing methods using SSIM and voxel-wise velocity analysis.

Key Findings

The proposed framework significantly reduces processing time, reconstructing a 0.75-s, 400 Hz ensemble covering a 25x27.4x27.4 mm3 volume in 0.52 s (70% of acquisition time). This speed is achieved while preserving ULM image quality, demonstrated by an SSIM of 0.93 for density maps and a strong voxel-wise velocity agreement (slope 0.93, R2 0.88) compared to conventional 3D ULM.

Clinical Impact

This research provides a pathway for the first-time potential of real-time 3D ULM feedback during clinical scanning. This capability is expected to significantly enhance diagnostic robustness, reduce the variability introduced by operator dependence, and streamline clinical workflows by providing immediate, detailed microvascular information. It could transform the utility of ULM from a research tool into a practical, rapid diagnostic imaging modality.

Limitations

Not explicitly stated in the abstract. However, the study was conducted using an *in vivo* pig kidney model, indicating that further validation in human subjects across various anatomical locations will be necessary for full clinical translation and widespread adoption.

Future Directions

The primary future direction is the translation of this framework to human clinical studies to validate its efficacy and safety in diverse patient populations. Further integration into existing real-time ultrasound platforms and exploration of its application in various organs and disease states are also crucial steps to realize its full potential in improving diagnostic accuracy and clinical efficiency.

Abstract

Three-dimensional ultrasound localization microscopy (ULM) enables comprehensive visualization of the vasculature, thereby improving diagnostic reliability. Nevertheless, its clinical translation remains challenging, as the exponential growth in voxel count for full 3D reconstruction imposes heavy computational demands and extensive post-processing time. In this row-column array (RCA)-based 3D in vivo pig kidney ULM study, we reformulate each step of the full 3D ULM pipeline, including beamforming, clutter filtering, motion estimation, microbubble separation and localization into a series of computational-efficient 2D operations, substantially reducing the number of voxels to be processed while maintaining comparable accuracy. The proposed framework reconstructs each 0.75-s ensemble acquired at frame rate of 400 Hz, covering a 25*27.4*27.4 mm3 volume, in 0.52 s (70% of the acquisition time) on a single RTX A6000 Ada GPU, while maintaining ULM image quality comparable to conventional 3D processing. Quantitatively, it achieves a structural similarity index (SSIM) of 0.93 between density maps and a voxel-wise velocity agreement with slope of 0.93 and R2 = 0.88, closely matching conventional 3D results, and for the first time, demonstrating potential for real-time feedback during scanning, which could improve robustness, reduce operator dependence and accelerate clinical workflows.
