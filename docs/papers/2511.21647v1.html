Summary

This paper introduces a novel projection-based processing framework for 3D Ultrasound Localization Microscopy (ULM) that significantly reduces computational demands while maintaining high image quality. By reformulating complex 3D ULM processing steps into efficient 2D operations, the framework enables rapid reconstruction of vascular images, demonstrating potential for real-time feedback during clinical scans. This advancement addresses the current bottleneck of slow post-processing and high computational cost in 3D ULM, making it more viable for widespread clinical application.

Medical Relevance

This innovation addresses a critical bottleneck in 3D ULM, making comprehensive visualization of microvasculature more practical and accessible for diagnostic applications. The ability to provide real-time feedback during scans could significantly improve diagnostic reliability, reduce procedural time, and enhance efficiency across various medical disciplines reliant on detailed vascular imaging.

Key Points

  • Conventional 3D ULM faces significant computational hurdles and lengthy post-processing due to high voxel counts, impeding its clinical translation for comprehensive vasculature visualization.
  • The proposed solution is a projection-based processing framework that reformulates the entire 3D ULM pipeline (including beamforming, clutter filtering, motion estimation, microbubble separation, and localization) into a series of computationally efficient 2D operations.
  • The methodology was validated using a row-column array (RCA)-based 3D in vivo pig kidney ULM study, demonstrating its practical application in a biologically relevant model.
  • The framework achieves remarkable computational efficiency, reconstructing a 0.75-s data ensemble (400 Hz, covering a 25x27.4x27.4 mm¬≥ volume) in just 0.52 seconds (70% of acquisition time) on a single RTX A6000 Ada GPU.
  • It maintains ULM image quality comparable to conventional 3D processing, evidenced by a high structural similarity index (SSIM) of 0.93 between density maps.
  • Quantitative accuracy for flow dynamics is also high, with a voxel-wise velocity agreement showing a slope of 0.93 and an R¬≤ of 0.88 when compared to conventional 3D results.
  • This is the first demonstration of 3D ULM processing speed capable of offering real-time feedback during scanning, which promises improved robustness, reduced operator dependence, and accelerated clinical workflows.

Abstract

Three-dimensional ultrasound localization microscopy (ULM) enables comprehensive visualization of the vasculature, thereby improving diagnostic reliability. Nevertheless, its clinical translation remains challenging, as the exponential growth in voxel count for full 3D reconstruction imposes heavy computational demands and extensive post-processing time. In this row-column array (RCA)-based 3D in vivo pig kidney ULM study, we reformulate each step of the full 3D ULM pipeline, including beamforming, clutter filtering, motion estimation, microbubble separation and localization into a series of computational-efficient 2D operations, substantially reducing the number of voxels to be processed while maintaining comparable accuracy. The proposed framework reconstructs each 0.75-s ensemble acquired at frame rate of 400 Hz, covering a 25*27.4*27.4 mm3 volume, in 0.52 s (70% of the acquisition time) on a single RTX A6000 Ada GPU, while maintaining ULM image quality comparable to conventional 3D processing. Quantitatively, it achieves a structural similarity index (SSIM) of 0.93 between density maps and a voxel-wise velocity agreement with slope of 0.93 and R2 = 0.88, closely matching conventional 3D results, and for the first time, demonstrating potential for real-time feedback during scanning, which could improve robustness, reduce operator dependence and accelerate clinical workflows.
