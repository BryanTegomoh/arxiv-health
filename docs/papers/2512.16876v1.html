Summary

This paper presents a novel application of Federated Learning (FL) to overcome the challenges of data scarcity and privacy in diagnosing rare diseases, specifically Collagen VI-related dystrophies (COL6-RD). By enabling collaborative AI model training across two international organizations while keeping patient data localized, the developed FL model significantly improved the accuracy and generalizability of classifying COL6-RD pathogenic mechanisms, outperforming isolated institutional models.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant as it provides a scalable and privacy-preserving framework for leveraging distributed rare disease data, which is crucial for advancing diagnosis and research in areas historically hampered by data siloing. It offers a tangible path towards more accurate and accessible diagnostic tools for conditions like COL6-RD, ultimately benefiting patient care.

AI Health Application

The paper applies Federated Learning (a subset of AI/ML) to develop an ML model for the diagnosis of collagen VI-related dystrophies. This AI model classifies patient immunofluorescence microscopy images into specific pathogenic mechanism groups. The application enables collaborative AI model training across decentralized medical institutions while preserving patient data privacy, leading to more accurate and generalized diagnostic tools for a rare genetic disease, and aiding in variant interpretation and sequencing strategy guidance.

Methodology

The study utilized a Federated Learning (FL) framework powered by the Sherpa.ai FL platform. The FL model was collaboratively trained across decentralized datasets located in two international organizations. The input data consisted of collagen VI immunofluorescence microscopy images obtained from patient-derived fibroblast cultures. The model's objective was to classify these images into three specific pathogenic mechanism groups associated with COL6-RD. Performance was evaluated using the F1-score and compared against models trained on data from single organizations.

Key Findings

The global Federated Learning model achieved a robust F1-score of 0.82 in classifying COL6-RD pathogenic mechanisms. This significantly outperformed individual models trained on data from single organizations, which showed F1-scores ranging from 0.57 to 0.75, demonstrating a substantial improvement in diagnostic utility and generalizability.

Clinical Impact

This approach has the potential to lead to more accurate and generalizable diagnostic tools for COL6-RD and other rare diseases, enabling faster and more confident diagnoses. It can also support the interpretation of variants of uncertain significance (VUS) and guide the prioritization of genetic sequencing strategies, ultimately improving patient management and accelerating the identification of novel pathogenic variants.

Abstract

The application of Machine Learning (ML) to the diagnosis of rare diseases, such as collagen VI-related dystrophies (COL6-RD), is fundamentally limited by the scarcity and fragmentation of available data. Attempts to expand sampling across hospitals, institutions, or countries with differing regulations face severe privacy, regulatory, and logistical obstacles that are often difficult to overcome. The Federated Learning (FL) provides a promising solution by enabling collaborative model training across decentralized datasets while keeping patient data local and private. Here, we report a novel global FL initiative using the Sherpa.ai FL platform, which leverages FL across distributed datasets in two international organizations for the diagnosis of COL6-RD, using collagen VI immunofluorescence microscopy images from patient-derived fibroblast cultures. Our solution resulted in an ML model capable of classifying collagen VI patient images into the three primary pathogenic mechanism groups associated with COL6-RD: exon skipping, glycine substitution, and pseudoexon insertion. This new approach achieved an F1-score of 0.82, outperforming single-organization models (0.57-0.75). These results demonstrate that FL substantially improves diagnostic utility and generalizability compared to isolated institutional models. Beyond enabling more accurate diagnosis, we anticipate that this approach will support the interpretation of variants of uncertain significance and guide the prioritization of sequencing strategies to identify novel pathogenic variants.
