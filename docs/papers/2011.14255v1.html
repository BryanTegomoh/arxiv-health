Optimal vaccination program for two infectious diseases with cross immunity

arXiv ID: 2011.14255v1
Published: 2020-11-29
Authors: Yang Ye, Qingpeng Zhang, Zhidong Cao, Daniel Dajun Zeng
Categories: q-bio.PE, physics.soc-ph
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00

Summary

This paper develops a two-layer Susceptible-Infected-Removed (SIR) model to identify optimal vaccination programs for two infectious diseases with cross-immunity, aiming to prevent outbreaks at minimum cost. It identifies three distinct scenarios for optimal vaccination and provides an analytical criterion based on vaccine costs to specify the best program.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a framework for public health agencies to make data-driven decisions on vaccine allocation when facing multiple diseases with complex immunological interactions, optimizing resource use to achieve maximum population protection and cost-effectiveness.

AI Health Application

The paper utilizes a Susceptible-Infected-Removed (SIR) mathematical model, which is a traditional epidemiological modeling approach. It does not describe or apply AI/machine learning technologies to health.

Key Points

  • Investigates optimal vaccination strategies for two infectious diseases exhibiting cross-immunity, circulating simultaneously within the same population.
  • Employs a two-layer Susceptible-Infected-Removed (SIR) epidemiological model to simulate disease spread and vaccine impact.
  • The primary objective is to identify vaccination programs that prevent outbreaks of *both* diseases while minimizing the total associated vaccination cost.
  • Assumes the availability of vaccines for both diseases, making resource allocation a key challenge.
  • Identifies three distinct scenarios that characterize the configuration of the optimal vaccination program.
  • Derives an analytical criterion that precisely specifies which of the three optimal program scenarios is most appropriate.
  • This analytical criterion is fundamentally based on the relative costs associated with the vaccines for each disease.

Methodology

The study utilizes a mathematical modeling approach, specifically a 'two-layer Susceptible-Infected-Removed (SIR) model,' to simulate the co-circulation of two diseases with cross-immunity. The core methodology involves analytical derivation to identify optimal vaccination strategies that minimize cost while ensuring the prevention of disease outbreaks.

Key Findings

The primary findings include the identification of three distinct scenarios that characterize the optimal vaccination program for preventing outbreaks of both diseases under varying conditions. Crucially, the paper analytically derives a specific criterion that, based on the relative costs of the two vaccines, precisely determines which of these optimal scenarios should be implemented.

This work offers a quantitative tool for public health decision-makers to design more efficient and cost-effective vaccination campaigns in complex epidemiological environments. It can guide vaccine procurement and deployment strategies, particularly in settings where resources are limited and multiple pathogens with immunological cross-talk are present, ultimately improving population health outcomes.

Abstract

There are often multiple diseases with cross immunity competing for vaccination resources. Here we investigate the optimal vaccination program in a two-layer Susceptible-Infected-Removed (SIR) model, where two diseases with cross immunity spread in the same population, and vaccines for both diseases are available. We identify three scenarios of the optimal vaccination program, which prevents the outbreaks of both diseases at the minimum cost. We analytically derive a criterion to specify the optimal program based on the costs for different vaccines.
