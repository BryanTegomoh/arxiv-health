Conveying Imagistic Thinking in Traditional Chinese Medicine Translation: A Prompt Engineering and LLM-Based Evaluation Framework

arXiv ID: 2512.01198v1
Published: 2025-12-01
Authors: Jiatong Han
Categories: cs.CL
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This study addresses the challenge of translating Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) theories, which are based on imagistic thinking (metaphor and metonymy), into English without losing their underlying conceptual networks crucial for clinical application. Utilizing a human-in-the-loop framework and prompt engineering with DeepSeek V3.1, the research developed prompt-adjusted LLM translations that significantly outperformed human and baseline machine translations across five cognitive dimensions, as evaluated by simulated readers powered by ChatGPT 5 Pro and Gemini 2.5 Pro.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and conceptually rich translation of ancient medical texts like the Huangdi Neijing is critical for the global understanding, study, and clinical application of Traditional Chinese Medicine. By improving the transfer of imagistic thinking, this research enables non-Chinese speaking practitioners and researchers to better grasp the core principles, diagnostic logic, and therapeutic strategies of TCM, potentially enhancing its integration and evidence-based practice worldwide.

AI Health Application

The paper applies Large Language Models (LLMs) and prompt engineering techniques to improve the translation of complex medical texts from Traditional Chinese Medicine. This AI application aims to make 'medical principles and diagnostic and therapeutic logic' more accessible and applicable in 'clinical practice' by overcoming linguistic and conceptual barriers, thereby facilitating the understanding and potential integration of TCM knowledge into broader healthcare contexts.

Key Points

  • Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) theory relies heavily on imagistic thinking, using metaphor and metonymy to structure medical principles, diagnostics, and therapeutics.
  • Existing English translations often employ literal rendering, hindering target-language readers from reconstructing crucial conceptual networks and applying TCM theory clinically.
  • A human-in-the-loop (HITL) framework was implemented, using prompt-based cognitive scaffolding to guide DeepSeek V3.1 in identifying and conveying metaphor/metonymy from Huangdi Neijing passages.
  • Evaluation involved ChatGPT 5 Pro and Gemini 2.5 Pro simulating three types of real-world readers to score human, baseline, and prompt-adjusted LLM translations across five cognitive dimensions.
  • Prompt-adjusted LLM translations consistently performed best across all five cognitive dimensions, demonstrating high cross-model and cross-role consistency among simulated evaluators.
  • Structured interviews and Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis (IPA) revealed insights into human vs. machine translation differences, effective strategies for metaphor/metonymy transfer, and reader cognitive preferences.
  • The study establishes a cognitive, efficient, and replicable HITL methodological pathway for translating ancient, concept-dense medical texts like those in TCM.

Methodology

The study employed a human-in-the-loop (HITL) framework. Four passages from Huangdi Neijing were selected. DeepSeek V3.1 was guided via prompt-based cognitive scaffolding to identify and convey metaphor and metonymy in translation. For evaluation, ChatGPT 5 Pro and Gemini 2.5 Pro were prompted to simulate three real-world reader types. These simulated readers scored human translations, baseline LLM translations, and prompt-adjusted LLM translations across five cognitive dimensions. The evaluation also included structured interviews and Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis (IPA) to explore qualitative aspects.

Key Findings

Prompt-adjusted LLM translations significantly outperformed human and baseline machine translations across all five cognitive dimensions, exhibiting high consistency across different evaluation models and simulated reader roles. Interview themes highlighted distinct differences between human and machine translation approaches, identified effective strategies for transferring metaphor and metonymy, and elucidated readers' specific cognitive preferences when engaging with TCM concepts.

Clinical Impact

This research provides a pathway to create more accurate and conceptually robust English translations of fundamental TCM texts, directly supporting the education and training of future TCM practitioners globally. Improved understanding of TCM's imagistic thinking will enable better clinical reasoning, more precise diagnostic interpretations, and more effective therapeutic applications for non-native Chinese speakers, thereby enhancing the global accessibility and practical utility of TCM knowledge in clinical settings.

Abstract

Traditional Chinese Medicine (TCM) theory is built on imagistic thinking, in which medical principles and diagnostic and therapeutic logic are structured through metaphor and metonymy. However, existing English translations largely rely on literal rendering, making it difficult for target-language readers to reconstruct the underlying conceptual networks and apply them in clinical practice. This study adopted a human-in-the-loop (HITL) framework and selected four passages from the medical canon Huangdi Neijing that are fundamental in theory. Through prompt-based cognitive scaffolding, DeepSeek V3.1 was guided to identify metaphor and metonymy in the source text and convey the theory in translation. In the evaluation stage, ChatGPT 5 Pro and Gemini 2.5 Pro were instructed by prompts to simulate three types of real-world readers. Human translations, baseline model translations, and prompt-adjusted translations were scored by the simulated readers across five cognitive dimensions, followed by structured interviews and Interpretative Phenomenological Analysis (IPA). Results show that the prompt-adjusted LLM translations perform best across all five dimensions, with high cross-model and cross-role consistency. The interview themes reveal differences between human and machine translation, effective strategies for metaphor and metonymy transfer, and readers' cognitive preferences. This study provides a cognitive, efficient, and replicable HITL methodological pathway for the translation of ancient, concept-dense texts such as TCM.
