Protein aggregation in Huntington's disease

Summary

This paper investigates protein aggregation in Huntington's disease (HD), proposing two primary mechanisms: hydrogen bonding via polar-zipper formation and covalent bonding catalyzed by transglutaminase. While cell culture models indicate hydrogen bonds are the main stabilizers with likely covalent contributions, the crucial nature of these bonds in the brains of HD patients remains unknown, posing a significant challenge for therapeutic development.

Medical Relevance

Understanding the precise molecular mechanisms of protein aggregation in Huntington's disease is paramount, as it directly dictates the potential targets and strategies for developing disease-modifying therapies to prevent neuronal death.

Key Points

  • Huntington's disease pathogenesis involves a toxic gain of function from expanded polyglutamine in the huntingtin protein, leading to protein aggregation.
  • Protein aggregation is considered a primary cause of neuronal death in HD.
  • Two main mechanisms for protein aggregation stabilization are proposed: non-covalent hydrogen bonding (polar-zipper formation) and covalent bonding through transglutaminase-catalyzed cross-linking.
  • In cell culture models of HD, aggregates are predominantly stabilized by hydrogen bonds, although covalent bonds are also considered likely to occur.
  • A critical knowledge gap exists regarding the specific nature of the bonds stabilizing aggregates in the brains of patients with Huntington's disease.
  • Understanding the type of bond stabilizing aggregates is posited as a fundamental question, directly influencing the design of effective therapeutic strategies for HD.

Methodology

The paper references observations made in 'cell culture models' of Huntington's disease to study the nature of protein aggregates and their stabilizing bonds.

Key Findings

In cell culture models of Huntington's disease, protein aggregates are primarily stabilized by hydrogen bonds, with a probable contribution from covalent bonds. However, the exact nature of these stabilizing bonds within human HD patient brains remains undetermined.

Clinical Impact

Identifying the dominant type of bond stabilizing aggregates in human brains would enable the development of highly specific therapeutic interventions, such as small molecules or enzyme inhibitors, designed to disrupt these particular bonds or prevent their formation, thereby potentially halting or slowing neuronal degeneration in HD patients.

Limitations

The major limitation highlighted is the lack of knowledge concerning the actual nature and types of bonds that stabilize protein aggregates within the brains of patients with Huntington's disease, indicating that findings from cell culture models may not fully extrapolate to the in vivo human condition.

Future Directions

The paper strongly implies that future research must focus on elucidating the nature of bonds stabilizing aggregates specifically within the brains of HD patients, as this knowledge is deemed essential for guiding and conditioning effective therapeutic approaches.

Abstract

The presence of an expanded polyglutamine produces a toxic gain of function in huntingtin. Protein aggregation resulting from this gain of function is likely to be the cause of neuronal death. Two main mechanisms of aggregation have been proposed: hydrogen bonding by polar-zipper formation and covalent bonding by transglutaminase-catalyzed cross-linking. In cell culture models of Huntington's disease, aggregates are mostly stabilized by hydrogen bonds, but covalent bonds are also likely to occur. Nothing is known about the nature of the bonds that stabilize the aggregates in the brain of patients with Huntington's disease. It seems that the nature of the bond stabilizing the aggregates is one of the most important questions, as the answer would condition the therapeutic approach to Huntington's disease.
