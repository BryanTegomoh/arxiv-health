Summary

This study comparatively analyzes low-compute rule-based NLP methods and Large Language Models (LLMs) for extracting structured medical insights from Polish Electronic Health Records (EHRs). It finds that rule-based methods offer higher accuracy for demographic data like age and sex, while LLMs provide greater adaptability, scalability, and excel in drug name recognition, even considering translation-induced information loss. The paper advocates for hybrid NLP approaches to achieve reliable and resource-efficient clinical NLP.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing clinical NLP in non-English contexts, particularly where resources are scarce, enabling more accurate and efficient extraction of vital patient data. This capability can significantly improve clinical decision support, epidemiological research, and healthcare management by transforming unstructured notes into actionable insights.

This enables the conversion of unstructured clinical text into structured medical insights, which can be used for various purposes such as improving data accessibility for clinicians, supporting medical research, enhancing public health surveillance, automating coding, and potentially informing clinical decision support systems.

Methodology

A comparative study analyzing information extraction capabilities of low-compute rule-based NLP methods and Large Language Models (LLMs) from unstructured Electronic Health Records (EHR) in Polish. Evaluation focused on extracting patient demographics, clinical findings, and prescribed medications, specifically assessing the influence of text normalisation and translation-induced information loss (Polish to English) on LLM performance.

Key Findings

Rule-based methods achieved higher accuracy for specific information retrieval tasks, notably patient age and sex. LLMs demonstrated superior adaptability and scalability, excelling particularly in drug name recognition. The study revealed significant trade-offs between accuracy, text normalisation, and computational cost across both approaches, and highlighted the impact of translation from original Polish to English on LLM effectiveness.

Clinical Impact

The findings directly contribute to designing more robust and resource-efficient clinical NLP systems, especially vital for non-English healthcare settings with limited resources. By informing the optimal application of rule-based or LLM approaches, or their combination, this research can enhance the precision and reliability of automated medical data extraction, thereby improving clinical workflow, patient care, and medical research capabilities within real-world hospital environments.

Limitations

The abstract implies limitations inherent in deploying NLP in non-English, resource-scarce contexts, necessitating trade-offs between accuracy, normalisation, and computational cost. It highlights that neither pure rule-based nor LLM approaches are universally optimal, underscoring their respective limitations in adaptability versus precision and susceptibility to translation effects.

Future Directions

The study explicitly argues for the development and deployment of hybrid NLP approaches that integrate the high precision of rule-based systems with the greater adaptability and scalability of Large Language Models. This combination is proposed as a practical path toward achieving more reliable and resource-efficient clinical NLP solutions in real-world hospital settings.

Abstract

Extracting structured medical insights from unstructured clinical text using Natural Language Processing (NLP) remains an open challenge in healthcare, particularly in non-English contexts where resources are scarce. This study presents a comparative analysis of NLP low-compute rule-based methods and Large Language Models (LLMs) for information extraction from electronic health records (EHR) obtained from the Voivodeship Rehabilitation Hospital for Children in Ameryka, Poland. We evaluate both approaches by extracting patient demographics, clinical findings, and prescribed medications while examining the effects of lack of text normalisation and translation-induced information loss. Results demonstrate that rule-based methods provide higher accuracy in information retrieval tasks, particularly for age and sex extraction. However, LLMs offer greater adaptability and scalability, excelling in drug name recognition. The effectiveness of the LLMs was compared with texts originally in Polish and those translated into English, assessing the impact of translation. These findings highlight the trade-offs between accuracy, normalisation, and computational cost when deploying NLP in healthcare settings. We argue for hybrid approaches that combine the precision of rule-based systems with the adaptability of LLMs, offering a practical path toward more reliable and resource-efficient clinical NLP in real-world hospitals.
