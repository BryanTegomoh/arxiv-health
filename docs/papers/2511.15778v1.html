Summary

This study comparatively analyzes low-compute rule-based methods and Large Language Models (LLMs) for extracting structured medical insights from unstructured Polish Electronic Health Records (EHRs). It evaluates their performance in extracting patient demographics, clinical findings, and medications, while also assessing the impact of text normalisation absence and translation-induced information loss. The findings highlight accuracy-adaptability trade-offs, advocating for hybrid NLP approaches for efficient clinical deployment in non-English contexts.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing the practical application of NLP in healthcare, especially in non-English speaking regions with scarce resources. By optimizing information extraction from EHRs, it can facilitate clinical decision-making, epidemiological research, and administrative efficiency, ultimately improving patient care.

AI Health Application

The paper applies AI (NLP and LLMs) to extract crucial medical information (patient demographics, clinical findings, prescribed medications) from unstructured clinical text in Electronic Health Records (EHRs). This enables the structuring of data for various downstream healthcare applications such as clinical decision support, medical research, public health surveillance, and hospital administration.

Key Points

  • The study compares rule-based NLP methods and LLMs for information extraction from real-world, unstructured Polish EHR data.
  • It evaluates the extraction of patient demographics (age, sex), clinical findings, and prescribed medications.
  • Rule-based methods demonstrated higher accuracy, particularly for precise information retrieval like age and sex extraction.
  • LLMs exhibited greater adaptability and scalability, performing better in tasks like drug name recognition.
  • The research examined the effects of a lack of text normalisation and the information loss incurred by translating Polish texts to English for LLM processing.
  • Key trade-offs between accuracy, normalisation requirements, and computational cost for NLP deployment in healthcare were identified.
  • The authors propose adopting hybrid NLP approaches that combine the precision of rule-based systems with the adaptability and scalability of LLMs.

Methodology

The study employed a comparative analysis approach, evaluating low-compute rule-based NLP methods against Large Language Models (LLMs). Both methods were applied to extract specific entities (patient demographics like age and sex, clinical findings, and prescribed medications) from unstructured Electronic Health Records (EHR) originating from a Polish rehabilitation hospital. The evaluation also assessed the impact of unnormalized text and information loss from translating Polish EHRs into English for LLM processing.

Key Findings

Rule-based methods achieved higher accuracy in information retrieval tasks, notably for patient age and sex extraction. Conversely, LLMs demonstrated superior adaptability, scalability, and excelled in drug name recognition. The study highlighted significant trade-offs between extraction accuracy, the necessity for text normalisation, and the associated computational costs. Translation of Polish texts to English for LLMs also introduced information loss, impacting effectiveness.

Clinical Impact

The findings advocate for hybrid NLP solutions in clinical settings, combining the high precision of rule-based systems for structured data (e.g., demographics) with the adaptability of LLMs for nuanced tasks (e.g., drug names or clinical findings). This pragmatic approach offers a path toward more reliable, resource-efficient, and scalable clinical NLP tools, particularly for extracting actionable insights from non-English EHRs in real-world hospitals, thereby enhancing data utility for patient care and research.

Limitations

The study implies limitations related to the scarcity of NLP resources for non-English contexts, the challenges of text normalisation, and information loss induced by translation. It also highlights the trade-offs between accuracy, computational cost, and adaptability, suggesting that no single method is universally optimal.

Future Directions

The paper strongly argues for the development and deployment of hybrid NLP approaches that integrate the precision of rule-based systems with the adaptability and scalability of LLMs. This is proposed as a practical path toward more reliable and resource-efficient clinical NLP solutions for real-world hospitals, particularly in non-English speaking environments.

Abstract

Extracting structured medical insights from unstructured clinical text using Natural Language Processing (NLP) remains an open challenge in healthcare, particularly in non-English contexts where resources are scarce. This study presents a comparative analysis of NLP low-compute rule-based methods and Large Language Models (LLMs) for information extraction from electronic health records (EHR) obtained from the Voivodeship Rehabilitation Hospital for Children in Ameryka, Poland. We evaluate both approaches by extracting patient demographics, clinical findings, and prescribed medications while examining the effects of lack of text normalisation and translation-induced information loss. Results demonstrate that rule-based methods provide higher accuracy in information retrieval tasks, particularly for age and sex extraction. However, LLMs offer greater adaptability and scalability, excelling in drug name recognition. The effectiveness of the LLMs was compared with texts originally in Polish and those translated into English, assessing the impact of translation. These findings highlight the trade-offs between accuracy, normalisation, and computational cost when deploying NLP in healthcare settings. We argue for hybrid approaches that combine the precision of rule-based systems with the adaptability of LLMs, offering a practical path toward more reliable and resource-efficient clinical NLP in real-world hospitals.
