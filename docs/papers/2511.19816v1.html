Breaking Bad: Norms for Valence, Arousal, and Dominance for over 10k English Multiword Expressions

arXiv ID: 2511.19816v1
Published: 2025-11-25
Authors: Saif M. Mohammad
Categories: cs.CL
Relevance Score: 0.85 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces the NRC VAD Lexicon v2, a significant expansion of emotion norms providing human-rated Valence, Arousal, and Dominance (VAD) scores for over 10,000 English Multiword Expressions (MWEs) and an additional 25,000 unigram words. This new, highly reliable resource allows for detailed analysis of the emotional characteristics and compositionality of complex linguistic units, serving as a foundational tool for various research fields.

Medical Relevance

This lexicon provides a critical computational linguistic tool for quantifying the emotional content of health-related texts, patient communications, and public health campaigns. By assigning VAD scores to both individual words and complex phrases, it enables more precise sentiment analysis and psychological state inference from textual data in medical and health contexts.

AI Health Application

This lexicon can be used to build or enhance AI models for Natural Language Processing (NLP) in various health applications. For instance, it can power AI systems for: 1) Sentiment analysis and emotion detection in health-related social media, patient forums, or health news to gauge public perception and emotional state. 2) Identifying emotional cues in patient narratives or electronic health records that may indicate distress or specific conditions. 3) Evaluating the emotional impact of health campaigns or warnings. 4) Supporting automated analysis of mental health discourse by providing fine-grained emotional understanding beyond simple sentiment. 5) Understanding the emotional tone of communications related to disease outbreaks or biosecurity events.

Key Points

  • The NRC VAD Lexicon v2 is presented, offering human ratings for Valence, Arousal, and Dominance (VAD) for 10,000 English Multiword Expressions (MWEs) and 25,000 new unigram words, complementing the previous NRC VAD Lexicon v1.
  • VAD are confirmed as the primary dimensions of word meaning, crucial for understanding and quantifying emotional language.
  • The lexicon covers diverse MWE types, including idioms, noun compounds, and verb particle constructions, enabling nuanced analysis beyond single words.
  • The VAD association ratings in the new lexicon are demonstrated to be highly reliable, ensuring data quality for research applications.
  • The research explores the inherent emotionality of MWEs and the degree to which their emotional scores are compositional from their constituent words.
  • This resource is designed to enable a broad spectrum of research in fields such as NLP, Psychology, Public Health, Digital Humanities, and Social Sciences.

Methodology

The core methodology involved collecting human ratings for Valence, Arousal, and Dominance (VAD) dimensions across 10,000 English Multiword Expressions (MWEs) and increasing the coverage of unigram words, notably including those that have become common since 2018. The reliability of these newly generated VAD associations was rigorously assessed, underpinning the quality and utility of the NRC VAD Lexicon v2. The VAD framework itself is based on established factor analysis studies.

Key Findings

['The successful development and validation of the NRC VAD Lexicon v2, significantly expanding VAD coverage to include 10,000 MWEs and substantially more unigrams with high reliability.', 'The lexicon provides a robust quantitative framework to assess the intrinsic emotionality of various multiword expression types.', 'It establishes a foundation for examining the emotional compositionality of MWEs, facilitating studies on how the VAD scores of phrases relate to their constituent words.']

Clinical Impact

['**Enhanced Mental Health Screening:** Improve computational analysis of patient narratives, social media posts, or clinical notes for earlier detection and monitoring of emotional distress, anxiety, depression, or suicidal ideation by recognizing nuanced emotional expressions in MWEs.', '**Optimized Public Health Communication:** Facilitate the design of more effective public health campaigns by enabling precise selection of emotionally impactful language in health messaging, ensuring desired emotional responses and better adherence to health guidelines.', '**Personalized Healthcare Delivery:** Aid in tailoring patient-provider communication and educational materials by assessing the emotional valence and arousal of medical terminology and explanations, improving patient comprehension and satisfaction.', '**Pharmacovigilance and Adverse Event Reporting:** Enable advanced analysis of textual data from adverse event reports to identify emotional patterns or sentiment shifts potentially linked to medication side effects, beyond simple positive/negative sentiment.']

Limitations

Not explicitly stated in the abstract, but potential limitations could include inherent subjectivity in human emotional ratings despite reliability checks, potential cultural biases within the rater pool, the focus solely on the English language, and the possibility of evolving MWE meanings over time not captured by a static lexicon.

Future Directions

The paper emphasizes the lexicon's utility in enabling a wide variety of research, implicitly suggesting future work will involve applying this resource to: developing advanced computational tools for mental health assessment and early intervention, optimizing public health communication strategies for specific populations, conducting large-scale computational psycholinguistic analyses of health-related discourse, and potentially extending this methodology to other languages for cross-cultural health research.

Abstract

Factor analysis studies have shown that the primary dimensions of word meaning are Valence (V), Arousal (A), and Dominance (D). Existing lexicons such as the NRC VAD Lexicon, published in 2018, include VAD association ratings for words. Here, we present a complement to it, which has human ratings of valence, arousal, and dominance for 10k English Multiword Expressions (MWEs) and their constituent words. We also increase the coverage of unigrams, especially words that have become more common since 2018. In all, the new NRC VAD Lexicon v2 now has entries for 10k MWEs and 25k words, in addition to the entries in v1. We show that the associations are highly reliable. We use the lexicon to examine emotional characteristics of MWEs, including: 1. The degree to which MWEs (idioms, noun compounds, and verb particle constructions) exhibit strong emotionality; 2. The degree of emotional compositionality in MWEs. The lexicon enables a wide variety of research in NLP, Psychology, Public Health, Digital Humanities, and Social Sciences. The NRC VAD Lexicon v2 is freely available through the project webpage: http://saifmohammad.com/WebPages/nrc-vad.html
