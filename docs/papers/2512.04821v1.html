LatentFM: A Latent Flow Matching Approach for Generative Medical Image Segmentation

Summary

LatentFM introduces a novel flow-based generative model for medical image segmentation, operating efficiently within a lower-dimensional latent space. It leverages two Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) and a conditional velocity field to produce diverse, highly accurate, and uncertainty-aware segmentation predictions, including explicit confidence maps for clinicians.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and uncertainty-aware medical image segmentation is paramount for precise diagnosis, effective treatment planning, and surgical guidance. LatentFM's ability to provide both highly accurate segmentations and quantifiable confidence maps empowers clinicians with a deeper understanding of model certainty, crucial for informed decision-making in high-stakes medical scenarios.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the development of advanced generative models for automated or semi-automated medical image segmentation. This tool can assist clinicians in accurately identifying and delineating structures, anomalies, or pathologies within medical images (e.g., skin lesions, colonic polyps), thereby aiding diagnosis, treatment planning, and disease monitoring. The provision of uncertainty quantification and confidence maps further enhances its utility by offering clinicians insights into the model's reliability, crucial for informed decision-making in a clinical setting.

Methodology

LatentFM employs two Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) to encode input medical images and their corresponding ground-truth masks into a shared, lower-dimensional latent space. Within this latent space, a conditional velocity field is estimated, conditioned on the input image, to guide a simulation-free flow matching process. By sampling multiple latent representations, the method synthesizes diverse segmentation outputs. The pixel-wise variance across these diverse outputs is then used to quantify uncertainty, and explicit confidence maps are generated.

Key Findings

The proposed LatentFM approach demonstrated superior segmentation accuracy on both ISIC-2018 and CVC-Clinic datasets compared to several prior deterministic and generative baseline models. It proved to be highly efficient due to its operation in the latent space and successfully provided robust, uncertainty-aware predictions through pixel-wise variance and explicit confidence maps.

Clinical Impact

LatentFM has the potential to significantly enhance clinical workflows by offering not only precise segmentation masks but also a quantifiable measure of the model's certainty. This additional information from confidence maps can aid clinicians in identifying regions where the model is less confident, prompting closer human review and potentially improving diagnostic accuracy, refining treatment strategies, and increasing patient safety by reducing misinterpretations.

Abstract

Generative models have achieved remarkable progress with the emergence of flow matching (FM). It has demonstrated strong generative capabilities and attracted significant attention as a simulation-free flow-based framework capable of learning exact data densities. Motivated by these advances, we propose LatentFM, a flow-based model operating in the latent space for medical image segmentation. To model the data distribution, we first design two variational autoencoders (VAEs) to encode both medical images and their corresponding masks into a lower-dimensional latent space. We then estimate a conditional velocity field that guides the flow based on the input image. By sampling multiple latent representations, our method synthesizes diverse segmentation outputs whose pixel-wise variance reliably captures the underlying data distribution, enabling both highly accurate and uncertainty-aware predictions. Furthermore, we generate confidence maps that quantify the model certainty, providing clinicians with richer information for deeper analysis. We conduct experiments on two datasets, ISIC-2018 and CVC-Clinic, and compare our method with several prior baselines, including both deterministic and generative approach models. Through comprehensive evaluations, both qualitative and quantitative results show that our approach achieves superior segmentation accuracy while remaining highly efficient in the latent space.
