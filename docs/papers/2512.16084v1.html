Can plants grow on the Moon and Mars: seed germination enhancement using magnesium oxide coated halloysite nanotubes (MgO HNTs)

arXiv ID: 2512.16084v1
Published: 2025-12-18
Authors: Zeinab Jabbari Velisdeh, David K. Mills
Categories: q-bio.QM
Relevance Score: 0.80 / 1.00

Summary

This study demonstrates that magnesium oxide coated halloysite nanotubes (MgO HNTs) significantly enhance seed germination and early plant development across terrestrial soil simulants and challenging lunar and Martian regolith analogs. The research identified optimal conditions for MgO HNT application, highlighting its potential as a nanomaterial amendment for sustainable food cultivation in both Earth and space environments, particularly critical for long-duration human missions.

Medical Relevance

This research is paramount for supporting human health and sustainability during long-duration space missions by enabling autonomous, on-site food production, thus ensuring a fresh and diverse nutritional supply for astronauts and reducing reliance on costly resupply. On Earth, it offers a strategy to enhance food security in challenging agricultural regions, indirectly impacting global public health by addressing malnutrition.

Methodology

The study involved the electrodeposition synthesis and SEM characterization of MgO HNTs. Growth experiments with Heirloom Cherry and Golden Tomato seeds were conducted across hydroponic, Earth soil, and simulated lunar and Martian regolith environments. A Box Behnken design within response surface methodology was employed to optimize variables including temperature, MgO HNT concentration, and light duration, with seedling length and root length stress tolerance index as primary outcome measures.

Key Findings

MgO HNTs demonstrably enhanced seed germination and early plant development. Optimal conditions (25 C, 12 h light, 100 mg/L MgO HNTs) maximized root and shoot growth in terrestrial soil simulants. Crucially, MgO HNTs facilitated germination, root penetration, and seedling vigor in nutrient-limited and high-stress extraterrestrial regolith simulants, with specific optimal concentrations (100 mg/L for lunar, 10 mg/L for Martian) tailored to the unique properties of each extraterrestrial substrate.

Clinical Impact

The ability to sustainably grow food in space offers a profound clinical impact for astronauts, ensuring access to fresh produce to meet nutritional requirements, mitigate micronutrient deficiencies, and provide psychological benefits during extended missions. This technology could reduce the logistical burden and risks associated with food resupply, making long-duration space exploration and habitation more feasible and safer for human crews.

Limitations

The study primarily utilized regolith simulants rather than actual extraterrestrial soil, which may not fully replicate all environmental complexities (e.g., radiation, vacuum, precise mineralogy). The abstract focuses on early plant development, suggesting that long-term growth cycles, crop yield, and the nutritional content of mature plants were not detailed within this scope.

Future Directions

Future research should explore the efficacy of MgO HNTs with actual extraterrestrial regolith samples, investigate their impact on long-term plant growth and crop yields, assess the nutritional profile of plants cultivated using this method, and rigorously evaluate the biocompatibility and safety of these nanomaterials in edible plants for human consumption.

Abstract

This study examines the application of metal coated nanotubes, specifically magnesium oxide coated halloysite nanotubes (MgO HNTs), to enhance seed germination and early plant development under Earth, lunar, and Martian soil conditions. MgO HNTs were synthesized through an electrodeposition process and characterized by scanning electron microscopy to confirm successful surface modification. Growth experiments using Heirloom Cherry Tomato and Golden Tomato seeds were conducted under hydroponic and soil based conditions and subsequently extended to lunar and Martian regolith simulants. A response surface methodology approach based on a Box Behnken design was used to evaluate the effects of temperature, MgO HNT concentration, and light duration on multiple growth responses. Seedling length and the root length stress tolerance index were identified as the most responsive indicators of MgO HNT treatment. Optimal conditions consisting of 25 C, 12 h light exposure, and 100 mg per L MgO HNTs produced the greatest increases in root and shoot length in Earth soil simulants. Validation experiments in extraterrestrial regolith analogs showed that MgO HNTs supported germination, root penetration, and overall seedling vigor under nutrient limited and high stress conditions. Lunar regolith exhibited maximal root development at 100 mg per L, whereas Martian regolith showed optimal growth at 10 mg per L, reflecting differences in mineral composition and oxidative characteristics. These findings demonstrate that MgO HNTs can enhance early plant development across both terrestrial and extraterrestrial substrates and support their potential use as nanomaterial based amendments for sustainable plant cultivation in Earth and space environments.
