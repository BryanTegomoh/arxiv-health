SAM-Fed: SAM-Guided Federated Semi-Supervised Learning for Medical Image Segmentation

Summary

SAM-Fed is a novel federated semi-supervised learning (FSSL) framework for medical image segmentation designed to overcome limitations of data scarcity and heterogeneous client resources. It leverages a high-capacity segmentation foundation model to guide lightweight client models through dual knowledge distillation and an adaptive agreement mechanism, enhancing pseudo-label reliability. Experiments on skin lesion and polyp segmentation demonstrate that SAM-Fed consistently outperforms state-of-the-art FSSL methods across various settings.

Key Points

  • Addresses critical challenges in FSSL for medical image segmentation: unreliable pseudo-labels from weak local models and constraints of compact/heterogeneous client architectures.
  • Introduces SAM-Fed, a framework that uniquely leverages a high-capacity segmentation foundation model (implicitly SAM) as a robust teacher to guide lightweight client models.
  • Employs dual knowledge distillation (DKD) as a core mechanism to efficiently transfer robust knowledge from the powerful foundation model to resource-constrained client models.
  • Incorporates an adaptive agreement mechanism to dynamically refine pixel-level pseudo-label supervision, significantly improving its quality and stability.
  • Validated on clinically relevant tasks, specifically skin lesion segmentation and polyp segmentation, showcasing its practical applicability in diverse medical imaging domains.
  • Consistently outperforms existing state-of-the-art FSSL methods in both homogeneous and heterogeneous federated settings.
  • Enables the deployment of more accurate and stable medical image segmentation models on client devices with limited computational resources, crucial for decentralized healthcare AI.

Methodology

SAM-Fed is a federated semi-supervised learning framework that combines several advanced techniques. It utilizes a high-capacity segmentation foundation model to provide strong supervision. This guidance is implemented via dual knowledge distillation (DKD), transferring knowledge to lightweight client models. An adaptive agreement mechanism is used to refine pixel-level pseudo-labels generated during the semi-supervised process, ensuring their reliability. The framework operates in a federated manner, allowing clients to train on local data while benefiting from global knowledge and foundation model guidance without data centralization.

This improved accuracy and stability were demonstrated across critical tasks like skin lesion and polyp segmentation, under both homogeneous and heterogeneous client architectural settings.

Abstract

Medical image segmentation is clinically important, yet data privacy and the cost of expert annotation limit the availability of labeled data. Federated semi-supervised learning (FSSL) offers a solution but faces two challenges: pseudo-label reliability depends on the strength of local models, and client devices often require compact or heterogeneous architectures due to limited computational resources. These constraints reduce the quality and stability of pseudo-labels, while large models, though more accurate, cannot be trained or used for routine inference on client devices. We propose SAM-Fed, a federated semi-supervised framework that leverages a high-capacity segmentation foundation model to guide lightweight clients during training. SAM-Fed combines dual knowledge distillation with an adaptive agreement mechanism to refine pixel-level supervision. Experiments on skin lesion and polyp segmentation across homogeneous and heterogeneous settings show that SAM-Fed consistently outperforms state-of-the-art FSSL methods.
