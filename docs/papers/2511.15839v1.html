Comparing Bayesian and Frequentist Inference in Biological Models: A Comparative Analysis of Accuracy, Uncertainty, and Identifiability

Published: 2025-11-19
Authors: Mohammed A. Y. Mohammed, Hamed Karami, Gerardo Chowell

Summary

This paper comparatively analyzes Bayesian and Frequentist inference frameworks across three biological models (Lotka-Volterra, generalized logistic, and SEIUR epidemic) using four datasets to assess their accuracy, uncertainty quantification, and identifiability. It concludes that Frequentist methods perform best with rich, fully observed data, while Bayesian inference excels with sparse data and high latent-state uncertainty, offering critical guidance for model selection in biological and medical research.

Medical Relevance

This research is directly relevant to medical and public health fields as it guides the selection of robust statistical inference methods for modeling infectious disease dynamics (mpox, COVID-19) and physiological conditions (lung injury), leading to more reliable forecasts and evidence-based interventions.

AI Health Application

The research provides methodological guidance for choosing between Bayesian and Frequentist inference frameworks for biological models. This is directly applicable to predictive modeling, forecasting, and uncertainty quantification in medical AI and machine learning applications, particularly in epidemiology, infectious disease surveillance, and public health where models like SEIUR are crucial for decision-making. The use of Hamiltonian Monte Carlo via Stan for Bayesian inference is a common technique in computational statistics and machine learning, underpinning many AI approaches in health.

Methodology

The study employed a comparative analysis of Bayesian and Frequentist inference frameworks on three biological models (Lotka-Volterra, generalized logistic, SEIUR epidemic) using four datasets, assuming a normal error structure for a fair comparison. Structural identifiability (theoretical parameter recovery) was initially assessed, followed by practical identifiability and forecasting performance using MAE, MSE, 95% PI coverage, and WIS. The Frequentist approach used QuantDiffForecast (QDF) in MATLAB, relying on nonlinear least squares for ODE fitting and parametric bootstrap for uncertainty. The Bayesian approach utilized BayesianFitForecast (BFF) with Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) sampling via Stan to generate posterior distributions and diagnose convergence.

Key Findings

Frequentist inference consistently performs best when datasets are rich and fully observed. Conversely, Bayesian inference demonstrates superior performance, particularly in terms of handling uncertainty, when data are sparse and characterized by high latent-state uncertainty, as exemplified by the SEIUR COVID-19 model. The study also highlighted the foundational role of structural identifiability, showing that while full data observability benefits both frameworks, limited observability fundamentally restricts parameter recovery regardless of the inference method.

Clinical Impact

This research provides critical, data-driven guidance for epidemiologists, public health officials, and clinical researchers in selecting the most appropriate inference framework (Bayesian or Frequentist) for modeling health outcomes and disease dynamics. By understanding when each method excels based on data richness, observability, and uncertainty levels, practitioners can generate more accurate forecasts, quantify uncertainty more reliably, and ultimately make better-informed decisions for public health interventions, resource allocation, and patient management.

Limitations

The comparative analysis used a normal error structure, which may limit generalizability to models requiring different error distributions. The conclusions are drawn from a specific set of biological models and datasets, suggesting that their applicability to all medical modeling scenarios might require further validation. The study implicitly highlights that Frequentist methods may be less reliable with sparse data and high latent-state uncertainty, while Bayesian methods might be less efficient or necessary with abundant, fully observed data.

Abstract

Mathematical models support inference and forecasting in ecology and epidemiology, but results depend on the estimation framework. We compare Bayesian and Frequentist approaches across three biological models using four datasets: Lotka-Volterra predator-prey dynamics (Hudson Bay), a generalized logistic model (lung injury and 2022 U.S. mpox), and an SEIUR epidemic model (COVID-19 in Spain). Both approaches use a normal error structure to ensure a fair comparison. We first assessed structural identifiability to determine which parameters can theoretically be recovered from the data. We then evaluated practical identifiability and forecasting performance using four metrics: mean absolute error (MAE), mean squared error (MSE), 95 percent prediction interval (PI) coverage, and weighted interval score (WIS). For the Lotka-Volterra model with both prey and predator data, we analyzed three scenarios: prey only, predator only, and both. The Frequentist workflow used QuantDiffForecast (QDF) in MATLAB, which fits ODE models via nonlinear least squares and quantifies uncertainty through parametric bootstrap. The Bayesian workflow used BayesianFitForecast (BFF), which employs Hamiltonian Monte Carlo sampling via Stan to generate posterior distributions and diagnostics such as the Gelman-Rubin R-hat statistic. Results show that Frequentist inference performs best when data are rich and fully observed, while Bayesian inference excels when latent-state uncertainty is high and data are sparse, as in the SEIUR COVID-19 model. Structural identifiability clarifies these patterns: full observability benefits both frameworks, while limited observability constrains parameter recovery. This comparison provides guidance for choosing inference frameworks based on data richness, observability, and uncertainty needs.
