Dataset creation for supervised deep learning-based analysis of microscopic images -- review of important considerations and recommendations

Summary

This review provides a comprehensive guide for creating high-quality, large-scale datasets essential for supervised deep learning models in microscopic image analysis. It addresses the inherent complexities and challenges, such as domain variability and bias, by offering practical recommendations across image acquisition, annotation software selection, and annotation creation. The ultimate goal is to enable the development of generalizable and robust deep learning models, particularly for pathology applications.

Medical Relevance

This paper is highly relevant to medicine as it tackles a core challenge in translating AI into clinical practice, particularly in digital pathology. By guiding the creation of robust, high-quality datasets from microscopic images, it enables the development of reliable deep learning models for automated diagnostics, ultimately improving accuracy, efficiency, and consistency in disease diagnosis.

AI Health Application

The research aims to improve the creation of high-quality datasets for training deep learning models that can automate and enhance the analysis of microscopic images in pathology, thereby assisting in disease diagnosis, prognosis, and medical research.

Key Points

  • High-quality, large-scale datasets are fundamental for developing and validating supervised deep learning models for automated microscopic image analysis.
  • Dataset creation is a complex and resource-intensive process, frequently hindered by challenges including time constraints, domain variability, and risks of bias in image collection and label generation.
  • Critical steps for dataset creation encompass image acquisition, appropriate selection of annotation software, and meticulous annotation creation.
  • It is crucial to identify and address sources of image variability, termed 'domain shifts' (e.g., related to slide preparation and digitization), to prevent algorithmic errors and ensure model generalizability.
  • Annotation quality is defined by the three 'C's: correctness, completeness, and consistency, with emphasis on advanced techniques to mitigate limitations inherent in single-annotator approaches.
  • A standard operating procedure (SOP) is provided (as supplemental material) to outline best practices for systematic dataset development.
  • The importance of open datasets is highlighted as a critical factor for fostering innovation and enhancing the reproducibility of deep learning research in this domain.

Methodology

This paper is a comprehensive review and guide. It synthesizes existing knowledge and best practices to provide a structured framework and recommendations for creating high-quality datasets for supervised deep learning-based analysis of microscopic images. It identifies critical steps, common challenges, and quality criteria, supplementing these with practical advice and a Standard Operating Procedure (SOP).

Key Findings

The key findings are a set of critical considerations and recommendations: the imperative of high-quality and large-scale datasets; the three crucial phases of dataset creation (image acquisition, annotation software, annotation creation); the necessity of addressing domain shifts for generalizable models; the 'three C's' (correctness, completeness, consistency) as annotation quality benchmarks; and the value of SOPs and open datasets for practical implementation and research reproducibility.

Clinical Impact

This work has the potential to significantly impact clinical pathology by enabling the development of more reliable, generalizable, and robust deep learning models for automated microscopic image analysis. This can lead to improved diagnostic accuracy, reduced inter-observer variability, faster turnaround times for pathology reports, and ultimately better patient outcomes through more efficient and precise disease detection and classification.

Limitations

The abstract highlights the inherent limitations and challenges in the process of dataset creation itself, which this review aims to mitigate. These include time constraints, significant domain variability (e.g., slide preparation, digitization protocols), and the persistent risks of bias during image collection and label creation.

Future Directions

The paper strongly advocates for continued efforts in the creation and widespread availability of high-quality, large-scale open datasets. This is essential to drive innovation, enhance the reproducibility of deep learning research, and ultimately contribute to the development of more generalizable and robust deep learning models for diverse pathology applications.

Abstract

Supervised deep learning (DL) receives great interest for automated analysis of microscopic images with an increasing body of literature supporting its potential. The development and validation of those DL models relies heavily on the availability of high-quality, large-scale datasets. However, creating such datasets is a complex and resource-intensive process, often hindered by challenges such as time constraints, domain variability, and risks of bias in image collection and label creation. This review provides a comprehensive guide to the critical steps in dataset creation, including: 1) image acquisition, 2) selection of annotation software, and 3) annotation creation. In addition to ensuring a sufficiently large number of images, it is crucial to address sources of image variability (domain shifts) - such as those related to slide preparation and digitization - that could lead to algorithmic errors if not adequately represented in the training data. Key quality criteria for annotations are the three "C"s: correctness, completeness, and consistency. This review explores methods to enhance annotation quality through the use of advanced techniques that mitigate the limitations of single annotators. To support dataset creators, a standard operating procedure (SOP) is provided as supplemental material, outlining best practices for dataset development. Furthermore, the article underscores the importance of open datasets in driving innovation and enhancing reproducibility of DL research. By addressing the challenges and offering practical recommendations, this review aims to advance the creation of and availability to high-quality, large-scale datasets, ultimately contributing to the development of generalizable and robust DL models for pathology applications.
