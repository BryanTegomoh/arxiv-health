Summary

FT-ARM introduces a fine-tuned multimodal large language model (MLLM) with an agentic self-reflection mechanism for accurate and interpretable pressure ulcer (PU) severity classification (Stages I-IV). It achieved 85% accuracy, outperforming prior CNN models by 4%, and is designed for live inference while providing clinically grounded natural-language explanations, addressing the critical need for consistent and explainable automated wound assessment.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and consistent pressure ulcer severity classification is fundamental for effective treatment and patient outcomes, but current manual methods are prone to subjectivity. This AI model offers a robust, interpretable, and real-time solution to standardize diagnosis, improve treatment planning, and reduce variability in wound care.

It uses a fine-tuned multimodal large language model (FT-ARM) that processes visual features and clinical text, employing an agentic self-reflection mechanism to mimic clinician-style reassessment. This enhances classification accuracy, consistency, and provides clinically grounded natural-language explanations, thereby improving interpretability and trust for real-time deployment in healthcare settings to aid in patient care.

Methodology

The study developed FT-ARM, a Fine-Tuned Multimodal Large Language Model (MLLM) based on LLaMA 3.2 90B. This model incorporates an agentic self-reflection mechanism that iteratively refines its pressure ulcer severity predictions by reasoning over both visual features extracted from images and encoded clinical knowledge from textual inputs. The system was evaluated on the publicly available Pressure Injury Image Dataset (PIID) for classifying PU stages I-IV, and unlike previous works, was designed and tested for live inference.

FT-ARM achieved an 85% accuracy in classifying pressure ulcer stages I-IV, representing a 4% improvement over prior CNN-based models. A key finding is its ability to produce clinically grounded natural-language explanations, which significantly enhances interpretability. Furthermore, its design for live inference demonstrates its potential for real-time clinical deployment.

By reducing subjective interpretation and providing explainable reasoning, it can support clinicians in making more precise and timely treatment decisions, ultimately leading to improved patient care, reduced healthcare costs associated with misdiagnosis, and better resource allocation in wound management. Abstract

Pressure ulcers (PUs) are a serious and prevalent healthcare concern. Accurate classification of PU severity (Stages I-IV) is essential for proper treatment but remains challenging due to subtle visual distinctions and subjective interpretation, leading to variability among clinicians. Prior AI-based approaches using Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) and Vision Transformers (ViTs) achieved promising accuracy but offered limited interpretability. We present FT-ARM (Fine-Tuned Agentic Reflection Multimodal model), a fine-tuned multimodal large language model (MLLM) with an agentic self-reflection mechanism for pressure ulcer severity classification. Inspired by clinician-style diagnostic reassessment, FT-ARM iteratively refines its predictions by reasoning over visual features and encoded clinical knowledge from text, enhancing both accuracy and consistency. On the publicly available Pressure Injury Image Dataset (PIID), FT-ARM, fine-tuned from LLaMA 3.2 90B, achieved 85% accuracy in classifying PU stages I-IV, surpassing prior CNN-based models by +4%. Unlike earlier CNN/ViT studies that relied solely on offline evaluations, FT-ARM is designed and tested for live inference, reflecting real-time deployment conditions. Furthermore, it produces clinically grounded natural-language explanations, improving interpretability and trust. By integrating fine-tuning and reflective reasoning across multimodal inputs, FT-ARM advances the reliability, transparency, and clinical applicability of automated wound assessment systems, addressing the critical need for consistent and explainable PU staging to support improved patient care.
