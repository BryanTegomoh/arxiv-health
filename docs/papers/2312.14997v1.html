The Effect of Vaccination on the Competitive Advantage of Two Strains of an Infectious Disease

Summary

This paper investigates how a population's natural and vaccine immunity influences the competitive balance between two infectious disease strains with differing epidemiological characteristics (one more transmissible, one more immune-resistant). It demonstrates that vaccination significantly alters strain dominance, with the specific outcome depending on the integration of natural and vaccine immunity mechanisms, providing crucial insights for public health policy.

Medical Relevance

This research is vital for public health and infectious disease management as it demonstrates how vaccination strategies can profoundly reshape the epidemiological landscape by altering the competitive advantage between pathogen strains, influencing disease prevalence, and guiding vaccine development and deployment policies.

Key Points

  • The study models the competition between two strains of an infectious disease: one more transmissible, the other more immune-resistant.
  • Competition is analyzed using two SIR-type mathematical models, incorporating both waning natural immunity and distinct mechanisms for vaccine immunity.
  • Waning natural immunity is represented as a gamma-distributed delay, mathematically transformed into a system of ordinary differential equations (ODEs) using the linear chain trick for analysis.
  • A primary finding is that vaccination has a significant, often dramatic, effect on the competitive balance, capable of flipping which strain dominates in the population.
  • The advantage gained by a particular strain with increasing population immunity depends critically on how natural immunity and vaccine immunity integrate and interact.
  • The research highlights the importance of considering these complex immunological interactions for effective public health policy and intervention strategies.

Methodology

The study utilizes two SIR-type mathematical models to simulate the competitive dynamics between two strains. Waning natural immunity is modeled as a gamma-distributed delay, which is then converted into a system of ordinary differential equations (ODEs) using the linear chain trick, enabling a tractable analysis of the delay differential equation systems.

Key Findings

Vaccination exerts a profound influence on the competitive balance between infectious disease strains, with the potential to cause dramatic shifts in which strain dominates. Furthermore, the specific strain that gains an advantage as population immunity rises is contingent upon the intricate integration and interaction mechanisms between natural and vaccine-induced immunity.

Clinical Impact

The findings have direct implications for vaccine design and public health interventions. Understanding how vaccination impacts strain competition can inform strategies to prevent the emergence or dominance of more virulent or resistant strains, optimize vaccine compositions (e.g., multivalent vaccines), guide immunization schedules, and improve disease surveillance, ultimately leading to more effective control of infectious diseases with multiple circulating strains.

Abstract

We investigate how a population's natural and vaccine immunity affects the competitive balance between two strains of an infectious disease with different epidemiological characteristics. Specifically, we consider the case where one strain is more transmissible and the other strain is more immune-resistant. The competition of these strains is modeled by two SIR-type models which incorporate waning natural immunity and which have distinct mechanisms for vaccine immunity. Waning immunity is implemented as a gamma-distributed delay, which is analyzed using the linear chain trick to transform the delay differential equation systems into a system of ordinary differential equations. Our analysis shows that vaccination has a significant effect on the competitive balance between two strains, potentially leading to dramatic flips from one strain dominating in the population to the other. We also show that which strain gains an advantage as a population's immunity level increases depends upon the integration between the mechanisms of natural and vaccine immunity. The results of this paper are consequently relevant for public policy.
