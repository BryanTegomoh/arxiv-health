The Effect of Vaccination on the Competitive Advantage of Two Strains of an Infectious Disease

arXiv ID: 2312.14997v1
Published: 2023-12-22
Authors: Matthew D. Johnston, Bruce Pell, Jared Pemberton, David A. Rubel
Categories: math.DS, q-bio.PE
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper investigates how natural and vaccine-induced immunity influence the competitive dynamics between two strains of an infectious disease with contrasting epidemiological traits (one more transmissible, the other more immune-resistant). It demonstrates that vaccination critically alters strain dominance, capable of causing dramatic shifts between prevalent strains, with the specific outcome depending on the integration of natural and vaccine immunity mechanisms.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for understanding the evolutionary pressures on pathogens exerted by vaccination, helping public health officials predict and manage the emergence or dominance of specific disease strains, especially those with increased transmissibility or immune evasion capabilities.

AI Health Application

While this paper primarily uses mathematical modeling (SIR-type models, differential equations) rather than AI, its findings are highly relevant for informing medical AI applications. AI models could be built upon these epidemiological principles to: 1) Predict future dominant strains based on real-time surveillance data and vaccination coverage, aiding in proactive public health responses. 2) Optimize vaccine distribution strategies and booster campaign timings to effectively manage strain competition and prevent the rise of more dangerous variants. 3) Develop AI-driven decision support systems for policymakers to simulate outcomes of different vaccination policies on population health and biosecurity.

Key Points

  • The study models the competition between two infectious disease strains: one more transmissible, the other more immune-resistant.
  • It employs two SIR-type mathematical models that incorporate both waning natural immunity and distinct mechanisms for vaccine immunity.
  • Waning natural immunity is specifically modeled as a gamma-distributed delay, mathematically handled by the linear chain trick to transform delay differential equations into a system of ordinary differential equations.
  • Vaccination is shown to have a significant impact on the competitive balance, potentially leading to dramatic flips in which strain dominates the population.
  • The advantage gained by a particular strain as population immunity increases is dependent on the specific integration and interaction between the mechanisms of natural and vaccine immunity.
  • The research provides results directly relevant for public health policy and informs strategies for managing infectious diseases.

Methodology

The study utilizes two SIR (Susceptible-Infected-Recovered)-type compartmental models to simulate the competitive interaction of two strains. A key feature is the incorporation of waning natural immunity, implemented as a gamma-distributed delay, which is mathematically transformed from a system of delay differential equations into a system of ordinary differential equations using the linear chain trick for analytical tractability. The models also distinguish between different mechanisms of vaccine immunity.

Key Findings

Vaccination has a profound and significant effect on the competitive balance between two co-circulating strains, capable of causing dramatic shifts in which strain achieves dominance. Furthermore, the specific strain that gains a competitive advantage as population immunity levels rise is determined by the intricate interplay and integration of the mechanisms governing natural immunity and vaccine-induced immunity.

Clinical Impact

These findings directly inform public health policy by providing insights into how vaccination strategies can influence the trajectory of strain evolution and prevalence. Understanding this dynamic is critical for optimizing vaccine development, deployment, and booster campaigns to effectively control outbreaks and prevent the dominance of more virulent or vaccine-resistant strains, thereby improving population health outcomes.

Abstract

We investigate how a population's natural and vaccine immunity affects the competitive balance between two strains of an infectious disease with different epidemiological characteristics. Specifically, we consider the case where one strain is more transmissible and the other strain is more immune-resistant. The competition of these strains is modeled by two SIR-type models which incorporate waning natural immunity and which have distinct mechanisms for vaccine immunity. Waning immunity is implemented as a gamma-distributed delay, which is analyzed using the linear chain trick to transform the delay differential equation systems into a system of ordinary differential equations. Our analysis shows that vaccination has a significant effect on the competitive balance between two strains, potentially leading to dramatic flips from one strain dominating in the population to the other. We also show that which strain gains an advantage as a population's immunity level increases depends upon the integration between the mechanisms of natural and vaccine immunity. The results of this paper are consequently relevant for public policy.
