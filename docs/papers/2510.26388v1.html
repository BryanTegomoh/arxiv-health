Monitoring Head Movement in a Brain PET Scanner

arXiv ID: 2510.26388v1
Published: 2025-10-30
Authors: Machiel Kolstein, Mokhtar Chmeissani, Andreu Pacheco
Categories: physics.med-ph
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a simulated head monitoring device, CrowN@22, designed for brain PET scanners to counteract motion artifacts during long acquisitions. By utilizing point sources of non-pure positron emitters like 22Na and detecting their unique accompanying gamma photons, the device achieves exceptional signal-to-noise ratio. The simulation demonstrates highly precise head movement detection, less than 0.5 mm or 0.3 degrees, at a 1 Hz sampling rate, even in the presence of high brain FDG activity, significantly improving PET image quality.

Medical Relevance

This technology directly addresses a critical challenge in PET imaging, enhancing diagnostic accuracy by minimizing motion-induced artifacts, which is crucial for precise quantification and localization of disease in neurological studies and for accurate treatment planning.

AI Health Application

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract. The paper describes a hardware-based solution for motion monitoring using specific isotope detection, rather than an AI or machine learning application. While the data collected *could* potentially be integrated into AI-enhanced image reconstruction or motion correction algorithms, the abstract itself does not detail such an application.

Key Points

  • Patient head movements during long PET scans (10-20+ minutes) severely degrade image quality, impacting reconstruction and attenuation corrections.
  • The proposed CrowN@22 device consists of six point sources of non-pure positron emitter isotopes (e.g., 22Na, 44Sc) mounted in crown-like rings around the patient's head.
  • A novel detection mechanism leverages an *extra* characteristic photon (e.g., 1274 keV for 22Na) emitted by the monitor isotopes, distinct from the 511 keV annihilation photons of the PET tracer, to precisely track source positions.
  • This unique photon tagging provides a superb signal-to-noise ratio, enabling detection of low monitor activity (10 kBq/source) against high background brain activity (75 MBq of 18F-FDG).
  • Simulation results indicate high precision movement detection of less than 0.3 degrees or 0.5 mm, which is on the order of PET spatial resolution.
  • The device is capable of a sampling rate of 1 Hz, facilitating near real-time motion tracking.
  • The study is based on simulations, establishing the theoretical feasibility and performance of the CrowN@22 concept.

Methodology

The study was a simulation study of a head monitoring device named CrowN@22. The device model incorporated six pre-defined point sources of non-pure positron emitter isotopes (e.g., 22Na or 44Sc) mounted in crown-like rings around a virtual patient's head. The methodology for position reconstruction relies on the unique detection of a characteristic additional high-energy photon (e.g., 1274 keV for 22Na) emitted by these isotopes, allowing for its signal to be distinguished from the 511 keV annihilation photons produced by the PET tracer (e.g., 18F-FDG) in the brain.

Key Findings

The simulation study demonstrated that the CrowN@22 device can detect brain movements with a precision of less than 0.3 degrees or 0.5 mm, which is comparable to the intrinsic spatial resolution of PET. This high precision is achievable at a sampling rate of 1 Hz, even when using very low monitor source activity (10 kBq per source) in the presence of high background activity from a typical brain PET tracer (75 MBq of 18F), due to the superb signal-to-noise ratio offered by the unique photon tagging mechanism.

Clinical Impact

By precisely tracking and correcting for head movements, this technology has the potential to significantly improve the diagnostic quality and quantitative accuracy of brain PET images. This would lead to more reliable assessment of brain metabolism, receptor binding, and lesion detection, benefiting the diagnosis, staging, and monitoring of neurological diseases like Alzheimer's, Parkinson's, and brain tumors, especially in patients prone to movement.

Limitations

The primary limitation noted is that this is a "simulation study," meaning the reported performance is theoretical and not yet validated with a physical prototype or in-vivo experiments. Practical challenges related to physical implementation, integration with existing PET scanners, patient comfort, and actual real-world performance under varying clinical conditions are not addressed.

Future Directions

While not explicitly detailed, the nature of a "simulation study" inherently implies future directions would involve the development and physical prototyping of the CrowN@22 device. Subsequent steps would include experimental validation, integration with dedicated brain PET scanners, and comprehensive in-vivo testing to confirm its performance, assess clinical feasibility, and evaluate its impact on human brain PET image quality and diagnostic accuracy.

Abstract

When acquiring PET images, body motions are unavoidable, given that the acquisition time could last 10-20 minutes or more. These motions can seriously deteriorate the quality of the final image at the level of image reconstruction and attenuation corrections. Movements can have rhythmic patterns, related to respiratory or cardiac motions, or they can be abrupt reflexive actions caused by the patient's discomfort. Many approaches, software and hardware, have been developed to mitigate this problem where each approach has its own advantages and disadvantages. In this work we present a simulation study of a head monitoring device, named CrowN@22, intended to be used in conjunction with a dedicated brain PET scanner. The CrowN@22 device consists of six point sources of non-pure positron emitter isotopes, such as 22Na or 44Sc, mounted in crown-like rings around the head of the patient. The relative positions of the point sources are predefined and their actual position, once mounted, can be reconstructed by tagging the extra 1274 keV photon (in the case of 22Na). These two factors contribute to a superb signal-to-noise ratio, distinguishing between the signal from the 22Na monitor point sources and the background signal from the FDG in the brain. Hence, even with a low activity for the monitor point sources, as low as 10 kBq per point source, in the presence of 75 MBq activity of 18F in the brain, one can detect brain movements with a precision of less than 0.3 degrees, or 0.5 mm, which is of the order of the PET spatial resolution, at a sampling rate of 1 Hz.
