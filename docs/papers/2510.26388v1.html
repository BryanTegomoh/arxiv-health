Monitoring Head Movement in a Brain PET Scanner

arXiv ID: 2510.26388v1
Published: 2025-10-30
Authors: Machiel Kolstein, Mokhtar Chmeissani, Andreu Pacheco
Categories: physics.med-ph
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper presents a simulation study of CrowN@22, a novel head monitoring device designed to mitigate patient head movement artifacts during brain PET imaging. By utilizing crown-like rings of low-activity non-pure positron emitter sources (e.g., 22Na) and uniquely tagging their additional gamma photon emissions, the device aims to precisely track head movements with a superb signal-to-noise ratio. The simulation demonstrates the ability to detect movements with high precision (< 0.3 degrees or 0.5 mm) at a 1 Hz sampling rate, even in the presence of high brain activity, significantly improving PET image quality.

Medical Relevance

Accurate monitoring and correction of patient head motion during PET scans are paramount for obtaining high-quality diagnostic images, enabling more precise lesion detection, quantification, and therapeutic assessment, particularly in neurologically compromised patients or for brain-specific pathologies.

Key Points

  • Patient head movement during prolonged PET scans severely deteriorates image quality, affecting reconstruction and attenuation corrections.
  • The proposed CrowN@22 device consists of six point sources of non-pure positron emitter isotopes (e.g., 22Na or 44Sc) mounted in crown-like rings around the patient's head.
  • Movement detection is achieved by reconstructing the actual position of these sources, leveraging the unique extra photon (e.g., 1274 keV for 22Na) emitted in coincidence with the positrons.
  • This method provides a superb signal-to-noise ratio, effectively distinguishing the monitor's signal from the background activity of the PET tracer (e.g., 18F-FDG) in the brain.
  • Simulation results indicate high positional precision, detecting movements less than 0.3 degrees or 0.5 mm, which is on the order of the PET spatial resolution.
  • The device operates with very low monitor source activity (as low as 10 kBq per source) even with high background brain activity (75 MBq of 18F), at a rapid sampling rate of 1 Hz.

Methodology

This is a simulation study of a proposed hardware device, CrowN@22. The device's concept involves using six low-activity point sources of non-pure positron emitters (e.g., 22Na) mounted in a crown configuration. Its performance in tracking head movements was simulated by leveraging the unique additional gamma photon emissions from these isotopes to differentiate them from the PET tracer signal and reconstruct their positions, even in the presence of high background brain activity.

Key Findings

The simulation study found that the CrowN@22 device can precisely detect brain movements with an accuracy of less than 0.3 degrees or 0.5 mm, which is comparable to the intrinsic spatial resolution of PET. This high precision is achieved at a sampling rate of 1 Hz, with remarkably low monitor source activity (10 kBq per source) even against a high background brain activity (75 MBq of 18F), attributed to a superb signal-to-noise ratio enabled by the unique photon tagging mechanism.

Clinical Impact

This technology has the potential to significantly enhance the diagnostic and quantitative accuracy of brain PET images by effectively mitigating motion artifacts, which are a major source of image degradation. This could lead to more confident and reliable diagnoses of neurological conditions, improved monitoring of disease progression, and more precise treatment planning, ultimately improving patient outcomes and reducing the need for repeat scans.

Limitations

The primary limitation explicitly stated is that this is a "simulation study," meaning the device's efficacy and performance have been evaluated theoretically and not yet validated through physical prototyping or in-vivo experimentation.

Future Directions

While not explicitly detailed in the abstract, the logical next steps following this simulation study would involve the physical development and construction of a CrowN@22 prototype, followed by rigorous experimental validation using phantoms and subsequent clinical testing to confirm its performance and clinical utility in real-world brain PET scanning environments.

Abstract

When acquiring PET images, body motions are unavoidable, given that the acquisition time could last 10-20 minutes or more. These motions can seriously deteriorate the quality of the final image at the level of image reconstruction and attenuation corrections. Movements can have rhythmic patterns, related to respiratory or cardiac motions, or they can be abrupt reflexive actions caused by the patient's discomfort. Many approaches, software and hardware, have been developed to mitigate this problem where each approach has its own advantages and disadvantages. In this work we present a simulation study of a head monitoring device, named CrowN@22, intended to be used in conjunction with a dedicated brain PET scanner. The CrowN@22 device consists of six point sources of non-pure positron emitter isotopes, such as 22Na or 44Sc, mounted in crown-like rings around the head of the patient. The relative positions of the point sources are predefined and their actual position, once mounted, can be reconstructed by tagging the extra 1274 keV photon (in the case of 22Na). These two factors contribute to a superb signal-to-noise ratio, distinguishing between the signal from the 22Na monitor point sources and the background signal from the FDG in the brain. Hence, even with a low activity for the monitor point sources, as low as 10 kBq per point source, in the presence of 75 MBq activity of 18F in the brain, one can detect brain movements with a precision of less than 0.3 degrees, or 0.5 mm, which is of the order of the PET spatial resolution, at a sampling rate of 1 Hz.
