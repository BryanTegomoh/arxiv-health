Monitoring Head Movement in a Brain PET Scanner

arXiv ID: 2510.26388v1
Published: 2025-10-30
Authors: Machiel Kolstein, Mokhtar Chmeissani, Andreu Pacheco
Categories: physics.med-ph
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper presents a simulation study of CrowN@22, a novel head monitoring device designed to mitigate motion artifacts during brain PET scans. Utilizing six low-activity non-pure positron emitter point sources (e.g., 22Na) in a crown-like arrangement, the device leverages specific gamma photon detection (e.g., 1274 keV from 22Na) to achieve a superb signal-to-noise ratio. The simulation demonstrates its capability to track head movements with a precision of less than 0.3 degrees or 0.5 mm at a 1 Hz sampling rate, even in the presence of high background brain activity.

Medical Relevance

This technology directly addresses a critical challenge in clinical PET imaging, significantly improving the diagnostic quality and quantitative accuracy of brain PET scans by compensating for patient motion. This leads to more reliable diagnoses and treatment evaluations for various neurological and oncological conditions.

AI Health Application

Not applicable. The paper describes a hardware device and simulation study for motion monitoring. It does not involve AI algorithms, machine learning, or deep learning applications.

Key Points

  • Patient body and head movements are a significant source of image degradation in PET scans, lasting 10-20 minutes or more.
  • The proposed device, CrowN@22, is a head monitoring system intended for use with dedicated brain PET scanners.
  • It consists of six point sources of non-pure positron emitter isotopes (e.g., 22Na, 44Sc) mounted in crown-like rings around the patient's head.
  • Motion detection relies on identifying and reconstructing the position of these sources by tagging specific secondary photons (e.g., 1274 keV from 22Na).
  • The method offers a superb signal-to-noise ratio, allowing differentiation between monitor sources and background 18F-FDG activity in the brain.
  • Simulation results indicate a movement detection precision of less than 0.3 degrees or 0.5 mm, which is comparable to PET spatial resolution.
  • This precision is achievable with very low monitor source activity (10 kBq per source) in the presence of high brain activity (75 MBq of 18F) at a 1 Hz sampling rate.

Methodology

The study employed a simulation approach to evaluate the performance of the CrowN@22 head monitoring device. This involved modeling the detection of specific gamma photons (e.g., 1274 keV) emitted by low-activity, non-pure positron emitter point sources (e.g., 22Na) positioned around a simulated head. The ability to distinguish these signals from a high background activity of 18F-FDG in the brain was assessed to determine the precision and sampling rate of motion detection.

Key Findings

The simulation demonstrated that the CrowN@22 system, utilizing distinct gamma photons from non-pure positron emitters, can achieve a superb signal-to-noise ratio for motion tracking. It can detect head movements with high precision (<0.3 degrees or 0.5 mm) at a 1 Hz sampling rate. This performance is maintained even with very low monitor source activity (10 kBq per point source) against a high activity background (75 MBq 18F in the brain), with the achieved precision being on the order of PET spatial resolution.

Clinical Impact

The implementation of CrowN@22 could dramatically enhance the quality and reliability of brain PET images by effectively correcting for patient motion, which currently introduces significant artifacts. This would allow for more accurate lesion detection, quantification of radiotracer uptake, and improved diagnostic confidence in conditions like Alzheimer's disease, Parkinson's disease, epilepsy, and brain tumors. Ultimately, it could lead to better patient management and potentially reduce the need for repeat scans.

Limitations

The study is presented as a 'simulation study,' meaning the proposed device has not yet undergone physical prototyping or experimental validation in real-world or clinical settings. Practical challenges related to device manufacturing, patient interface, robustness to environmental factors, or potential interference from metallic implants were not addressed in this abstract.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated in the abstract, the logical next steps would involve developing a physical prototype of the CrowN@22 device. Subsequent research would focus on experimental validation, followed by pre-clinical and clinical trials to confirm its performance, usability, and impact on image quality in actual PET scanning environments.

Abstract

When acquiring PET images, body motions are unavoidable, given that the acquisition time could last 10-20 minutes or more. These motions can seriously deteriorate the quality of the final image at the level of image reconstruction and attenuation corrections. Movements can have rhythmic patterns, related to respiratory or cardiac motions, or they can be abrupt reflexive actions caused by the patient's discomfort. Many approaches, software and hardware, have been developed to mitigate this problem where each approach has its own advantages and disadvantages. In this work we present a simulation study of a head monitoring device, named CrowN@22, intended to be used in conjunction with a dedicated brain PET scanner. The CrowN@22 device consists of six point sources of non-pure positron emitter isotopes, such as 22Na or 44Sc, mounted in crown-like rings around the head of the patient. The relative positions of the point sources are predefined and their actual position, once mounted, can be reconstructed by tagging the extra 1274 keV photon (in the case of 22Na). These two factors contribute to a superb signal-to-noise ratio, distinguishing between the signal from the 22Na monitor point sources and the background signal from the FDG in the brain. Hence, even with a low activity for the monitor point sources, as low as 10 kBq per point source, in the presence of 75 MBq activity of 18F in the brain, one can detect brain movements with a precision of less than 0.3 degrees, or 0.5 mm, which is of the order of the PET spatial resolution, at a sampling rate of 1 Hz.
