Designing Beyond Language: Sociotechnical Barriers in AI Health Technologies for Limited English Proficiency

Authors: Michelle Huang, Violeta J. Rodriguez, Koustuv Saha, Tal August

Summary

This paper investigates sociotechnical barriers to AI health technologies for Limited English Proficiency (LEP) patients, extending beyond mere language to encompass systemic procedural and institutional constraints. Through interviews with patient navigators, it identifies critical tensions like linguistic/cultural misunderstandings, privacy concerns, and trust issues, while proposing design considerations for AI to equitably support LEP individuals and care teams.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing health equity by providing a nuanced understanding of the sociotechnical challenges and opportunities of AI for vulnerable LEP patient populations, guiding the ethical and effective integration of AI in healthcare to avoid widening existing health disparities.

AI Health Application

The paper explores AI applications such as on-demand translation and visit preparation tools designed to improve communication and support care workflows for Limited English Proficiency (LEP) patients and their care teams in healthcare settings. It focuses on the sociotechnical design of these AI tools to address barriers and minimize disruptions.

Key Points

  • LEP patients in the U.S. confront systemic healthcare barriers beyond language, including significant procedural and institutional constraints.
  • AI advancements offer potential for communication and care (e.g., on-demand translation), but concurrently risk exacerbating existing health inequalities for LEP populations.
  • The study utilized storyboard-driven interviews with 14 patient navigators to explore hypothetical AI impacts on care experiences for Spanish-speaking LEP individuals.
  • Key tensions identified include linguistic and cultural misunderstandings, privacy concerns, misinformation risks, and the potential disruption of human camaraderie in care.
  • Structural factors such as sensitive information disclosure, unstable technology access, and low digital literacy were highlighted as significant undermines of trust in AI systems.
  • While AI can potentially alleviate social barriers and institutional constraints, its implementation carries inherent risks, particularly regarding trust and misinformation.
  • Design considerations for equitable AI emphasize rapport-building, patient/care team education, robust language support, and minimizing disruptions to existing clinical practices.

Methodology

The study employed qualitative research methods, specifically conducting storyboard-driven interviews with 14 patient navigators. This approach allowed for exploring their perspectives on how AI could hypothetically shape care experiences for Spanish-speaking LEP individuals.

Key Findings

The study found that AI for LEP patients presents significant tensions around linguistic and cultural misunderstandings, privacy concerns, and the risk of misinformation and undermining human camaraderie. Structural factors like sensitive information disclosure, unstable technology access, and low digital literacy were identified as major inhibitors of trust in AI. While AI offers opportunities to augment care workflows and alleviate systemic barriers, these benefits are contingent on careful design addressing these sociotechnical challenges.

Clinical Impact

The findings provide actionable design considerations for AI developers and healthcare providers to implement AI tools that foster trust, respect cultural nuances, provide comprehensive language support, educate users effectively, and integrate seamlessly into existing care practices. This can help prevent AI from exacerbating health disparities for LEP patients and enhance the quality and accessibility of care.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations. However, implicit limitations include the study's focus on a specific LEP demographic (Spanish-speaking) and participant group (patient navigators), which may limit generalizability. The use of hypothetical storyboard scenarios, rather than real-world AI interactions, might also present different challenges upon actual implementation.

Future Directions

Future research should focus on developing and evaluating AI tools incorporating the identified design considerations (rapport-building, education, language support, minimal disruption). Further investigation into strategies for mitigating structural factors like technology access and digital literacy, as well as exploring direct patient perspectives on AI, are also warranted.

Abstract

Limited English proficiency (LEP) patients in the U.S. face systemic barriers to healthcare beyond language and interpreter access, encompassing procedural and institutional constraints. AI advances may support communication and care through on-demand translation and visit preparation, but also risk exacerbating existing inequalities. We conducted storyboard-driven interviews with 14 patient navigators to explore how AI could shape care experiences for Spanish-speaking LEP individuals. We identified tensions around linguistic and cultural misunderstandings, privacy concerns, and opportunities and risks for AI to augment care workflows. Participants highlighted structural factors that can undermine trust in AI systems, including sensitive information disclosure, unstable technology access, and low digital literacy. While AI tools can potentially alleviate social barriers and institutional constraints, there are risks of misinformation and uprooting human camaraderie. Our findings contribute design considerations for AI that support LEP patients and care teams via rapport-building, education, and language support, and minimizing disruptions to existing practices.
