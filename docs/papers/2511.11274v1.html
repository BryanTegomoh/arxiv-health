Polarization-Sensitive Module for Optical Coherence Tomography Instruments

Summary

This paper introduces a novel modular framework for Polarization-Sensitive Optical Coherence Tomography (PS-OCT) that significantly enhances its compatibility with standard commercial OCT systems. By integrating a detachable rotating achromatic half-wave plate in the sample arm, the system reconstructs tissue polarization properties from multiple measurements, reliably quantifying retardance and optic axis orientation. This innovation aims to overcome existing technical barriers, facilitating the widespread adoption of PS-OCT in both research and clinical environments.

Medical Relevance

This technology makes advanced tissue characterization via PS-OCT accessible to a much wider range of medical practitioners and researchers. The ability to non-invasively quantify tissue birefringence and optic axis orientation is critical for early diagnosis, disease monitoring, and understanding pathological changes in various medical conditions.

AI Health Application

Not directly applicable. The paper focuses on hardware modifications and a signal processing strategy (retarder-constrained phase optimization) to improve an imaging instrument. It does not describe an AI-driven health application, although the data generated by such an instrument could potentially be used in conjunction with AI for downstream analysis or diagnosis.

  • Proposes a modular PS-OCT framework integrating a detachable rotating achromatic half-wave plate into the sample arm of a standard spectral-domain OCT platform.

  • Requires only minimal hardware modification, making it readily deployable and compatible with mainstream, existing OCT systems.

Methodology

The methodology involves integrating a detachable rotating achromatic half-wave plate into the sample arm of a standard spectral-domain OCT system. The waveplate modulates the polarization states of both incident and backscattered light. By acquiring three or more repeated OCT measurements at distinct waveplate orientations, the system captures sufficient polarization information. A key aspect is the introduction of a retarder-constrained phase optimization strategy to compensate for random phase variations between these sequential measurements, ultimately enabling the reconstruction of the sample's round-trip Jones matrix and the derivation of its polarization properties (retardance and optic axis orientation).

Key Findings

The developed modular PS-OCT framework successfully and reliably reconstructs both the retardance and the optic axis orientation of samples. This was rigorously demonstrated through imaging studies of known birefringent phantoms and, crucially, validated *in vivo* within the human retina, confirming its practical applicability.

Clinical Impact

This modular PS-OCT approach has the potential to revolutionize clinical diagnostics by providing a cost-effective and easily implementable tool for advanced tissue characterization. Its compatibility with existing OCT systems means clinics can upgrade their capabilities without extensive hardware overhaul, enabling non-invasive detection and monitoring of diseases linked to changes in tissue birefringence (e.g., glaucoma, neurodegeneration, cancer staging), thereby improving patient outcomes through earlier diagnosis and more precise treatment planning.

Limitations

While the abstract highlights solutions, the explicit mention of introducing a "retarder-constrained phase optimization strategy" suggests that mitigating random phase variations between the multiple required measurements is a critical challenge in sequential acquisition methods. The need for three or more repeated measurements implies a trade-off in acquisition speed compared to single-shot PS-OCT methods, although this is not explicitly discussed as a limitation in the abstract.

Future Directions

The authors strongly imply future directions centered on the widespread deployment of this modular PS-OCT framework across diverse biomedical applications. This includes exploring its utility in various research fields and integrating it into routine clinical practice for a broader range of diagnostic and monitoring purposes beyond the retina.

Abstract

Polarization-sensitive optical coherence tomography (PS-OCT) extends OCT by analyzing the polarization states of backscattered light to quantify tissue birefringence. However, conventional implementations require polarization-diverse detection and are therefore incompatible with most commercial OCT systems. As a result, PS-OCT has largely remained restricted to specialized research groups, limiting its broader scientific and clinical use. Here, we present a modular PS-OCT framework that integrates with a standard spectral-domain OCT platform through a detachable rotating achromatic half-wave plate in the sample arm. This waveplate modulates both incident and reflected polarization states. Three or more repeated measurements at distinct waveplate orientations enable reconstruction of the sample's round-trip Jones matrix and the corresponding polarization properties. To mitigate random phase variations between repeated measurements, we introduce a retarder-constrained phase optimization strategy. We validate the framework with imaging of birefringent phantoms and human retina in vivo, demonstrating reliable reconstruction of retardance and optic axis orientation. This approach requires only minimal hardware modification and is readily deployable on mainstream OCT systems. Lowering technical barriers paves the way for rapid and widespread deployment of PS-OCT across diverse biomedical applications in both research and clinical environments.
