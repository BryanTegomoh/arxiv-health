PIANO: Physics-informed Dual Neural Operator for Precipitation Nowcasting

arXiv ID: 2512.01062v1
Published: 2025-11-30
Authors: Seokhyun Chin, Junghwan Park, Woojin Cho
Categories: cs.LG, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.75 / 1.00

Summary

This paper introduces PIANO, a novel physics-informed dual neural operator, designed to improve precipitation nowcasting by integrating fundamental advection-diffusion physics into a deep learning framework. By leveraging satellite imagery and a generative model to produce radar images, PIANO achieves superior accuracy in predicting moderate and short-term heavy precipitation events with robust generalization across seasons, addressing the limitations of computationally expensive traditional methods.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and accessible precipitation nowcasting is critical for public health, enabling timely early warning of natural disasters like floods and landslides, which can prevent injuries, fatalities, and the spread of waterborne or vector-borne diseases, especially in vulnerable populations.

AI Health Application

This AI model provides an application for public health emergency management and disaster risk reduction. By improving the accuracy and accessibility of precipitation nowcasting, it enables healthcare systems and public health agencies to better prepare for and respond to health crises stemming from extreme weather events (e.g., floods, landslides, disease outbreaks exacerbated by weather). This contributes to mitigating the health impacts of climate change.

  • Addresses the challenge of computationally expensive and restrictive precipitation nowcasting methods, which limit access for many countries.
  • Proposes a novel Physics-Informed Dual Neural Operator (PIANO) that incorporates physics constraints (advection-diffusion equation) during training using a PINN loss.
  • PIANO predicts satellite imagery, which is then converted into radar images via a generative model for precipitation nowcasting.
  • Demonstrates notable improvement in predicting moderate (4mm/h) and short-term heavy (8mm/h) precipitation events compared to baseline models.
  • Exhibits low seasonal variability in predictions, indicating enhanced robustness and generalization capabilities.
  • Aims to provide a more accessible and accurate solution for early warning of disasters, particularly in regions with limited resources.
  • Serves as a promising baseline for future research in physics-informed precipitation nowcasting.

Methodology

The study proposes PIANO, a physics-informed dual neural operator. It utilizes satellite imagery as input. During training, the model enforces the fundamental advection-diffusion equation through a Physics-Informed Neural Network (PINN) loss to predict future satellite imagery. Subsequently, a generative model is employed to convert these predicted satellite images into radar images, which are then used for precipitation nowcasting.

Key Findings

PIANO significantly improves prediction accuracy for moderate (4mm/h) and short-term heavy (8mm/h) precipitation events. The model also shows low seasonal variability in its predictions, indicating strong robustness and better generalization across different times of the year, which is crucial for reliable disaster warning systems.

Clinical Impact

Enhanced precipitation nowcasting directly translates to improved disaster preparedness and public health outcomes. Better predictions facilitate more effective allocation of medical resources, timely evacuation orders, pre-positioning of emergency supplies, and implementation of preventative measures against flood-related injuries, waterborne diseases (e.g., cholera, typhoid), and vector-borne diseases (e.g., malaria, dengue) whose prevalence can increase after heavy rainfall. Its accessibility can particularly benefit underserved regions with limited infrastructure.

Limitations

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract, but common considerations for such models include the need for extensive validation across diverse geographical regions and climate zones, potential dependency on satellite data quality, and the computational demands of the generative model component.

Future Directions

The study positions PIANO as a good baseline, suggesting future research can build upon this physics-informed framework for further advancements in precipitation nowcasting, potentially exploring more complex physical phenomena or integrating additional data sources.

Abstract

Precipitation nowcasting, key for early warning of disasters, currently relies on computationally expensive and restrictive methods that limit access to many countries. To overcome this challenge, we propose precipitation nowcasting using satellite imagery with physics constraints for improved accuracy and physical consistency. We use a novel physics-informed dual neural operator (PIANO) structure to enforce the fundamental equation of advection-diffusion during training to predict satellite imagery using a PINN loss. Then, we use a generative model to convert satellite images to radar images, which are used for precipitation nowcasting. Compared to baseline models, our proposed model shows a notable improvement in moderate (4mm/h) precipitation event prediction alongside short-term heavy (8mm/h) precipitation event prediction. It also demonstrates low seasonal variability in predictions, indicating robustness for generalization. This study suggests the potential of the PIANO and serves as a good baseline for physics-informed precipitation nowcasting.
