Toward Scalable Early Cancer Detection: Evaluating EHR-Based Predictive Models Against Traditional Screening Criteria

Summary

This paper evaluates the efficacy of EHR-based predictive models for early cancer detection against traditional screening criteria. It demonstrates that these models significantly outperform traditional risk factors, achieving a 3- to 6-fold higher enrichment of true cancer cases in high-risk groups, and an advanced EHR foundation model further improves prediction across multiple cancer types. The findings highlight the clinical potential of leveraging comprehensive patient data for more precise and scalable early cancer detection strategies.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing early cancer detection by offering a data-driven approach that surpasses the limitations of current screening guidelines. It paves the way for more precise, personalized, and scalable strategies to identify individuals at high risk for various cancers, potentially leading to earlier diagnoses and improved patient outcomes.

This serves as a clinical decision support tool to enhance cancer screening and early intervention strategies.

Methodology

The study involved a systematic evaluation of EHR-based predictive models against traditional risk factors (including age, smoking history, gene mutations, and family history of cancer). Data was drawn from the All of Us Research Program, which integrates EHR, genomic, and survey data from over 865,000 participants. Performance was assessed using a 'baseline modeling approach' for 8 major cancer types, and further explored with a 'state-of-the-art EHR foundation model' for 26 cancer types. The primary metric for comparison was the 'enrichment of true cancer cases among individuals identified as high risk'.

Key Findings

EHR-based predictive models demonstrated a 3- to 6-fold higher enrichment of true cancer cases in high-risk individuals compared to identification using traditional risk factors alone. This superior performance was consistent whether the EHR models were used independently or in conjunction with traditional criteria. Furthermore, an advanced EHR foundation model exhibited improved predictive performance across 26 different cancer types, highlighting its broader applicability and potential.

This could lead to the development of more personalized and proactive screening programs, enabling earlier interventions, potentially reducing cancer mortality, and making early detection strategies more scalable and efficient across healthcare systems.

Abstract

Current cancer screening guidelines cover only a few cancer types and rely on narrowly defined criteria such as age or a single risk factor like smoking history, to identify high-risk individuals. Predictive models using electronic health records (EHRs), which capture large-scale longitudinal patient-level health information, may provide a more effective tool for identifying high-risk groups by detecting subtle prediagnostic signals of cancer. Recent advances in large language and foundation models have further expanded this potential, yet evidence remains limited on how useful HER-based models are compared with traditional risk factors currently used in screening guidelines. We systematically evaluated the clinical utility of EHR-based predictive models against traditional risk factors, including gene mutations and family history of cancer, for identifying high-risk individuals across eight major cancers (breast, lung, colorectal, prostate, ovarian, liver, pancreatic, and stomach), using data from the All of Us Research Program, which integrates EHR, genomic, and survey data from over 865,000 participants. Even with a baseline modeling approach, EHR-based models achieved a 3- to 6-fold higher enrichment of true cancer cases among individuals identified as high risk compared with traditional risk factors alone, whether used as a standalone or complementary tool. The EHR foundation model, a state-of-the-art approach trained on comprehensive patient trajectories, further improved predictive performance across 26 cancer types, demonstrating the clinical potential of EHR-based predictive modeling to support more precise and scalable early detection strategies.
