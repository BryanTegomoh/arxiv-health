SHIELD: Securing Healthcare IoT with Efficient Machine Learning Techniques for Anomaly Detection

arXiv ID: 2511.03661v1
Published: 2025-11-05
Authors: Mahek Desai, Apoorva Rumale, Marjan Asadinia
Categories: cs.LG

Summary

This study proposes SHIELD, a machine learning-driven framework designed to enhance healthcare IoT security by effectively detecting both malicious cyberattacks and faulty device anomalies. Evaluating eight diverse ML models on a 200,000-record dataset, the research identified XGBoost as achieving 99% accuracy for anomaly detection, while K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) demonstrated near-perfect performance for attack detection with minimal computational overhead. These findings significantly improve early threat detection, aiming to prevent data breaches and ensure safe medical device operations.

Medical Relevance

Securing healthcare IoT devices is paramount for patient safety, data privacy, and continuous care delivery. This framework directly contributes to maintaining the integrity and reliability of medical systems, preventing life-threatening malfunctions or malicious attacks that could compromise patient health data or disrupt critical healthcare services.

AI Health Application

The research applies various machine learning models (e.g., XGBoost, KNN, GAN, VAE, SVM, Isolation Forest, GNN, LSTM Autoencoders) for anomaly detection to secure healthcare IoT devices. This involves identifying malicious cyberattacks and faulty device anomalies to protect patient data, minimize system downtime, and ensure safe operation of medical devices.

Methodology

The study developed an ML-driven framework, SHIELD, utilizing a dataset of 200,000 records. Eight distinct machine learning models were selected and evaluated: XGBoost and K-NN (supervised); GAN and VAE (semi-supervised); and One-Class SVM, Isolation Forest, Graph Neural Networks (GNN), and LSTM Autoencoders (unsupervised). These models were benchmarked across multiple metrics including F1-score, precision, recall, accuracy, ROC-AUC, and computational efficiency to identify their effectiveness in detecting both malicious cyberattacks and faulty device anomalies.

Key Findings

For general anomaly detection (e.g., faulty devices), XGBoost achieved 99% accuracy with a minimal computational overhead of 0.04s, while Isolation Forest effectively balanced precision and recall. LSTM Autoencoders underperformed with lower accuracy and higher latency. For attack detection (malicious cyberattacks), K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) demonstrated near-perfect precision, recall, and F1-score with the lowest computational cost (0.05s), followed by VAE at 97% accuracy. Conversely, GAN exhibited the highest computational cost with the lowest accuracy and ROC-AUC for attack detection.

Clinical Impact

The proposed SHIELD framework offers a robust mechanism for early detection of cyber threats and device failures in healthcare IoT environments. This capability is critical for preventing sensitive patient data breaches, minimizing system downtime that could affect patient care, and ensuring the uninterrupted, safe, and reliable operation of connected medical devices, thereby safeguarding patient trust and health outcomes in an increasingly interconnected healthcare landscape.

Abstract

The integration of IoT devices in healthcare introduces significant security and reliability challenges, increasing susceptibility to cyber threats and operational anomalies. This study proposes a machine learning-driven framework for (1) detecting malicious cyberattacks and (2) identifying faulty device anomalies, leveraging a dataset of 200,000 records. Eight machine learning models are evaluated across three learning approaches: supervised learning (XGBoost, K-Nearest Neighbors (K- NN)), semi-supervised learning (Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN), Variational Autoencoders (VAE)), and unsupervised learning (One-Class Support Vector Machine (SVM), Isolation Forest, Graph Neural Networks (GNN), and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) Autoencoders). The comprehensive evaluation was conducted across multiple metrics like F1-score, precision, recall, accuracy, ROC-AUC, computational efficiency. XGBoost achieved 99\% accuracy with minimal computational overhead (0.04s) for anomaly detection, while Isolation Forest balanced precision and recall effectively. LSTM Autoencoders underperformed with lower accuracy and higher latency. For attack detection, KNN achieved near-perfect precision, recall, and F1-score with the lowest computational cost (0.05s), followed by VAE at 97% accuracy. GAN showed the highest computational cost with lowest accuracy and ROC-AUC. These findings enhance IoT-enabled healthcare security through effective anomaly detection strategies. By improving early detection of cyber threats and device failures, this framework has the potential to prevent data breaches, minimize system downtime, and ensure the continuous and safe operation of medical devices, ultimately safeguarding patient health and trust in IoT-driven healthcare solutions.
