SHIELD: Securing Healthcare IoT with Efficient Machine Learning Techniques for Anomaly Detection

arXiv ID: 2511.03661v1
Published: 2025-11-05
Authors: Mahek Desai, Apoorva Rumale, Marjan Asadinia
Categories: cs.LG

Summary

This study proposes SHIELD, a machine learning-driven framework to enhance healthcare IoT security by detecting both malicious cyberattacks and faulty device anomalies. Evaluating eight diverse models across supervised, semi-supervised, and unsupervised learning, it identifies XGBoost as highly effective for anomaly detection (99% accuracy, 0.04s) and K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) for attack detection (near-perfect metrics, 0.05s). These findings provide strategies for improving early threat and failure detection, safeguarding patient health and critical medical infrastructure.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for medicine and health as it directly tackles the escalating security and reliability risks associated with integrated IoT devices in healthcare environments. By offering robust solutions for detecting cyber threats and device malfunctions, it protects sensitive patient data, ensures the operational integrity of medical equipment, and ultimately preserves patient safety and trust in digital healthcare systems.

This application directly aims to enhance the security, reliability, and safe operation of medical devices and protect patient data and health.

  • Addresses critical security and reliability challenges posed by IoT devices in healthcare, including cyber threats and operational anomalies.
  • Proposes a machine learning framework, SHIELD, designed for two primary functions: detecting malicious cyberattacks and identifying faulty device anomalies.

  • XGBoost achieved the highest performance for device anomaly detection, reaching 99% accuracy with minimal computational overhead (0.04s).

  • The evaluation was comprehensive, utilizing a dataset of 200,000 records and assessing models based on F1-score, precision, recall, accuracy, ROC-AUC, and computational efficiency.
  • The framework's implementation can significantly improve early detection capabilities, preventing data breaches, minimizing system downtime, and ensuring continuous, safe operation of medical devices.

Methodology

The study implemented a comparative machine learning methodology, evaluating eight distinct models for anomaly and cyberattack detection on a dataset of 200,000 records. Models included supervised (XGBoost, K-NN), semi-supervised (GAN, VAE), and unsupervised learning (One-Class SVM, Isolation Forest, GNN, LSTM Autoencoders). Performance was rigorously assessed using multiple metrics: F1-score, precision, recall, accuracy, ROC-AUC, and computational efficiency, to determine the most effective techniques for securing healthcare IoT.

Key Findings

For anomaly detection, XGBoost achieved superior performance with 99% accuracy and minimal computational overhead (0.04s), while Isolation Forest effectively balanced precision and recall. LSTM Autoencoders were found to underperform with lower accuracy and higher latency. In the context of attack detection, K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN) demonstrated near-perfect precision, recall, and F1-score at the lowest computational cost (0.05s), with Variational Autoencoders (VAE) following at 97% accuracy. Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN) exhibited the highest computational cost and lowest accuracy and ROC-AUC for attack detection.

Abstract

The integration of IoT devices in healthcare introduces significant security and reliability challenges, increasing susceptibility to cyber threats and operational anomalies. This study proposes a machine learning-driven framework for (1) detecting malicious cyberattacks and (2) identifying faulty device anomalies, leveraging a dataset of 200,000 records. Eight machine learning models are evaluated across three learning approaches: supervised learning (XGBoost, K-Nearest Neighbors (K- NN)), semi-supervised learning (Generative Adversarial Networks (GAN), Variational Autoencoders (VAE)), and unsupervised learning (One-Class Support Vector Machine (SVM), Isolation Forest, Graph Neural Networks (GNN), and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) Autoencoders). The comprehensive evaluation was conducted across multiple metrics like F1-score, precision, recall, accuracy, ROC-AUC, computational efficiency. XGBoost achieved 99\% accuracy with minimal computational overhead (0.04s) for anomaly detection, while Isolation Forest balanced precision and recall effectively. LSTM Autoencoders underperformed with lower accuracy and higher latency. For attack detection, KNN achieved near-perfect precision, recall, and F1-score with the lowest computational cost (0.05s), followed by VAE at 97% accuracy. GAN showed the highest computational cost with lowest accuracy and ROC-AUC. These findings enhance IoT-enabled healthcare security through effective anomaly detection strategies. By improving early detection of cyber threats and device failures, this framework has the potential to prevent data breaches, minimize system downtime, and ensure the continuous and safe operation of medical devices, ultimately safeguarding patient health and trust in IoT-driven healthcare solutions.
