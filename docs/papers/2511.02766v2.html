Summary

This paper reviews the critical role of the gut microbiota in regulating sleep physiology via the gut-microbiota-brain axis (GMBA), detailing how microbial composition influences neural, endocrine, and immune pathways, as well as neurotransmitter production, circadian rhythms, and metabolic homeostasis. It highlights that gut dysbiosis contributes to sleep disorders and associated pathologies by altering key metabolic pathways, proposing microbiota-targeted therapies as promising interventions.

Medical Relevance

The findings underscore the gut microbiota as a crucial, modifiable factor in sleep health, offering novel therapeutic avenues for widespread sleep disorders, neurodegenerative conditions, metabolic syndromes, and mood disorders by targeting gut health and its intricate communication with the brain and other systems.

Key Points

  • The gut microbiota acts as a fundamental regulator of sleep physiology through the gut-microbiota-brain axis (GMBA), influencing neural, endocrine, and immune pathways.
  • The GMBA modulates critical physiological processes including neurotransmitter (e.g., serotonin, GABA) production, circadian rhythms, and metabolic homeostasis.
  • Disruptions in gut microbial composition (dysbiosis) are directly linked to the pathogenesis of sleep disorders, neuroinflammation, and systemic immune dysfunction.
  • Gut dysbiosis contributes to sleep disturbances by altering the metabolism of key compounds like serotonin, GABA, and short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs).
  • These microbiota-mediated disruptions in sleep have significant implications for a range of severe health conditions, including neurodegenerative diseases, metabolic syndromes, and mood disorders.
  • Emerging microbiota-targeted therapies such as probiotics, fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT), and chrononutrition are proposed as potential interventions to restore sleep homeostasis and improve health outcomes.

Abstract

The gut microbiota has emerged as a fundamental regulator of sleep physiology, influencing neural, endocrine, and immune pathways through the gut-microbiota-brain axis (GMBA). This bidirectional communication system modulates neurotransmitter production, circadian rhythms, and metabolic homeostasis, while disruptions in microbial composition have been linked to sleep disorders, neuroinflammation, and systemic immune dysfunction. Recent findings suggest that gut dysbiosis contributes to sleep disturbances by altering serotonin, GABA, and short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) metabolism, with implications for neurodegenerative diseases, metabolic syndromes, and mood disorders. Additionally, the gut microbiota interacts with the endocrine and immune systems, shaping inflammatory responses and stress adaptation mechanisms. This review explores the intricate connections between sleep and the gut microbiota, integrating emerging research on microbiota-targeted therapies, such as probiotics, fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT), and chrononutrition, as potential interventions to restore sleep homeostasis and improve health outcomes
