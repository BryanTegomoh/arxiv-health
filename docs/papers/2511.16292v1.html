Summary

This paper presents a proof-of-concept demonstrating secure, distributed agent communication across independent systems in healthcare using only natural-language messages and strict data locality. It showcases how organizations like a Clinic, Insurer, and Specialist Network can cooperatively facilitate decision support without sharing identifiers, structured schemas, or centralizing sensitive patient data, primarily utilizing pseudonymized tokens and local data processing.

Medical Relevance

This research addresses critical challenges in healthcare interoperability and privacy by demonstrating a secure method for disparate organizations to collaborate on patient care decisions without centralizing or compromising sensitive health data. It offers a paradigm for future systems to navigate complex regulatory landscapes like HIPAA and GDPR while enhancing care coordination.

AI Health Application

This research presents a multi-agent AI system designed to enable secure, privacy-preserving communication and collaborative reasoning between independent healthcare organizations (e.g., clinics, insurers, specialist networks). It uses natural language messages and strict data locality to facilitate complex interactions, such as insurance coverage evaluation and specialist appropriateness recommendations, without centralizing patient data or compromising identity. This directly applies AI to improve interoperability and data governance within the healthcare ecosystem.

Key Points

  • A proof-of-concept system for agent-to-agent communication across distributed healthcare organizations (Clinic, Insurer, Specialist Network) is presented.
  • Communication relies exclusively on natural-language messages (concise summaries) exchanged via `OperationRelay` calls, bypassing shared identifiers, structured schemas, or centralized data exchange.
  • Strict data locality is enforced, meaning each agent operates solely on its local data (e.g., synthetic clinic records, insurance tables, clinical guidance extracts) without receiving or reconstructing patient identity.
  • Patient privacy is maintained through the use of HMAC-based pseudonymized case tokens, generated by the Clinic, which allow for linking interactions without revealing personal health information.
  • The Orpius platform serves as the underlying infrastructure for multi-agent orchestration, tool execution, and privacy-preserving communication.
  • The prototype demonstrates a workflow where an Insurer evaluates coverage and consults a Specialist agent based on a pseudonymized request, leading to an appropriateness recommendation.
  • The work highlights architectural patterns and communication flows enabling distributed reasoning among specialized agents while adhering to stringent data locality and privacy requirements.

Methodology

The authors developed a proof-of-concept prototype leveraging the Orpius platform for multi-agent orchestration. They implemented three distinct agents representing a Clinic, an Insurer, and a Specialist Network, each operating on its own synthetic local data. Communication between agents was restricted to natural-language summaries via `OperationRelay` calls. Privacy was enforced by using HMAC-based pseudonymized case tokens, ensuring no patient identity was shared or reconstructed across systems.

Key Findings

The study successfully demonstrated the feasibility of distributed agent reasoning across independent healthcare systems using natural-language communication and strict data locality, entirely without shared identifiers or centralized data. It established that complex inter-organizational workflows, such as insurance coverage evaluation and specialist recommendations, can be achieved securely while preventing any agent from reconstructing patient identity via pseudonymized tokens and localized data processing.

Clinical Impact

This architecture could significantly enhance secure patient care coordination by enabling real-time collaboration between clinics, insurers, and specialists for tasks like referrals, pre-authorizations, and adherence to clinical guidelines. It has the potential to streamline administrative processes, reduce errors, and accelerate decision-making, ultimately leading to more efficient and patient-centric healthcare delivery while maintaining the highest standards of data privacy and compliance.

Limitations

The work is explicitly a proof-of-concept, not a clinically validated or production-ready system. No clinician review was conducted, and evaluation was limited to basic functional runs, without rigorous performance or clinical outcome assessment. The data used was synthetic, not real patient data.

Future Directions

The authors suggest opportunities for more rigorous evaluation of the system, including performance metrics, security audits, and clinical validation. They also highlight the potential for future research in broader applications of decentralised multi-agent systems within healthcare and beyond.

Abstract

This paper presents a proof-of-concept demonstration of agent-to-agent communication across distributed systems, using only natural-language messages and without shared identifiers, structured schemas, or centralised data exchange. The prototype explores how multiple organisations (represented here as a Clinic, Insurer, and Specialist Network) can cooperate securely via pseudonymised case tokens, local data lookups, and controlled operational boundaries. The system uses Orpius as the underlying platform for multi-agent orchestration, tool execution, and privacy-preserving communication. All agents communicate through OperationRelay calls, exchanging concise natural-language summaries. Each agent operates on its own data (such as synthetic clinic records, insurance enrolment tables, and clinical guidance extracts), and none receives or reconstructs patient identity. The Clinic computes an HMAC-based pseudonymous token, the Insurer evaluates coverage rules and consults the Specialist agent, and the Specialist returns an appropriateness recommendation. The goal of this prototype is intentionally limited: to demonstrate feasibility, not to provide a clinically validated, production-ready system. No clinician review was conducted, and no evaluation beyond basic functional runs was performed. The work highlights architectural patterns, privacy considerations, and communication flows that enable distributed reasoning among specialised agents while keeping data local to each organisation. We conclude by outlining opportunities for more rigorous evaluation and future research in decentralised multi-agent systems.
