Summary

This paper presents a proof-of-concept for secure, distributed agent-to-agent communication across independent healthcare systems (Clinic, Insurer, Specialist Network) using only natural-language messages. It demonstrates feasibility for cooperative reasoning without shared identifiers, structured schemas, or centralized data exchange, ensuring strict data locality and patient privacy via pseudonymised tokens.

Medical Relevance

This work directly addresses critical challenges in healthcare: enabling secure data exchange and coordinated care among disparate organizations (e.g., clinics, insurers, specialists) while rigorously preserving patient privacy and adhering to strict data locality principles, crucial for compliance and trust.

AI Health Application

This paper presents a medical AI application demonstrating a secure, privacy-preserving multi-agent system for collaboration across different healthcare entities (clinics, insurers, specialists). It uses AI agents for distributed reasoning, facilitating data exchange, evaluating insurance coverage, and providing clinical appropriateness recommendations, all while ensuring strict data locality and patient identity privacy.

Key Points

  • **Distributed Agent Reasoning**: Prototyped a system enabling intelligent agents from independent organizations to communicate and reason cooperatively.
  • **Strict Data Locality & Privacy**: Achieved communication without sharing patient identity, structured schemas, or central data exchange, with each agent operating on its local data.
  • **Natural Language Communication**: Agents communicate exclusively through concise natural-language summaries via 'OperationRelay' calls.
  • **Pseudonymisation**: The Clinic agent generates HMAC-based pseudonymous case tokens, preventing recipient agents from reconstructing patient identity.
  • **Multi-Organizational Workflow**: Demonstrated a functional flow involving a Clinic computing a token, an Insurer evaluating coverage and consulting a Specialist agent, and the Specialist providing an appropriateness recommendation.
  • **Orpius Platform**: Utilized Orpius for multi-agent orchestration, tool execution, and privacy-preserving communication.
  • **Architectural Patterns**: Highlighted architectural patterns and communication flows for secure, decentralized multi-agent systems in healthcare.

Methodology

The study involved developing a proof-of-concept prototype using the Orpius platform. It simulated a multi-organizational healthcare workflow (Clinic, Insurer, Specialist Network) with synthetic clinic records, insurance tables, and clinical guidance. Communication was facilitated via natural-language 'OperationRelay' calls, and patient privacy was maintained using HMAC-based pseudonymous tokens to prevent identity reconstruction by downstream agents.

Key Findings

The primary finding is the demonstrated feasibility of distributed agent reasoning across independent systems, maintaining strict data locality and privacy. The prototype successfully showcased how specialized agents can cooperate through natural language summaries and pseudonymised tokens without sharing sensitive patient identifiers or requiring a centralized data hub, thereby highlighting robust architectural patterns for secure, decentralized healthcare interactions.

Clinical Impact

This prototype offers a potential blueprint for enhancing secure interoperability in healthcare, enabling more efficient and private workflows for tasks like referrals, prior authorizations, and distributed clinical decision support. It could facilitate better-coordinated patient care across different providers and payers while rigorously upholding data privacy and compliance requirements.

Limitations

The authors explicitly state that this is a proof-of-concept, not a clinically validated or production-ready system. No clinician review was conducted, and evaluation was limited to basic functional runs, without extensive performance or clinical outcome assessment. The data used was synthetic, not real-world patient data.

Future Directions

The paper concludes by outlining opportunities for more rigorous evaluation of the system, including performance and security analyses, and further research into developing and deploying decentralized multi-agent systems within healthcare and other data-sensitive domains.

Abstract

This paper presents a proof-of-concept demonstration of agent-to-agent communication across distributed systems, using only natural-language messages and without shared identifiers, structured schemas, or centralised data exchange. The prototype explores how multiple organisations (represented here as a Clinic, Insurer, and Specialist Network) can cooperate securely via pseudonymised case tokens, local data lookups, and controlled operational boundaries. The system uses Orpius as the underlying platform for multi-agent orchestration, tool execution, and privacy-preserving communication. All agents communicate through OperationRelay calls, exchanging concise natural-language summaries. Each agent operates on its own data (such as synthetic clinic records, insurance enrolment tables, and clinical guidance extracts), and none receives or reconstructs patient identity. The Clinic computes an HMAC-based pseudonymous token, the Insurer evaluates coverage rules and consults the Specialist agent, and the Specialist returns an appropriateness recommendation. The goal of this prototype is intentionally limited: to demonstrate feasibility, not to provide a clinically validated, production-ready system. No clinician review was conducted, and no evaluation beyond basic functional runs was performed. The work highlights architectural patterns, privacy considerations, and communication flows that enable distributed reasoning among specialised agents while keeping data local to each organisation. We conclude by outlining opportunities for more rigorous evaluation and future research in decentralised multi-agent systems.
