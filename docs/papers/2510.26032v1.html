Artificial Intelligence-Enabled Analysis of Radiology Reports: Epidemiology and Consequences of Incidental Thyroid Findings

Summary

This study developed and deployed a transformer-based Natural Language Processing (NLP) pipeline to analyze radiology reports for Incidental Thyroid Findings (ITFs). It found that ITFs are common (7.8% of patients) and significantly associated with a cascade of downstream procedures and the diagnosis of mostly small, low-risk papillary thyroid cancers, highlighting a substantial role in overdiagnosis.

Medical Relevance

This study quantifies the prevalence and clinical impact of incidental thyroid findings, demonstrating how they trigger diagnostic cascades that often lead to the detection of low-risk cancers, directly informing debates on overdiagnosis and the urgent need for refined clinical guidelines for patient follow-up and management.

AI Health Application

The AI application is a transformer-based Natural Language Processing (NLP) pipeline designed to automatically identify incidental thyroid findings (ITFs) and extract detailed nodule characteristics from unstructured text within radiology reports. This AI system facilitates large-scale analysis of medical records to understand the prevalence, features, and clinical outcomes of ITFs, contributing to insights on thyroid cancer overdiagnosis and informing better clinical follow-up strategies.

Key Points

  • A transformer-based NLP pipeline was developed, validated, and deployed to identify Incidental Thyroid Findings (ITFs) and extract nodule characteristics from radiology reports across multiple imaging modalities and body regions.
  • Among 115,683 adult patients without prior thyroid disease, 7.8% (9,077) had an ITF, with 92.9% of these being thyroid nodules.
  • ITFs were more likely to be detected in women, older adults, individuals with higher BMI, when imaging was ordered by oncology or internal medicine, and via neck CT, PET, and nuclear medicine scans compared to chest CT.
  • Documentation of nodule characteristics in radiology reports was poor, with size reported in only 44% of cases and other critical features (e.g., calcifications) in fewer than 15%.
  • Patients with ITFs had significantly higher odds of subsequent thyroid nodule diagnosis, downstream thyroid ultrasound, biopsy, thyroidectomy, and thyroid cancer diagnosis compared to patients without ITFs.
  • Most cancers detected following ITFs were papillary, and these cancers were larger when detected after ITFs compared to cancers found in patients without prior ITFs.
  • The findings underscore that ITFs frequently trigger diagnostic cascades leading to the detection of small, low-risk cancers, raising concerns about thyroid cancer overdiagnosis and emphasizing the need for standardized reporting and more selective follow-up.

Methodology

A retrospective cohort study analyzed data from adults without prior thyroid disease who underwent thyroid-capturing imaging at Mayo Clinic sites from July 1, 2017, to September 30, 2023. A transformer-based Natural Language Processing (NLP) pipeline was developed, validated, and deployed to identify Incidental Thyroid Findings (ITFs) and extract nodule characteristics from radiology reports spanning multiple imaging modalities and body regions. Logistic regression was employed to identify demographic and imaging-related factors associated with ITFs and their downstream clinical outcomes.

Key Findings

The study identified ITFs in 7.8% (9,077 of 115,683) of patients, predominantly as nodules (92.9%). ITFs were more prevalent in women, older adults, those with higher BMI, and when imaging was ordered by oncology or internal medicine, with neck CT, PET, and nuclear medicine scans more likely to detect them than chest CT. Nodule characteristics were poorly documented, with size reported in only 44% and other features in less than 15%. Patients with ITFs had significantly higher odds of subsequent thyroid nodule diagnosis, biopsy, thyroidectomy, and thyroid cancer diagnosis. Most cancers found were papillary and larger when detected after an ITF compared to those found without an ITF history.

Clinical Impact

The high prevalence of ITFs and their strong association with diagnostic cascades leading to small, low-risk thyroid cancers significantly contributes to thyroid cancer overdiagnosis. This research emphasizes the critical need for standardized reporting guidelines for thyroid nodules within radiology reports and the development of more selective, risk-stratified follow-up protocols for ITFs to mitigate unnecessary procedures, patient anxiety, and healthcare resource expenditure.

Limitations

Not explicitly stated in the abstract.

Future Directions

The findings underscore the need for standardized reporting of incidental thyroid findings in radiology reports and the development and implementation of more selective, evidence-based follow-up strategies to address the issue of thyroid cancer overdiagnosis effectively.

Abstract

Importance Incidental thyroid findings (ITFs) are increasingly detected on imaging performed for non-thyroid indications. Their prevalence, features, and clinical consequences remain undefined. Objective To develop, validate, and deploy a natural language processing (NLP) pipeline to identify ITFs in radiology reports and assess their prevalence, features, and clinical outcomes. Design, Setting, and Participants Retrospective cohort of adults without prior thyroid disease undergoing thyroid-capturing imaging at Mayo Clinic sites from July 1, 2017, to September 30, 2023. A transformer-based NLP pipeline identified ITFs and extracted nodule characteristics from image reports from multiple modalities and body regions. Main Outcomes and Measures Prevalence of ITFs, downstream thyroid ultrasound, biopsy, thyroidectomy, and thyroid cancer diagnosis. Logistic regression identified demographic and imaging-related factors. Results Among 115,683 patients (mean age, 56.8 [SD 17.2] years; 52.9% women), 9,077 (7.8%) had an ITF, of which 92.9% were nodules. ITFs were more likely in women, older adults, those with higher BMI, and when imaging was ordered by oncology or internal medicine. Compared with chest CT, ITFs were more likely via neck CT, PET, and nuclear medicine scans. Nodule characteristics were poorly documented, with size reported in 44% and other features in fewer than 15% (e.g. calcifications). Compared with patients without ITFs, those with ITFs had higher odds of thyroid nodule diagnosis, biopsy, thyroidectomy and thyroid cancer diagnosis. Most cancers were papillary, and larger when detected after ITFs vs no ITF. Conclusions ITFs were common and strongly associated with cascades leading to the detection of small, low-risk cancers. These findings underscore the role of ITFs in thyroid cancer overdiagnosis and the need for standardized reporting and more selective follow-up.
