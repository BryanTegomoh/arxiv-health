Artificial Intelligence-Enabled Analysis of Radiology Reports: Epidemiology and Consequences of Incidental Thyroid Findings

Summary

This research developed an AI-enabled Natural Language Processing (NLP) pipeline to analyze radiology reports for incidental thyroid findings (ITFs) in a large patient cohort. It revealed ITFs are common (7.8%) and significantly trigger diagnostic cascades (ultrasound, biopsy, thyroidectomy), often leading to the detection of small, low-risk papillary thyroid cancers, underscoring their role in thyroid cancer overdiagnosis.

Medical Relevance

This study is highly relevant as it quantifies the prevalence and diagnostic implications of incidental thyroid findings, a growing clinical concern. It directly addresses the issue of thyroid cancer overdiagnosis, providing data to inform more judicious management of ITFs and potentially reduce unnecessary patient interventions and healthcare burdens.

AI Health Application

Development, validation, and deployment of a transformer-based Natural Language Processing (NLP) pipeline to automatically identify incidental thyroid findings and extract nodule characteristics from unstructured radiology reports. This AI application enables large-scale epidemiological analysis of medical findings and their clinical consequences, informing diagnostic pathways and treatment strategies.

Key Points

  • A transformer-based NLP pipeline was developed and deployed to identify incidental thyroid findings (ITFs) and extract nodule characteristics from diverse radiology reports.
  • In a retrospective cohort of 115,683 adults without prior thyroid disease, ITFs were present in 7.8% of patients, with 92.9% being thyroid nodules.
  • ITFs were more likely in women, older adults, those with higher BMI, and when imaging was ordered by oncology or internal medicine, as well as via neck CT, PET, and nuclear medicine scans compared to chest CT.
  • Documentation of nodule characteristics in reports was poor; size was reported in only 44% of cases, and other features (e.g., calcifications) in less than 15%.
  • Patients with ITFs had significantly higher odds of downstream thyroid nodule diagnosis, biopsy, thyroidectomy, and thyroid cancer diagnosis.
  • Most detected cancers were papillary and found to be larger when diagnosed after an ITF, suggesting ITFs contribute to thyroid cancer overdiagnosis by triggering investigations for small, low-risk lesions.
  • The study emphasizes the critical need for standardized reporting of thyroid nodule characteristics and more selective follow-up strategies for ITFs to mitigate overdiagnosis and overtreatment.

Methodology

The study employed a retrospective cohort design, analyzing data from 115,683 adults without pre-existing thyroid disease who underwent thyroid-capturing imaging at Mayo Clinic sites from July 2017 to September 2023. A transformer-based Natural Language Processing (NLP) pipeline was developed, validated, and deployed to automatically identify ITFs and extract nodule characteristics from multi-modality radiology reports. Logistic regression models were used to identify demographic and imaging-related factors associated with ITF prevalence and downstream clinical outcomes.

Key Findings

7.8% (9,077) of patients had an ITF, predominantly nodules (92.9%). ITFs were more prevalent in women, older adults, those with higher BMI, and when imaging was ordered by oncology or internal medicine, or performed via neck CT, PET, or nuclear medicine scans. Nodule characteristics were poorly documented (size in 44%, other features <15%). ITFs were strongly associated with significantly increased odds of downstream thyroid nodule diagnosis, biopsy, thyroidectomy, and thyroid cancer diagnosis. The majority of cancers detected were papillary and larger post-ITF detection, suggesting a link to thyroid cancer overdiagnosis.

Clinical Impact

The findings underscore the necessity for standardized reporting of incidental thyroid nodule characteristics in radiology to enable better risk stratification. Clinically, this research advocates for the development and implementation of more selective and evidence-based follow-up protocols for ITFs, aiming to reduce the diagnostic cascade that leads to overdiagnosis and potential overtreatment of indolent thyroid cancers, thereby minimizing patient anxiety, morbidity from unnecessary procedures, and healthcare costs.

Limitations

The abstract implicitly suggests limitations, such as the current lack of standardized reporting for nodule characteristics, which impacts data consistency. As a single-institution, retrospective study at Mayo Clinic, generalizability to other populations or healthcare systems might be limited. The abstract does not detail the specific validation metrics for the NLP pipeline.

Future Directions

The paper explicitly calls for the need for standardized reporting of incidental thyroid findings in radiology reports. It also implicitly suggests the development and implementation of more selective, risk-stratified follow-up guidelines for ITFs to reduce overdiagnosis. Further research could focus on validating these guidelines in prospective studies and refining AI tools for more comprehensive nodule characterization.

Abstract

Importance Incidental thyroid findings (ITFs) are increasingly detected on imaging performed for non-thyroid indications. Their prevalence, features, and clinical consequences remain undefined. Objective To develop, validate, and deploy a natural language processing (NLP) pipeline to identify ITFs in radiology reports and assess their prevalence, features, and clinical outcomes. Design, Setting, and Participants Retrospective cohort of adults without prior thyroid disease undergoing thyroid-capturing imaging at Mayo Clinic sites from July 1, 2017, to September 30, 2023. A transformer-based NLP pipeline identified ITFs and extracted nodule characteristics from image reports from multiple modalities and body regions. Main Outcomes and Measures Prevalence of ITFs, downstream thyroid ultrasound, biopsy, thyroidectomy, and thyroid cancer diagnosis. Logistic regression identified demographic and imaging-related factors. Results Among 115,683 patients (mean age, 56.8 [SD 17.2] years; 52.9% women), 9,077 (7.8%) had an ITF, of which 92.9% were nodules. ITFs were more likely in women, older adults, those with higher BMI, and when imaging was ordered by oncology or internal medicine. Compared with chest CT, ITFs were more likely via neck CT, PET, and nuclear medicine scans. Nodule characteristics were poorly documented, with size reported in 44% and other features in fewer than 15% (e.g. calcifications). Compared with patients without ITFs, those with ITFs had higher odds of thyroid nodule diagnosis, biopsy, thyroidectomy and thyroid cancer diagnosis. Most cancers were papillary, and larger when detected after ITFs vs no ITF. Conclusions ITFs were common and strongly associated with cascades leading to the detection of small, low-risk cancers. These findings underscore the role of ITFs in thyroid cancer overdiagnosis and the need for standardized reporting and more selective follow-up.
