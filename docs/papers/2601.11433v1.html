Inter-patient ECG Arrhythmia Classification with LGNs and LUTNs

arXiv ID: 2601.11433v1
Published: 2026-01-16
Authors: Wout Mommen, Lars Keuninckx, Paul Detterer, Achiel Colpaert, Piet Wambacq
Categories: cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces Deep Differentiable Logic Gate Networks (LGNs) and Lookup Table Networks (LUTNs) for inter-patient ECG arrhythmia classification, demonstrating their suitability with extremely low computational cost. The models achieve high accuracy on the MIT-BIH dataset, leveraging novel preprocessing, MUX-based LUT training, and rate coding, making them ideal for highly efficient, low-power medical devices.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant for developing next-generation medical devices like heart implants and wearable ECG monitors due to its ultra-low power consumption and high processing speed, enabling continuous, accurate arrhythmia detection even in unseen patients.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the development of highly efficient, low-power machine learning models (LGNs and LUTNs) for real-time, automatic detection and classification of cardiac arrhythmias using ECG data. These models are specifically designed for deployment in resource-constrained medical devices such as heart implants and wearable health monitors, enabling continuous and pervasive cardiac health monitoring.

Key Points

  • LGNs and LUTNs are proposed and benchmarked for automatic ECG arrhythmia classification using the challenging inter-patient paradigm on the MIT-BIH dataset.
  • Achieved up to 94.28% accuracy and a 0.683 jŒ∫ index for a four-class classification problem, significantly outperforming previous LGN results.
  • Demonstrated extreme computational efficiency, requiring between 2.89k and 6.17k FLOPs (3 to 6 orders of magnitude less than SOTA methods), including preprocessing and readout.
  • Introduced a novel preprocessing method that showed superior performance across both mixed-patient and inter-patient paradigms.
  • Devised a novel method for training Lookup Tables (LUTs) in LUTNs using the Boolean equation of a multiplexer (MUX).
  • Utilized rate coding for the first time in LGNs and LUTNs, leading to enhanced performance for LGNs.
  • Hardware implementation estimates on an Artix 7 FPGA suggest low resource usage (2000-2990 LUTs) and extremely low power consumption (5-7 mW or 50-70 pJ per inference), suitable for embedded applications.

Methodology

The study utilized Deep Differentiable Logic Gate Networks (LGNs) and Lookup Table Networks (LUTNs) for ECG classification. It employed a novel preprocessing method for the ECG signals and a new training method for LUTNs based on the Boolean equation of a multiplexer. Rate coding was integrated into the LGN and LUTN architectures. Benchmarking was performed on the MIT-BIH arrhythmia dataset using the inter-patient paradigm, where models are tested on patients not included in the training set.

Key Findings

The LGN and LUTN models achieved up to 94.28% accuracy and a 0.683 jŒ∫ index for a four-class arrhythmia classification problem. Critically, these models operate with exceptionally low computational complexity (2.89k-6.17k FLOPs), which is 3 to 6 orders of magnitude more efficient than current SOTA methods. They also demonstrated very low power requirements (5-7 mW) and compact FPGA implementation (2000-2990 LUTs), making them highly suitable for resource-constrained embedded systems.

Clinical Impact

This technology has the potential to revolutionize continuous cardiac monitoring by enabling highly accurate, real-time arrhythmia detection directly within ultralow-power heart implants or wearable devices. This could lead to earlier diagnosis, improved patient management, and better outcomes, especially for patients with intermittent or asymptomatic arrhythmias, without significant battery drain or need for cloud processing.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state any limitations of the proposed LGN/LUTN approach itself, primarily focusing on its advantages in efficiency and performance compared to previous LGN results and SOTA methods' computational cost.

Future Directions

The positive results strongly suggest that LGNs and LUTNs can be effectively utilized for the detection of arrhythmias in extremely low-power and high-speed applications such as heart implants or wearable devices, even for patients whose data were not part of the training set. This implies a future direction towards clinical deployment and integration into commercial medical devices.

Abstract

Deep Differentiable Logic Gate Networks (LGNs) and Lookup Table Networks (LUTNs) are demonstrated to be suitable for the automatic classification of electrocardiograms (ECGs) using the inter-patient paradigm. The methods are benchmarked using the MIT-BIH arrhythmia data set, achieving up to 94.28% accuracy and a $jŒ∫$ index of 0.683 on a four-class classification problem. Our models use between 2.89k and 6.17k FLOPs, including preprocessing and readout, which is three to six orders of magnitude less compared to SOTA methods. A novel preprocessing method is utilized that attains superior performance compared to existing methods for both the mixed-patient and inter-patient paradigms. In addition, a novel method for training the Lookup Tables (LUTs) in LUTNs is devised that uses the Boolean equation of a multiplexer (MUX). Additionally, rate coding was utilized for the first time in these LGNs and LUTNs, enhancing the performance of LGNs. Furthermore, it is the first time that LGNs and LUTNs have been benchmarked on the MIT-BIH arrhythmia dataset using the inter-patient paradigm. Using an Artix 7 FPGA, between 2000 and 2990 LUTs were needed, and between 5 to 7 mW (i.e. 50 pJ to 70 pJ per inference) was estimated for running these models. The performance in terms of both accuracy and $jŒ∫$-index is significantly higher compared to previous LGN results. These positive results suggest that one can utilize LGNs and LUTNs for the detection of arrhythmias at extremely low power and high speeds in heart implants or wearable devices, even for patients not included in the training set.
