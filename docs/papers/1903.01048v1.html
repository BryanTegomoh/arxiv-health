Early Detection of Influenza outbreaks in the United States

Published: 2019-03-04
Authors: Kai Liu, Ravi Srinivasan, Lauren Ancel Meyers

Summary

This paper presents a multivariate algorithm that utilizes freely available online data, specifically Google search and Wikipedia pageview time series, to provide early warning of emerging influenza epidemics in the United States. It demonstrates that this method can detect seasonal influenza outbreaks an average of 16.4 weeks and the 2009 H1N1 pandemic 5 weeks prior to traditional CDC surveillance thresholds being met, without relying on traditional health records.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a powerful, open-source tool for public health officials to gain significantly earlier insights into influenza activity, enabling more proactive and effective public health interventions, resource allocation, and preparedness strategies.

AI Health Application

The paper describes a multivariate algorithm that integrates scan statistics with machine learning to provide early warning of influenza epidemics and emerging infectious disease threats. This AI application leverages freely available online data (Google search and Wikipedia pageviews) for enhanced public health surveillance and outbreak detection, complementing traditional surveillance systems.

Methodology

The study involved evaluating 240 candidate predictors, primarily derived from Google search and Wikipedia pageview time series related to influenza-like topics. A multivariate algorithm was developed using a combination of scan statistics and machine learning to identify the most predictive features. The algorithm's performance was assessed via cross-validation on 2010-2016 data for seasonal influenza and an out-of-sample test for the 2009 H1N1 pandemic, with alarm timing compared against CDC surveillance thresholds.

Key Findings

The optimal predictors for early influenza outbreak detection were eight specific Google search and Wikipedia pageview time series, demonstrating superior performance over traditional surveillance or electronic health record data. The algorithm achieved an average of 16.4 weeks of lead time for seasonal influenza and five weeks for the 2009 H1N1 pandemic, significantly outperforming simpler, less adaptive detection methods.

Clinical Impact

By providing significantly earlier warnings, this algorithm can empower public health agencies to implement timely interventions such as targeted vaccination campaigns, distribution of antiviral medications, public health messaging, and resource mobilization. This proactive approach has the potential to mitigate the spread and severity of influenza outbreaks, reduce healthcare burdens, and ultimately save lives by improving response times to emerging infectious disease threats.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations of the developed algorithm itself. It primarily highlights the deficiencies of existing surveillance systems that the proposed method aims to overcome.

Future Directions

The study suggests this robust methodology, integrating scan statistics and machine learning, can be generalized to design next-generation outbreak detection algorithms for providing early warning of diverse emerging infectious disease threats worldwide.

Abstract

Public health surveillance systems often fail to detect emerging infectious diseases, particularly in resource limited settings. By integrating relevant clinical and internet-source data, we can close critical gaps in coverage and accelerate outbreak detection. Here, we present a multivariate algorithm that uses freely available online data to provide early warning of emerging influenza epidemics in the US. We evaluated 240 candidate predictors and found that the most predictive combination does \textit{not} include surveillance or electronic health records data, but instead consists of eight Google search and Wikipedia pageview time series reflecting changing levels of interest in influenza-related topics. In cross validation on 2010-2016 data, this algorithm sounds alarms an average of 16.4 weeks prior to influenza activity reaching the Center for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) threshold for declaring the start of the season. In an out-of-sample test on data from the rapidly-emerging fall wave of the 2009 H1N1 pandemic, it recognized the threat five weeks in advance of this surveillance threshold. Simpler algorithms, including fixed week-of-the-year triggers, lag the optimized alarms by only a few weeks when detecting seasonal influenza, but fail to provide early warning in the 2009 pandemic scenario. This demonstrates a robust method for designing next generation outbreak detection algorithms. By combining scan statistics with machine learning, it identifies tractable combinations of data sources (from among thousands of candidates) that can provide early warning of emerging infectious disease threats worldwide.
