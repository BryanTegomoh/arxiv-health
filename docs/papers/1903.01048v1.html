Early Detection of Influenza outbreaks in the United States

arXiv ID: 1903.01048v1
Published: 2019-03-04
Authors: Kai Liu, Ravi Srinivasan, Lauren Ancel Meyers
Categories: stat.AP, q-bio.PE

Summary

This paper introduces a multivariate algorithm utilizing freely available online data (Google search and Wikipedia pageviews) for early detection of influenza outbreaks in the US. It demonstrates that a specific combination of eight such time series can provide significantly earlier warnings than traditional surveillance, predicting seasonal influenza activity an average of 16.4 weeks ahead and recognizing the 2009 H1N1 pandemic five weeks in advance of CDC thresholds.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a powerful and resource-efficient method for significantly accelerating the detection of influenza outbreaks, offering public health agencies crucial lead time to implement interventions, allocate resources, and communicate risks, thereby potentially reducing morbidity and mortality.

AI Health Application

The research describes an AI-driven early warning system for infectious disease outbreaks. It uses machine learning and scan statistics to process freely available online data (Google search, Wikipedia pageviews) to predict and detect emerging influenza epidemics and pandemics earlier than traditional surveillance methods. This is a direct application of AI for public health surveillance and predictive modeling in healthcare.

Key Points

  • The study addresses the critical gap in traditional public health surveillance systems, which often fail to detect emerging infectious diseases effectively.
  • A multivariate algorithm was developed to predict influenza outbreaks using only freely available online data, specifically Google search and Wikipedia pageview time series.
  • The most predictive combination of data, surprisingly, did not include traditional surveillance or electronic health records data, but rather eight selected online search and view series.
  • In cross-validation (2010-2016), the algorithm provided alarms an average of 16.4 weeks prior to the CDC's threshold for declaring the start of the influenza season.
  • During an out-of-sample test, the algorithm successfully recognized the rapidly emerging 2009 H1N1 pandemic threat five weeks in advance of the surveillance threshold.
  • Simpler detection methods, such as fixed week-of-the-year triggers, lagged by several weeks for seasonal influenza and entirely failed to provide early warning for the 2009 pandemic.
  • The methodology combines scan statistics with machine learning, presenting a robust framework for designing advanced outbreak detection algorithms for various infectious diseases globally.

Methodology

The study involved evaluating 240 candidate predictors derived from freely available online data (Google search and Wikipedia pageviews). A multivariate algorithm was developed, combining scan statistics with machine learning, to identify the most predictive set of eight time series. This algorithm was cross-validated using 2010-2016 data for seasonal influenza detection and tested out-of-sample on data from the 2009 H1N1 pandemic.

Key Findings

The most effective predictors for influenza outbreaks are specific combinations of Google search and Wikipedia pageview data, not traditional surveillance or EHR. This digital-based algorithm provided an average of 16.4 weeks' early warning for seasonal influenza and detected the 2009 H1N1 pandemic five weeks in advance, significantly outperforming simpler, fixed-week detection methods, especially during pandemic events.

Clinical Impact

The implementation of such an algorithm could enable public health systems to initiate influenza vaccination campaigns, antiviral distribution, and public awareness campaigns significantly earlier. This proactive approach would lead to more effective mitigation of outbreaks, reduced burden on healthcare systems, and improved patient outcomes, particularly crucial during novel pandemic threats.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations of the presented methodology or findings.

Future Directions

The study demonstrates a robust method for designing next-generation outbreak detection algorithms, suggesting its applicability in identifying tractable data combinations for providing early warning of emerging infectious disease threats worldwide, extending beyond influenza.

Abstract

Public health surveillance systems often fail to detect emerging infectious diseases, particularly in resource limited settings. By integrating relevant clinical and internet-source data, we can close critical gaps in coverage and accelerate outbreak detection. Here, we present a multivariate algorithm that uses freely available online data to provide early warning of emerging influenza epidemics in the US. We evaluated 240 candidate predictors and found that the most predictive combination does \textit{not} include surveillance or electronic health records data, but instead consists of eight Google search and Wikipedia pageview time series reflecting changing levels of interest in influenza-related topics. In cross validation on 2010-2016 data, this algorithm sounds alarms an average of 16.4 weeks prior to influenza activity reaching the Center for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) threshold for declaring the start of the season. In an out-of-sample test on data from the rapidly-emerging fall wave of the 2009 H1N1 pandemic, it recognized the threat five weeks in advance of this surveillance threshold. Simpler algorithms, including fixed week-of-the-year triggers, lag the optimized alarms by only a few weeks when detecting seasonal influenza, but fail to provide early warning in the 2009 pandemic scenario. This demonstrates a robust method for designing next generation outbreak detection algorithms. By combining scan statistics with machine learning, it identifies tractable combinations of data sources (from among thousands of candidates) that can provide early warning of emerging infectious disease threats worldwide.
