Early Detection of Influenza outbreaks in the United States

arXiv ID: 1903.01048v1
Published: 2019-03-04
Authors: Kai Liu, Ravi Srinivasan, Lauren Ancel Meyers
Categories: stat.AP, q-bio.PE
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This research introduces a multivariate algorithm for early detection of influenza outbreaks in the US, leveraging freely available online data. It demonstrates that a combination of eight Google search and Wikipedia pageview time series can provide significantly earlier warnings (16.4 weeks for seasonal flu, 5 weeks for 2009 H1N1 pandemic) than traditional surveillance, offering a robust method for next-generation outbreak detection.

Medical Relevance

This study is highly relevant to public health by offering a novel, data-driven approach to significantly improve early detection of influenza outbreaks, including potential pandemics. This extended lead time can enable more effective and timely public health interventions, vaccine distribution, and resource allocation.

AI Health Application

The research develops and evaluates a multivariate algorithm combining scan statistics with machine learning to utilize internet-source data (Google search and Wikipedia page views) for the early detection and prediction of influenza outbreaks and emerging infectious disease threats. This is a direct application of AI/ML for predictive analytics in public health and disease surveillance.

Key Points

  • Traditional public health surveillance systems often fail to detect emerging infectious diseases, particularly in resource-limited settings.
  • A multivariate algorithm was developed to use freely available online data (Google search and Wikipedia pageview time series) for early warning of influenza epidemics in the US.
  • The most predictive combination of data sources, identified from 240 candidates, did not include surveillance or electronic health records data, but rather eight specific internet search/pageview time series.
  • In cross-validation on 2010-2016 data, the algorithm sounded alarms an average of 16.4 weeks prior to influenza activity reaching the CDC threshold for declaring the start of the season.
  • For the rapidly emerging 2009 H1N1 pandemic, the algorithm recognized the threat five weeks in advance of the CDC surveillance threshold in an out-of-sample test.
  • Simpler algorithms, such as fixed week-of-the-year triggers, lagged the optimized alarms and critically failed to provide early warning in the 2009 pandemic scenario.
  • The methodology combines scan statistics with machine learning to identify optimal data source combinations for designing robust outbreak detection algorithms.

Methodology

The study involved evaluating 240 candidate predictors, primarily comprising Google search and Wikipedia pageview time series related to influenza topics. A multivariate algorithm was constructed by integrating scan statistics with machine learning techniques to identify the most predictive combination of these data sources. The algorithm's performance was rigorously assessed through cross-validation using 2010-2016 seasonal influenza data and an out-of-sample test on the 2009 H1N1 pandemic data, comparing its alarm timing against CDC surveillance thresholds and simpler baseline models.

Key Findings

The key finding is that a combination of eight specific Google search and Wikipedia pageview time series, independent of traditional surveillance or electronic health records data, constitutes the most effective predictor for early influenza outbreak detection. This algorithm provided an average of 16.4 weeks early warning for seasonal influenza (2010-2016) and 5 weeks for the 2009 H1N1 pandemic, outperforming simpler algorithms that failed to provide early warning in pandemic scenarios.

Clinical Impact

The ability to detect influenza outbreaks weeks to months in advance has significant clinical impact. It provides public health authorities with crucial lead time for initiating preventative measures like vaccine campaigns, antiviral drug stockpiling and distribution, and public awareness campaigns. This proactive approach can reduce disease burden, mitigate healthcare system strain, and enhance preparedness and response strategies for both seasonal epidemics and novel pandemic threats.

Limitations

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract, but potential considerations include the generalizability of specific search terms or pageviews to other geographical regions or cultural contexts, and the dynamic nature of internet search behavior over time.

Future Directions

The authors suggest that this robust method, combining scan statistics with machine learning, can be applied to design 'next generation outbreak detection algorithms' for identifying 'tractable combinations of data sources' to provide early warning of 'emerging infectious disease threats worldwide,' indicating a future direction of broader applicability across diseases and global regions.

Abstract

Public health surveillance systems often fail to detect emerging infectious diseases, particularly in resource limited settings. By integrating relevant clinical and internet-source data, we can close critical gaps in coverage and accelerate outbreak detection. Here, we present a multivariate algorithm that uses freely available online data to provide early warning of emerging influenza epidemics in the US. We evaluated 240 candidate predictors and found that the most predictive combination does \textit{not} include surveillance or electronic health records data, but instead consists of eight Google search and Wikipedia pageview time series reflecting changing levels of interest in influenza-related topics. In cross validation on 2010-2016 data, this algorithm sounds alarms an average of 16.4 weeks prior to influenza activity reaching the Center for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) threshold for declaring the start of the season. In an out-of-sample test on data from the rapidly-emerging fall wave of the 2009 H1N1 pandemic, it recognized the threat five weeks in advance of this surveillance threshold. Simpler algorithms, including fixed week-of-the-year triggers, lag the optimized alarms by only a few weeks when detecting seasonal influenza, but fail to provide early warning in the 2009 pandemic scenario. This demonstrates a robust method for designing next generation outbreak detection algorithms. By combining scan statistics with machine learning, it identifies tractable combinations of data sources (from among thousands of candidates) that can provide early warning of emerging infectious disease threats worldwide.
