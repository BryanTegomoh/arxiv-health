Spatial Insight: How Data-Driven Regions of Interest Selection Enhances Single-Trial P300 Classification in EEG-Based BCIs

arXiv ID: 2511.02735v1
Published: 2025-11-04
Authors: Eva Guttmann-Flury, Jian Zhao, Mohamad Sawan
Categories: q-bio.QM
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a novel framework that combines eLORETA source localization with cross-subject functional connectivity to overcome spatial specificity limitations in single-trial P300 classification for EEG-based Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs). The method reliably identifies stable, task-relevant regions of interest (ROIs) in deeper cortical-subcortical structures, such as the insula and parietal regions, crucial for Alzheimer's disease biomarkers, and demonstrates improved classification accuracy compared to whole-brain approaches.

Medical Relevance

This research significantly enhances the accuracy and spatial specificity of single-trial P300 detection, a critical component for both advanced BCI control and the early diagnosis and monitoring of neurodegenerative diseases like Alzheimer's, by enabling better access to deeper brain structures with a robust and portable tool.

AI Health Application

The research develops a novel, data-driven framework combining source localization and functional connectivity for enhanced single-trial P300 classification. This computational classification method is applied to EEG data to improve the accuracy and robustness of diagnosing neurodegenerative diseases (like Alzheimer's) and BCI control. The 'data-driven' region selection and 'hybrid classification' are applications of AI/ML principles to medical data for diagnostic improvement and potential clinical decision support.

Key Points

  • A novel framework for P300 classification is presented, integrating eLORETA source localization with cross-subject functional connectivity to identify stable Regions of Interest (ROIs).
  • The study utilized 62-channel EEG data from 31 subjects across 63 sessions, comprising 2,520 trials, to validate the proposed method.
  • Phase-lagged connectivity metrics reliably isolate task-relevant hubs in deeper cortical-subcortical structures (e.g., insula and parietal regions), which are critical for Alzheimer's disease (AD) biomarkers, bypassing scalp EEG depth constraints.
  • The hybrid classification approach, combining spatially stable ROIs with dynamic temporal agreement, systematically outperforms whole-brain P300 classification by up to 5.4% across different frequency bands while maintaining millisecond-level temporal precision.
  • This is the first study to establish cross-subject ROI consensus through source-space connectivity, enabling a deeper probe into AD-relevant networks.
  • The framework demonstrates robustness to noise and compatibility with portable EEG systems, suggesting its potential for global deployment in early neurodegenerative disease detection.
  • The current maximum classification accuracy achieved is 81.57% in the 1-15 Hz frequency range.

Methodology

The study employs a novel framework involving eLORETA source localization to project scalp EEG data into source space. Cross-subject functional connectivity, specifically using phase-lagged connectivity metrics, is then applied to these source-localized signals to identify stable, task-relevant Regions of Interest (ROIs) that show consensus across subjects and sessions. These spatially stable ROIs are subsequently integrated into a hybrid classification framework that also considers dynamic temporal agreement for single-trial P300 detection, comparing its performance against whole-brain approaches using data from 31 subjects (63 sessions, 2,520 trials).

Key Findings

The framework successfully identifies task-relevant hubs in deeper cortical-subcortical structures (e.g., insula, parietal regions) critical for Alzheimer's disease biomarkers. The hybrid classification, leveraging these spatially stable ROIs, consistently outperforms whole-brain P300 classification by up to 5.4% across various frequency bands, while maintaining millisecond-level temporal precision. This is the first demonstration of cross-subject ROI consensus via source-space connectivity for P300, overcoming scalp EEG's depth limitations to probe AD-relevant networks. The approach also shows robustness to noise and compatibility with portable EEG systems, achieving a current max accuracy of 81.57% in the 1-15 Hz range.

Clinical Impact

This methodology has the potential to significantly improve the accuracy and reliability of P300-based neurodiagnostic tools, particularly for the early detection and monitoring of neurodegenerative diseases like Alzheimer's. Its robustness and compatibility with portable systems could facilitate widespread, global deployment, offering a more accessible and precise method for clinical assessment and BCI control applications.

Limitations

The current maximum accuracy achieved is 81.57% in the 1-15 Hz range, suggesting room for further optimization to enhance diagnostic precision for clinical deployment.

Future Directions

Future research should focus on integrating individualized anatomical data and exploring adaptive parameter optimization techniques to further refine the tool for widespread clinical deployment and improve classification accuracy.

Abstract

EEG-based Brain-Computer Interfaces (BCIs) frequently face spatial specificity limitations in detecting single-trial P300 potentials, a neurophysiological hallmark leveraged for both BCI control and neurodegenerative disease diagnostics. We present a novel framework combining eLORETA source localization with cross-subject functional connectivity to identify stable regions of interest (ROIs) across sessions. Analyzing 62-channel EEG data from 31 subjects (63 sessions, 2,520 trials), we demonstrate that phase-lagged connectivity metrics can reliably isolate task-relevant hubs in deeper cortical-subcortical structures like the insula and parietal regions - critical for Alzheimer's disease biomarkers. By integrating spatially stable ROIs with dynamic temporal agreement, our hybrid classification systematically outperforms whole-brain approaches in different frequency bands (up to 5.4% depending on the connectivity method and the spectral range) while maintaining millisecond-level temporal precision. To the best of our knowledge, this is the first study to establish cross-subject ROI consensus through source-space connectivity, bypassing scalp EEG's depth constraints to probe Alzheimer's-relevant networks. The framework's robustness to noise and compatibility with portable systems offer significant potential for global deployment in early neurodegenerative disease detection. Future integration of individualized anatomical data or adaptive parameter optimization could refine this tool for clinical deployment, enhancing the current max accuracy of 81.57% in the 1-15 Hz range.
