Fraction-variant VMAT planning for patients with complex gynecological and head-and-neck cancer

arXiv ID: 2510.26563v1
Published: 2025-10-30
Authors: Nathan Torelli, Madalyne Day, Jan Unkelbach
Categories: physics.med-ph
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a novel fraction-variant VMAT planning approach where different optimal treatment plans are delivered in different fractions, optimized simultaneously based on their cumulative physical dose. The study demonstrates that this method significantly improves dosimetric quality, achieving better target coverage and reduced organ-at-risk dose for complex gynecological and head-and-neck cancers. Crucially, it achieves this while also reducing per-fraction delivery times compared to conventional fraction-invariant VMAT, addressing patient discomfort and motion concerns.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly medically relevant as it offers a practical solution to enhance radiation therapy efficacy and patient experience. By improving dosimetric quality for complex tumors while simultaneously reducing per-fraction treatment times, it can lead to more precise treatments, less patient discomfort, and reduced risks associated with intra-fractional motion.

AI Health Application

The paper describes the development of a 'direct aperture optimization algorithm' for VMAT planning. This algorithm simultaneously optimizes different VMAT plans for delivery in different fractions. Such computational optimization techniques are a direct application of AI/computational intelligence in medicine, specifically in medical imaging and treatment planning, enabling more precise, efficient, and personalized radiation therapy for cancer patients.

Key Points

  • VMAT plans with more arcs improve dosimetric quality but increase delivery time, leading to patient discomfort and intra-fractional motion.
  • A direct aperture optimization (DAO) algorithm was developed to simultaneously optimize different VMAT plans for delivery in distinct fractions, based on cumulative physical dose.
  • Each fractional VMAT plan is designed to deliver a uniform dose to the target, ensuring robust treatment against inter-fractional setup errors and maintaining the fractionation scheme.
  • Evaluated in-silico on ten patients with complex gynecological and head-and-neck cancers.
  • Fraction-variant treatments consistently achieved better target coverage and reduced dose to organs-at-risk compared to fraction-invariant treatments, with benefits increasing with the number of different fractional plans.
  • 1-arc and 2-arc fraction-variant treatments could approximate the dosimetric quality of 3-arc fraction-invariant treatments.
  • Delivery times were significantly reduced: 1-arc fraction-variant (60 s), 2-arc fraction-variant (120 s) compared to 3-arc fraction-invariant (180 s).

Methodology

The study involved the development of a novel direct aperture optimization (DAO) algorithm. This algorithm is designed to simultaneously optimize multiple distinct VMAT plans, each intended for delivery in a different fraction of a patient's overall treatment course. The optimization process is based on the cumulative physical dose delivered over all fractions. A key constraint ensures that each individual VMAT plan delivers a uniform dose within the target volume, thereby maintaining the intended fractionation scheme and enhancing robustness against inter-fractional setup errors. This fraction-variant VMAT approach was then evaluated in-silico using computed tomography data from ten patients diagnosed with complex gynecological and head-and-neck cancers.

Key Findings

Fraction-variant VMAT treatments consistently achieved superior dosimetric quality across all evaluated patients, demonstrating improved target coverage and reduced dose to critical organs-at-risk when compared to conventional fraction-invariant treatments. The study found that the dosimetric benefit increased with the number of different plans delivered across fractions. Furthermore, 1-arc and 2-arc fraction-variant treatments were able to approximate the high dosimetric quality typically achieved by 3-arc fraction-invariant treatments, while significantly reducing the per-fraction delivery time from 180 seconds (for 3-arc fraction-invariant) to 60 seconds and 120 seconds, respectively.

Clinical Impact

This research has significant potential to improve clinical radiation therapy by allowing for highly conformal and precise dose delivery to complex tumors, such as those found in gynecological and head-and-neck regions, while simultaneously shortening the treatment duration per fraction. Shorter treatment times can directly enhance patient comfort, reduce intra-fractional motion artifacts, and potentially increase the throughput capacity of radiotherapy clinics, leading to more efficient and safer treatment delivery.

Limitations

The primary limitation explicitly stated within the abstract is that the evaluation of this novel approach was conducted "in-silico." This implies that the findings are based on computational simulations and require prospective clinical validation in a real-world setting to confirm their efficacy and safety in patients.

Future Directions

The abstract does not explicitly state future research directions. However, logical next steps would likely include prospective clinical trials to validate the in-silico findings, further investigation into the optimal number and design of fraction-variant plans for different tumor sites and complexities, and exploring the integration of this planning approach with advanced adaptive radiotherapy techniques.

Abstract

Background and Purpose: Increasing the number of arcs in volumetric modulated arc therapy (VMAT) allows for better intensity modulation and may improve plan quality. However, this leads to longer delivery times, which may cause patient discomfort and increase intra-fractional motion. In this study, it was investigated whether the delivery of different VMAT plans in different fractions may improve the dosimetric quality and delivery efficiency for the treatment of patients with complex tumor geometries. Materials and Methods: A direct aperture optimization algorithm was developed which allows for the simultaneous optimization of different VMAT plans to be delivered in different fractions, based on their cumulative physical dose. Each VMAT plan is constrained to deliver a uniform dose within the target volume, such that the entire treatment does not alter the fractionation scheme and is robust against inter-fractional setup errors. This approach was evaluated in-silico for ten patients with gynecological and head-and-neck cancer. Results: For all patients, fraction-variant treatments achieved better target coverage and reduced the dose to critical organs-at-risk compared to fraction-invariant treatments that deliver the same plan in every fraction, where the dosimetric benefit was shown to increase with the number of different plans to be delivered. In addition, 1-arc and 2-arc fraction-variant treatments could approximate the dosimetric quality of 3-arc fraction-invariant treatments, while reducing the delivery time from 180 s to 60 s and 120 s, respectively. Conclusions: Fraction-variant VMAT treatments may achieve excellent dosimetric quality for patients with complex tumor geometries, while keeping the delivery time per fraction viable.
