Fraction-variant VMAT planning for patients with complex gynecological and head-and-neck cancer

arXiv ID: 2510.26563v1
Published: 2025-10-30
Authors: Nathan Torelli, Madalyne Day, Jan Unkelbach
Categories: physics.med-ph
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a novel "fraction-variant" volumetric modulated arc therapy (VMAT) planning strategy, where different VMAT plans are delivered across individual treatment fractions. Evaluated in-silico for complex gynecological and head-and-neck cancers, this approach significantly improved dosimetric quality (target coverage, OAR sparing) and reduced treatment delivery times compared to traditional fraction-invariant VMAT. It demonstrates that superior dose distribution can be achieved without increasing the per-fraction delivery time.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to radiation oncology as it offers a pragmatic solution to enhance treatment precision and efficiency for patients with challenging tumor anatomies. It could lead to improved therapeutic outcomes by better protecting healthy tissues while maintaining excellent tumor coverage, all within clinically viable treatment times.

AI Health Application

The paper describes the development of a 'direct aperture optimization algorithm' for simultaneously optimizing different VMAT plans across multiple fractions. This computational optimization approach is a core component of modern medical AI in treatment planning. While not explicitly stated as 'machine learning' or 'deep learning,' advanced optimization algorithms, especially for complex, multi-parameter problems like radiation therapy planning, often fall under the umbrella of computational intelligence or AI applications in medicine, seeking to automate and improve the precision and efficiency of patient-specific treatments.

Key Points

  • Traditional VMAT with more arcs improves plan quality but increases delivery time and potential for patient motion.
  • The study proposes fraction-variant VMAT, delivering different optimized VMAT plans in different fractions, to address this trade-off.
  • A novel direct aperture optimization algorithm was developed to simultaneously optimize these fraction-variant plans based on cumulative physical dose.
  • Each fraction-variant plan was constrained to deliver a uniform dose to the target, ensuring robustness against inter-fractional setup errors and maintaining the prescribed fractionation.
  • In-silico evaluation on 10 complex gynecological and head-and-neck cancer patients showed superior target coverage and reduced organ-at-risk (OAR) dose with fraction-variant treatments compared to fraction-invariant methods.
  • The dosimetric benefits of the fraction-variant approach increased with the number of unique plans delivered across fractions.
  • Significantly, 1-arc and 2-arc fraction-variant treatments achieved dosimetric quality comparable to 3-arc fraction-invariant treatments, while reducing delivery time per fraction from 180s to 60s and 120s, respectively.

Methodology

The study involved the development of a direct aperture optimization algorithm designed for the simultaneous optimization of multiple distinct VMAT plans. These plans were intended for delivery in different fractions, with the cumulative physical dose as the optimization objective. A crucial constraint was that each individual VMAT plan delivered a uniform dose within the target volume, ensuring the overall treatment's robustness against inter-fractional setup variations. This novel approach was then evaluated computationally (in-silico) using patient data from ten cases diagnosed with complex gynecological and head-and-neck cancers.

Key Findings

Fraction-variant VMAT treatments consistently resulted in improved target coverage and reduced dose to critical organs-at-risk across all patient cases, outperforming traditional fraction-invariant methods. The dosimetric benefit was directly proportional to the number of distinct plans delivered throughout the treatment course. Notably, 1-arc and 2-arc fraction-variant treatments achieved dosimetric quality comparable to 3-arc fraction-invariant treatments. This high quality was attained with significant reductions in per-fraction delivery time, from 180 seconds (3-arc invariant) to 60 seconds (1-arc variant) and 120 seconds (2-arc variant), respectively.

Clinical Impact

The implementation of fraction-variant VMAT could lead to more effective and safer radiotherapy treatments for patients with complex tumor geometries. By achieving superior dosimetric quality with shorter per-fraction delivery times, this method could enhance patient comfort, reduce intra-fractional motion artifacts, potentially lower rates of treatment-related toxicities, and improve clinic throughput. This could ultimately translate into better disease control rates and enhanced quality of life for cancer patients.

Limitations

The primary limitation noted is that the study was conducted entirely in-silico. While promising, the findings require rigorous prospective clinical validation to confirm their real-world efficacy, safety, and robustness in diverse patient populations and clinical settings. The abstract does not specify the exact dosimetric criteria or constraints used for OARs, which could influence the generalizability of the reported dosimetric benefits.

Future Directions

The abstract does not explicitly state future research directions. However, logical next steps would include experimental validation in a phantom study, followed by prospective clinical trials to assess the feasibility, safety, and clinical outcomes of fraction-variant VMAT in real patients.

Abstract

Background and Purpose: Increasing the number of arcs in volumetric modulated arc therapy (VMAT) allows for better intensity modulation and may improve plan quality. However, this leads to longer delivery times, which may cause patient discomfort and increase intra-fractional motion. In this study, it was investigated whether the delivery of different VMAT plans in different fractions may improve the dosimetric quality and delivery efficiency for the treatment of patients with complex tumor geometries. Materials and Methods: A direct aperture optimization algorithm was developed which allows for the simultaneous optimization of different VMAT plans to be delivered in different fractions, based on their cumulative physical dose. Each VMAT plan is constrained to deliver a uniform dose within the target volume, such that the entire treatment does not alter the fractionation scheme and is robust against inter-fractional setup errors. This approach was evaluated in-silico for ten patients with gynecological and head-and-neck cancer. Results: For all patients, fraction-variant treatments achieved better target coverage and reduced the dose to critical organs-at-risk compared to fraction-invariant treatments that deliver the same plan in every fraction, where the dosimetric benefit was shown to increase with the number of different plans to be delivered. In addition, 1-arc and 2-arc fraction-variant treatments could approximate the dosimetric quality of 3-arc fraction-invariant treatments, while reducing the delivery time from 180 s to 60 s and 120 s, respectively. Conclusions: Fraction-variant VMAT treatments may achieve excellent dosimetric quality for patients with complex tumor geometries, while keeping the delivery time per fraction viable.
