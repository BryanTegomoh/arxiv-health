Summary

This paper introduces a multiparametric framework combining fractal, multifractal, a novel fractal functional distribution analysis, and inverse participation ratio (IPR) analysis to quantify subtle microstructural alterations in Alzheimer's disease (AD) brain tissue. The framework effectively identifies threshold-dependent fractal signatures and demonstrates increased structural disorder with disease progression, providing a robust method for early diagnosis and improved pathological assessment.

Medical Relevance

Early and sensitive detection of microstructural changes in Alzheimer's disease is critical for timely intervention and effective disease management. This research provides advanced quantitative tools that could lead to more accurate early diagnoses and improved monitoring of AD progression, surpassing the limitations of current diagnostic methods.

AI Health Application

The novel fractal and multifractal parameters, along with the derived Gaussian distribution parameters, developed in this paper could serve as robust, quantitative features for machine learning and AI algorithms. These features could be used to train AI models for automated early diagnosis of Alzheimer's disease, classification of disease stages, and objective pathological assessment from microscopy or advanced imaging data, thereby enhancing diagnostic accuracy and efficiency in clinical settings.

Key Points

  • Developed a multiparametric framework leveraging fractal and multifractal analysis, and their distributions, to quantify microstructural deterioration in AD brain tissue.
  • Introduced a novel 'fractal functional distribution method' that transforms fractal distributions into a statistically interpretable Gaussian form, capable of distinguishing control from diseased tissues.
  • Identified pronounced threshold-dependent behavior of fractal and multifractal parameters, which serve as quantitative signatures reflecting the intrinsic sparsity and heterogeneous intensity landscape of AD tissue.
  • Utilized inverse participation ratio (IPR) analysis, an established light localization technique, to quantify structural disorder and complexity in AD brain tissue.
  • IPR-based analysis demonstrated that increasing IPR pixel size directly correlates with an elevation of structural alterations, indicative of disease progression.
  • The identified threshold-sensitive signatures and distribution parameters offer potential biomarkers for early pathological transitions and quantitative stage detection in AD.
  • The integrated analyses establish a robust, multi-scale quantitative framework for detecting subtle microstructural changes, addressing limitations of conventional imaging and histopathology.

Methodology

The study applied a multiparametric framework to human brain tissue samples. The key analytical methods included: 1. **Fractal and Multifractal Analysis**: Quantifying the scaling properties and heterogeneity of brain tissue structures. 2. **Fractal Functional Distribution Method**: A novel technique to transform fractal distributions into Gaussian forms for easier statistical interpretation and differentiation of tissue states. 3. **Inverse Participation Ratio (IPR) Analysis**: An established light localization technique utilized to assess structural disorder and complexity, examining how IPR pixel size correlates with observed alterations.

Key Findings

The study found that fractal and multifractal parameters exhibit pronounced threshold-dependent behavior, providing unique signatures indicative of AD progression. The novel fractal functional distribution method yields statistically interpretable parameters capable of differentiating healthy and AD-affected tissues. Furthermore, IPR-based analysis demonstrated a clear elevation of structural alterations with disease progression, especially highlighted by increasing IPR pixel size, collectively establishing a robust, multi-scale quantitative framework for detecting microstructural alterations in AD.

It offers a quantitative approach for disease staging and monitoring progression, potentially allowing for more timely therapeutic interventions and personalized treatment strategies beyond what conventional imaging and histopathology can provide.

Abstract

Alzheimer's disease (AD) is characterized by progressive microstructural deterioration in brain tissue, yet conventional imaging and histopathology often lack the sensitivity needed to detect subtle early-stage changes. Here, we present a multiparametric framework combining fractal and multifractal analysis and their distributions to quantify structural alterations in human brain tissue affected by AD. Moreover, from the fractal and multifractal formalism, we introduced an innovative fractal functional distribution method, a novel technique that transforms fractal distribution into a Gaussian form. Statistically, these distribution parameters are easy to interpret and can distinguish between control and diseased tissues. Across samples, we identify pronounced threshold-dependent behavior of fractal and multifractal parameters, reflecting the intrinsic sparsity and heterogeneous intensity landscape of brain tissue. These threshold-sensitive signatures provide a framework for quantitative stage detection and may serve as biomarkers for early pathological transitions. In addition, we studied structural disorder and complexity using our established light localization technique, inverse participation ratio (IPR) analysis. IPR-based analysis demonstrates that increasing IPR pixel size highlights the elevation of structural alterations with disease progression. Together, these integrative analyses establish a robust, multi-scale quantitative framework for detecting microstructural alterations in AD, providing a promising foundation for early diagnosis and improved pathological assessment.
