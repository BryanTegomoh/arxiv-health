MetaboNet: The Largest Publicly Available Consolidated Dataset for Type 1 Diabetes Management

Summary

This paper introduces MetaboNet, the largest publicly available consolidated dataset designed to overcome the fragmentation and lack of standardization in existing Type 1 Diabetes (T1D) management datasets. By unifying data from multiple sources, MetaboNet provides a comprehensive resource for developing and evaluating T1D algorithms, aiming to improve their comparability and generalizability.

Medical Relevance

MetaboNet is crucial for accelerating the development of more accurate and robust algorithms for T1D management. By enabling better prediction of glucose levels and more precise insulin dosing, it has the potential to significantly improve glycemic control and reduce the burden of T1D on patients.

AI Health Application

The dataset is explicitly designed for the development of AI/ML algorithms for Type 1 Diabetes management. This includes applications such as glucose prediction, personalized insulin dosing recommendations, detection of hypoglycemic/hyperglycemic events, and the advancement of closed-loop insulin delivery systems (artificial pancreas systems), all of which are direct medical AI applications aimed at improving patient care and health outcomes.

Key Points

  • **Problem Addressed:** Fragmentation, lack of standardization, and difficult access/processing of existing T1D datasets hinder algorithm development and comparability.
  • **Solution:** Creation of MetaboNet, a unified and accessible data resource consolidating multiple publicly available T1D datasets.
  • **Inclusion Criteria:** Datasets were included if they provided both continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) data and corresponding insulin pump dosing records, retaining auxiliary information like carbohydrate intake and physical activity where available.
  • **Scale and Significance:** MetaboNet comprises 3135 subjects and 1228 patient-years of overlapping CGM and insulin data, making it substantially larger than existing standalone benchmark datasets.
  • **Accessibility:** The dataset is distributed through a fully public subset available for immediate download (metabo-net.org) and a Data Use Agreement (DUA)-restricted subset accessible via application, with provided processing pipelines for standardization.
  • **Impact on Research:** MetaboNet covers a broad range of glycemic profiles and demographics, which is expected to yield more generalizable algorithmic performance compared to research based on individual, smaller datasets.
  • **Standardization:** The resource aims to standardize data formats, reducing the time and effort previously required for data integration and processing across different sources.

Methodology

The methodology involved consolidating multiple publicly available T1D datasets into a unified structure. Inclusion was based on the availability of both continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) and insulin pump dosing records, along with retention of auxiliary data like carbohydrate intake and physical activity. Processing pipelines are provided to convert DUA-restricted datasets into the standardized MetaboNet format, facilitating data integration and access.

Key Findings

The primary finding is the successful creation and consolidation of MetaboNet, which stands as the largest publicly available dataset for T1D management. It encompasses data from 3135 subjects and 1228 patient-years, providing a unified and standardized resource that addresses the long-standing issue of data fragmentation in T1D research.

Clinical Impact

The availability of MetaboNet is expected to lead to the development of more accurate, robust, and generalizable T1D management algorithms. This can translate into improved automated insulin delivery systems, better decision support for patients and clinicians, optimized insulin dosing, and ultimately, better glycemic control and reduced T1D complications for patients.

Abstract

Progress in Type 1 Diabetes (T1D) algorithm development is limited by the fragmentation and lack of standardization across existing T1D management datasets. Current datasets differ substantially in structure and are time-consuming to access and process, which impedes data integration and reduces the comparability and generalizability of algorithmic developments. This work aims to establish a unified and accessible data resource for T1D algorithm development. Multiple publicly available T1D datasets were consolidated into a unified resource, termed the MetaboNet dataset. Inclusion required the availability of both continuous glucose monitoring (CGM) data and corresponding insulin pump dosing records. Additionally, auxiliary information such as reported carbohydrate intake and physical activity was retained when present. The MetaboNet dataset comprises 3135 subjects and 1228 patient-years of overlapping CGM and insulin data, making it substantially larger than existing standalone benchmark datasets. The resource is distributed as a fully public subset available for immediate download at https://metabo-net.org/ , and with a Data Use Agreement (DUA)-restricted subset accessible through their respective application processes. For the datasets in the latter subset, processing pipelines are provided to automatically convert the data into the standardized MetaboNet format. A consolidated public dataset for T1D research is presented, and the access pathways for both its unrestricted and DUA-governed components are described. The resulting dataset covers a broad range of glycemic profiles and demographics and thus can yield more generalizable algorithmic performance than individual datasets.
