Summary

This paper introduces the Multi-Organ medical image REconstruction (MORE) dataset, a comprehensive collection of CT scans spanning 9 diverse anatomies and 15 lesion types, designed to address the generalization limitations of existing deep learning CT reconstruction methods. It enables robust model training and rigorous evaluation of generalization capabilities. The authors also establish a strong optimization-based baseline model that demonstrates enhanced robustness on unseen anatomies, affirming the value of comprehensive datasets for improving model generalization.

Medical Relevance

Improving the generalization of CT reconstruction across a wide range of anatomies and pathologies is critical for accurate and reliable radiological diagnosis, enabling AI systems to assist in diverse clinical scenarios without requiring extensive re-training for every new condition or body part.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the development and evaluation of deep learning models for Multi-Organ Medical Image REconstruction from CT scans. These models aim to produce higher quality, more generalizable images for radiologists, thereby improving the accuracy and efficiency of diagnosis and treatment planning.

Key Points

  • Current deep learning CT reconstruction methods lack generalization ability to unseen anatomies and lesions due to reliance on specific anatomies and datasets.
  • The MORE dataset is a novel, extensive collection of CT scans, encompassing 9 diverse anatomies and 15 distinct lesion types.
  • Its primary purposes are to facilitate robust training of deep learning models on heterogeneous data and enable rigorous evaluation of model generalization for CT reconstruction.
  • A strong baseline solution, employing optimization-based methods, is established, outperforming prior approaches under challenging multi-organ conditions.
  • The research demonstrates that utilizing a comprehensive, heterogeneous dataset significantly enhances the generalization capability of deep learning models.
  • Optimization-based reconstruction methods are identified as offering enhanced robustness when applied to unseen anatomical structures.
  • The MORE dataset is made freely accessible under a CC-BY-NC 4.0 license for research and development.

Methodology

The core methodology involved creating and curating the MORE dataset, which comprises CT scans covering 9 distinct anatomies and 15 lesion types. Following dataset creation, the authors established a strong baseline solution utilizing 'optimization-based methods,' which was then evaluated against prior deep learning approaches using the challenging MORE dataset to demonstrate improved performance and generalization capabilities.

Key Findings

1. A comprehensive and heterogeneous dataset significantly improves the generalization capability of deep learning models for CT reconstruction. 2. Optimization-based deep learning methods offer enhanced robustness when processing previously unseen anatomical structures during CT reconstruction.

Clinical Impact

This work can lead to the development of more robust and broadly applicable AI-powered CT reconstruction algorithms, reducing the need for domain-specific models and potentially accelerating diagnostic workflows. It could improve diagnostic accuracy in complex or rare cases by enabling models to generalize across diverse patient populations and pathologies, ultimately enhancing patient care and treatment planning.

Abstract

CT reconstruction provides radiologists with images for diagnosis and treatment, yet current deep learning methods are typically limited to specific anatomies and datasets, hindering generalization ability to unseen anatomies and lesions. To address this, we introduce the Multi-Organ medical image REconstruction (MORE) dataset, comprising CT scans across 9 diverse anatomies with 15 lesion types. This dataset serves two key purposes: (1) enabling robust training of deep learning models on extensive, heterogeneous data, and (2) facilitating rigorous evaluation of model generalization for CT reconstruction. We further establish a strong baseline solution that outperforms prior approaches under these challenging conditions. Our results demonstrate that: (1) a comprehensive dataset helps improve the generalization capability of models, and (2) optimization-based methods offer enhanced robustness for unseen anatomies. The MORE dataset is freely accessible under CC-BY-NC 4.0 at our project page https://more-med.github.io/
