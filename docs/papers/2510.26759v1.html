Summary

This paper introduces MORE, a Multi-Organ Medical Image REconstruction dataset, to address the lack of generalization in current deep learning-based CT reconstruction methods. MORE comprises CT scans from 9 diverse anatomies and 15 lesion types, designed to facilitate robust model training and rigorous evaluation of generalization capabilities. The authors also establish a strong baseline solution, demonstrating that comprehensive datasets improve model generalization and that optimization-based methods enhance robustness for unseen anatomies.

Medical Relevance

This research is critical for improving the reliability and applicability of CT reconstruction in clinical practice. By providing a diverse dataset and demonstrating enhanced generalization, it enables the development of more robust AI tools that can accurately process CT scans across various patient anatomies and pathological conditions, ultimately leading to more consistent and reliable diagnostic images for radiologists.

AI Health Application

The development of a robust dataset for training and evaluating deep learning models to perform CT image reconstruction. This aims to improve the quality, accuracy, and generalization capability of medical AI applications used by radiologists for diagnosis and treatment planning, especially for diverse anatomical structures and various lesion types.

Methodology

The core methodology involves the creation and release of a large, diverse dataset named MORE, specifically curated for CT reconstruction. This dataset includes CT scans spanning 9 distinct anatomies and encompassing 15 different lesion types. A strong deep learning baseline solution was developed and evaluated on this dataset to assess model performance and generalization capabilities, with a specific focus on comparing optimization-based methods against prior approaches.

Key Findings

The study's main findings are twofold: firstly, the availability and use of a comprehensive and heterogeneous dataset significantly improve the generalization capability of deep learning models in CT reconstruction. Secondly, optimization-based methods prove to be more robust for handling and reconstructing images from unseen anatomies, which is a critical aspect for real-world clinical deployment.

Clinical Impact

The development and release of the MORE dataset, combined with the demonstrated improvements in model generalization and robustness, will have a direct clinical impact. It enables the creation of more reliable and versatile CT reconstruction algorithms that can be deployed across a wider range of clinical scenarios and patient populations without needing extensive retraining for specific anatomies or pathologies. This will lead to more consistent, high-quality diagnostic images, potentially improving diagnostic accuracy and patient care across various medical specialties.

Abstract

CT reconstruction provides radiologists with images for diagnosis and treatment, yet current deep learning methods are typically limited to specific anatomies and datasets, hindering generalization ability to unseen anatomies and lesions. To address this, we introduce the Multi-Organ medical image REconstruction (MORE) dataset, comprising CT scans across 9 diverse anatomies with 15 lesion types. This dataset serves two key purposes: (1) enabling robust training of deep learning models on extensive, heterogeneous data, and (2) facilitating rigorous evaluation of model generalization for CT reconstruction. We further establish a strong baseline solution that outperforms prior approaches under these challenging conditions. Our results demonstrate that: (1) a comprehensive dataset helps improve the generalization capability of models, and (2) optimization-based methods offer enhanced robustness for unseen anatomies. The MORE dataset is freely accessible under CC-BY-NC 4.0 at our project page https://more-med.github.io/
