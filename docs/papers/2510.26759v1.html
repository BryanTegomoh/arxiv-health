Summary

This paper introduces MORE (Multi-Organ Medical Image REconstruction Dataset) to address the critical limitation of current deep learning CT reconstruction methods regarding generalization to diverse anatomies and lesion types. Comprising CT scans across 9 distinct anatomies and 15 lesion types, MORE facilitates robust model training and rigorous evaluation of generalization capabilities. The authors also establish a strong baseline solution that outperforms prior approaches, demonstrating that comprehensive datasets improve generalization and optimization-based methods enhance robustness for unseen anatomies.

Medical Relevance

This work directly addresses a major hurdle in deploying AI for medical imaging by improving the generalization and robustness of CT reconstruction models. It enables more accurate and reliable diagnostic images across a broader spectrum of patient conditions and anatomical variations, reducing misdiagnosis risks and improving treatment planning.

AI Health Application

The paper focuses on developing and evaluating deep learning models for improving CT image reconstruction. This AI application aims to provide radiologists with higher quality and more reliable images for diagnosis and treatment planning, especially for unseen anatomies and diverse lesion types, thereby enhancing medical decision-making and patient outcomes.

  • The MORE dataset comprises CT scans from 9 diverse anatomical regions and features 15 distinct lesion types.

  • A strong baseline solution was developed, demonstrating superior performance compared to prior approaches under challenging generalization conditions.
  • Key finding: A comprehensive and diverse dataset significantly improves the generalization capability of CT reconstruction models.
  • Key finding: Optimization-based methods offer enhanced robustness when applied to CT reconstruction for unseen anatomies.

Methodology

The core methodology involved compiling the MORE dataset, which consists of a large collection of CT scans covering 9 diverse anatomical regions and 15 distinct lesion types. Using this dataset, the authors trained and evaluated deep learning models, establishing a strong baseline solution. The evaluation specifically focused on assessing model generalization capabilities to unseen anatomies and comparing the robustness of deep learning versus optimization-based reconstruction methods.

Key Findings

The study's primary findings indicate that the availability of a comprehensive and diverse dataset like MORE is crucial for significantly improving the generalization capability of deep learning models in CT reconstruction. Furthermore, the research highlighted that optimization-based methods provide superior robustness, particularly when applied to CT reconstruction tasks involving previously unseen anatomical structures.

Clinical Impact

The MORE dataset and the insights derived from this research will accelerate the development of more clinically relevant and deployable AI systems for CT reconstruction. This will lead to higher quality, more consistent, and generalizable diagnostic images for radiologists, potentially improving diagnostic accuracy and efficiency across diverse patient populations and pathological conditions, ultimately enhancing patient care and reducing the need for costly and time-consuming model re-training.

Limitations

The abstract primarily highlights the limitations of *prior* deep learning methods for CT reconstruction, specifically their poor generalization to unseen anatomies and lesions. It does not explicitly state limitations inherent to the MORE dataset or the authors' proposed baseline solution itself. However, the necessity of creating such a diverse dataset implicitly suggests that previous datasets were insufficient for addressing these generalization challenges.

Future Directions

By providing a publicly accessible, comprehensive multi-organ dataset, the work implicitly encourages future research to leverage MORE for developing and rigorously evaluating next-generation deep learning models. This will allow researchers to further improve CT reconstruction's generalization capabilities, explore novel optimization techniques, and enhance robustness across a wider range of clinical complexities and patient demographics.

Abstract

CT reconstruction provides radiologists with images for diagnosis and treatment, yet current deep learning methods are typically limited to specific anatomies and datasets, hindering generalization ability to unseen anatomies and lesions. To address this, we introduce the Multi-Organ medical image REconstruction (MORE) dataset, comprising CT scans across 9 diverse anatomies with 15 lesion types. This dataset serves two key purposes: (1) enabling robust training of deep learning models on extensive, heterogeneous data, and (2) facilitating rigorous evaluation of model generalization for CT reconstruction. We further establish a strong baseline solution that outperforms prior approaches under these challenging conditions. Our results demonstrate that: (1) a comprehensive dataset helps improve the generalization capability of models, and (2) optimization-based methods offer enhanced robustness for unseen anatomies. The MORE dataset is freely accessible under CC-BY-NC 4.0 at our project page https://more-med.github.io/
