Generative Modeling with Manifold Percolation

Summary

This paper introduces a novel framework for generative modeling by reframing the task as disentangling geometric support from probability distributions using Continuum Percolation. It establishes an isomorphism between Random Geometric Graphs and data manifold topology, proposing a "Percolation Shift metric" and a differentiable loss function to effectively mitigate structural pathologies like implicit mode collapse, ultimately achieving models with enhanced diversity, fidelity, and topological expansion.

Medical Relevance

Improving generative model robustness against mode collapse and manifold shrinkage is paramount in medicine. This ensures that synthetic medical data (e.g., images, patient records, molecular structures) is diverse, realistic, and representative, leading to safer AI model training, more comprehensive drug discovery, and reliable simulation of biological processes.

AI Health Application

This research provides foundational improvements to generative AI models. In health, these enhanced models can be applied to generate more realistic and diverse synthetic medical images for training diagnostic AI, create privacy-preserving synthetic patient datasets for research and development, design novel drug candidates by better exploring molecular chemical space, and synthesize biological sequences for bioinformatics studies. By preventing issues like mode collapse and promoting 'Hyper-Generalization,' these models will be more effective at representing the full diversity and complexity of medical data, leading to more robust and reliable AI systems for diagnosis, treatment, and drug development.

Key Points

  • Generative modeling is reinterpreted as a problem of disentangling the geometric support from the probability distribution, particularly from an observer's perspective.
  • Continuum Percolation (CP) is proposed as a highly suitable method for support analysis, converting high-dimensional density estimation into a geometric counting problem on the data manifold.
  • A rigorous isomorphism is established between the topological phase transitions observed in Random Geometric Graphs (RGGs) and the structure of the underlying data manifold in high-dimensional spaces.
  • The paper introduces a novel "Percolation Shift metric" which is demonstrated to effectively identify and capture structural pathologies, such as implicit mode collapse, that traditional statistical metrics like FID may fail to detect.
  • The identified topological phenomena are translated into a differentiable loss function, enabling direct guidance and optimization of generative model training.
  • Experimental validation confirms that this approach successfully prevents manifold shrinkage, ensuring the generative model produces a broader and more representative range of outputs.
  • The methodology drives the generative model towards a state of "Hyper-Generalization," characterized by good fidelity to the real data and verified topological expansion, indicating superior diversity and coverage of the true data distribution.

Methodology

The core methodology involves applying Continuum Percolation to analyze the geometric support of high-dimensional data, thereby establishing an isomorphism between Random Geometric Graph topology and the data manifold. A novel "Percolation Shift metric" is developed to quantify structural pathologies. This topological understanding is then formalized into a differentiable loss function to guide and optimize the training of generative models, aiming to expand and preserve the data manifold's structure.

The "Percolation Shift metric" is shown to be superior to FID in detecting structural pathologies like implicit mode collapse. The proposed differentiable loss function effectively prevents manifold shrinkage and drives generative models to a state of "Hyper-Generalization," characterized by high fidelity, verified topological expansion, and improved coverage of the underlying data manifold.

Abstract

Generative modeling is typically framed as learning mapping rules, but from an observer's perspective without access to these rules, the task manifests as disentangling the geometric support from the probability distribution. We propose that Continuum Percolation is uniquely suited for this support analysis, as the sampling process effectively projects high-dimensional density estimation onto a geometric counting problem on the support. In this work, we establish a rigorous isomorphism between the topological phase transitions of Random Geometric Graphs and the underlying data manifold in high-dimensional space. By analyzing the relationship between our proposed Percolation Shift metric and FID, we demonstrate that our metric captures structural pathologies (such as implicit mode collapse) where statistical metrics fail. Finally, we translate this topological phenomenon into a differentiable loss function to guide training. Experimental results confirm that this approach not only prevents manifold shrinkage but drives the model toward a state of "Hyper-Generalization," achieving good fidelity and verified topological expansion.
