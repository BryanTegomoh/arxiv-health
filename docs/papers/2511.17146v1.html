Learning to Look Closer: A New Instance-Wise Loss for Small Cerebral Lesion Segmentation

Published: 2025-11-21
Authors: Luc Bouteille, Alexander Jaus, Jens Kleesiek, Rainer Stiefelhagen, Lukas Heine

Summary

This paper addresses the challenge of under-segmentation of small cerebral lesions by traditional loss functions in medical image analysis. It introduces CC-DiceCE, a novel instance-wise loss function designed to improve detection on a per-lesion basis. The study demonstrates that CC-DiceCE significantly increases lesion detection (recall) with minimal impact on segmentation quality, outperforming existing methods like blob loss, albeit with a slight increase in false positives.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and early detection of small cerebral lesions is paramount for timely diagnosis, disease monitoring, and effective treatment planning in various neurological conditions, directly impacting patient prognosis and care quality.

AI Health Application

This research develops an AI application for automated medical image analysis. Specifically, it improves the robustness and accuracy of deep learning models (AI) for segmenting small cerebral lesions in medical scans. This application can assist radiologists and neurologists in more reliably detecting and characterizing brain abnormalities, potentially leading to earlier diagnosis, better treatment planning, and monitoring of neurological diseases.

  • The authors propose CC-DiceCE, a new instance-wise loss function derived from the CC-Metrics framework, specifically tailored to evaluate and improve segmentation quality on a per-lesion basis.

Methodology

The study introduced CC-DiceCE, a new instance-wise loss function built upon the CC-Metrics framework. This novel loss was implemented and rigorously compared against a traditional DiceCE baseline and an existing blob loss. All experiments were conducted within the highly robust and standardized nnU-Net framework, and evaluations were performed across multiple datasets to ensure generalizability.

Key Findings

CC-DiceCE loss notably boosts the detection rate (recall) of small cerebral lesions. This improvement maintains segmentation performance while incurring a slight increase in false positives. The multi-dataset evaluation confirmed CC-DiceCE's superior performance over blob loss.

Clinical Impact

This research has the potential to enhance automated tools for detecting subtle cerebral abnormalities that might otherwise be missed by current methods. This could lead to earlier diagnosis of conditions like microbleeds, small tumors, or early signs of neurodegenerative diseases, enabling more timely intervention, personalized treatment strategies, and improved patient outcomes in neuro-oncology and neurovascular imaging.

Limitations

The primary limitation identified is that the increased detection sensitivity (recall) achieved by CC-DiceCE is accompanied by a slight increase in false positives, which would necessitate careful consideration in clinical deployment, potentially requiring additional human review.

Future Directions

While not explicitly stated in the abstract, implied future directions would likely include exploring strategies to mitigate the increased false positive rate, validating the approach on a wider variety of lesion types and imaging modalities, and integrating this loss function into prospective clinical trials to assess its real-world impact on patient care.

Abstract

Traditional loss functions in medical image segmentation, such as Dice, often under-segment small lesions because their small relative volume contributes negligibly to the overall loss. To address this, instance-wise loss functions and metrics have been proposed to evaluate segmentation quality on a per-lesion basis. We introduce CC-DiceCE, a loss function based on the CC-Metrics framework, and compare it with the existing blob loss. Both are benchmarked against a DiceCE baseline within the nnU-Net framework, which provides a robust and standardized setup. We find that CC-DiceCE loss increases detection (recall) with minimal to no degradation in segmentation performance, albeit at the cost of slightly more false positives. Furthermore, our multi-dataset study shows that CC-DiceCE generally outperforms blob loss.
