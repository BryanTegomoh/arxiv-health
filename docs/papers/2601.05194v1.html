An interpretable data-driven approach to optimizing clinical fall risk assessment

arXiv ID: 2601.05194v1
Published: 2026-01-08
Authors: Fardin Ganjkhanloo, Emmett Springer, Erik H. Hoyer, Daniel L. Young, Holley Farley, Kimia Ghobadi
Categories: cs.LG
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This study developed and validated an interpretable data-driven approach, using Constrained Score Optimization (CSO), to recalibrate the Johns Hopkins Fall Risk Assessment Tool (JHFRAT). The CSO model significantly improved fall risk prediction (AUC-ROC=0.91 vs. 0.86 for original JHFRAT) over a retrospective cohort of 54,209 inpatient admissions, translating to protecting an additional 35 high-risk patients weekly.

Medical Relevance

Inpatient falls represent a major patient safety challenge with significant morbidity, mortality, and economic burden. This research directly addresses this by enhancing the accuracy and clinical utility of an existing fall risk assessment tool, enabling more precise identification of at-risk patients and facilitating targeted, effective preventative interventions.

AI Health Application

The research applies machine learning techniques, specifically constrained score optimization and XGBoost, to clinical data to reweight and optimize an existing clinical fall risk assessment tool (JHFRAT). This AI-driven approach aims to improve the accuracy of identifying patients at high risk of falls in hospital settings, leading to enhanced patient safety protocols, better resource allocation for fall prevention, and improved overall patient care within health systems.

Key Points

  • The primary goal was to optimize the Johns Hopkins Fall Risk Assessment Tool (JHFRAT) by better aligning its fall risk predictions with additional clinically meaningful measures through a data-driven approach.
  • A retrospective cohort analysis was conducted on 54,209 inpatient admissions from three Johns Hopkins Health System hospitals between March 2022 and October 2023, identifying 20,208 high fall risk and 13,941 low fall risk encounters.
  • Constrained Score Optimization (CSO) models were employed to reweight JHFRAT scoring items, crucially preserving the tool's additive structure, established clinical thresholds, and interpretability for clinical utility.
  • The recalibrated CSO model demonstrated significant predictive performance improvement, achieving an AUC-ROC of 0.91, a notable gain over the current JHFRAT's AUC-ROC of 0.86.
  • This performance enhancement translates directly to clinical impact, potentially identifying and protecting an additional 35 high-risk patients per week across the Johns Hopkins Health System.
  • CSO models showed strong robustness, performing similarly with and without additional EHR variables and exhibiting greater resilience to variations in risk labeling compared to a higher-performing but less interpretable black-box XGBoost model (AUC-ROC=0.94).
  • The study provides an evidence-based and robust framework for health systems to enhance inpatient fall prevention protocols, improve patient safety, and optimize resource allocation through data-driven risk assessment.

Methodology

The study utilized a retrospective cohort analysis of 54,209 inpatient admissions to develop and validate Constrained Score Optimization (CSO) models. These models were designed to reweight the existing JHFRAT scoring items while explicitly maintaining its additive structure, interpretability, and clinical thresholds. Model performance was evaluated using AUC-ROC, comparing the CSO model against the original JHFRAT and a benchmark black-box XGBoost model. The study also assessed the CSO model's performance with and without additional EHR variables and its robustness to variations in risk labeling.

Key Findings

The CSO model significantly improved fall risk prediction, achieving an AUC-ROC of 0.91, a 0.05 increase over the current JHFRAT's AUC-ROC of 0.86. This performance gain is projected to protect an additional 35 high-risk patients weekly within the Johns Hopkins Health System. Notably, CSO models demonstrated consistent performance with and without the inclusion of additional EHR variables, indicating efficiency. While a black-box XGBoost model achieved a higher AUC-ROC of 0.94, the CSO model exhibited superior robustness to variations in risk labeling, making it more reliable for clinical deployment.

Clinical Impact

The optimized JHFRAT, leveraging CSO, promises a more accurate and efficient identification of inpatients at high risk for falls. This precision allows healthcare providers to implement timely and appropriately scaled fall prevention interventions, reducing fall rates, preventing patient injuries, and potentially shortening hospital stays. Its interpretable nature ensures that clinicians can understand and trust the updated scoring, facilitating seamless integration into existing workflows and optimizing the allocation of scarce healthcare resources.

Limitations

While not explicitly stated as limitations, the abstract highlights a trade-off where a black-box model (XGBoost) achieved a higher predictive AUC (0.94) compared to the CSO model (0.91), suggesting that the chosen approach prioritizes interpretability and robustness over maximal predictive power. The study was also conducted within a single health system, and generalizability to diverse patient populations and healthcare settings may require further external validation.

Abstract

In this study, we aim to better align fall risk prediction from the Johns Hopkins Fall Risk Assessment Tool (JHFRAT) with additional clinically meaningful measures via a data-driven modelling approach. We conducted a retrospective cohort analysis of 54,209 inpatient admissions from three Johns Hopkins Health System hospitals between March 2022 and October 2023. A total of 20,208 admissions were included as high fall risk encounters, and 13,941 were included as low fall risk encounters. To incorporate clinical knowledge and maintain interpretability, we employed constrained score optimization (CSO) models to reweight the JHFRAT scoring weights, while preserving its additive structure and clinical thresholds. Recalibration refers to adjusting item weights so that the resulting score can order encounters more consistently by the study's risk labels, and without changing the tool's form factor or deployment workflow. The model demonstrated significant improvements in predictive performance over the current JHFRAT (CSO AUC-ROC=0.91, JHFRAT AUC-ROC=0.86). This performance improvement translates to protecting an additional 35 high-risk patients per week across the Johns Hopkins Health System. The constrained score optimization models performed similarly with and without the EHR variables. Although the benchmark black-box model (XGBoost), improves upon the performance metrics of the knowledge-based constrained logistic regression (AUC-ROC=0.94), the CSO demonstrates more robustness to variations in risk labeling. This evidence-based approach provides a robust foundation for health systems to systematically enhance inpatient fall prevention protocols and patient safety using data-driven optimization techniques, contributing to improved risk assessment and resource allocation in healthcare settings.
