An interpretable data-driven approach to optimizing clinical fall risk assessment

arXiv ID: 2601.05194v1
Published: 2026-01-08
Authors: Fardin Ganjkhanloo, Emmett Springer, Erik H. Hoyer, Daniel L. Young, Holley Farley, Kimia Ghobadi
Categories: cs.LG
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This study developed an interpretable data-driven approach, Constrained Score Optimization (CSO), to recalibrate the Johns Hopkins Fall Risk Assessment Tool (JHFRAT) for improved fall risk prediction. By reweighting JHFRAT items while preserving its structure and clinical thresholds, the CSO model significantly outperformed the original tool, demonstrating improved predictive performance (AUC-ROC 0.91 vs. 0.86). This enhancement translates to protecting an additional 35 high-risk patients weekly across the health system and provides a robust, interpretable solution for optimizing inpatient fall prevention protocols.

Medical Relevance

Inpatient falls are a major patient safety concern, leading to injuries, increased healthcare costs, and prolonged hospital stays. Optimizing the accuracy of fall risk assessment tools like JHFRAT directly enhances patient safety by enabling more precise identification of at-risk individuals and more effective allocation of preventive interventions.

AI Health Application

This research applies interpretable machine learning (constrained score optimization models) and other AI techniques (XGBoost) to enhance the predictive accuracy of a clinical fall risk assessment tool. The AI application aims to provide more precise patient fall risk scores, leading to improved patient safety through better fall prevention protocols, more efficient resource allocation within health systems, and data-driven decision-making in clinical practice.

Methodology

A retrospective cohort analysis was performed on 54,209 inpatient admissions. The dataset included 20,208 high fall risk and 13,941 low fall risk encounters. Constrained Score Optimization (CSO) models were developed to recalibrate the JHFRAT by optimizing item weights while preserving its additive structure, interpretability, and existing clinical thresholds. Model performance was evaluated using AUC-ROC and compared against the original JHFRAT and a benchmark XGBoost model.

Key Findings

The CSO model achieved a significantly improved AUC-ROC of 0.91, outperforming the original JHFRAT (AUC-ROC=0.86). This improvement directly correlates to protecting an additional 35 high-risk patients weekly. CSO models demonstrated similar performance with and without EHR variables and showed superior robustness to variations in risk labeling compared to a higher-performing black-box XGBoost model (AUC-ROC=0.94).

Clinical Impact

This optimized approach provides a more accurate and robust tool for identifying inpatients at high risk of falls, leading to better-targeted preventive interventions. It can enhance patient safety, reduce fall-related injuries and associated healthcare costs, and improve resource allocation within healthcare settings. The interpretability of the CSO model facilitates easier adoption and integration into existing clinical workflows, promoting clinician trust and adherence.

Abstract

In this study, we aim to better align fall risk prediction from the Johns Hopkins Fall Risk Assessment Tool (JHFRAT) with additional clinically meaningful measures via a data-driven modelling approach. We conducted a retrospective cohort analysis of 54,209 inpatient admissions from three Johns Hopkins Health System hospitals between March 2022 and October 2023. A total of 20,208 admissions were included as high fall risk encounters, and 13,941 were included as low fall risk encounters. To incorporate clinical knowledge and maintain interpretability, we employed constrained score optimization (CSO) models to reweight the JHFRAT scoring weights, while preserving its additive structure and clinical thresholds. Recalibration refers to adjusting item weights so that the resulting score can order encounters more consistently by the study's risk labels, and without changing the tool's form factor or deployment workflow. The model demonstrated significant improvements in predictive performance over the current JHFRAT (CSO AUC-ROC=0.91, JHFRAT AUC-ROC=0.86). This performance improvement translates to protecting an additional 35 high-risk patients per week across the Johns Hopkins Health System. The constrained score optimization models performed similarly with and without the EHR variables. Although the benchmark black-box model (XGBoost), improves upon the performance metrics of the knowledge-based constrained logistic regression (AUC-ROC=0.94), the CSO demonstrates more robustness to variations in risk labeling. This evidence-based approach provides a robust foundation for health systems to systematically enhance inpatient fall prevention protocols and patient safety using data-driven optimization techniques, contributing to improved risk assessment and resource allocation in healthcare settings.
