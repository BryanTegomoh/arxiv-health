Analyzing Images of Blood Cells with Quantum Machine Learning Methods: Equilibrium Propagation and Variational Quantum Circuits to Detect Acute Myeloid Leukemia

arXiv ID: 2601.18710v1
Published: 2026-01-26
Authors: A. Bano, L. Liebovitch
Categories: cs.ET, cs.LG, quant-ph
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This feasibility study demonstrates that quantum machine learning (QML) algorithms, specifically Equilibrium Propagation (EP) and Variational Quantum Circuits (VQCs), can achieve competitive performance for automated Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML) detection from blood cell microscopy images. Despite severe constraints like classical simulation and reduced image resolution, QML methods attained 83.0-86.4% accuracy, only 12-15% below classical CNNs, with VQCs showing significant data efficiency.

Medical Relevance

Automated and accurate detection of Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML) is crucial for timely diagnosis and effective treatment. This research explores novel quantum computational approaches that could offer advantages like data efficiency, potentially leading to more accessible and robust diagnostic tools, especially in scenarios with limited labeled medical data.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the automated detection and classification of Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML) from blood cell microscopy images, utilizing quantum machine learning methods (Equilibrium Propagation and Variational Quantum Circuits) to aid in diagnosis.

Key Points

  • **Feasibility Validation**: The paper presents a feasibility study validating that quantum machine learning algorithms can achieve competitive performance in real-world medical imaging applications.
  • **Algorithms & Constraints**: Evaluates Equilibrium Propagation (EP), an energy-based method suitable for quantum systems due to its independence from backpropagation, and Variational Quantum Circuits (VQCs) for binary classification of AML vs. Healthy blood cells.
  • **Performance Metrics**: EP reached 86.4% accuracy, while a 4-qubit VQC attained 83.0% accuracy, positioning them only 12-15% below classical CNNs under challenging conditions.
  • **Operational Constraints**: QML methods operated under constraints including reduced image resolution (64x64 pixels), engineered 20D features, and classical simulation via Qiskit, highlighting their robustness.
  • **Data Efficiency**: The 4-qubit VQC demonstrated superior data efficiency, maintaining 83% performance with only 50 samples per class, whereas classical CNNs required 250 samples (5x more data) to reach 98% accuracy.
  • **Medical Application**: Applied to the AML-Cytomorphology dataset for automated detection of Acute Myeloid Leukemia from expert-annotated blood cell microscopy images.
  • **NISQ-era Relevance**: The results establish reproducible baselines for QML in healthcare, validating the technology's potential and feasibility within the current noisy intermediate-scale quantum (NISQ) era.

Methodology

The study employed Equilibrium Propagation (EP) and Variational Quantum Circuits (VQCs) for binary classification (AML vs. Healthy). These quantum methods were trained on limited subsets (50-250 samples per class) of the AML-Cytomorphology dataset, using reduced 64x64 pixel images and engineered 20D features. Performance was evaluated via classical simulation using Qiskit and compared against classical Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs).

Key Findings

The quantum methods achieved competitive accuracy: EP reached 86.4% and the 4-qubit VQC attained 83.0%, a gap of only 12-15% compared to classical CNNs. Crucially, the VQC demonstrated high data efficiency, maintaining stable 83% accuracy with only 50 samples per class, significantly fewer than the 250 samples CNNs required for higher performance. These results establish QML's feasibility for medical imaging in the NISQ era.

Clinical Impact

This research lays a foundation for future quantum-enhanced diagnostic tools for leukemia, potentially enabling more data-efficient and robust automated detection from blood cell images. Such advancements could accelerate diagnosis, inform treatment planning more rapidly, and be particularly beneficial in settings where obtaining large, diverse datasets for classical deep learning models is challenging.

Limitations

The quantum methods were evaluated through classical simulation (Qiskit) rather than on actual quantum hardware. The input data was constrained by reduced image resolution (64x64 pixels) and reliance on engineered 20D features. While competitive, the QML performance was still 12-15% below classical CNNs, indicating a gap for real-world deployment.

Future Directions

The paper implies future work focused on migrating these reproducible baselines to actual NISQ-era quantum hardware. Further research could explore larger-scale problems, optimize quantum circuit designs, and investigate hybrid quantum-classical approaches to improve accuracy and leverage the unique advantages of quantum computation for complex medical imaging tasks.

Abstract

This paper presents a feasibility study demonstrating that quantum machine learning (QML) algorithms achieve competitive performance on real-world medical imaging despite operating under severe constraints. We evaluate Equilibrium Propagation (EP), an energy-based learning method that does not use backpropagation (incompatible with quantum systems due to state-collapsing measurements) and Variational Quantum Circuits (VQCs) for automated detection of Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML) from blood cell microscopy images using binary classification (2 classes: AML vs. Healthy). Key Result: Using limited subsets (50-250 samples per class) of the AML-Cytomorphology dataset (18,365 expert-annotated images), quantum methods achieve performance only 12-15% below classical CNNs despite reduced image resolution (64x64 pixels), engineered features (20D), and classical simulation via Qiskit. EP reaches 86.4% accuracy (only 12% below CNN) without backpropagation, while the 4-qubit VQC attains 83.0% accuracy with consistent data efficiency: VQC maintains stable 83% performance with only 50 samples per class, whereas CNN requires 250 samples (5x more data) to reach 98%. These results establish reproducible baselines for QML in healthcare, validating NISQ-era feasibility.
