Summary

This paper introduces HACHI, an iterative human-in-the-loop framework that utilizes AI agents to accelerate the development of fully interpretable clinical prediction models (CPMs) by exploring concepts in unstructured clinical notes. HACHI significantly outperforms existing approaches, discovers novel clinically relevant concepts, and enhances model generalizability across sites and time for prediction tasks like acute kidney injury and traumatic brain injury. It highlights the critical role of human experts in guiding the AI learning process to build safer and more effective CPMs.

Medical Relevance

This work addresses a major bottleneck in translating AI research into clinical practice by making the development of powerful, interpretable, and generalizable clinical prediction models faster and more efficient, ultimately improving patient care through data-driven insights.

AI Health Application

The paper describes HACHI, an iterative human-in-the-loop AI framework for the co-design and accelerated development of fully interpretable clinical prediction models (CPMs). This AI application focuses on extracting and exploring concepts from unstructured clinical notes to build models that predict patient outcomes for specific medical conditions (e.g., acute kidney injury, traumatic brain injury), enhancing model accuracy, generalizability, and clinical utility while maintaining transparency for clinical teams.

Key Points

  • Addresses the challenge of time- and resource-intensive traditional CPM development, especially when incorporating unstructured clinical notes, which limits models reaching clinical practice.
  • Introduces HACHI, an iterative human-in-the-loop framework that alternates between an AI agent exploring/evaluating candidate concepts and clinical/domain experts providing feedback.
  • Concepts are defined as simple yes-no questions and integrated into linear models, ensuring full interpretability and transparency for clinical teams.
  • Demonstrates superior performance compared to existing approaches in two real-world prediction tasks: acute kidney injury (AKI) and traumatic brain injury (TBI).
  • Successfully surfaces new, clinically relevant concepts from unstructured notes that were not included in commonly-used CPMs.
  • Improves model generalizability, leading to more robust performance across different clinical sites and time periods.
  • Emphasizes the critical role of the clinical AI team in directing AI exploration, adjusting concept granularity, modifying objective functions, and identifying data bias/leakage.

Methodology

HACHI employs an iterative human-in-the-loop framework. It alternates between an AI agent rapidly exploring and evaluating candidate concepts (defined as simple yes-no questions derived from unstructured clinical notes) and clinical/domain experts providing feedback to refine the CPM learning process. The models are linear for interpretability, allowing transparent review, refinement, and validation by the clinical AI team in each round.

Key Findings

HACHI significantly outperformed existing CPM approaches in predicting acute kidney injury and traumatic brain injury. It successfully identified novel, clinically relevant concepts from unstructured notes that were previously uncaptured by standard models. The framework also demonstrated improved model generalizability across different clinical sites and time periods. Crucially, the study highlighted the indispensable role of human experts in guiding the AI agent, refining concepts, aligning objectives with clinical goals, and detecting data quality issues like bias and leakage.

Clinical Impact

HACHI has the potential to accelerate the development and deployment of safe, effective, and interpretable clinical prediction models into routine practice. By uncovering new insights from rich unstructured data and enhancing model generalizability, it can lead to more accurate diagnostics, better risk stratification, and personalized treatment plans, ultimately improving patient outcomes and resource allocation.

Limitations

Abstract

Developing safe, effective, and practically useful clinical prediction models (CPMs) traditionally requires iterative collaboration between clinical experts, data scientists, and informaticists. This process refines the often small but critical details of the model building process, such as which features/patients to include and how clinical categories should be defined. However, this traditional collaboration process is extremely time- and resource-intensive, resulting in only a small fraction of CPMs reaching clinical practice. This challenge intensifies when teams attempt to incorporate unstructured clinical notes, which can contain an enormous number of concepts. To address this challenge, we introduce HACHI, an iterative human-in-the-loop framework that uses AI agents to accelerate the development of fully interpretable CPMs by enabling the exploration of concepts in clinical notes. HACHI alternates between (i) an AI agent rapidly exploring and evaluating candidate concepts in clinical notes and (ii) clinical and domain experts providing feedback to improve the CPM learning process. HACHI defines concepts as simple yes-no questions that are used in linear models, allowing the clinical AI team to transparently review, refine, and validate the CPM learned in each round. In two real-world prediction tasks (acute kidney injury and traumatic brain injury), HACHI outperforms existing approaches, surfaces new clinically relevant concepts not included in commonly-used CPMs, and improves model generalizability across clinical sites and time periods. Furthermore, HACHI reveals the critical role of the clinical AI team, such as directing the AI agent to explore concepts that it had not previously considered, adjusting the granularity of concepts it considers, changing the objective function to better align with the clinical objectives, and identifying issues of data bias and leakage.
