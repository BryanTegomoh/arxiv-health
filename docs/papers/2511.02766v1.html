Summary

This review synthesizes current understanding of how the gut microbiota fundamentally regulates sleep physiology via the gut-microbiota-brain axis (GMBA), influencing neural, endocrine, and immune pathways. It highlights how microbial dysbiosis contributes to sleep disturbances by altering key neurotransmitter and metabolic pathways (e.g., serotonin, GABA, SCFAs), with significant implications for neurodegenerative, metabolic, and mood disorders, and explores microbiota-targeted interventions.

Medical Relevance

Understanding the gut microbiota's role in sleep offers novel diagnostic biomarkers and therapeutic targets for a wide range of conditions, including primary sleep disorders, neurodegenerative diseases, metabolic syndromes, and psychiatric disorders, by addressing underlying gut dysbiosis.

AI Health Application

AI/Machine Learning could be applied to analyze complex gut microbiota profiles for personalized diagnostics of sleep disorders or related health conditions. It could also optimize precision interventions, such as tailoring probiotic regimens, FMT protocols, or chrononutrition strategies for individual patients based on their unique microbiota composition and health markers, to enhance therapeutic efficacy and predict outcomes.

Key Points

  • The gut microbiota is a fundamental regulator of sleep physiology through its influence on neural, endocrine, and immune pathways via the gut-microbiota-brain axis (GMBA).
  • The GMBA bidirectionally modulates neurotransmitter production (e.g., serotonin, GABA), circadian rhythms, and metabolic homeostasis.
  • Disruptions in gut microbial composition (dysbiosis) are directly linked to sleep disorders, neuroinflammation, and systemic immune dysfunction.
  • Gut dysbiosis contributes to sleep disturbances by altering the metabolism of crucial sleep-modulating compounds like serotonin, GABA, and short-chain fatty acids (SCFAs).
  • The interplay between gut microbiota, sleep, and dysbiosis has profound implications for the pathogenesis and progression of neurodegenerative diseases, metabolic syndromes, and mood disorders.
  • The gut microbiota interacts with the endocrine and immune systems to shape inflammatory responses and stress adaptation mechanisms, further impacting sleep.
  • Microbiota-targeted therapies, including probiotics, fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT), and chrononutrition, are emerging as potential interventions to restore sleep homeostasis and improve overall health outcomes.

Methodology

This paper is a review that explores and integrates emerging research on the intricate connections between sleep and the gut microbiota, synthesizing findings on physiological mechanisms and potential therapeutic interventions.

Key Findings

The gut microbiota fundamentally regulates sleep via the GMBA, influencing neural, endocrine, and immune systems by modulating neurotransmitter production, circadian rhythms, and metabolic homeostasis. Gut dysbiosis, specifically altering serotonin, GABA, and SCFA metabolism, is directly linked to sleep disturbances, neuroinflammation, and a spectrum of neurodegenerative, metabolic, and mood disorders.

Clinical Impact

The insights provided suggest that microbiota-targeted therapies such as probiotics, fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT), and chrononutrition could serve as innovative and effective interventions to restore sleep homeostasis, alleviate sleep disorders, and improve health outcomes in associated neurological, metabolic, and psychiatric conditions.

Limitations

Future Directions

Future research should focus on rigorously evaluating the efficacy and mechanisms of microbiota-targeted therapies (probiotics, FMT, chrononutrition) in clinical settings for various sleep disorders and associated conditions, alongside further elucidating specific microbial-host molecular interactions.

Abstract

The gut microbiota has emerged as a fundamental regulator of sleep physiology, influencing neural, endocrine, and immune pathways through the gut-microbiota-brain axis (GMBA). This bidirectional communication system modulates neurotransmitter production, circadian rhythms, and metabolic homeostasis, while disruptions in microbial composition have been linked to sleep disorders, neuroinflammation, and systemic immune dysfunction. Recent findings suggest that gut dysbiosis contributes to sleep disturbances by altering serotonin, GABA, and short-chain fatty acid (SCFA) metabolism, with implications for neurodegenerative diseases, metabolic syndromes, and mood disorders. Additionally, the gut microbiota interacts with the endocrine and immune systems, shaping inflammatory responses and stress adaptation mechanisms. This review explores the intricate connections between sleep and the gut microbiota, integrating emerging research on microbiota-targeted therapies, such as probiotics, fecal microbiota transplantation (FMT), and chrononutrition, as potential interventions to restore sleep homeostasis and improve health outcomes
