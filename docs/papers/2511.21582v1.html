Deep Learning-Based Multiclass Classification of Oral Lesions with Stratified Augmentation

Authors: Joy Naoum, Revana Salama, Ali Hamdi

Summary

This research develops a deep learning-based multiclass classifier for sixteen different oral lesions, aiming to improve early oral cancer diagnosis. The model addresses challenges of limited and imbalanced datasets by integrating stratified data splitting with advanced data augmentation and oversampling techniques. Achieving high performance metrics, the proposed system demonstrates superiority over existing methods and holds significant promise for clinical computer-aided diagnostics.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medical practice as it directly targets the critical issue of late-stage oral cancer diagnosis. By enabling early and accurate differentiation of various oral lesions, it has the potential to significantly improve patient prognoses, guide timely interventions, and enhance overall public health outcomes related to oral cancer.

AI Health Application

The AI application is a deep learning-based multiclass classifier for the early detection and diagnosis of 16 different oral lesions (benign, precancerous, and malignant) to assist clinicians in the identification of oral cancer and improve patient outcomes.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical problem of late-stage oral cancer diagnosis stemming from the visual similarity of benign, precancerous, and malignant oral lesions.
  • Proposes a deep learning framework for multiclass classification of sixteen distinct types of oral lesions.
  • Implements a novel data handling strategy combining stratified data splitting with advanced data augmentation and oversampling to overcome limitations of small and imbalanced datasets.
  • Achieved impressive experimental results: 83.33% accuracy, 89.12% precision, and 77.31% recall.
  • Demonstrates superiority over current state-of-the-art methods in oral lesion classification.
  • Highlights the significant effectiveness of the integrated oversampling and augmentation strategies, particularly in enhancing classification performance for minority classes.
  • Positions the developed framework as a foundational step towards building trustworthy computer-aided diagnostic systems for early oral cancer detection in clinical environments.

Methodology

The study employs a deep learning architecture for multiclass classification. To counteract the challenges of limited and imbalanced datasets, the methodology incorporates stratified data splitting, ensuring proportional representation of all sixteen lesion classes during training and validation. This is further enhanced by advanced data augmentation techniques and oversampling strategies, which artificially expand the dataset and mitigate class imbalance by generating synthetic examples for minority classes.

Key Findings

The proposed deep learning model achieved an overall accuracy of 83.33%, precision of 89.12%, and recall of 77.31% across sixteen oral lesion classes. These metrics indicate a superior performance compared to existing state-of-the-art methods. A key finding was the demonstrated effectiveness of the combined stratified data splitting, augmentation, and oversampling techniques, particularly in significantly improving classification performance for the underrepresented minority classes.

Clinical Impact

This framework offers a significant potential clinical impact by providing a robust, AI-powered tool for early and accurate diagnosis of oral lesions, including precancerous and malignant stages. Its implementation in clinical settings could enable healthcare professionals to identify oral cancer at much earlier stages, facilitating prompt treatment, improving patient survival rates, and reducing the burden of advanced disease.

Abstract

Oral cancer is highly common across the globe and is mostly diagnosed during the later stages due to the close visual similarity to benign, precancerous, and malignant lesions in the oral cavity. Implementing computer aided diagnosis systems early on has the potential to greatly improve clinical outcomes. This research intends to use deep learning to build a multiclass classifier for sixteen different oral lesions. To overcome the challenges of limited and imbalanced datasets, the proposed technique combines stratified data splitting and advanced data augmentation and oversampling to perform the classification. The experimental results, which achieved 83.33 percent accuracy, 89.12 percent precision, and 77.31 percent recall, demonstrate the superiority of the suggested model over state of the art methods now in use. The suggested model effectively conveys the effectiveness of oversampling and augmentation strategies in situations where the minority class classification performance is noteworthy. As a first step toward trustworthy computer aided diagnostic systems for the early detection of oral cancer in clinical settings, the suggested framework shows promise.
