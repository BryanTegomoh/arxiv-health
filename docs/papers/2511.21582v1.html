Deep Learning-Based Multiclass Classification of Oral Lesions with Stratified Augmentation

arXiv ID: 2511.21582v1
Published: 2025-11-26
Authors: Joy Naoum, Revana Salama, Ali Hamdi
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This research proposes a deep learning-based multiclass classifier for differentiating sixteen types of oral lesions, aiming to improve early diagnosis of oral cancer. It addresses common challenges of limited and imbalanced datasets by implementing stratified data splitting combined with advanced data augmentation and oversampling techniques. The model achieved strong performance metrics (83.33% accuracy, 89.12% precision, 77.31% recall) and demonstrated superiority over existing state-of-the-art methods, particularly in classifying minority classes.

Medical Relevance

Oral cancer is highly prevalent globally and frequently diagnosed at late stages, leading to poor prognoses. This research offers a crucial tool for earlier and more accurate differentiation of oral lesions, potentially revolutionizing early detection and significantly improving patient outcomes by enabling timely intervention.

AI Health Application

The AI application is a deep learning-based multiclass classifier for identifying and distinguishing between benign, precancerous, and malignant oral lesions. It aims to function as a computer-aided diagnostic system for the early detection of oral cancer in clinical settings, thereby improving patient outcomes.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical problem of late-stage oral cancer diagnosis due to the visual similarity of benign, precancerous, and malignant oral lesions.
  • Develops a deep learning-based multiclass classifier capable of distinguishing between 16 different types of oral lesions.
  • Employs advanced data handling strategies including stratified data splitting, sophisticated data augmentation, and oversampling to overcome limitations of small and imbalanced datasets.
  • Achieved notable performance metrics: 83.33% accuracy, 89.12% precision, and 77.31% recall in classifying oral lesions.
  • Demonstrated the model's superiority over current state-of-the-art methods in oral lesion classification.
  • Validated the effectiveness of the integrated oversampling and augmentation strategies, particularly highlighting their impact on improving minority class classification performance.
  • Represents a promising foundational step towards developing reliable computer-aided diagnostic (CAD) systems for early oral cancer detection in clinical settings.

Methodology

The study utilized a deep learning approach to construct a multiclass classifier. To address the inherent challenges of limited and imbalanced datasets, the methodology incorporated stratified data splitting to ensure representative distribution across classes, along with advanced data augmentation techniques and oversampling strategies. These methods collectively aimed to enrich the training data and improve the model's generalization capabilities, especially for less represented lesion types.

Key Findings

The proposed deep learning model achieved an overall accuracy of 83.33%, precision of 89.12%, and recall of 77.31% across 16 different oral lesion classes. These results demonstrate a superior performance compared to existing state-of-the-art methods. A significant finding was the confirmation of the effectiveness of the employed oversampling and augmentation strategies, particularly evidenced by the noteworthy improvement in classification performance for minority classes.

Clinical Impact

This research lays the groundwork for developing trustworthy computer-aided diagnostic (CAD) systems that can assist clinicians in the early and accurate detection of oral cancer and other oral lesions. Such systems have the potential to significantly improve clinical outcomes by enabling earlier intervention, reducing misdiagnosis rates, and standardizing the diagnostic process in diverse clinical settings, thereby saving lives and improving quality of life for patients.

Limitations

The abstract implicitly notes that the presented framework is a "first step toward trustworthy computer aided diagnostic systems." This suggests that while promising, the model is not yet fully validated or robust enough for widespread clinical deployment and requires further development and rigorous testing in real-world, diverse clinical environments to establish full trustworthiness and generalizability.

Future Directions

The paper indicates that future research should focus on further developing the framework to become a "trustworthy computer aided diagnostic system for the early detection of oral cancer in clinical settings." This implies subsequent work would involve extensive validation with larger, more diverse datasets, testing across different patient populations and imaging modalities, and potentially integrating the system into existing clinical workflows for practical application and assessment of real-world impact.

Abstract

Oral cancer is highly common across the globe and is mostly diagnosed during the later stages due to the close visual similarity to benign, precancerous, and malignant lesions in the oral cavity. Implementing computer aided diagnosis systems early on has the potential to greatly improve clinical outcomes. This research intends to use deep learning to build a multiclass classifier for sixteen different oral lesions. To overcome the challenges of limited and imbalanced datasets, the proposed technique combines stratified data splitting and advanced data augmentation and oversampling to perform the classification. The experimental results, which achieved 83.33 percent accuracy, 89.12 percent precision, and 77.31 percent recall, demonstrate the superiority of the suggested model over state of the art methods now in use. The suggested model effectively conveys the effectiveness of oversampling and augmentation strategies in situations where the minority class classification performance is noteworthy. As a first step toward trustworthy computer aided diagnostic systems for the early detection of oral cancer in clinical settings, the suggested framework shows promise.
