Deep Learning-Based Multiclass Classification of Oral Lesions with Stratified Augmentation

arXiv ID: 2511.21582v1
Published: 2025-11-26
Authors: Joy Naoum, Revana Salama, Ali Hamdi
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This research introduces a deep learning-based multiclass classifier designed to differentiate between sixteen distinct types of oral lesions, aiming to improve early detection of oral cancer. By addressing challenges of limited and imbalanced datasets through stratified data splitting, advanced augmentation, and oversampling, the proposed model achieved superior performance metrics compared to existing methods, demonstrating the effectiveness of its data handling strategies.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medical practice as it offers a sophisticated tool for the early and accurate differentiation of oral lesions, which is crucial for timely diagnosis and intervention for oral cancer. Improved early detection can significantly enhance patient outcomes and reduce mortality rates.

AI Health Application

The AI application is a deep learning-based multiclass classifier designed as a computer-aided diagnostic (CAD) system for the early detection and classification of various oral lesions (benign, precancerous, and malignant) in clinical settings.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical issue of late oral cancer diagnosis, often due to the visual similarity between benign, precancerous, and malignant oral lesions.
  • Proposes a deep learning model capable of multiclass classification for sixteen different oral lesion types.
  • Combats limited and imbalanced datasets by implementing a robust methodology combining stratified data splitting with advanced data augmentation and oversampling techniques.
  • Achieved impressive performance metrics: 83.33% accuracy, 89.12% precision, and 77.31% recall.
  • Demonstrates the model's superiority over current state-of-the-art methods for oral lesion classification.
  • Highlights the significant contribution and effectiveness of the oversampling and augmentation strategies, particularly in improving classification performance for minority classes.
  • Positions the developed framework as a promising initial step towards creating reliable computer-aided diagnostic systems for early oral cancer detection in clinical settings.

Methodology

The study employed a deep learning framework for multiclass classification. To counteract the challenges of limited and imbalanced datasets, the methodology integrated stratified data splitting to ensure balanced class representation across training and validation sets. This was complemented by advanced data augmentation techniques (e.g., geometric transformations, color space adjustments) and oversampling strategies to artificially expand and balance the dataset, particularly for underrepresented classes, thereby enhancing model generalization and robustness.

Key Findings

The proposed deep learning model achieved an overall accuracy of 83.33%, a precision of 89.12%, and a recall of 77.31% across the sixteen different oral lesion classes. These results demonstrated the model's superiority compared to existing state-of-the-art methods, with particular emphasis on the notable improvement in classification performance for minority classes attributed to the effective oversampling and augmentation strategies.

Clinical Impact

This multiclass classification system holds substantial potential for clinical impact by providing clinicians with a powerful, trustworthy computer-aided diagnostic tool for oral lesions. Such a system could facilitate earlier and more accurate identification of malignant and precancerous conditions, leading to prompt referral and treatment, ultimately improving patient prognoses and reducing the burden of advanced oral cancer.

Limitations

While promising, the abstract indicates this is a "first step" towards fully trustworthy systems, implying that further extensive clinical validation, testing on larger and more diverse real-world datasets, and rigorous evaluation in various clinical settings are likely required before widespread clinical deployment.

Future Directions

The research suggests the framework serves as a promising foundation for the development of fully trustworthy computer-aided diagnostic systems for early oral cancer detection. Future work would likely involve further refinement, extensive clinical trials, and integration into existing clinical workflows to achieve practical implementation and widespread adoption.

Abstract

Oral cancer is highly common across the globe and is mostly diagnosed during the later stages due to the close visual similarity to benign, precancerous, and malignant lesions in the oral cavity. Implementing computer aided diagnosis systems early on has the potential to greatly improve clinical outcomes. This research intends to use deep learning to build a multiclass classifier for sixteen different oral lesions. To overcome the challenges of limited and imbalanced datasets, the proposed technique combines stratified data splitting and advanced data augmentation and oversampling to perform the classification. The experimental results, which achieved 83.33 percent accuracy, 89.12 percent precision, and 77.31 percent recall, demonstrate the superiority of the suggested model over state of the art methods now in use. The suggested model effectively conveys the effectiveness of oversampling and augmentation strategies in situations where the minority class classification performance is noteworthy. As a first step toward trustworthy computer aided diagnostic systems for the early detection of oral cancer in clinical settings, the suggested framework shows promise.
