Robust Multi-Disease Retinal Classification via Xception-Based Transfer Learning and W-Net Vessel Segmentation

arXiv ID: 2512.10608v1
Published: 2025-12-11
Authors: Mohammad Sadegh Gholizadeh, Amir Arsalan Rezapour
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a novel deep learning pipeline for robust multi-disease retinal classification, addressing the 'black-box' nature of standard CNNs. It combines Xception-based transfer learning for deep feature extraction with interpretable W-Net vessel segmentation, using the latter as an auxiliary task to guide and ground classification predictions in clinically relevant morphological features.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for enhancing early diagnosis and management of vision-threatening eye diseases, offering a scalable, accurate, and more interpretable automated screening tool. By improving diagnostic accuracy and trust, it can alleviate the burden on ophthalmologists and improve patient outcomes.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the automated diagnosis and screening of multiple retinal diseases using deep learning, specifically focusing on interpretable models and high-fidelity retinal vessel segmentation to enhance accuracy, reduce false positives, and facilitate clinical deployment.

Key Points

  • Addresses the rising incidence of vision-threatening eye diseases by proposing scalable and accurate automated screening solutions.
  • Combats the 'black-box' problem of standard Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) by integrating interpretable image processing modules.
  • Utilizes an Xception-based architecture for transfer learning to perform deep feature extraction for retinal disease classification.
  • Implements high-fidelity retinal vessel segmentation, specifically using a W-Net, as a crucial auxiliary task to enhance model interpretability.
  • Aims to ground classification predictions in clinically relevant morphological features derived from vessel segmentation.
  • Seeks to reduce false positives and improve the trustworthiness and deployment viability of AI models in clinical settings.
  • The proposed pipeline is designed to bridge the gap between algorithmic outputs and expert medical validation.

Methodology

The methodology involves a deep learning pipeline integrating an Xception-based model for transfer learning and deep feature extraction in multi-disease retinal classification. This is complemented by a W-Net architecture specifically for high-fidelity retinal vessel segmentation, which serves as an auxiliary task to provide interpretable, morphology-driven guidance for the classification predictions.

Key Findings

The paper aims to demonstrate that by incorporating an interpretable retinal vessel segmentation module (W-Net) to guide an Xception-based classification model, it is possible to significantly improve the interpretability, reduce false positives, and enhance the overall robustness and clinical deployment viability of automated multi-disease retinal diagnostic systems.

Clinical Impact

Potential for widespread automated screening programs for eye diseases, leading to earlier detection and intervention. Improved diagnostic accuracy and reduced false positives can build clinician trust, facilitating the integration of AI tools into routine clinical workflows and potentially increasing access to expert-level diagnostics in underserved areas.

Limitations

The primary limitation addressed by this work is the 'black-box' nature of standard deep learning CNNs, which hinders their clinical adoption due to lack of interpretability and trust from medical professionals.

Future Directions

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract, but implied directions include further validation across diverse patient populations and integration into real-world clinical decision support systems.

Abstract

In recent years, the incidence of vision-threatening eye diseases has risen dramatically, necessitating scalable and accurate screening solutions. This paper presents a comprehensive study on deep learning architectures for the automated diagnosis of ocular conditions. To mitigate the "black-box" limitations of standard convolutional neural networks (CNNs), we implement a pipeline that combines deep feature extraction with interpretable image processing modules. Specifically, we focus on high-fidelity retinal vessel segmentation as an auxiliary task to guide the classification process. By grounding the model's predictions in clinically relevant morphological features, we aim to bridge the gap between algorithmic output and expert medical validation, thereby reducing false positives and improving deployment viability in clinical settings.
