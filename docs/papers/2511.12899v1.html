FDP: A Frequency-Decomposition Preprocessing Pipeline for Unsupervised Anomaly Detection in Brain MRI

Summary

This paper introduces FDP, a novel Frequency-Decomposition Preprocessing pipeline designed to enhance Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) in brain MRI by leveraging insights from frequency-domain analysis. FDP addresses the limitations of current UAD methods, which rely on simulated anomalies lacking biophysical fidelity, by enabling simultaneous pathology suppression and anatomical preservation through frequency-domain reconstruction, leading to consistent performance improvements across various UAD architectures.

Medical Relevance

This research significantly advances the capabilities of unsupervised anomaly detection in brain MRI, which is crucial for identifying neurological conditions without requiring large, annotated datasets of rare pathologies. Improved detection accuracy can lead to earlier and more precise diagnosis of brain diseases.

AI Health Application

The AI application is an unsupervised anomaly detection system (FDP framework) designed to identify pathological structures (lesions, anomalies) in brain MRI scans. This can serve as a diagnostic aid for clinicians (e.g., neurologists, radiologists) to detect brain diseases or conditions that manifest as structural anomalies, thereby improving the accuracy and efficiency of medical diagnosis.

Key Points

  • Unsupervised Anomaly Detection (UAD) in brain MRI is challenging due to the diversity of brain anatomy and the scarcity of annotated pathological data.
  • Existing UAD methods often use artificially generated noise perturbations for training, which lack the biophysical fidelity and morphological complexity of real clinical lesions.
  • The authors conducted the first systematic frequency-domain analysis of pathological signatures in brain MRI, revealing that anomalies have unique frequency patterns distinguishable from normal anatomy, and low-frequency signals are consistent across healthy scans.
  • Based on these insights, FDP (Frequency-Decomposition Preprocessing) is proposed as the first UAD method to utilize frequency-domain reconstruction for simultaneous pathology suppression and normal anatomical preservation.
  • FDP is designed to integrate seamlessly with existing anomaly simulation techniques and UAD generative model architectures.
  • Experimental results demonstrate that FDP consistently improves anomaly detection performance across diverse baselines.
  • Notably, FDP achieved a significant 17.63% increase in DICE score when integrated with LDM (Latent Diffusion Model), while maintaining robust improvements with other methods.

Methodology

The methodology involves a systematic frequency-domain analysis of brain MRI scans to identify characteristic frequency patterns of anomalies versus healthy tissue. Based on these observations, the Frequency-Decomposition Preprocessing (FDP) framework was developed. FDP leverages frequency-domain reconstruction to selectively suppress pathological signals while preserving normal anatomical structures. It is designed as a modular preprocessing step that can be integrated with existing generative model-based UAD architectures that typically rely on anomaly simulation.

Key Findings

Key findings include the discovery that brain anomalies exhibit unique and distinguishable frequency patterns compared to normal anatomy, and that low-frequency signals remain consistently represented across healthy brain scans. The FDP framework, built on these findings, consistently enhances UAD performance, achieving a notable 17.63% DICE score improvement with LDM and robust gains across multiple baseline methods, demonstrating its effectiveness in pathology suppression and anatomical preservation.

Clinical Impact

The enhanced accuracy in unsupervised anomaly detection provided by FDP has the potential to significantly improve the diagnosis of a wide range of neurological conditions from brain MRIs, especially those characterized by rare or subtle lesions. By reducing reliance on scarce expert-annotated pathological data, FDP can facilitate the deployment of more robust and generalizable UAD tools in clinical settings, potentially leading to earlier intervention and better patient outcomes.

Limitations

The abstract primarily highlights the strengths and improvements offered by FDP over existing methods (e.g., lack of biophysical fidelity in simulated anomalies). It does not explicitly state specific limitations of the FDP framework itself.

Abstract

Due to the diversity of brain anatomy and the scarcity of annotated data, supervised anomaly detection for brain MRI remains challenging, driving the development of unsupervised anomaly detection (UAD) approaches. Current UAD methods typically utilize artificially generated noise perturbations on healthy MRIs to train generative models for normal anatomy reconstruction, enabling anomaly detection via residual mapping. However, such simulated anomalies lack the biophysical fidelity and morphological complexity characteristic of true clinical lesions. To advance UAD in brain MRI, we conduct the first systematic frequency-domain analysis of pathological signatures, revealing two key properties: (1) anomalies exhibit unique frequency patterns distinguishable from normal anatomy, and (2) low-frequency signals maintain consistent representations across healthy scans. These insights motivate our Frequency-Decomposition Preprocessing (FDP) framework, the first UAD method to leverage frequency-domain reconstruction for simultaneous pathology suppression and anatomical preservation. FDP can integrate seamlessly with existing anomaly simulation techniques, consistently enhancing detection performance across diverse architectures while maintaining diagnostic fidelity. Experimental results demonstrate that FDP consistently improves anomaly detection performance when integrated with existing methods. Notably, FDP achieves a 17.63% increase in DICE score with LDM while maintaining robust improvements across multiple baselines. The code is available at https://github.com/ls1rius/MRI_FDP.
