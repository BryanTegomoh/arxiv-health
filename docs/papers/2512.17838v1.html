ReX-MLE: The Autonomous Agent Benchmark for Medical Imaging Challenges

arXiv ID: 2512.17838v1
Published: 2025-12-19
Authors: Roshan Kenia, Xiaoman Zhang, Pranav Rajpurkar
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces ReX-MLE, a novel benchmark comprising 20 medical imaging challenges, designed to rigorously evaluate autonomous LLM-based agents on end-to-end workflows under realistic constraints. The study reveals a severe performance gap, with state-of-the-art agents consistently ranking in the 0th percentile compared to human experts due to profound domain-knowledge and engineering limitations. ReX-MLE thus exposes critical bottlenecks for developing truly domain-aware autonomous AI systems in medicine.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for understanding the current limitations of autonomous AI agents in solving real-world medical imaging problems, which are foundational for diagnosis, treatment planning, and research in healthcare. It highlights the urgent need for specialized AI development that integrates deep medical domain understanding to build reliable tools for clinical use.

AI Health Application

Developing autonomous AI agents capable of independently managing data preprocessing, model training, and submission for a wide range of medical imaging tasks. This would enable AI to assist or potentially automate aspects of medical image analysis, diagnosis, and research, thereby improving efficiency and accuracy in healthcare.

Key Points

  • LLM-based autonomous coding agents, while effective in general tasks, are inadequate for complex, domain-specific scientific problems like medical imaging.
  • Medical imaging is a highly demanding domain, characterized by long training cycles, high-dimensional data, and specialized preprocessing/validation pipelines, which existing agent benchmarks fail to measure.
  • ReX-MLE is introduced as a new benchmark consisting of 20 challenges derived from high-impact medical imaging competitions, covering diverse modalities and task types.
  • Unlike previous benchmarks, ReX-MLE evaluates full end-to-end workflows, requiring agents to independently manage data preprocessing, model training, and submission under realistic compute and time constraints.
  • Evaluation of state-of-the-art agents (AIDE, ML-Master, R&D-Agent) with advanced LLM backends (GPT-5, Gemini, Claude) revealed a severe performance deficit.
  • Most agent submissions ranked in the 0th percentile when compared to human experts, indicating a profound inability to solve these medical imaging challenges.
  • The observed failures are attributed to fundamental limitations in the agents' domain-knowledge and engineering capabilities specific to medical imaging.
  • ReX-MLE serves as a critical tool to expose these bottlenecks and establish a foundation for the development of more robust, domain-aware autonomous AI systems.

Methodology

The researchers developed ReX-MLE, a benchmark of 20 medical imaging challenges sourced from high-impact competitions. This benchmark mandates that autonomous agents handle the entire end-to-end workflow, encompassing data preprocessing, model training, and submission, all within realistic compute and time constraints. They then evaluated multiple state-of-the-art autonomous agents (AIDE, ML-Master, R&D-Agent) powered by various large language model backends (GPT-5, Gemini, Claude) against these challenges, comparing their performance to human expert benchmarks.

Key Findings

The study conclusively found that state-of-the-art autonomous agents exhibit a severe performance gap in tackling complex medical imaging challenges. Most agent submissions achieved a 0th percentile ranking when compared to human experts. This significant underperformance was primarily attributed to fundamental deficiencies in the agents' medical domain-knowledge and their engineering capabilities required for managing high-dimensional medical data and specialized pipelines.

Clinical Impact

Currently, the findings indicate that autonomous LLM-based agents are not proficient enough for independent application in developing solutions for medical imaging tasks in clinical settings. The research underscores that substantial advancements are necessary in equipping AI with specialized medical knowledge and robust engineering skills before it can reliably contribute to clinical diagnostics, treatment planning, or the development of new medical imaging tools.

Limitations

The primary limitation identified is the profound inadequacy of current state-of-the-art autonomous agents, despite leveraging advanced LLMs, to effectively address complex, domain-specific medical imaging challenges. Their inherent lack of specialized domain-knowledge and engineering sophistication severely restricts their practical utility in this critical field.

Future Directions

The paper suggests that ReX-MLE provides a crucial foundation for future research aimed at developing 'domain-aware autonomous AI systems.' This implies a need for enhancing LLM agents with deeper, context-specific medical expertise and more sophisticated engineering capabilities to navigate the complexities of high-dimensional medical data and specialized computational pipelines, enabling them to operate effectively under real-world constraints.

Abstract

Autonomous coding agents built on large language models (LLMs) can now solve many general software and machine learning tasks, but they remain ineffective on complex, domain-specific scientific problems. Medical imaging is a particularly demanding domain, requiring long training cycles, high-dimensional data handling, and specialized preprocessing and validation pipelines, capabilities not fully measured in existing agent benchmarks. To address this gap, we introduce ReX-MLE, a benchmark of 20 challenges derived from high-impact medical imaging competitions spanning diverse modalities and task types. Unlike prior ML-agent benchmarks, ReX-MLE evaluates full end-to-end workflows, requiring agents to independently manage data preprocessing, model training, and submission under realistic compute and time constraints. Evaluating state-of-the-art agents (AIDE, ML-Master, R&D-Agent) with different LLM backends (GPT-5, Gemini, Claude), we observe a severe performance gap: most submissions rank in the 0th percentile compared to human experts. Failures stem from domain-knowledge and engineering limitations. ReX-MLE exposes these bottlenecks and provides a foundation for developing domain-aware autonomous AI systems.
