A Physics-Informed Loss Function for Boundary-Consistent and Robust Artery Segmentation in DSA Sequences

Published: 2025-11-25
Authors: Muhammad Irfan, Nasir Rahim, Khalid Mahmood Malik

Summary

This paper introduces a novel Physics-Informed Loss (PIL) function designed for accurate and robust cerebral artery segmentation in Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA) sequences. By modeling boundary interactions as an elastic process inspired by dislocation theory, PIL enforces geometric and physical consistency, overcoming limitations of conventional pixel-wise loss functions. Experimental results demonstrate PIL's superior performance in terms of sensitivity, F1 score, and boundary coherence across various segmentation architectures.

Medical Relevance

Accurate segmentation of cerebral arteries from DSA images is foundational for developing reliable clinical management models for complex cerebrovascular diseases, directly impacting diagnosis, treatment planning, and prognostic assessment.

This research applies AI (deep learning with a novel loss function) to enhance medical image analysis, specifically for precise and robust segmentation of arteries in DSA scans. This application aids in the diagnosis, monitoring, and clinical management of cerebrovascular diseases by providing more accurate anatomical information from angiographic images.

Methodology

The core methodology involves developing a novel Physics-Informed Loss (PIL) function. This loss models the interaction between predicted and ground-truth vascular boundaries as an elastic process, drawing principles from dislocation theory in materials physics. It incorporates a physics-based regularization term to ensure smooth contour evolution and structural consistency, thereby improving the capture of fine vascular geometry. The PIL was integrated into multiple deep learning segmentation architectures (U-Net, U-Net++, SegFormer, MedFormer) and evaluated against conventional loss functions (Cross-Entropy, Dice, Active Contour, Surface losses) on two public benchmark datasets, DIAS and DSCA.

Key Findings

The study conclusively found that the proposed Physics-Informed Loss (PIL) consistently and significantly outperformed conventional loss functions across all tested segmentation architectures and benchmarks. PIL achieved superior metrics, specifically in sensitivity, F1 score, and boundary coherence, demonstrating a marked improvement in both the precision and robustness of vascular segmentation, particularly in capturing fine anatomical details and ensuring structural consistency.

Clinical Impact

This improved precision and robustness in cerebral artery segmentation has significant clinical impact, enabling more reliable and accurate clinical management models for complex cerebrovascular diseases. Clinicians can utilize this technology for enhanced diagnosis, more precise pre-surgical planning (e.g., for aneurysm coiling, stent placement, or AVM embolization), and better monitoring of disease progression or treatment efficacy, ultimately leading to improved patient outcomes and reduced complications.

Abstract

Accurate extraction and segmentation of the cerebral arteries from digital subtraction angiography (DSA) sequences is essential for developing reliable clinical management models of complex cerebrovascular diseases. Conventional loss functions often rely solely on pixel-wise overlap, overlooking the geometric and physical consistency of vascular boundaries, which can lead to fragmented or unstable vessel predictions. To overcome this limitation, we propose a novel \textit{Physics-Informed Loss} (PIL) that models the interaction between the predicted and ground-truth boundaries as an elastic process inspired by dislocation theory in materials physics. This formulation introduces a physics-based regularization term that enforces smooth contour evolution and structural consistency, allowing the network to better capture fine vascular geometry. The proposed loss is integrated into several segmentation architectures, including U-Net, U-Net++, SegFormer, and MedFormer, and evaluated on two public benchmarks: DIAS and DSCA. Experimental results demonstrate that PIL consistently outperforms conventional loss functions such as Cross-Entropy, Dice, Active Contour, and Surface losses, achieving superior sensitivity, F1 score, and boundary coherence. These findings confirm that the incorporation of physics-based boundary interactions into deep neural networks improves both the precision and robustness of vascular segmentation in dynamic angiographic imaging. The implementation of the proposed method is publicly available at https://github.com/irfantahir301/Physicsis_loss.
