A Physics-Informed Loss Function for Boundary-Consistent and Robust Artery Segmentation in DSA Sequences

Published: 2025-11-25
Authors: Muhammad Irfan, Nasir Rahim, Khalid Mahmood Malik
Categories: cs.CV, cs.LG

Summary

This paper introduces a novel Physics-Informed Loss (PIL) function designed to enhance the accuracy and robustness of cerebral artery segmentation in Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA) sequences. By modeling boundary interactions as an elastic process inspired by dislocation theory, PIL enforces geometric and physical consistency, leading to smoother contours and structurally coherent vessel predictions. The proposed loss consistently outperforms conventional loss functions across various segmentation architectures and public benchmarks.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and robust segmentation of cerebral arteries from DSA sequences is critical for developing reliable clinical management models for complex cerebrovascular diseases, impacting diagnosis, treatment planning, and prognostic assessment.

AI Health Application

This paper applies AI (deep neural networks) to improve the segmentation of cerebral arteries in DSA medical images. This application would assist clinicians in the diagnosis, assessment, and management of cerebrovascular diseases by providing more accurate and robust automated analysis of vascular structures. This can lead to more reliable clinical management models, better surgical planning, and improved patient outcomes for conditions affecting brain blood vessels.

Key Points

  • The proposed Physics-Informed Loss (PIL) models the interaction between predicted and ground-truth boundaries as an elastic process, drawing inspiration from dislocation theory in materials physics.
  • PIL introduces a physics-based regularization term that enforces smooth contour evolution and structural consistency, thereby improving the capture of fine vascular geometry.
  • The loss function was integrated and evaluated across multiple deep neural network segmentation architectures, including U-Net, U-Net++, SegFormer, and MedFormer.
  • Evaluations on two public benchmarks (DIAS and DSCA) demonstrated that PIL consistently achieves superior sensitivity, F1 score, and boundary coherence compared to conventional losses like Cross-Entropy, Dice, Active Contour, and Surface losses.

Methodology

The core methodology involves proposing a Physics-Informed Loss (PIL) function. This loss models the interaction between predicted and ground-truth boundaries as an elastic process, drawing principles from dislocation theory in materials physics to introduce a physics-based regularization term. This term is designed to enforce smooth contour evolution and structural consistency in segmentations. PIL was integrated into established deep learning segmentation architectures (U-Net, U-Net++, SegFormer, MedFormer) and evaluated against conventional loss functions (Cross-Entropy, Dice, Active Contour, Surface losses) on two public DSA benchmarks: DIAS and DSCA.

Key Findings

The Physics-Informed Loss (PIL) consistently and significantly outperforms conventional loss functions (Cross-Entropy, Dice, Active Contour, Surface losses) across various segmentation architectures (U-Net, U-Net++, SegFormer, MedFormer). PIL achieved superior sensitivity, F1 score, and crucially, improved boundary coherence, demonstrating enhanced precision and robustness in vascular segmentation of DSA sequences.

Abstract

Accurate extraction and segmentation of the cerebral arteries from digital subtraction angiography (DSA) sequences is essential for developing reliable clinical management models of complex cerebrovascular diseases. Conventional loss functions often rely solely on pixel-wise overlap, overlooking the geometric and physical consistency of vascular boundaries, which can lead to fragmented or unstable vessel predictions. To overcome this limitation, we propose a novel \textit{Physics-Informed Loss} (PIL) that models the interaction between the predicted and ground-truth boundaries as an elastic process inspired by dislocation theory in materials physics. This formulation introduces a physics-based regularization term that enforces smooth contour evolution and structural consistency, allowing the network to better capture fine vascular geometry. The proposed loss is integrated into several segmentation architectures, including U-Net, U-Net++, SegFormer, and MedFormer, and evaluated on two public benchmarks: DIAS and DSCA. Experimental results demonstrate that PIL consistently outperforms conventional loss functions such as Cross-Entropy, Dice, Active Contour, and Surface losses, achieving superior sensitivity, F1 score, and boundary coherence. These findings confirm that the incorporation of physics-based boundary interactions into deep neural networks improves both the precision and robustness of vascular segmentation in dynamic angiographic imaging. The implementation of the proposed method is publicly available at https://github.com/irfantahir301/Physicsis_loss.
