A Physics-Informed Loss Function for Boundary-Consistent and Robust Artery Segmentation in DSA Sequences

arXiv ID: 2511.20501v1
Published: 2025-11-25
Authors: Muhammad Irfan, Nasir Rahim, Khalid Mahmood Malik
Categories: cs.CV, cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00

Summary

This paper introduces a novel Physics-Informed Loss (PIL) function designed for accurate and robust cerebral artery segmentation in Digital Subtraction Angiography (DSA) sequences. PIL models boundary interactions as an elastic process, drawing from materials physics, to enforce geometric consistency and smooth contour evolution. This approach significantly improves segmentation precision and robustness compared to conventional loss functions across various deep learning architectures.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and robust extraction of cerebral arteries from DSA sequences is critical for developing reliable clinical management models of complex cerebrovascular diseases, directly impacting diagnosis, treatment planning, and patient outcomes.

AI Health Application

This research contributes to the development of more precise and robust AI models for medical image analysis. Specifically, it enhances the capability of deep learning networks to accurately segment cerebral arteries from DSA images. This application is crucial for automated or semi-automated diagnosis, prognosis, and treatment planning for conditions like aneurysms, stroke, or stenosis, thereby improving clinical decision-making and patient outcomes in cerebrovascular disease management.

Key Points

  • **Problem Statement:** Conventional deep learning loss functions for DSA artery segmentation often produce fragmented or unstable vessel predictions because they primarily rely on pixel-wise overlap, neglecting geometric and physical consistency of vascular boundaries.
  • **Proposed Solution:** A novel Physics-Informed Loss (PIL) function is introduced, which conceptualizes the interaction between predicted and ground-truth segmentation boundaries as an elastic process.
  • **Theoretical Foundation:** PIL's elastic process model is directly inspired by dislocation theory in materials physics, providing a robust, physics-based regularization term.
  • **Mechanism & Benefits:** This regularization term enforces smooth contour evolution and structural consistency, enabling neural networks to better capture fine vascular geometry and produce more coherent segmentations.
  • **Integration & Evaluation:** PIL was integrated into multiple segmentation architectures (U-Net, U-Net++, SegFormer, MedFormer) and rigorously evaluated on two public benchmarks: DIAS and DSCA datasets.
  • **Superior Performance:** Experimental results consistently demonstrated that PIL outperforms conventional loss functions, including Cross-Entropy, Dice, Active Contour, and Surface losses.
  • **Key Metrics:** PIL achieved superior sensitivity, F1 score, and boundary coherence, confirming its enhanced precision and robustness in dynamic angiographic imaging.

Methodology

The authors developed a Physics-Informed Loss (PIL) function that models the interaction between predicted and ground-truth segmentation boundaries as an elastic process, drawing inspiration from dislocation theory in materials physics. This physics-based regularization term was integrated into several deep neural network segmentation architectures, including U-Net, U-Net++, SegFormer, and MedFormer. The performance of these models, augmented with PIL, was evaluated against models utilizing conventional loss functions (Cross-Entropy, Dice, Active Contour, Surface losses) on two public benchmark datasets: DIAS and DSCA.

Key Findings

The Physics-Informed Loss (PIL) consistently demonstrated superior performance over conventional loss functions across various deep learning architectures (U-Net, U-Net++, SegFormer, MedFormer) and benchmarks (DIAS, DSCA). Specifically, PIL achieved superior sensitivity, F1 score, and boundary coherence, indicating a significant improvement in both the precision and robustness of vascular segmentation by enforcing smooth contour evolution and structural consistency.

Clinical Impact

This method has the potential to significantly improve the accuracy and reliability of automated cerebral artery segmentation in DSA, leading to more precise diagnosis, better-informed treatment planning (e.g., for aneurysm clipping/coiling, AVM embolization), and enhanced monitoring for patients with complex cerebrovascular diseases. This can reduce fragmentation or instability in vessel predictions, providing clinicians with more consistent and clinically actionable anatomical information.

Abstract

Accurate extraction and segmentation of the cerebral arteries from digital subtraction angiography (DSA) sequences is essential for developing reliable clinical management models of complex cerebrovascular diseases. Conventional loss functions often rely solely on pixel-wise overlap, overlooking the geometric and physical consistency of vascular boundaries, which can lead to fragmented or unstable vessel predictions. To overcome this limitation, we propose a novel \textit{Physics-Informed Loss} (PIL) that models the interaction between the predicted and ground-truth boundaries as an elastic process inspired by dislocation theory in materials physics. This formulation introduces a physics-based regularization term that enforces smooth contour evolution and structural consistency, allowing the network to better capture fine vascular geometry. The proposed loss is integrated into several segmentation architectures, including U-Net, U-Net++, SegFormer, and MedFormer, and evaluated on two public benchmarks: DIAS and DSCA. Experimental results demonstrate that PIL consistently outperforms conventional loss functions such as Cross-Entropy, Dice, Active Contour, and Surface losses, achieving superior sensitivity, F1 score, and boundary coherence. These findings confirm that the incorporation of physics-based boundary interactions into deep neural networks improves both the precision and robustness of vascular segmentation in dynamic angiographic imaging. The implementation of the proposed method is publicly available at https://github.com/irfantahir301/Physicsis_loss.
