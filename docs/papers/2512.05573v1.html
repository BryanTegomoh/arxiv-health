Refined HLA Linkage Disequilibrium Architectures of World Populations by a Novel Allelic Correlation Measure

Summary

This paper introduces the Conditional Informatics Correlation Coefficient (CICC), a novel and robust measure designed to overcome limitations of existing methods in characterizing complex linkage disequilibrium (LD) within the human leukocyte antigen (HLA) region. By applying CICC to global population datasets, the study identified 10 novel high-LD regions and nine strongly linked regions consistently shared across five global populations, offering a refined understanding of HLA architecture.

Medical Relevance

Accurate characterization of HLA linkage disequilibrium is fundamental for identifying genetic markers associated with autoimmune disorders and other complex diseases, which can lead to improved risk prediction, diagnosis, and personalized treatment strategies.

Methodology

The study introduced the Conditional Informatics Correlation Coefficient (CICC), a novel, robust LD measure that integrates conditional probability, information content, and haplotype-aware XOR logic. This method was applied to high-resolution haploid genomes obtained from the Human Pangenome Reference Consortium (HPRC) and further validated using data from the 1000 Genomes Project and Genome Asia datasets to analyze LD architectures across five diverse global populations.

Key Findings

The CICC method successfully identified 10 novel high-LD regions within the HLA complex. Crucially, it revealed nine strongly linked regions (five in HLA Class I and four in HLA Class II) that are consistently shared across five global populations, providing a refined and more robust understanding of HLA architectural complexity.

Clinical Impact

The refined understanding of HLA LD architectures provided by CICC can significantly improve the identification of disease-associated genetic markers, enhancing the accuracy of disease gene mapping. This advancement is expected to accelerate the development of personalized risk assessments, diagnostic tools, and targeted therapies for autoimmune and other HLA-associated diseases, thereby directly guiding and advancing precision medicine initiatives.

Abstract

Numerous diseases, particularly autoimmune disorders, are associated with the human leukocyte antigen (HLA), a small genomic region located on human chromosome 6. Adequate characterization of linkage disequilibrium (LD) in the HLA across populations is crucial for identifying genetic markers associated with specific traits and phenotypes. However, current LD measures often fail to capture HLA's structural complexity due to methodological limitations and sensitivity to low-frequency variants, marginal allele frequencies, and haplotype composition. To address these challenges, we introduced the Conditional Informatics Correlation Coefficient (CICC), which integrates conditional probability, information content, and haplotype-aware XOR logic to quantify LD robustly. When applied to high-resolution haploid genomes from the Human Pangenome Reference Consortium (HPRC), CICC revealed 10 novel high-LD regions in HLA. Further analyses using the 1000 Genomes Project and Genome Asia datasets identified nine strongly linked regions shared across five global populations-five in Class I and four in Class II. These results demonstrate CICC's ability to capture complex HLA LD structures across populations, highlighting its broad potential for disease gene mapping, population genomics, and guiding precision medicine.
