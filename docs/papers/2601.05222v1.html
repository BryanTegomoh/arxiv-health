Oscillatory Regimes in a Game-Theoretic Model for Mosquito Population Dynamics under Breeding Site Control

Summary

This paper introduces a game-theoretic model to investigate how spontaneous household decision-making regarding breeding site control influences mosquito population dynamics. It reveals that the feedback loop between perceived risks, household control actions, and mosquito abundance can lead to sustained oscillations in mosquito populations, independent of environmental factors. The study characterizes conditions under which these human-behaviorally driven oscillations emerge.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for understanding an underexplored driver of mosquito population dynamics: human behavioral responses to perceived risk. It provides insights into how public health campaigns requiring sustained community participation might interact with mosquito population cycles, potentially explaining variability in control effectiveness and re-emergence patterns of vector-borne diseases.

Key Points

  • A novel game-theoretic model is developed to simulate household participation in breeding site control, driven by imitation dynamics and perceived risks.
  • Household control actions dynamically regulate the carrying capacity of the aquatic mosquito stage, establishing a direct feedback loop with mosquito population growth.
  • A simplified model with constant payoffs characterizes four locally stable equilibria, representing combinations of full/no household control and mosquito presence/absence.
  • When the perceived risk of not controlling breeding sites is dependent on mosquito prevalence, an additional equilibrium emerges, characterized by partial household engagement.
  • This partial engagement equilibrium can undergo a Hopf bifurcation, demonstrating that sustained oscillations in mosquito populations can arise solely from the dynamic interaction between mosquito abundance and human behavioral responses.
  • These oscillatory regimes are distinct from environmentally driven cycles, highlighting an endogenous source of population fluctuations.
  • Numerical simulations and parameter explorations are employed to further describe the amplitude and phase properties of these behaviorally-induced oscillations.

Methodology

The study employs a game-theoretic model, where the fraction of households performing breeding site control evolves via imitation dynamics based on perceived risks. Mathematical analysis includes characterizing locally stable equilibria and deriving conditions for Hopf bifurcations. Numerical simulations and parameter explorations are utilized to describe the amplitude and phase characteristics of oscillatory regimes.

Key Findings

Four locally stable equilibria corresponding to full/no control and mosquito presence/absence were identified in a simplified model. With prevalence-dependent perceived risk, a fifth equilibrium with partial household engagement emerges. Crucially, this partial engagement equilibrium can undergo a Hopf bifurcation, demonstrating that sustained oscillations in mosquito population abundance can arise *solely* from the dynamic feedback between mosquito prevalence and human behavioral responses to risk, not external environmental factors. These oscillations' amplitude and phase properties were further characterized.

Clinical Impact

This work suggests that public health interventions for mosquito-borne diseases must account for the dynamic interplay between vector populations and human behavioral feedback. Interventions that do not sustain consistent household engagement might inadvertently trigger or perpetuate oscillatory mosquito populations due to fluctuating risk perception and adherence. This necessitates adaptive and continuous strategies, potentially involving dynamic risk communication and community engagement models, to maintain effective breeding site control and prevent cycles of disease resurgence.

Abstract

Mosquito-borne diseases remain a major public-health threat, and the effective control of mosquito populations requires sustained household participation in removing breeding sites. While environmental drivers of mosquito oscillations have been extensively studied, the influence of spontaneous household decision-making on the dynamics of mosquito populations remains poorly understood. We introduce a game-theoretic model in which the fraction of households performing breeding site control evolves through imitation dynamics driven by perceived risks. Household behavior regulates the carrying capacity of the aquatic mosquito stage, creating a feedback between control actions and mosquito population growth. For a simplified model with constant payoffs, we characterize four locally stable equilibria, corresponding to full or no household control and the presence or absence of mosquito populations. When the perceived risk of not controlling breeding sites depends on mosquito prevalence, the system admits an additional equilibrium with partial household engagement. We derive conditions under which this equilibrium undergoes a Hopf bifurcation, yielding sustained oscillations arising solely from the interaction between mosquito abundance and household behavior. Numerical simulations and parameter explorations further describe the amplitude and phase properties of these oscillatory regimes.
