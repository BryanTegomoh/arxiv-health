Computational Analysis of Disease Progression in Pediatric Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension

Summary

This study developed and calibrated patient-specific, multi-scale computational cardiovascular models for four pediatric Pulmonary Arterial Hypertension (PAH) patients using longitudinal MRI and catheterization data. By coupling 3D fluid-structure interaction models of pulmonary arteries with 0D heart/Windkessel models, the researchers successfully non-invasively captured patient-specific hemodynamic adaptation over time, with model-derived metrics aligning with clinical indicators of disease severity and progression. This approach offers a promising tool for monitoring pediatric PAH and informing future treatment strategies.

Medical Relevance

Pediatric PAH is a severe, progressive cardiopulmonary disease in a vulnerable population with limited diagnostic and therapeutic strategies. This study offers a novel, non-invasive computational method to precisely monitor patient-specific hemodynamic changes and disease progression, which is crucial for personalized medicine and improving outcomes in this understudied group.

Methodology

The study involved developing multi-scale, patient-specific cardiovascular models using the CRIMSON simulation framework. This coupled 3D Fluid-Structure Interaction (FSI) models of the pulmonary arteries with 0D lumped-parameter heart and Windkessel models. The models were calibrated against longitudinal MRI and catheterization data from four pediatric PAH patients, collected two years apart. An automated Python-based optimizer was employed to rapidly calibrate boundary conditions by minimizing discrepancies between simulated and clinical hemodynamic metrics.

Key Findings

The developed computational models were able to non-invasively capture patient-specific hemodynamic adaptation over approximately two years in pediatric PAH patients. Crucially, the model-derived metrics such as arterial stiffness, pulse wave velocity, resistance, and compliance were found to align consistently and accurately with established clinical indicators of disease severity and progression.

It holds significant potential to inform personalized treatment strategies, assess the efficacy of therapeutic interventions, and improve risk stratification by offering deep insights into complex hemodynamic changes that are difficult to measure through conventional means.

Abstract

Pulmonary arterial hypertension (PAH) is a progressive cardiopulmonary disease that leads to increased pulmonary pressures, vascular remodeling, and eventual right ventricular (RV) failure. Pediatric PAH remains understudied due to limited data and the lack of targeted diagnostic and therapeutic strategies. In this study, we developed and calibrated multi-scale, patient-specific cardiovascular models for four pediatric PAH patients using longitudinal MRI and catheterization data collected approximately two years apart. Using the CRIMSON simulation framework, we coupled three-dimensional fluid-structure interaction (FSI) models of the pulmonary arteries with zero-dimensional (0D) lumped-parameter heart and Windkessel models to simulate patient hemodynamics. An automated Python-based optimizer was developed to calibrate boundary conditions by minimizing discrepancies between simulated and clinical metrics, reducing calibration time from weeks to days. Model-derived metrics such as arterial stiffness, pulse wave velocity, resistance, and compliance were found to align with clinical indicators of disease severity and progression. Our findings demonstrate that computational modeling can non-invasively capture patient-specific hemodynamic adaptation over time, offering a promising tool for monitoring pediatric PAH and informing future treatment strategies.
