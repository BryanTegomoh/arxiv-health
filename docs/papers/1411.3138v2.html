Introduction to statistical inference for infectious diseases

Summary

This paper offers an introduction to statistical inference for infectious diseases, commencing with the general stochastic epidemic model. It outlines methods for inferring critical parameters such as the basic reproduction number ($R_0$) and vaccination coverage ($v_c$) from diverse outbreak data, considering individual heterogeneities and heterogeneous mixing. The paper also provides an overview of statistical techniques for parameter estimation and addresses the challenge of early outbreak detection in surveillance.

Medical Relevance

This paper is critically relevant to public health and infectious disease management by providing essential statistical frameworks for understanding disease transmission, accurately assessing epidemic potential, and evaluating intervention efficacy. The methods enable timely and data-driven public health responses, crucial for mitigating the impact of outbreaks and informing strategic resource allocation.

Abstract

In this paper we first introduce the general stochastic epidemic model for the spread of infectious diseases. Then we give methods for inferring model parameters such as the basic reproduction number $R_0$ and vaccination coverage $v_c$ assuming different types of data from an outbreak such as final outbreak details and temporal data or observations from an ongoing outbreak. Both individual heterogeneities and heterogeneous mixing are discussed. We also provide an overview of statistical methods to perform parameter estimation for stochastic epidemic models. In the last section we describe the problem of early outbreak detection in infectious disease surveillance and statistical models used for this purpose.
