Introduction to statistical inference for infectious diseases

Published: 2014-11-12
Authors: Tom Britton, Federica Giardina
Categories: stat.ME, q-bio.PE
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00

Summary

This paper serves as an introduction to statistical inference for infectious diseases, beginning with the general stochastic epidemic model. It comprehensively outlines methods for estimating crucial model parameters like the basic reproduction number ($R_0$) and vaccination coverage ($v_c$) from various data types, including final outbreak details, temporal data, or ongoing outbreak observations, while considering individual and mixing heterogeneities. Additionally, it surveys statistical techniques for parameter estimation and addresses the critical issue of early outbreak detection in infectious disease surveillance.

Medical Relevance

Understanding and applying these statistical inference methods are critical for public health decision-making, enabling accurate assessment of disease transmissibility, effectiveness of control measures like vaccination, and timely intervention during outbreaks to mitigate health impacts and save lives.

AI Health Application

While the paper focuses on statistical inference, the problems it addresses (parameter estimation for epidemic models, early outbreak detection, and infectious disease surveillance) are prime areas for AI and machine learning applications in health. AI can enhance these statistical models by: automating data analysis for real-time surveillance, improving predictive accuracy of disease spread, optimizing resource allocation during outbreaks, and developing intelligent early warning systems for emerging pathogens. The 'statistical models used for early outbreak detection' could be considered foundational for AI-powered public health surveillance systems.

Key Points

  • Introduces the foundational general stochastic epidemic model for understanding disease spread.
  • Details various methods for inferring key epidemiological parameters such as the basic reproduction number ($R_0$) and vaccination coverage ($v_c$).
  • Discusses parameter inference using different types of outbreak data, including retrospective final outbreak details and prospective temporal or ongoing observations.
  • Highlights the importance of incorporating individual heterogeneities and heterogeneous mixing patterns into epidemic models for more accurate inference.
  • Provides an overview of general statistical methodologies used for parameter estimation within stochastic epidemic models.
  • Explores statistical models and methods specifically designed for the problem of early outbreak detection in infectious disease surveillance systems.

Methodology

The paper primarily utilizes a descriptive and review-based methodology, introducing the theoretical framework of the general stochastic epidemic model. It then outlines and discusses various statistical inference techniques used for parameter estimation (e.g., maximum likelihood, Bayesian approaches, though not explicitly named, are implied by 'statistical methods') and specific models for early outbreak detection. It emphasizes the analytical consideration of different data structures and sources (final, temporal, ongoing) and the incorporation of complexities like individual and mixing heterogeneities within these models.

Key Findings

The paper demonstrates that a range of statistical methods exist and are crucial for inferring critical epidemiological parameters like $R_0$ and $v_c$ from diverse data sources, even under complex scenarios involving individual and mixing heterogeneities. It establishes that early outbreak detection is a distinct problem addressable by specific statistical models for effective disease surveillance.

Clinical Impact

This analytical framework directly informs public health strategies by providing tools to: 1) estimate disease transmissibility ($R_0$) to gauge outbreak potential, 2) evaluate vaccine effectiveness and necessary coverage ($v_c$) for herd immunity, 3) guide resource allocation for interventions, and 4) enable rapid identification of emerging outbreaks through surveillance, facilitating timely clinical and public health responses.

Limitations

As an introductory paper, it likely provides a broad overview rather than in-depth technical details or comparative analyses of specific statistical algorithms. It may not delve into the computational challenges associated with complex stochastic models or specific software implementations. The abstract does not suggest novel methodological contributions but rather a synthesis of existing knowledge.

Future Directions

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract, as the paper serves as an introduction. However, implicitly, future research would involve developing more robust inference methods for sparse/incomplete data, better handling of complex heterogeneities, integrating diverse data streams (e.g., genomic, mobility), and enhancing real-time outbreak detection algorithms.

Abstract

In this paper we first introduce the general stochastic epidemic model for the spread of infectious diseases. Then we give methods for inferring model parameters such as the basic reproduction number $R_0$ and vaccination coverage $v_c$ assuming different types of data from an outbreak such as final outbreak details and temporal data or observations from an ongoing outbreak. Both individual heterogeneities and heterogeneous mixing are discussed. We also provide an overview of statistical methods to perform parameter estimation for stochastic epidemic models. In the last section we describe the problem of early outbreak detection in infectious disease surveillance and statistical models used for this purpose.
