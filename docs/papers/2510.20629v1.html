Equitable Survival Prediction: A Fairness-Aware Survival Modeling (FASM) Approach

arXiv ID: 2510.20629v1
Published: 2025-10-23
Authors: Mingxuan Liu, Yilin Ning, Haoyuan Wang, Chuan Hong, Matthew Engelhard, Danielle S. Bitterman, William G. La Cava, Nan Liu
Categories: cs.LG, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 1.00 / 1.00

Summary

This paper introduces Fairness-Aware Survival Modeling (FASM), an approach designed to mitigate algorithmic bias in survival prediction by addressing both intra-group and cross-group risk ranking disparities over time. Applied to SEER breast cancer prognosis data, FASM significantly enhances fairness without compromising discrimination performance, maintaining stable improvements over a 10-year horizon, particularly in the mid-term.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for ensuring that advanced AI tools in healthcare do not exacerbate existing health disparities but rather promote equitable care and accurate risk assessment for all patients, especially in critical areas like cancer prognosis. It directly contributes to building trust and ethical deployment of predictive models in medicine.

AI Health Application

The AI application is a fairness-aware survival modeling approach (FASM) designed to predict patient outcomes and prognosis (e.g., survival in breast cancer) by mitigating algorithmic bias in risk rankings. This aims to provide more equitable and accurate risk assessments, supporting clinical decision-making and advancing fairness as a core principle in clinical care.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical problem of machine learning models perpetuating or amplifying structural inequities and social biases in healthcare, specifically within survival analysis.
  • Highlights the overlooked issue of disparities in cross-group rankings (e.g., misranking high-risk Black patients below lower-risk White patients), which can reinforce biological essentialism.
  • Proposes FASM (Fairness-Aware Survival Modeling), a novel approach to mitigate algorithmic bias concerning both intra-group (within a group) and cross-group (between groups) risk rankings over time.
  • Evaluated using breast cancer prognosis data from the Surveillance, Epidemiology, and End Results (SEER) program.
  • Demonstrates that FASM substantially improves fairness performance compared to fairness-unaware models, while preserving comparable discrimination (accuracy) performance.
  • Shows that FASM maintains stable fairness over a 10-year follow-up period, with the most significant improvements observed during the mid-term.
  • Aims to enable the development of survival models that prioritize both predictive accuracy and equity as fundamental principles in clinical decision-making.

Methodology

The authors propose a Fairness-Aware Survival Modeling (FASM) approach. FASM is specifically designed to mitigate algorithmic bias by explicitly addressing disparities in both intra-group and cross-group risk rankings over time in survival analysis. The method's effectiveness was validated using real-world SEER breast cancer data, employing time-stratified evaluations to assess fairness and discrimination performance over a 10-year horizon.

Key Findings

FASM substantially improves fairness metrics in survival prediction without compromising discrimination performance, which remains comparable to fairness-unaware models. This improved fairness is robust and stable over a 10-year follow-up period, with the greatest enhancements observed during the mid-term of patient follow-up. It effectively addresses the problem of cross-group misranking.

Clinical Impact

The development of FASM can lead to more equitable and trustworthy prognostic models in clinical practice, particularly for complex diseases like cancer. This would enable clinicians to make more fair and informed decisions regarding patient risk stratification, treatment allocation, and resource planning, thereby potentially reducing health disparities and ensuring that high-risk individuals from all demographic groups receive appropriate and timely interventions.

Abstract

As machine learning models become increasingly integrated into healthcare, structural inequities and social biases embedded in clinical data can be perpetuated or even amplified by data-driven models. In survival analysis, censoring and time dynamics can further add complexity to fair model development. Additionally, algorithmic fairness approaches often overlook disparities in cross-group rankings, e.g., high-risk Black patients may be ranked below lower-risk White patients who do not experience the event of mortality. Such misranking can reinforce biological essentialism and undermine equitable care. We propose a Fairness-Aware Survival Modeling (FASM), designed to mitigate algorithmic bias regarding both intra-group and cross-group risk rankings over time. Using breast cancer prognosis as a representative case and applying FASM to SEER breast cancer data, we show that FASM substantially improves fairness while preserving discrimination performance comparable to fairness-unaware survival models. Time-stratified evaluations show that FASM maintains stable fairness over a 10-year horizon, with the greatest improvements observed during the mid-term of follow-up. Our approach enables the development of survival models that prioritize both accuracy and equity in clinical decision-making, advancing fairness as a core principle in clinical care.
