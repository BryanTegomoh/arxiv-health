Summary

This paper introduces Fairness-Aware Survival Modeling (FASM), an approach designed to mitigate algorithmic bias in survival predictions, specifically addressing disparities in both intra-group and cross-group risk rankings over time. Applied to SEER breast cancer data, FASM significantly enhances fairness while maintaining prediction accuracy, demonstrating stable improvements over a 10-year period, particularly in the mid-term.

Methodology

The paper proposes a novel Fairness-Aware Survival Modeling (FASM) approach. This method is specifically designed to address algorithmic bias by mitigating disparities in both intra-group (within a specific demographic group) and cross-group (between different demographic groups) risk rankings over time. It accounts for the unique complexities of survival analysis, such as censoring and time-varying risks. FASM was applied to real-world SEER breast cancer data for prognosis prediction, with time-stratified evaluations conducted over a 10-year horizon.

Key Findings

FASM substantially improved fairness in survival predictions for breast cancer patients, particularly in mitigating disparities in risk rankings. Crucially, these fairness gains were achieved while preserving discrimination performance comparable to traditional, fairness-unaware survival models. The model demonstrated stable fairness over a 10-year follow-up period, with the most significant improvements in fairness observed during the mid-term of patient follow-up.

Abstract

As machine learning models become increasingly integrated into healthcare, structural inequities and social biases embedded in clinical data can be perpetuated or even amplified by data-driven models. In survival analysis, censoring and time dynamics can further add complexity to fair model development. Additionally, algorithmic fairness approaches often overlook disparities in cross-group rankings, e.g., high-risk Black patients may be ranked below lower-risk White patients who do not experience the event of mortality. Such misranking can reinforce biological essentialism and undermine equitable care. We propose a Fairness-Aware Survival Modeling (FASM), designed to mitigate algorithmic bias regarding both intra-group and cross-group risk rankings over time. Using breast cancer prognosis as a representative case and applying FASM to SEER breast cancer data, we show that FASM substantially improves fairness while preserving discrimination performance comparable to fairness-unaware survival models. Time-stratified evaluations show that FASM maintains stable fairness over a 10-year horizon, with the greatest improvements observed during the mid-term of follow-up. Our approach enables the development of survival models that prioritize both accuracy and equity in clinical decision-making, advancing fairness as a core principle in clinical care.
