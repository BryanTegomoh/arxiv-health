Summary

This study investigates how state-of-the-art deterministic AI-based weather models can be extended towards probabilistic forecasting of extreme events through initial-condition perturbations. By generating 50-member ensembles for major 2022 events (Pakistan floods, China heatwave), the research demonstrates that flow-dependent perturbations significantly enhance ensemble spread realism and probabilistic skill, narrowing but not fully closing the performance gap with traditional numerical weather prediction ensembles.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and probabilistic forecasting of extreme weather events like heatwaves and floods is paramount for public health, as it enables proactive emergency response, resource allocation for medical services, and implementation of preventative health measures to mitigate mortality, injuries, and disease outbreaks.

AI Health Application

The AI application is in developing predictive models for environmental factors (extreme weather) that directly impact public health. Specifically, it enables AI-driven early warning systems crucial for public health agencies to anticipate, prepare for, and respond to health crises stemming from climate-related disasters, thereby reducing morbidity and mortality.

Key Points

  • Deterministic AI weather models (FuXi, GraphCast, SFNO) show limitations in representing uncertainty and capturing extreme events effectively.
  • The study generated 50-member ensembles for two significant August 2022 events (Pakistan floods, China heatwave) using three perturbation strategies: Gaussian noise, Hemispheric Centered Bred Vectors (HCBV), and Huge Ensembles.
  • Ensemble skill was evaluated against ERA5 reanalysis and compared with the ECMWF's IFS ENS and the probabilistic AIFSENS model, utilizing both deterministic and probabilistic metrics.
  • Flow-dependent perturbation strategies, such as Hemispheric Centered Bred Vectors, were found to produce the most realistic ensemble spread and highest probabilistic skill among the AI-based ensembles.
  • AI-based models demonstrated better predictive skill for temperature extremes compared to precipitation extremes across the tested variables.
  • While ensemble methods significantly improved AI model performance for extremes and uncertainty quantification, a performance gap with traditional numerical weather prediction (NWP) ensembles like IFS ENS persists.
  • The findings suggest that input perturbations can effectively extend deterministic AI models for probabilistic forecasting, paving the way for more reliable AI-driven early warning systems.

Methodology

The study utilized state-of-the-art deterministic AI weather models (FuXi, GraphCast, SFNO). To assess uncertainty, 50-member ensembles were generated for each model using three distinct initial-condition perturbation strategies: Gaussian noise, Hemispheric Centered Bred Vectors (HCBV), and Huge Ensembles. These ensembles were applied to forecast the Pakistan floods and China heatwave in August 2022. Performance was evaluated against ERA5 reanalysis and benchmarked against the ECMWF's IFS ENS and the probabilistic AIFSENS model using a combination of deterministic and probabilistic metrics.

Key Findings

The most significant finding is that flow-dependent perturbation strategies substantially improve the realism of ensemble spread and the probabilistic forecast skill of AI-based weather models for extreme events. AI models showed greater efficacy in capturing temperature extremes than precipitation extremes. Despite these improvements, the AI ensembles did not fully close the performance gap with established numerical weather prediction ensembles.

Clinical Impact

Enhanced predictive skill for extreme weather events directly supports the development of more reliable and timely early warning systems crucial for healthcare planning and disaster response. This can lead to more effective allocation of medical resources (e.g., emergency personnel, cooling centers, medical supplies for trauma or infectious diseases), targeted public health advisories for vulnerable populations, and ultimately, a reduction in weather-related morbidity and mortality during crises like heatwaves, floods, and storms.

Limitations

A primary limitation noted is that the AI-based ensembles, despite improvements from perturbation strategies, did not fully close the performance gap with traditional numerical weather prediction ensembles. The study focused on two specific extreme events in a particular month, which may limit the generalizability of the findings across all types of extreme events or geographical regions.

Future Directions

The authors suggest combining flow-dependent perturbations with more advanced uncertainty modeling techniques, such as generative or latent-space uncertainty modeling, to further enhance the reliability and accuracy of AI-driven early warning systems for extreme weather events.

Abstract

Accurate prediction of extreme weather events remains a major challenge for artificial intelligence based weather prediction systems. While deterministic models such as FuXi, GraphCast, and SFNO have achieved competitive forecast skill relative to numerical weather prediction, their ability to represent uncertainty and capture extremes is still limited. This study investigates how state of the art deterministic artificial intelligence based models respond to initial-condition perturbations and evaluates the resulting ensembles in forecasting extremes. Using three perturbation strategies (Gaussian noise, Hemispheric Centered Bred Vectors, and Huge Ensembles), we generate 50 member ensembles for two major events in August 2022: the Pakistan floods and the China heatwave. Ensemble skill is assessed against ERA5 and compared with IFS ENS and the probabilistic AIFSENS model using deterministic and probabilistic metrics. Results show that flow dependent perturbations produce the most realistic ensemble spread and highest probabilistic skill, narrowing but not closing the performance gap with numerical weather prediction ensembles. Across variables, artificial intelligence based weather models capture temperature extremes more effectively than precipitation. These findings demonstrate that input perturbations can extend deterministic models toward probabilistic forecasting, paving the way for approaches that combine flow dependent perturbations with generative or latent-space uncertainty modeling for reliable artificial intelligence-driven early warning systems.
