StressRoBERTa: Cross-Condition Transfer Learning from Depression, Anxiety, and PTSD to Stress Detection

arXiv ID: 2512.23813v1
Published: 2025-12-29
Authors: Amal Alqahtani, Efsun Kayi, Mona Diab
Categories: cs.CL, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

StressRoBERTa is a novel cross-condition transfer learning approach for the automatic detection of self-reported chronic stress in English tweets. By continually training a RoBERTa model on data from conditions highly comorbid with stress (depression, anxiety, PTSD), it achieved an 82% F1-score, outperforming the best prior system by 3 percentage points and demonstrating superior performance compared to general mental health models.

Medical Relevance

Chronic stress is a significant public health concern with widespread implications for mental and physical health. This research provides a sophisticated tool for early and passive detection of self-reported chronic stress from social media, which can inform public health surveillance, facilitate timely interventions, and support mental health resource allocation.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the development of StressRoBERTa, a natural language processing model, for the automatic detection of self-reported chronic stress from social media text. It employs cross-condition transfer learning from clinically related mental health disorders (depression, anxiety, PTSD) to improve stress detection, which could be used for early identification, monitoring, or population-level mental health surveillance.

Key Points

  • Introduces StressRoBERTa, a cross-condition transfer learning model specifically designed for automatic detection of self-reported chronic stress in English tweets.
  • Employs a RoBERTa base model continually trained on the Stress-SMHD corpus, consisting of 108 million words from users with self-reported diagnoses of depression, anxiety, and PTSD.
  • The model was fine-tuned on the SMM4H 2022 Task 8 dataset and achieved an F1-score of 82%, surpassing the best shared task system (79% F1) by 3 percentage points.
  • Demonstrates that focused cross-condition transfer from clinically related disorders (+1% F1 over vanilla RoBERTa) provides stronger representations for stress detection compared to general mental health training approaches.
  • The approach leverages the high comorbidity between chronic stress and conditions like depression, anxiety, and PTSD to enhance model performance.
  • Achieved an 81% F1-score on the Dreaddit dataset, confirming its transferability and effectiveness in detecting situational stress discussions in addition to clinical mental health contexts.

Methodology

The study utilized a cross-condition transfer learning paradigm. A RoBERTa language model was continually trained on the Stress-SMHD corpus, a large dataset (108M words) derived from Twitter users self-reporting diagnoses of depression, anxiety, and PTSD. This continually trained model, named StressRoBERTa, was then fine-tuned on the SMM4H 2022 Task 8 dataset for chronic stress detection. Its performance was evaluated using the F1-score metric on both the SMM4H 2022 Task 8 and the external Dreaddit dataset.

Key Findings

StressRoBERTa achieved a state-of-the-art 82% F1-score for self-reported chronic stress detection on the SMM4H 2022 Task 8, outperforming the previous best system by 3 percentage points. The focused cross-condition transfer learning from stress-related disorders yielded stronger representations, improving performance by 1% F1 over a vanilla RoBERTa model. Furthermore, the model demonstrated robust transferability, achieving an 81% F1-score on the Dreaddit dataset, indicating its efficacy across different contexts of stress discussion.

Clinical Impact

This technology offers a promising avenue for public health surveillance by enabling the passive identification of individuals or populations experiencing chronic stress via social media. Clinicians and public health practitioners could leverage such models to identify at-risk individuals who might benefit from early intervention, improve epidemiological tracking of stress prevalence, and inform resource allocation for mental health services, potentially mitigating the impact of chronic stress and its comorbid conditions.

Limitations

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract. However, potential limitations in general could include reliance on self-reported data which may lack clinical verification, generalizability to languages other than English, and the ethical considerations surrounding data privacy and potential misinterpretation of social media posts.

Future Directions

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract. Potential future directions could involve exploring multilingual stress detection, integrating multimodal data (e.g., images, videos), developing models to assess stress severity or type, or conducting longitudinal studies to track stress patterns and intervention effectiveness.

Abstract

The prevalence of chronic stress represents a significant public health concern, with social media platforms like Twitter serving as important venues for individuals to share their experiences. This paper introduces StressRoBERTa, a cross-condition transfer learning approach for automatic detection of self-reported chronic stress in English tweets. The investigation examines whether continual training on clinically related conditions (depression, anxiety, PTSD), disorders with high comorbidity with chronic stress, improves stress detection compared to general language models and broad mental health models. RoBERTa is continually trained on the Stress-SMHD corpus (108M words from users with self-reported diagnoses of depression, anxiety, and PTSD) and fine-tuned on the SMM4H 2022 Task 8 dataset. StressRoBERTa achieves 82% F1-score, outperforming the best shared task system (79% F1) by 3 percentage points. The results demonstrate that focused cross-condition transfer from stress-related disorders (+1% F1 over vanilla RoBERTa) provides stronger representations than general mental health training. Evaluation on Dreaddit (81% F1) further demonstrates transfer from clinical mental health contexts to situational stress discussions.
