Transparent Early ICU Mortality Prediction with Clinical Transformer and Per-Case Modality Attribution

arXiv ID: 2511.15847v1
Published: 2025-11-19
Authors: Alexander Bakumenko, Janine Hoelscher, Hudson Smith
Categories: cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper presents a transparent and robust multimodal deep learning ensemble for early prediction of in-hospital mortality in intensive care unit (ICU) patients. By fusing physiological time-series data and unstructured clinical notes from the first 48 hours, the system achieves improved predictive performance and crucial multilevel interpretability, addressing key barriers to clinical adoption of AI in critical care.

Medical Relevance

This research provides a powerful, transparent, and reliable tool for clinicians to identify high-risk ICU patients early, enabling proactive interventions and optimizing resource utilization. The interpretability feature is paramount for clinician trust and responsible integration of AI into high-stakes clinical decision-making, ultimately aiming to improve patient outcomes.

AI Health Application

The AI application is an early ICU mortality prediction system. It utilizes a multimodal ensemble of machine learning models (LSTM for vital signs and a finetuned ClinicalModernBERT transformer for clinical notes) to provide transparent and robust risk estimates for in-hospital mortality in intensive care patients. This system is designed to support clinicians in making timely interventions and optimizing resource allocation by identifying high-risk patients, acting as a prognostic tool and a form of clinical decision support.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical need for early, transparent, and robust prediction of in-hospital mortality in ICU patients, which is vital for timely intervention and resource allocation.
  • Proposes a lightweight, multimodal late-fusion ensemble combining physiological time-series measurements (vitals) and unstructured clinical notes from the initial 48 hours of an ICU stay.
  • The architecture consists of two modality-specific models (a bidirectional LSTM for vitals and a finetuned ClinicalModernBERT transformer for notes), whose predictions are combined by a logistic regression model.
  • Features multilevel interpretability, providing both feature attributions within each modality and direct per-case modality attributions to quantify the influence of vitals and notes on individual predictions.
  • Achieved improved discrimination on the MIMIC-III benchmark compared to the best single model (AUPRC 0.565 vs. 0.526; AUROC 0.891 vs. 0.876).
  • Demonstrates robustness through a calibrated fallback mechanism when one data modality is missing, and maintains well-calibrated predictions.
  • Emphasizes that competitive performance combined with reliable, auditable risk estimates and transparent, predictable operation are crucial for clinical use and adoption.

Methodology

The study utilizes a late-fusion multimodal ensemble architecture. It processes physiological time-series measurements (vitals) with a bidirectional Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) network and unstructured clinical notes with a finetuned ClinicalModernBERT transformer. The predictions from these two modality-specific deep learning models are then combined by a logistic regression model to produce the final mortality risk estimate. The system also incorporates a calibrated fallback mechanism for robustness when one data modality is unavailable.

Key Findings

The multimodal ensemble model significantly improved predictive performance on the MIMIC-III benchmark, yielding an AUPRC of 0.565 and an AUROC of 0.891, which are superior to the best single-modality model (AUPRC 0.526; AUROC 0.876). The system maintained well-calibrated predictions and demonstrated robustness through a calibrated fallback mechanism when a modality was missing. A key finding is its provision of multilevel interpretability, offering both feature attributions within modalities and per-case modality attributions to explain risk predictions.

Clinical Impact

This model offers a robust and transparent early warning system for ICU clinicians, facilitating the proactive identification of patients at high risk of in-hospital mortality. This enables timely and targeted clinical interventions, optimizing patient management and efficient allocation of critical resources. The interpretability features are expected to foster clinician trust and aid in shared decision-making, accelerating the adoption of AI-driven tools in critical care settings.

Abstract

Early identification of intensive care patients at risk of in-hospital mortality enables timely intervention and efficient resource allocation. Despite high predictive performance, existing machine learning approaches lack transparency and robustness, limiting clinical adoption. We present a lightweight, transparent multimodal ensemble that fuses physiological time-series measurements with unstructured clinical notes from the first 48 hours of an ICU stay. A logistic regression model combines predictions from two modality-specific models: a bidirectional LSTM for vitals and a finetuned ClinicalModernBERT transformer for notes. This traceable architecture allows for multilevel interpretability: feature attributions within each modality and direct per-case modality attributions quantifying how vitals and notes influence each decision. On the MIMIC-III benchmark, our late-fusion ensemble improves discrimination over the best single model (AUPRC 0.565 vs. 0.526; AUROC 0.891 vs. 0.876) while maintaining well-calibrated predictions. The system remains robust through a calibrated fallback when a modality is missing. These results demonstrate competitive performance with reliable, auditable risk estimates and transparent, predictable operation, which together are crucial for clinical use.
