Summary

This study introduces autoStrain, the first automated deep learning (DL) pipeline for Segmental Longitudinal Strain (SLS) estimation in Transesophageal Echocardiography (TEE), addressing the significant manual effort and expertise currently required. The pipeline compares two DL models, TeeFlow and TeeTracker, trained and evaluated on a unique synthetic TEE dataset, with TeeTracker demonstrating superior accuracy. Clinical validation on real patients confirmed the pipeline's alignment with clinical references, indicating its potential to enhance the precision and efficiency of cardiac function assessment.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to cardiology as it automates the measurement of Segmental Longitudinal Strain, a crucial prognostic indicator for Left Ventricular dysfunction and myocardial ischemia. Automating this process could lead to more efficient, precise, and standardized assessments, ultimately improving patient diagnosis and management in conditions like myocardial ischemia.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the 'autoStrain' pipeline, which uses deep learning (specifically, TeeTracker based on the CoTracker model) to automate the estimation of Segmental Longitudinal Strain (SLS) from Transesophageal Echocardiography (TEE) images. This automates a previously manual and resource-intensive process, improving the efficiency and precision of evaluating regional left ventricular dysfunction, particularly for diagnosing and managing myocardial ischemia. This makes it a medical AI application for automated cardiac diagnostic imaging analysis.

Key Points

  • **Problem Addressed**: Current SLS estimation in TEE is resource-intensive and manual, limiting its efficiency and applicability for continuous monitoring of regional LV dysfunction and myocardial ischemia.
  • **Novel Automated Pipeline**: Introduction of autoStrain, the first fully automated deep learning-based pipeline for SLS estimation in TEE, designed to streamline myocardial motion analysis.
  • **Comparative DL Models**: Evaluated two distinct deep learning approaches for motion estimation: TeeFlow (based on RAFT for dense optical flow) and TeeTracker (based on CoTracker for sparse, long-sequence point trajectories).
  • **Synthetic Data Generation**: Due to the scarcity of real ground truth motion data, a unique simulation pipeline (SIMUS) was used to generate a highly realistic synthetic TEE (synTEE) dataset of 80 patients, complete with ground truth myocardial motion, for model training and evaluation.
  • **Superior Model Performance**: TeeTracker outperformed TeeFlow in accuracy on the synTEE test dataset, achieving a mean distance error of 0.65 mm in motion estimation.
  • **Clinical Validation**: On a separate cohort of 16 real patients, the autoStrain pipeline's SLS estimation aligned well with clinical references, showing a mean difference of 1.09% (95% limits of agreement: -8.90% to 11.09%).
  • **Enhanced Ischemia Quantification**: Incorporating simulated ischemia into the synTEE training data improved the models' accuracy in quantifying abnormal deformation, a critical aspect for diagnosing myocardial ischemia.

Methodology

The study developed autoStrain, an automated deep learning pipeline for SLS estimation in TEE. It involved comparing two DL models for motion estimation: TeeFlow (RAFT-based for dense optical flow) and TeeTracker (CoTracker-based for sparse point trajectories). These models were trained and evaluated on a novel, highly realistic synthetic TEE (synTEE) dataset derived from 80 patients via a SIMUS pipeline, which provided ground truth myocardial motion. Clinical validation was subsequently performed on 16 real patients.

Key Findings

The TeeTracker model demonstrated superior accuracy in motion estimation, achieving a mean distance error of 0.65 mm on the synthetic TEE test dataset. Clinical validation showed autoStrain's SLS estimations aligned closely with clinical references, with a mean difference of 1.09% (95% limits of agreement: -8.90% to 11.09%). Furthermore, integrating simulated ischemia into the synthetic training data significantly improved the models' capability to quantify abnormal myocardial deformation.

Clinical Impact

The autoStrain pipeline holds significant clinical potential by automating and enhancing the precision and efficiency of cardiac function assessment, particularly for detecting regional LV dysfunction and diagnosing myocardial ischemia. This could reduce the manual workload for sonographers and cardiologists, enable more consistent and frequent patient monitoring, and facilitate earlier, more accurate diagnostic and therapeutic decisions in clinical settings.

Limitations

A primary limitation is the inherent difficulty in acquiring ground truth motion data from real echocardiographic sequences, necessitating the reliance on a synthetic TEE dataset for initial model training and comprehensive evaluation. While clinical validation was performed, the generalizability and robustness across a broader spectrum of real-world clinical cases and diverse patient populations require further extensive testing.

Future Directions

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract, but implied future directions include further rigorous clinical validation on larger and more diverse patient cohorts, optimizing the models for various pathological conditions, and seamlessly integrating the autoStrain pipeline into existing clinical workflows to maximize its practical utility and impact.

Abstract

Segmental longitudinal strain (SLS) of the left ventricle (LV) is an important prognostic indicator for evaluating regional LV dysfunction, in particular for diagnosing and managing myocardial ischemia. Current techniques for strain estimation require significant manual intervention and expertise, limiting their efficiency and making them too resource-intensive for monitoring purposes. This study introduces the first automated pipeline, autoStrain, for SLS estimation in transesophageal echocardiography (TEE) using deep learning (DL) methods for motion estimation. We present a comparative analysis of two DL approaches: TeeFlow, based on the RAFT optical flow model for dense frame-to-frame predictions, and TeeTracker, based on the CoTracker point trajectory model for sparse long-sequence predictions. As ground truth motion data from real echocardiographic sequences are hardly accessible, we took advantage of a unique simulation pipeline (SIMUS) to generate a highly realistic synthetic TEE (synTEE) dataset of 80 patients with ground truth myocardial motion to train and evaluate both models. Our evaluation shows that TeeTracker outperforms TeeFlow in accuracy, achieving a mean distance error in motion estimation of 0.65 mm on a synTEE test dataset. Clinical validation on 16 patients further demonstrated that SLS estimation with our autoStrain pipeline aligned with clinical references, achieving a mean difference (95\% limits of agreement) of 1.09% (-8.90% to 11.09%). Incorporation of simulated ischemia in the synTEE data improved the accuracy of the models in quantifying abnormal deformation. Our findings indicate that integrating AI-driven motion estimation with TEE can significantly enhance the precision and efficiency of cardiac function assessment in clinical settings.
