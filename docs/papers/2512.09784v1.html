Predicting Polymer Solubility in Solvents Using SMILES Strings

arXiv ID: 2512.09784v1
Published: 2025-12-10
Authors: Andrew Reinhard
Categories: cs.LG
Relevance Score: 0.70 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a deep learning framework for predicting polymer solubility (weight percent) in various solvents directly from their SMILES representations. The model, a six-hidden-layer neural network, was trained on a large dataset of 8,049 simulated polymer-solvent pairs and achieved strong agreement with actual solubility, demonstrating generalizability on unseen experimental data. This approach enables scalable and high-throughput solvent screening for diverse applications, including green chemistry and materials design.

Medical Relevance

Predicting polymer solubility is critical for developing advanced drug delivery systems, designing biocompatible medical devices, and optimizing pharmaceutical formulations. This methodology provides a high-throughput, data-driven approach to select appropriate polymers and solvents for various medical and health applications, accelerating research and development.

AI Health Application

The research develops a deep learning model using SMILES strings to predict polymer solubility. This AI application can be used for high-throughput screening and rapid prediction of suitable polymer-solvent combinations, significantly accelerating the design, development, and optimization of pharmaceutical formulations, drug delivery systems, and materials for medical devices.

Key Points

  • A deep learning framework was developed to predict polymer solubility (wt%) using SMILES strings for both polymers and solvents.
  • The model utilized a fully connected neural network with six hidden layers, trained on a large dataset of 8,049 polymer-solvent pairs derived from calibrated molecular dynamics simulations at 25¬∞C.
  • SMILES strings were transformed into a comprehensive 2,394-feature representation (molecular descriptors and fingerprints) for model input.
  • The neural network was trained using the Adam optimizer with mean squared error (MSE) loss, demonstrating strong agreement between predicted and actual solubility values.
  • Generalizability was validated on 25 unseen, experimentally measured polymer-solvent combinations from the Materials Genome Project, maintaining high accuracy.
  • The findings support the viability of SMILES-based machine learning for scalable solubility prediction and high-throughput solvent screening.
  • This methodology has significant implications for applications in green chemistry, polymer processing, materials design, and critically, pharmaceutical formulation and biocompatible materials.

Methodology

The study employed a deep learning framework. A dataset of 8,049 polymer-solvent pairs at 25¬∞C, sourced from calibrated molecular dynamics simulations, was used for training. SMILES strings of polymers and solvents were converted into a 2,394-feature representation (molecular descriptors and fingerprints). A fully connected neural network with six hidden layers was trained using the Adam optimizer and evaluated with mean squared error loss. Model generalizability was further assessed using 25 experimentally measured data points from the Materials Genome Project.

Key Findings

The deep learning model successfully predicted polymer solubility (wt%) with strong agreement to both simulated and unseen experimental data. The study demonstrated the high accuracy and generalizability of using SMILES-based machine learning for scalable and high-throughput prediction of polymer-solvent interactions, making it a viable tool for accelerating materials discovery and development.

Clinical Impact

This prediction framework can significantly accelerate the design and development of innovative polymer-based drug delivery systems (e.g., controlled-release tablets, nanoparticles, implants) by rapidly identifying optimal polymer-solvent combinations for synthesis and formulation. It can also aid in the selection of biocompatible polymers for medical devices and implants, improving patient safety and efficacy by predicting material behavior in biological environments, and streamline the formulation of active pharmaceutical ingredients (APIs) and excipients.

Limitations

The primary training dataset consisted of simulated data, although validated with experimental data. The model's predictions are currently limited to a single temperature (25¬∞C), which may not directly translate to physiological conditions (37¬∞C). The abstract does not detail the specific chemical diversity or size distribution of polymers and solvents included in the dataset, which could affect model applicability to novel chemical spaces.

Future Directions

Future research could involve expanding the dataset to include polymer-solvent pairs at physiological temperatures (e.g., 37¬∞C) and varying pH, crucial for biological applications. Exploring the prediction of other critical properties beyond solubility, such as degradation rates or biocompatibility, using similar SMILES-based approaches, could further enhance its medical utility. Integrating more diverse experimental data and applying the framework directly to specific drug formulation or medical device challenges are also promising avenues.

Abstract

Understanding and predicting polymer solubility in various solvents is critical for applications ranging from recycling to pharmaceutical formulation. This work presents a deep learning framework that predicts polymer solubility, expressed as weight percent (wt%), directly from SMILES representations of both polymers and solvents. A dataset of 8,049 polymer solvent pairs at 25 deg C was constructed from calibrated molecular dynamics simulations (Zhou et al., 2023), and molecular descriptors and fingerprints were combined into a 2,394 feature representation per sample. A fully connected neural network with six hidden layers was trained using the Adam optimizer and evaluated using mean squared error loss, achieving strong agreement between predicted and actual solubility values. Generalizability was demonstrated using experimentally measured data from the Materials Genome Project, where the model maintained high accuracy on 25 unseen polymer solvent combinations. These findings highlight the viability of SMILES based machine learning models for scalable solubility prediction and high-throughput solvent screening, supporting applications in green chemistry, polymer processing, and materials design.
