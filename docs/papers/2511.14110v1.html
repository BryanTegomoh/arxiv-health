A Patient-Independent Neonatal Seizure Prediction Model Using Reduced Montage EEG and ECG

Summary

This research proposes a novel patient-independent convolutional neural network (CNN) model for the early prediction of neonatal seizures, distinguishing between interictal and preictal states using reduced montage EEG and ECG signals. The model achieved high accuracy, sensitivity, specificity, and F1-score (all above 96%) in predicting seizures up to 30 minutes prior to onset, demonstrating strong generalization capabilities and potential for minimally supervised deployment.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and early prediction of neonatal seizures is critical for preventing short and long-term neurological impairments. This model offers a non-invasive, timely, and potentially automated method to identify impending seizures, enabling earlier intervention and potentially mitigating brain injury in vulnerable neonates.

It utilizes mel-frequency cepstral coefficient matrices extracted from multichannel EEG and ECG signals. Methodology

The study utilized a convolutional neural network (CNN) architecture. Input features were Mel-frequency cepstral coefficient (MFCC) matrices derived from multichannel EEG and ECG signals. The model was designed to be patient-independent, trained, and validated on the Helsinki neonatal EEG dataset using 10-fold cross-validation. An attention mechanism was incorporated to enhance performance, and SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) was used for model interpretability, complemented by scalp plots for seizure focus localization.

Key Findings

The model achieved high predictive performance, with 97.52% accuracy, 98.31% sensitivity, 96.39% specificity, and 97.95% F1-score for predicting neonatal seizures up to 30 minutes before onset. The inclusion of ECG data significantly improved the F1-score by 1.42%, and an attention mechanism further boosted it by 0.5%. The model demonstrated strong generalization capabilities across unseen subjects and provided explainability through SHAP and seizure focus localization via scalp plots.

Clinical Impact

This model has the potential to enable timely and reliable prediction of neonatal seizures in Neonatal Intensive Care Units (NICUs), reducing the need for constant, expert-supervised continuous video electroencephalogram (cEEG) monitoring. Early prediction allows for prompt therapeutic intervention, which could significantly reduce the duration of untreated seizure activity and mitigate subsequent brain injury, ultimately improving neurological outcomes for neonates.

Abstract

Neonates are highly susceptible to seizures, often leading to short or long-term neurological impairments. However, clinical manifestations of neonatal seizures are subtle and often lead to misdiagnoses. This increases the risk of prolonged, untreated seizure activity and subsequent brain injury. Continuous video electroencephalogram (cEEG) monitoring is the gold standard for seizure detection. However, this is an expensive evaluation that requires expertise and time. In this study, we propose a convolutional neural network-based model for early prediction of neonatal seizures by distinguishing between interictal and preictal states of the EEG. Our model is patient-independent, enabling generalization across multiple subjects, and utilizes mel-frequency cepstral coefficient matrices extracted from multichannel EEG and electrocardiogram (ECG) signals as input features. Trained and validated on the Helsinki neonatal EEG dataset with 10-fold cross-validation, the proposed model achieved an average accuracy of 97.52%, sensitivity of 98.31%, specificity of 96.39%, and F1-score of 97.95%, enabling accurate seizure prediction up to 30 minutes before onset. The inclusion of ECG alongside EEG improved the F1-score by 1.42%, while the incorporation of an attention mechanism yielded an additional 0.5% improvement. To enhance transparency, we incorporated SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) as an explainable artificial intelligence method to interpret the model and provided localization of seizure focus using scalp plots. The overall results demonstrate the model's potential for minimally supervised deployment in neonatal intensive care units, enabling timely and reliable prediction of neonatal seizures, while demonstrating strong generalization capability across unseen subjects through transfer learning.
