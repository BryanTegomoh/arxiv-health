Fitness inference tested by in silico population genetics

arXiv ID: 2510.20500v1
Published: 2025-10-23
Authors: Hong-Li Zeng, Yu-Han Huang, John Barton, Erik Aurell
Categories: q-bio.PE, physics.bio-ph
Relevance Score: 0.90 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This research investigates the feasibility of inferring fitness parameters and the order of genotype fitness from population-wide, whole-genome, time-stratified genomic data. Using *in silico* simulations of populations evolving under natural selection, mutation, and recombination, the study successfully delineates specific parameter ranges where such inference is possible and where it is not. The work provides a crucial framework for assessing when fitness inference is robustly feasible from genomic surveillance data.

Medical Relevance

This research is vital for understanding the evolutionary dynamics of human pathogens, including the development of drug resistance and vaccine escape, by establishing when their fitness landscapes can be reliably inferred from genomic surveillance data. This knowledge can inform public health interventions and therapeutic strategies.

AI Health Application

AI/machine learning models could be applied to perform the 'fitness inference' described, utilizing large-scale genomic and temporal data from pathogen populations. This would allow for the prediction of evolutionary trajectories of pathogens, identification of mutations conferring increased fitness (e.g., drug resistance or virulence), and real-time surveillance for emerging threats. Such AI applications could inform public health interventions, drug development strategies, and biosecurity measures.

Key Points

  • The core problem addressed is the inference of fitness parameters and the relative fitness order of genotypes from observed genomic data.
  • The study uses *in silico* (computer-simulated) populations that evolve under the forces of natural selection, mutation, and genetic recombination.
  • The hypothetical input data considered for inference are whole genomes from all or a representative selection of individuals, collected over time (time-stratified).
  • A key finding is the identification of specific parameter ranges where fitness inference is feasible and accurate, contrasted with ranges where it proves unreliable.
  • The research establishes a general framework for determining the conditions under which fitness inference from population-wide genomic data is practically viable.
  • The authors provide a brief survey of biological model organisms and human pathogens that possess characteristics suitable for analysis within this proposed framework.

Methodology

The study utilized *in silico* population genetics simulations to model populations evolving under various conditions of natural selection, mutation, and recombination. Within these simulations, the authors systematically tested the ability to accurately infer underlying fitness parameters and the fitness order of different genotypes from the generated genomic data, thereby delineating the conditions under which such inference is successful.

Key Findings

The primary finding is the identification of distinct parameter ranges (e.g., related to population size, mutation rates, selection coefficients, recombination rates) within which fitness parameters and genotype fitness order can be reliably inferred from genomic data. Conversely, the study also identified ranges where such inference is not feasible, providing a clear boundary for the applicability of these methods. This robust delineation forms a predictive framework for fitness inference.

Clinical Impact

By defining the feasibility landscape for fitness inference, this framework can guide the design of genomic surveillance programs for rapidly evolving pathogens (e.g., viruses like SARS-CoV-2, drug-resistant bacteria). It allows researchers and public health officials to assess the likelihood of accurately predicting pathogen evolution, anticipating the emergence of new variants with altered transmissibility, virulence, or drug resistance, thereby enabling more timely and effective clinical interventions and public health responses.

Limitations

The primary limitation noted is that fitness inference is only feasible within specific parameter ranges, implying that current methods are not universally applicable. The study's reliance on *in silico* simulations means its findings need validation with real-world genomic data from biological systems, though the paper surveys potential candidates for this application.

Future Directions

The paper suggests future research involves applying the developed framework to real-world genomic datasets from the surveyed biological model organisms and human pathogens. This would serve to validate the *in silico* findings and utilize the framework's predictive power for understanding actual evolutionary processes in medical contexts.

Abstract

We consider populations evolving according to natural selection, mutation, and recombination, and assume that the genomes of all or a representative selection of individuals are known. We pose the problem if it is possible to infer fitness parameters and genotype fitness order from such data. We tested this hypothesis in simulated populations. We delineate parameter ranges where this is possible and other ranges where it is not.Our work provides a framework for determining when fitness inference is feasible from population-wide, whole-genome, time-stratified data and highlights settings where it is not. We give a brief survey of biological model organisms and human pathogens that fit into this framework.
