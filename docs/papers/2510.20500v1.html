Fitness inference tested by in silico population genetics

arXiv ID: 2510.20500v1
Published: 2025-10-23
Authors: Hong-Li Zeng, Yu-Han Huang, John Barton, Erik Aurell
Categories: q-bio.PE, physics.bio-ph
Relevance Score: 0.75 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This research investigates the feasibility of inferring fitness parameters and genotype fitness order from genomic data in populations evolving under natural selection, mutation, and recombination. Through *in silico* simulations, the authors delineate specific parameter ranges where such inference is possible and where it is not, thereby providing a crucial framework for assessing the utility of population-wide, whole-genome, time-stratified data for fitness inference. The study also identifies relevant biological systems, including human pathogens, where this framework can be applied.

Medical Relevance

Understanding the fitness landscape of pathogens is paramount for predicting their evolutionary trajectories, including the emergence of drug resistance, increased virulence, and vaccine escape. This framework provides a critical tool for public health and infectious disease researchers to determine when genomic surveillance data can reliably inform these predictions, guiding effective disease control strategies.

AI Health Application

The paper provides a foundational computational framework for fitness inference from complex genomic and time-series data. While not explicitly an AI application, these advanced inference methods are directly applicable and could be integrated into AI/ML systems for real-time pathogen surveillance, predictive modeling of disease evolution (e.g., drug resistance, transmissibility), and informing data-driven public health and biosecurity strategies.

Key Points

  • The study addresses the core problem of inferring fitness parameters and the relative fitness order of genotypes from observed genomic data in evolving populations.
  • Methodology relies on *in silico* simulations of populations subjected to natural selection, mutation, and recombination.
  • A key finding is the precise delineation of specific parameter ranges (e.g., population size, mutation rates, selection coefficients, data resolution) under which reliable fitness inference is achievable.
  • Conversely, the research also identifies conditions and parameter ranges where accurate fitness inference from genomic data is not possible or reliable.
  • The work establishes a comprehensive framework for determining the practical feasibility of fitness inference, particularly when utilizing population-wide, whole-genome, and time-stratified genomic datasets.
  • The authors surveyed a variety of biological model organisms and human pathogens that fit the data criteria required by their framework, indicating direct applicability to real-world biological and medical challenges.
  • The findings highlight the critical importance of data type and collection strategy (e.g., time-stratified whole genomes) for successful evolutionary parameter inference.

Methodology

The study utilized *in silico* (computer-simulated) populations. These simulations modeled evolution under key genetic forces: natural selection, mutation, and recombination. The researchers assumed the availability of whole-genome or representative genomic sequences, potentially sampled across different time points (time-stratified), from these simulated populations to test and delineate the conditions under which fitness parameters and genotype fitness order could be accurately inferred.

Key Findings

The primary finding is the establishment of precise parameter ranges (e.g., related to population dynamics, evolutionary rates, and data density) where fitness parameters and the relative fitness of genotypes can be reliably inferred from genomic data. Equally important, the study identified scenarios and parameter settings where such inference is unreliable, providing a practical guide for assessing the feasibility of fitness inference given specific genomic datasets and biological contexts.

Clinical Impact

This framework offers a critical guideline for public health and clinical microbiology laboratories engaged in pathogen genomic surveillance. It enables a more informed decision-making process regarding whether collected genomic data is sufficient to infer pathogen fitness, predict the evolution of drug resistance, or anticipate vaccine escape. This can optimize resource allocation for genomic sequencing, enhance the predictive power of evolutionary models for outbreak management, and inform the development of more robust antimicrobial or antiviral strategies.

Limitations

The study itself identifies inherent limitations to the process of fitness inference, delineating specific parameter ranges where it is not possible or reliable. While the abstract does not detail specific technical limitations of the simulation model, the *in silico* nature implies that the model's assumptions might not fully capture all complexities of real-world biological systems, potentially introducing further challenges in practical applications.

Future Directions

A clear future direction, as indicated by the authors' brief survey, is the application of this newly developed framework to real-world biological model organisms and, critically, human pathogens. This would involve testing the inference capabilities with actual population-wide, whole-genome, time-stratified genomic datasets from clinical and epidemiological settings.

Abstract

We consider populations evolving according to natural selection, mutation, and recombination, and assume that the genomes of all or a representative selection of individuals are known. We pose the problem if it is possible to infer fitness parameters and genotype fitness order from such data. We tested this hypothesis in simulated populations. We delineate parameter ranges where this is possible and other ranges where it is not.Our work provides a framework for determining when fitness inference is feasible from population-wide, whole-genome, time-stratified data and highlights settings where it is not. We give a brief survey of biological model organisms and human pathogens that fit into this framework.
