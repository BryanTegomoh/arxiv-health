Fitness inference tested by in silico population genetics

arXiv ID: 2510.20500v1
Published: 2025-10-23
Authors: Hong-Li Zeng, Yu-Han Huang, John Barton, Erik Aurell
Categories: q-bio.PE, physics.bio-ph
Relevance Score: 0.85 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper investigates the feasibility of inferring fitness parameters and genotype fitness order from time-stratified, whole-genome sequencing data in populations evolving under natural selection, mutation, and recombination. Through *in silico* simulations, the authors delineated specific parameter ranges where accurate fitness inference is possible and others where it is not. The work establishes a framework for determining the reliability of fitness inference from population-wide genomic data, particularly relevant for understanding the evolution of human pathogens.

Medical Relevance

Understanding the fitness landscape of pathogens, including the fitness costs or benefits associated with specific genotypes (e.g., drug resistance mutations), is crucial for predicting evolutionary trajectories and informing public health interventions. This research provides a critical tool to determine when genomic surveillance data can reliably be used to infer these fitness dynamics, thereby enhancing our ability to combat infectious diseases.

AI Health Application

The framework developed for fitness inference from large-scale genomic data provides foundational insights that could inform and guide the development of AI and machine learning applications in health. Specifically, AI/ML models could leverage this framework to predict pathogen evolution (e.g., viral mutations, bacterial resistance mechanisms), forecast epidemic dynamics, identify advantageous pathogen strains, or optimize drug and vaccine strategies based on evolutionary insights derived from genomic surveillance data.

Key Points

  • The study addresses the fundamental question of whether fitness parameters and genotype fitness order can be reliably inferred from population-wide, whole-genome, time-stratified genomic data.
  • Methodology involved testing this hypothesis in *in silico* (simulated) populations evolving under the influence of natural selection, mutation, and recombination.
  • A key finding is the successful delineation of specific parameter ranges (e.g., population size, mutation rates, selection coefficients) where accurate fitness inference is robustly achievable.
  • Conversely, the research also identified parameter ranges where such fitness inference is not feasible, highlighting the inherent limitations of the approach under certain evolutionary conditions.
  • The work provides a practical framework to guide researchers in assessing when fitness inference is a viable strategy for analyzing real-world genomic datasets.
  • The framework underscores the necessity of specific data characteristics—population-wide, whole-genome, and time-stratified—for attempting fitness inference.
  • The authors briefly survey relevant biological model organisms and human pathogens whose evolutionary dynamics and available data structures are suitable for this inference framework.

Methodology

The study utilized *in silico* population genetics simulations. Populations were modeled to evolve under natural selection, mutation, and recombination. The researchers then attempted to infer fitness parameters and genotype fitness order from the simulated genomic data, thereby testing the feasibility and robustness of various inference methods across different evolutionary parameter spaces.

Key Findings

The primary finding is that fitness inference from population-wide, whole-genome, time-stratified data is indeed possible, but its accuracy and reliability are critically dependent on the underlying population genetic parameters. The study successfully mapped specific parameter ranges where such inference yields accurate results and, importantly, identified ranges where it does not, providing clear boundaries for the method's applicability.

Clinical Impact

This research can directly impact clinical and public health strategies by guiding the interpretation of pathogen genomic surveillance data. It can help determine if observed genetic changes in pathogens (e.g., during an outbreak or spread of drug resistance) are indicative of fitness advantages, thus enabling more informed decisions on treatment protocols, infection control, and vaccine strain selection. It also helps optimize genomic data collection by clarifying what data types are essential for meaningful evolutionary insights.

Limitations

The study's main limitation, as explicitly highlighted, is that fitness inference is only feasible and reliable within specific, delineated parameter ranges of the evolutionary process. Outside these ranges, the methods for inferring fitness become unreliable, implying that not all real-world genomic datasets will be amenable to accurate fitness estimation using this framework.

Future Directions

The abstract points to future application by mentioning a survey of biological model organisms and human pathogens that fit into this framework. This implies a direction towards validating and applying this theoretical framework to real-world genomic datasets from clinically relevant pathogens, moving from *in silico* testing to practical implementation in genomic surveillance and evolutionary epidemiology.

Abstract

We consider populations evolving according to natural selection, mutation, and recombination, and assume that the genomes of all or a representative selection of individuals are known. We pose the problem if it is possible to infer fitness parameters and genotype fitness order from such data. We tested this hypothesis in simulated populations. We delineate parameter ranges where this is possible and other ranges where it is not.Our work provides a framework for determining when fitness inference is feasible from population-wide, whole-genome, time-stratified data and highlights settings where it is not. We give a brief survey of biological model organisms and human pathogens that fit into this framework.
