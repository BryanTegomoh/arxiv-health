LiMT: A Multi-task Liver Image Benchmark Dataset

Summary

This paper introduces LiMT, a novel multi-task liver image benchmark dataset designed to overcome the limitations of existing single-task datasets in computer-aided diagnosis (CAD) of liver conditions. LiMT comprises arterial phase-enhanced CT scans from 150 cases, enabling research in liver and tumor segmentation, multi-label lesion classification, and lesion detection simultaneously. The dataset aims to facilitate the development of more robust CAD technologies by allowing exploration of task correlations and mitigating data heterogeneity challenges.

Medical Relevance

This dataset is highly relevant for advancing computer-aided diagnosis in hepatology by providing a comprehensive, multi-task resource for AI model training and evaluation. Improved CAD systems developed using LiMT can lead to more accurate and timely detection, classification, and quantification of liver lesions, thereby aiding clinicians in treatment planning and patient management.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the development of advanced Computer-aided diagnosis (CAD) systems for liver imaging. These systems, powered by AI models trained on datasets like LiMT, can perform automated segmentation of the liver and tumors, classify different types of liver lesions, and detect abnormalities from CT scans. This assists clinicians in improving diagnostic accuracy, expediting treatment planning for liver diseases and cancers, and potentially monitoring disease progression.

Key Points

  • The LiMT dataset addresses the constraint of existing single-task medical imaging datasets by providing a multi-task benchmark for liver CAD.
  • It supports three primary computer vision tasks: liver and tumor segmentation, multi-label lesion classification, and lesion detection.
  • The dataset consists of CT volumes from 150 distinct cases, all acquired during the arterial phase-enhanced imaging.
  • It encompasses four types of liver diseases in addition to normal cases, offering a diverse range of pathologies.
  • All images are meticulously annotated and calibrated by experienced clinicians, ensuring high data quality and clinical relevance.
  • LiMT is designed to enable researchers to explore the correlations between different liver imaging tasks and to bypass issues of heterogeneity between task-specific datasets during model training.
  • The dataset is publicly available and includes baseline experimental results along with a review of existing liver-related datasets and methods.

Methodology

The methodology involved constructing a new multi-task liver image benchmark dataset (LiMT) by collecting arterial phase-enhanced computed tomography (CT) volumes from 150 different clinical cases. These volumes were then carefully annotated and calibrated by experienced clinicians to support simultaneous tasks: liver and tumor segmentation, multi-label lesion classification, and lesion detection. The paper also provides baseline experimental results using this newly created dataset.

Key Findings

The primary finding is the successful creation and release of the LiMT dataset, a comprehensive multi-task benchmark designed to address the limitations of single-task datasets in liver computer-aided diagnosis. This dataset uniquely integrates annotations for liver and tumor segmentation, multi-label lesion classification, and lesion detection across 150 arterial phase-enhanced CT volumes with diverse liver conditions, offering a valuable resource for future research.

Clinical Impact

The availability of the LiMT dataset has the potential to significantly enhance the development of advanced AI models for comprehensive liver lesion assessment. This could lead to earlier, more accurate, and integrated diagnosis and characterization of liver diseases, ultimately assisting clinicians in making better-informed treatment decisions and improving patient outcomes through timely intervention.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state any limitations or caveats regarding the LiMT dataset itself or the methodology used to create it. It primarily focuses on overcoming the limitations of existing single-task datasets.

Future Directions

The LiMT dataset is presented as an exploratory solution to investigate the correlation between different liver imaging tasks and is expected to become a valuable resource for the medical imaging research community. Future research will likely focus on leveraging this dataset to develop more integrated and robust multi-task computer-aided diagnosis systems and explore the interplay between segmentation, classification, and detection tasks.

Abstract

Computer-aided diagnosis (CAD) technology can assist clinicians in evaluating liver lesions and intervening with treatment in time. Although CAD technology has advanced in recent years, the application scope of existing datasets remains relatively limited, typically supporting only single tasks, which has somewhat constrained the development of CAD technology. To address the above limitation, in this paper, we construct a multi-task liver dataset (LiMT) used for liver and tumor segmentation, multi-label lesion classification, and lesion detection based on arterial phase-enhanced computed tomography (CT), potentially providing an exploratory solution that is able to explore the correlation between tasks and does not need to worry about the heterogeneity between task-specific datasets during training. The dataset includes CT volumes from 150 different cases, comprising four types of liver diseases as well as normal cases. Each volume has been carefully annotated and calibrated by experienced clinicians. This public multi-task dataset may become a valuable resource for the medical imaging research community in the future. In addition, this paper not only provides relevant baseline experimental results but also reviews existing datasets and methods related to liver-related tasks. Our dataset is available at https://drive.google.com/drive/folders/1l9HRK13uaOQTNShf5pwgSz3OTanWjkag?usp=sharing.
