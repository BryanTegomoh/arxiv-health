Tackling a Challenging Corpus for Early Detection of Gambling Disorder: UNSL at MentalRiskES 2025

Summary

This paper details UNSL's participation in MentalRiskES 2025, Task 1, focusing on Early Risk Detection (ERD) of gambling disorder from social media. Utilizing a CPI+DMC approach with SS3, BERT, and SBERT models combined with historical user analysis, two of their proposals achieved the top two positions in the challenge. Despite this success, the study highlighted challenges in clearly distinguishing between high and low-risk users, underscoring the need for improved data interpretation and more transparent ERD systems.

Medical Relevance

This research is medically relevant as it provides a scalable, early identification method for gambling disorder, a serious behavioral addiction. Timely detection can enable proactive interventions, potentially mitigating severe physical, psychological, and social consequences for at-risk individuals.

AI Health Application

The AI application involves using natural language processing models (BERT, SBERT) to analyze social media activity for the early detection and classification of individuals at high or low risk of developing a gambling disorder, thereby serving as an early warning system for mental health intervention.

Methodology

The methodology involved developing three distinct methods based on a CPI+DMC approach. Textual content was processed using SS3, BERT (enhanced with an extended vocabulary), and SBERT models. These models served as components for generating representations or classifications. Subsequently, decision policies were applied, leveraging historical user analysis to make the final high-risk or low-risk classification for gambling disorder. The evaluation considered both predictive effectiveness and decision-making speed.

Key Findings

Two of the proposed methods achieved the top two positions in the MentalRiskES 2025 challenge's official results, demonstrating notable performance in decision metrics despite the complexity of the corpus. A critical finding was the inherent difficulty in consistently distinguishing between high and low-risk users, suggesting that subtle cues may be challenging to identify or that current data representations lack sufficient discriminative power.

Clinical Impact

The potential clinical impact is significant, offering a scalable, non-invasive tool for early screening and risk stratification of individuals susceptible to gambling disorder based on their online activity. This could enable clinicians and public health initiatives to implement early interventions, provide targeted support, and refer individuals to appropriate treatment programs much earlier than traditional diagnostic methods, thereby improving patient outcomes and reducing the burden of advanced addiction.

Limitations

The primary limitation identified was the challenge in distinguishing effectively between high and low-risk users, despite the overall strong performance. This suggests that while the models performed well relative to other challenge participants, there remains an inherent difficulty in capturing the subtle nuances required for clear separation of risk levels, possibly due to data quality or the nature of early risk indicators themselves.

Future Directions

Future research should prioritize exploring advanced strategies to improve data interpretation and quality, especially concerning the nuanced indicators of gambling disorder risk. There is also a strong emphasis on developing more transparent and reliable Early Risk Detection (ERD) systems for mental disorders, potentially through explainable AI techniques or robust uncertainty quantification.

Abstract

Gambling disorder is a complex behavioral addiction that is challenging to understand and address, with severe physical, psychological, and social consequences. Early Risk Detection (ERD) on the Web has become a key task in the scientific community for identifying early signs of mental health behaviors based on social media activity. This work presents our participation in the MentalRiskES 2025 challenge, specifically in Task 1, aimed at classifying users at high or low risk of developing a gambling-related disorder. We proposed three methods based on a CPI+DMC approach, addressing predictive effectiveness and decision-making speed as independent objectives. The components were implemented using the SS3, BERT with extended vocabulary, and SBERT models, followed by decision policies based on historical user analysis. Although it was a challenging corpus, two of our proposals achieved the top two positions in the official results, performing notably in decision metrics. Further analysis revealed some difficulty in distinguishing between users at high and low risk, reinforcing the need to explore strategies to improve data interpretation and quality, and to promote more transparent and reliable ERD systems for mental disorders.
