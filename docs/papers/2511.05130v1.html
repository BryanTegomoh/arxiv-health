Modelling variability of the immunity build-up and waning following RNA-based vaccination

arXiv ID: 2511.05130v1
Published: 2025-11-07
Authors: Juan Magalang, Tyll Krueger, Joerg Galle
Categories: q-bio.PE, q-bio.CB
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00

Summary

This paper introduces a mathematical model to explain the fast and variable waning immunity observed after RNA-based vaccination, particularly for SARS-CoV-2. It posits that an antibody-mediated negative feedback loop limits long-lasting immunity, and its computational simulations reveal that individual immune variability drives a broad distribution of protection times, while also suggesting an optimal 5-week interval for a second vaccine dose.

Medical Relevance

Understanding the mechanisms behind variable and rapidly waning immunity is critical for optimizing future RNA vaccine design and deployment strategies. This model provides a quantitative framework to predict protection durations and inform optimal dosing schedules for both existing and future pathogens.

AI Health Application

The paper employs mathematical modeling and computational simulation to predict outcomes such as vaccine protection times and optimal dosing schedules. While not a paper on novel AI algorithm development, this falls under computational biology and predictive analytics in health. These computational models provide insights that can inform or be integrated into AI applications for personalized vaccination strategies, public health forecasting, and scenario planning for disease outbreaks, effectively using quantitative methods to derive actionable medical and health-related recommendations.

Key Points

  • A mathematical model for RNA-based vaccination kinetics is developed, focusing on the build-up and waning of immunity.
  • The model incorporates germinal center reactions, B-cell differentiation into memory B-cells and plasma cells, and a key negative feedback loop where newly synthesized antibodies shut down further immune cell differentiation.
  • This negative feedback mechanism is proposed to be responsible for limiting long-lasting immunity and causing fast waning.
  • Computational simulations, informed by detailed SARS-CoV-2 data, analyze the protection conferred by single and double vaccine doses.
  • Individual variability in immune response (e.g., immune cell densities) leads to a broad, log-normal-like distribution of vaccine-induced protection times, peaking around 100 days.
  • Protection durations decrease significantly for virus variants with mutated antibody binding sites or increased replication rates.
  • The simulations suggest an optimal timing for a second vaccine dose approximately 5 weeks after the first, which aligns with observations from clinical trials.

Methodology

The study utilized a mathematical model to describe the kinetics of the immune response to RNA-based vaccination. This model incorporates specific biological processes such as antigen presentation, germinal center reactions, B-cell differentiation into memory B-cells and plasma cells, and an antibody-mediated negative feedback mechanism. Computational simulations were then performed using detailed SARS-CoV-2 infection and vaccination data to evaluate protection times and optimal dosing strategies.

Key Findings

The model identifies an antibody-driven negative feedback loop as a primary mechanism limiting long-lasting immunity and causing fast waning. Simulations show that inter-individual variability in immune parameters leads to a broad, log-normal-like distribution of vaccine-induced protection times, centered around 100 days. Furthermore, protection times are diminished by viral variants possessing mutated antibody binding sites or higher replication rates. Crucially, the model suggests an optimal interval of approximately 5 weeks between the first and second vaccine doses.

Clinical Impact

This research offers a mechanistic explanation for the observed rapid waning of immunity following RNA vaccination, which can guide the development of next-generation vaccines. It provides evidence-based guidance for vaccine scheduling, specifically suggesting an optimal 5-week interval for a second dose, which is congruent with clinical trial data. The findings emphasize the importance of considering individual immune heterogeneity and viral evolution when formulating vaccination strategies and public health policies.

Limitations

Abstract

RNA-based vaccination has been broadly applied in the COVID pandemic. A characteristic of the immunization was fast waning immunity. However, the time scale of this process varied considerable for virus subtypes and among individuals. Understanding the origin of this variability is crucial in order to improve future vaccination strategies. Here, we introduce a mathematical model of RNA-based vaccination and the kinetics of the induced immune response. In the model, antigens produced following vaccination rise an immune response leading to germinal center reactions and accordingly B-cell differentiation into memory B-cells and plasma cells. In a negative feedback loop, the antibodies synthesized by newly specified plasma cells shut down the germinal center reaction as well as antigen-induced differentiation of memory B-cell into plasma cells. This limits the build-up of long-lasting immunity and thus is accompanied by fast waning immunity. The detailed data available on infection with and vaccination against SARS-CoV-2 enabled computational simulation of essential processes of the immune response. By simulation, we analyzed to which extent a single or double dose vaccination provides protection against infection. We find that variability of the immune response in individuals, originating e.g. in different immune cell densities, results in a broad log-normal-like distribution of the vaccine-induced protection times that peaks around 100 days. Protection times decrease for virus variants with mutated antibody binding sites or increased replication rates. Independent of these virus specifics, our simulations suggest optimal timing of a second dose about 5 weeks after the first in agreement with clinical trials.
