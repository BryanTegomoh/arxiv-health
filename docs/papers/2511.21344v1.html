Summary

This paper introduces Spatiotemporal Emission Reconstruction from Prompt-Gamma Timing (SER-PGT) as a promising method to monitor proton therapy in real-time, providing crucial insights into particle range and stopping power. Initial experimental results, using a 226.9 MeV proton beam on a homogeneous phantom at sub-clinical intensity, demonstrate the feasibility of SER-PGT. The method achieved an 8% error in stopping power estimation and successfully identified range shifts with a 3 mm standard deviation.

Reducing uncertainties in proton range and stopping power allows for more accurate and adaptive treatment planning, leading to optimized tumor control and minimized damage to surrounding healthy tissues.

Methodology

The study employed a 226.9 MeV synchrotron-proton beam at a sub-clinical intensity (2 - 4 x 10^7 pps) directed at a homogeneous phantom. The SER-PGT method utilized a multi-detector setup: a segmented Low Gain Avalanche Diode (LGAD) for proton detection and Lanthanum Bromide (LaBr3)-based crystals for prompt gamma photon detection. Data from these detectors were processed to reconstruct spatiotemporal emission profiles, allowing for the determination of proton range and stopping power.

Key Findings

The estimated stopping power profile achieved an 8% ¬± 3% average error compared to NIST PSTAR values and a 2% ¬± 2% deviation relative to water at 100 MeV. Range assessment in a phantom with a 4 cm air-gap successfully identified the expected range shift with a 3 mm standard deviation, demonstrating the method's capability to recover both range and stopping power information.

Clinical Impact

The ability of SER-PGT to provide real-time, in-situ monitoring of proton range and stopping power directly addresses a major limitation in current proton therapy. This breakthrough can enable truly adaptive proton therapy, allowing clinicians to dynamically adjust treatment plans during therapy to account for patient anatomical changes or setup errors, thereby maximizing dose conformity to the tumor and significantly improving treatment outcomes and patient safety.

Limitations

The experiments were conducted at a sub-clinical proton beam intensity and utilized a homogeneous phantom. Further validation is required at clinically relevant intensities and with more complex, heterogeneous phantoms or in-vivo conditions to fully assess its clinical applicability.

Future Directions

Future research should focus on validating SER-PGT at clinical proton beam intensities and applying the methodology to heterogeneous phantoms, and eventually in-vivo. Further advancements in detector technology and reconstruction algorithms will be essential to improve accuracy, spatial resolution, and temporal performance for integration into clinical adaptive proton therapy workflows.

Abstract

Proton therapy's full potential is limited by uncertainties that prevent optimal dose distribution. Monitoring techniques can reduce these uncertainties and enable adaptive treatment planning. Spatiotemporal Emission Reconstruction from Prompt-Gamma Timing (SER-PGT) is a promising method that provides insights into both particle range and stopping power, whose calculation would normally require knowledge about patient tissue properties that cannot be directly measured. We present the first experimental results using a 226.9 MeV synchrotron-proton beam impinging on a homogeneous phantom at a sub-clinical intensity (2 - 4 x 10^7 pps). SER-PGT uses data from a multi-detector setup: a thin and segmented Low Gain Avalanche Diode for proton detection and Lanthanum Bromide-based crystals for photon detection. The estimated stopping power profile showed an 8% +- 3% average error compared to NIST PSTAR values, and 2% +- 2% deviation relative to water at 100 MeV. Range assessment in a phantom with a 4 cm air-gap successfully identified the range shift with a 3 mm standard deviation. These results demonstrate the feasibility of using SER-PGT to recover both range and stopping power information through particle kinematics and PGT measurements.
