Blind Adaptive Local Denoising for CEST Imaging

Summary

This paper introduces Blind Adaptive Local Denoising (BALD), a novel method designed to overcome the challenges of spatially varying and heteroscedastic noise inherent in Chemical Exchange Saturation Transfer (CEST) MRI data. BALD adaptively stabilizes noise distributions and employs a two-stage denoising process to accurately disentangle molecular signals. Validated extensively, BALD consistently outperforms existing CEST denoisers, significantly improving the accuracy of quantitative contrast mapping and downstream tasks like cancer detection, thereby accelerating CEST's clinical translation.

Medical Relevance

CEST MRI offers molecular-level visualization of metabolites, crucial for early disease detection and monitoring. By accurately denoising CEST data, BALD enables more reliable quantitative contrast mapping, directly enhancing the diagnostic and prognostic capabilities of this advanced imaging technique for various medical conditions.

AI Health Application

While BALD itself is an advanced image processing and signal processing technique rather than a direct AI model for diagnosis, it plays a critical role as an enabling technology for medical AI applications. By providing blind adaptive local denoising for CEST MRI data, it significantly improves the quality and accuracy of input data. High-quality, denoised medical images are essential for training robust and accurate AI models for tasks like cancer detection, quantitative molecular mapping, and disease prognostication, which are explicitly mentioned as downstream tasks improved by this method.

Key Points

  • CEST MRI's clinical translation is hampered by spatially varying and heteroscedastic noise, which compromises quantitative contrast mapping.
  • Traditional denoising methods are inadequate for CEST data's complex noise structure and often distort crucial underlying information.
  • BALD exploits the self-similar nature of CEST data to derive an adaptive variance-stabilizing transform, equalizing noise distributions without prior knowledge of noise characteristics.
  • The method employs a two-stage denoising process on a linear transformation of data (specifically, local SVD) to effectively separate molecular signals from noise while preventing spatial and spectral artifacts.
  • Extensive validation experiments on multiple phantoms and *in vivo* CEST scans demonstrate BALD's superior performance.
  • BALD consistently outperforms state-of-the-art CEST denoisers in both denoising metrics and critical downstream tasks, including molecular concentration maps estimation and cancer detection.
  • The improved accuracy in quantitative mapping provided by BALD is crucial for enhancing the reliability and clinical utility of CEST imaging.

Methodology

The BALD method first leverages the self-similar nature of CEST data to develop an adaptive variance-stabilizing transform, which homogenizes noise distributions across CEST pixels without requiring prior knowledge of noise characteristics. It then applies a two-stage denoising process to a linear transformation of the data. This linear transform is implemented as a local Singular Value Decomposition (SVD), which is crucial for disentangling molecular signals from noise while actively preventing the introduction of spatial and spectral denoising artifacts.

Key Findings

BALD consistently outperformed state-of-the-art CEST denoisers across extensive validation experiments on phantoms and *in vivo* scans. Its superior performance was observed not only in general denoising metrics but, more critically, in enhancing the accuracy of downstream applications such as molecular concentration map estimation and cancer detection, directly addressing critical challenges in CEST imaging's clinical utility.

Clinical Impact

By significantly improving the accuracy of quantitative contrast mapping in CEST MRI, BALD enhances the reliability of molecular-level visualization, which is vital for clinical decision-making. This advancement can lead to more precise early diagnosis of diseases like cancer, better assessment of treatment response, and ultimately accelerate the translation of advanced CEST imaging protocols into routine clinical practice, benefiting patient care.

Abstract

Chemical Exchange Saturation Transfer (CEST) MRI enables molecular-level visualization of low-concentration metabolites by leveraging proton exchange dynamics. However, its clinical translation is hindered by inherent challenges: spatially varying noise arising from hardware limitations, and complex imaging protocols introduce heteroscedasticity in CEST data, perturbing the accuracy of quantitative contrast mapping such as amide proton transfer (APT) imaging. Traditional denoising methods are not designed for this complex noise and often alter the underlying information that is critical for biomedical analysis. To overcome these limitations, we propose a new Blind Adaptive Local Denoising (BALD) method. BALD exploits the self-similar nature of CEST data to derive an adaptive variance-stabilizing transform that equalizes the noise distributions across CEST pixels without prior knowledge of noise characteristics. Then, BALD performs two-stage denoising on a linear transformation of data to disentangle molecular signals from noise. A local SVD decomposition is used as a linear transform to prevent spatial and spectral denoising artifacts. We conducted extensive validation experiments on multiple phantoms and \textit{in vivo} CEST scans. In these experiments, BALD consistently outperformed state-of-the-art CEST denoisers in both denoising metrics and downstream tasks such as molecular concentration maps estimation and cancer detection.
