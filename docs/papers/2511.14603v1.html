A Method for Characterizing Disease Progression from Acute Kidney Injury to Chronic Kidney Disease

arXiv ID: 2511.14603v1
Published: 2025-11-18
Authors: Yilu Fang, Jordan G. Nestor, Casey N. Ta, Jerard Z. Kneifati-Hayek, Chunhua Weng

Summary

This study developed a data-driven method using electronic health record (EHR) data to characterize the progression from acute kidney injury (AKI) to chronic kidney disease (CKD). By identifying fifteen distinct post-AKI clinical states and their varying CKD risks, the research offers a novel approach to pinpoint high-risk AKI patients more precisely. This paves the way for targeted early interventions and the development of intelligent decision-support tools in clinical settings.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant for improving patient outcomes by enabling earlier and more precise identification of AKI patients at elevated risk of developing CKD. Such stratification allows for personalized monitoring, preventative interventions, and resource allocation, potentially slowing or preventing CKD progression.

AI Health Application

The paper describes the development of a data-driven approach using AI/ML techniques (clustering, multi-state modeling, survival analysis) to characterize disease progression from AKI to CKD. This forms the foundation for AI-driven decision-support tools designed to identify high-risk AKI patients, enable early CKD detection, and guide timely interventions in clinical practice. It aims to improve patient outcomes by personalizing risk assessment and informing therapeutic strategies.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical challenge of identifying AKI patients at highest risk of progressing to CKD using real-world EHR data.
  • Employs a multi-stage methodology: clustering patient vectors from longitudinal medical codes and creatinine to define clinical states, multi-state modeling for transition probabilities and CKD progression, and survival analysis for risk factor identification.
  • Analyzed a large cohort of 20,699 patients with AKI at admission, with 17% (3,491 patients) subsequently developing CKD.
  • Identified fifteen distinct post-AKI clinical states, each characterized by different probabilities of CKD development.
  • Found that most patients (75%, n=15,607) followed relatively stable trajectories, remaining in a single state or making only one transition during the study period.
  • Uncovered both established (e.g., AKI severity, diabetes, hypertension, heart failure, liver disease) and novel CKD risk factors, importantly noting their impact varied across the identified clinical states.
  • Demonstrates a robust data-driven framework that can support the development of precision decision-support tools for early CKD detection and targeted intervention strategies in AKI patients.

Methodology

The study utilized electronic health record (EHR) data, specifically longitudinal medical codes and creatinine measurements, to create patient vectors. Post-AKI clinical states were identified by clustering these vectors. Multi-state modeling was then employed to estimate transition probabilities between these states and to model progression to CKD. Finally, survival analysis was used to identify CKD risk factors within the distinct AKI subpopulations, assessing how their impact varied across the identified clinical states.

Clinical Impact

This research has the potential to significantly impact clinical practice by providing a more granular understanding of AKI-to-CKD progression. It enables the development of advanced clinical decision-support tools that can dynamically identify high-risk AKI patients in real-time. This precision risk stratification will facilitate earlier, targeted interventions, personalized management strategies, and potentially improve long-term renal outcomes by preventing or delaying the onset of CKD.

Abstract

Patients with acute kidney injury (AKI) are at high risk of developing chronic kidney disease (CKD), but identifying those at greatest risk remains challenging. We used electronic health record (EHR) data to dynamically track AKI patients' clinical evolution and characterize AKI-to-CKD progression. Post-AKI clinical states were identified by clustering patient vectors derived from longitudinal medical codes and creatinine measurements. Transition probabilities between states and progression to CKD were estimated using multi-state modeling. After identifying common post-AKI trajectories, CKD risk factors in AKI subpopulations were identified through survival analysis. Of 20,699 patients with AKI at admission, 3,491 (17%) developed CKD. We identified fifteen distinct post-AKI states, each with different probabilities of CKD development. Most patients (75%, n=15,607) remained in a single state or made only one transition during the study period. Both established (e.g., AKI severity, diabetes, hypertension, heart failure, liver disease) and novel CKD risk factors, with their impact varying across these clinical states. This study demonstrates a data-driven approach for identifying high-risk AKI patients, supporting the development of decision-support tools for early CKD detection and intervention.
