Enhancing Clinical Note Generation with ICD-10, Clinical Ontology Knowledge Graphs, and Chain-of-Thought Prompting Using GPT-4

Published: 2025-12-04
Authors: Ivan Makohon, Mohamad Najafi, Jian Wu, Mathias Brochhausen, Yaohang Li

Summary

This paper introduces an advanced method for generating clinical notes using GPT-4, addressing the significant time physicians spend on manual documentation. The approach combines Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompting with semantic search results and a clinical ontology knowledge graph, utilizing ICD codes and patient information as inputs. The study found that this enhanced prompting technique substantially outperforms standard one-shot prompts in generating high-quality clinical notes on a subset of the CodiEsp dataset.

Medical Relevance

Automating and enhancing the quality of clinical note generation has direct and profound medical relevance by freeing up physician time for patient interaction, reducing diagnostic delays, and improving the accuracy and completeness of electronic health records, which are crucial for patient safety and continuity of care.

AI Health Application

The primary AI health application is the development of an AI-powered system for automated or AI-assisted clinical note generation. This system leverages large language models (GPT-4), Chain-of-Thought prompting, and clinical ontology knowledge graphs to produce high-quality, domain-specific clinical documentation, thereby enhancing clinical efficiency and supporting healthcare professionals.

Key Points

  • Addresses the significant burden of manual clinical note writing, which consumes physician time and can delay patient care.
  • Proposes leveraging Large Language Models (LLMs), specifically GPT-4, for automated clinical note generation.
  • Utilizes a Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompt engineering strategy to enhance the LLM's reasoning and response quality.
  • Integrates semantic search results and infuses domain-specific knowledge from a clinical ontology knowledge graph into the CoT prompts.
  • Employs International Classification of Diseases (ICD) codes and basic patient information as foundational inputs for note generation.
  • Evaluated the proposed technique on six clinical cases extracted from the CodiEsp test dataset.
  • Results demonstrate that the new prompting method significantly outperformed clinical notes generated via standard one-shot prompts.

Methodology

The study utilized GPT-4 for clinical note generation, employing Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompt engineering. This core prompting technique was enhanced by integrating semantic search results and infusing a knowledge graph (KG) constructed from clinical ontology. Inputs included ICD codes and basic patient information. The effectiveness of this augmented CoT prompting was evaluated against standard one-shot prompts using six clinical cases from the CodiEsp test dataset.

Key Findings

The primary finding is that the novel prompting technique, which combines Chain-of-Thought with semantic search results and clinical ontology knowledge graph infusion, significantly improved the quality of clinical notes generated by GPT-4. This method consistently produced superior results compared to standard one-shot prompts on the evaluated clinical cases.

Clinical Impact

By generating higher-quality, more accurate, and semantically enriched clinical notes, it could lead to improved diagnostic efficiency, enhanced patient safety through better record keeping, and overall more streamlined healthcare operations.

Abstract

In the past decade a surge in the amount of electronic health record (EHR) data in the United States, attributed to a favorable policy environment created by the Health Information Technology for Economic and Clinical Health (HITECH) Act of 2009 and the 21st Century Cures Act of 2016. Clinical notes for patients' assessments, diagnoses, and treatments are captured in these EHRs in free-form text by physicians, who spend a considerable amount of time entering and editing them. Manually writing clinical notes takes a considerable amount of a doctor's valuable time, increasing the patient's waiting time and possibly delaying diagnoses. Large language models (LLMs) possess the ability to generate news articles that closely resemble human-written ones. We investigate the usage of Chain-of-Thought (CoT) prompt engineering to improve the LLM's response in clinical note generation. In our prompts, we use as input International Classification of Diseases (ICD) codes and basic patient information. We investigate a strategy that combines the traditional CoT with semantic search results to improve the quality of generated clinical notes. Additionally, we infuse a knowledge graph (KG) built from clinical ontology to further enrich the domain-specific knowledge of generated clinical notes. We test our prompting technique on six clinical cases from the CodiEsp test dataset using GPT-4 and our results show that it outperformed the clinical notes generated by standard one-shot prompts.
