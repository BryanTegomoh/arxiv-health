How Close Are We? Limitations and Progress of AI Models in Banff Lesion Scoring

arXiv ID: 2510.27158v1
Published: 2025-10-31
Authors: Yanfan Zhu, Juming Xiong, Ruining Deng, Yu Wang, Yaohong Wang, Shilin Zhao, Mengmeng Yin, Yuqing Liu, Haichun Yang, Yuankai Huo

Summary

This paper investigates the current capabilities of deep learning models in computationally replicating the Banff Classification for renal transplant biopsies. By employing a modular, rule-based framework to approximate Banff lesion scores, the study reveals partial successes but also critical limitations, including structural omission, hallucination, and detection ambiguity, highlighting that current AI pipelines struggle with expert-level grading despite occasional matching final scores.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for improving the consistency and objectivity of renal transplant biopsy evaluation, a critical process for patient management. By identifying specific AI limitations, it guides the development of more reliable automated tools that could reduce inter-observer variability among pathologists and enhance diagnostic accuracy in transplant pathology.

AI Health Application

The AI application involves using deep learning models for image analysis (segmentation, detection) to automate or assist in the standardized scoring of lesions in renal transplant biopsies according to the Banff Classification. This aims to reduce inter-observer variability, improve consistency, and potentially enhance the efficiency and accuracy of diagnosis in transplant pathology, ultimately benefiting patient care in renal transplantation.

Key Points

  • The Banff Classification, while standard for renal transplant biopsy evaluation, faces challenges for computational replication due to its semi-quantitative nature, complex criteria, and inter-observer variability.
  • A modular, rule-based framework was developed to approximate Banff lesion scores, decomposing indicators (e.g., glomerulitis 'g', peritubular capillaritis 'ptc', intimal arteritis 'v') into structural and inflammatory components.
  • Existing deep learning segmentation and detection tools were utilized to compute these constituent components, with outputs mapped to Banff scores using heuristic rules aligned with expert guidelines.
  • Evaluation against expert-annotated ground truths revealed critical failure modes such as structural omission (missing actual lesions), hallucination (detecting non-existent lesions), and detection ambiguity.
  • A significant finding was the inconsistency in AI's intermediate representations, even when final Banff scores matched expert annotations, undermining interpretability and reliability.
  • The study emphasizes that current AI pipelines have significant limitations in fully replicating computational expert-level grading for transplant pathology.
  • The authors advocate for modular evaluation and the development of computational Banff grading standards to guide future AI model development in this domain.

Methodology

The study employed a modular, rule-based framework where Banff indicators (like g, ptc, v) were decomposed into elementary structural and inflammatory components. Existing deep learning models for segmentation and detection were used to identify these components from biopsy images. The model outputs were then translated into Banff scores using heuristic rules aligned with expert guidelines. Performance was evaluated by comparing these AI-generated scores and intermediate representations against expert-annotated ground truths.

Key Findings

The AI models achieved partial successes in approximating Banff scores but suffered from critical failure modes including structural omission, hallucination of features, and ambiguous detection of lesions. Even when final Banff scores matched expert annotations, the underlying intermediate representations from the AI were often inconsistent, indicating a lack of robust understanding and limiting interpretability. These results collectively demonstrate the current limitations of AI in fully replicating expert-level computational grading in transplant pathology.

Clinical Impact

The findings underscore the challenges in deploying fully autonomous AI for critical diagnostic tasks like Banff scoring, but also lay the groundwork for developing AI-assisted tools that could reduce pathologist workload and standardize reporting. By pinpointing specific AI failure modes, this research can guide the creation of more accurate and clinically trustworthy AI systems, ultimately leading to more consistent and potentially improved patient care following renal transplantation.

Limitations

The study explicitly noted that current AI pipelines are not yet capable of replicating computational expert-level grading due to issues like structural omission (failing to detect existing lesions), hallucination (detecting non-existent lesions), and detection ambiguity. A significant limitation is the inconsistency found in AI's intermediate representations, which undermines the interpretability and reliability of even correctly predicted final scores.

Future Directions

The authors emphasize the importance of modular evaluation frameworks for AI models in this field, allowing for more granular assessment of performance. They also advocate for the development of computational Banff grading standards, which would provide clearer targets and benchmarks for future AI model development, thereby guiding progress in automated transplant pathology.

Abstract

The Banff Classification provides the global standard for evaluating renal transplant biopsies, yet its semi-quantitative nature, complex criteria, and inter-observer variability present significant challenges for computational replication. In this study, we explore the feasibility of approximating Banff lesion scores using existing deep learning models through a modular, rule-based framework. We decompose each Banff indicator - such as glomerulitis (g), peritubular capillaritis (ptc), and intimal arteritis (v) - into its constituent structural and inflammatory components, and assess whether current segmentation and detection tools can support their computation. Model outputs are mapped to Banff scores using heuristic rules aligned with expert guidelines, and evaluated against expert-annotated ground truths. Our findings highlight both partial successes and critical failure modes, including structural omission, hallucination, and detection ambiguity. Even when final scores match expert annotations, inconsistencies in intermediate representations often undermine interpretability. These results reveal the limitations of current AI pipelines in replicating computational expert-level grading, and emphasize the importance of modular evaluation and computational Banff grading standard in guiding future model development for transplant pathology.
