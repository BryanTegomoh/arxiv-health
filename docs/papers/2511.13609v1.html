AtlasMorph: Learning conditional deformable templates for brain MRI

arXiv ID: 2511.13609v1
Published: 2025-11-17
Authors: Marianne Rakic, Andrew Hoopes, S. Mazdak Abulnaga, Mert R. Sabuncu, John V. Guttag, Adrian V. Dalca

Summary

This paper introduces AtlasMorph, a novel machine learning framework utilizing convolutional registration neural networks to address the scarcity and sub-optimality of medical image templates (atlases) due to their high computational cost. The framework efficiently learns to generate high-quality, deformable brain MRI templates conditioned on subject-specific attributes like age and sex, while also incorporating anatomical segmentation maps. The authors demonstrate that these annotated conditional templates enable superior image registration compared to existing methods and their unconditional counterparts.

Medical Relevance

This research significantly advances medical image analysis by providing an efficient method to generate highly representative, population-specific anatomical templates, which are critical for accurate population studies, disease progression monitoring, and personalized medical diagnostics and treatment planning based on individual patient characteristics.

AI Health Application

The AI application involves using convolutional neural networks to efficiently learn and generate conditional deformable templates (atlases) for brain MRI. This AI model can also perform medical image registration and segmentation. This improves the accuracy and efficiency of medical image analysis tasks, enabling better population studies, personalized anatomical modeling, and potentially enhancing diagnostic capabilities by providing more representative anatomical references for patients.

Key Points

  • **Problem Addressed:** The high computational cost of traditional template generation results in a limited availability of anatomical templates, often leading to the use of sub-optimal templates that are not truly representative of diverse study populations, especially those with significant variations (e.g., age, sex).
  • **Proposed Solution (AtlasMorph):** A machine learning framework built on convolutional registration neural networks that learns a function to efficiently output deformable templates.
  • **Conditional Template Generation:** AtlasMorph generates templates conditioned on subject-specific attributes (e.g., age, sex), ensuring they are more representative of specific demographic sub-populations.
  • **Enhanced Templates with Segmentation:** The framework leverages existing anatomical segmentations (when available) to produce probabilistic anatomical label maps for the generated templates, adding rich anatomical context.
  • **Dual Functionality:** The learned network is capable of both generating conditional templates and performing image registration of new subject images to these tailored templates.
  • **Demonstrated Performance:** Evaluated on 3D brain MRI datasets, AtlasMorph successfully learns high-quality templates that accurately represent specific populations.
  • **Superior Registration:** Annotated conditional templates significantly improve registration accuracy compared to unlabeled unconditional templates and outperform other conventional template construction methods.

Methodology

The authors propose AtlasMorph, a machine learning framework that uses convolutional registration neural networks (CNNs) to learn a function mapping subject-specific attributes (e.g., age, sex) to a deformable anatomical template. The framework is trained on a compilation of 3D brain MRI datasets. It also incorporates anatomical segmentations, when available, to produce probabilistic label maps for the generated templates. The learned network is designed to perform both conditional template generation and subject-to-template image registration.

Key Findings

The AtlasMorph framework successfully learns to generate high-quality, representative deformable templates for brain MRI, which are conditioned on attributes like age and sex. These annotated conditional templates demonstrate superior performance in image registration tasks compared to both unlabeled unconditional templates and other established template construction methods, enabling more precise anatomical alignment.

Clinical Impact

This technology holds substantial clinical impact by enabling the creation of highly personalized anatomical reference atlases for specific patient demographics (e.g., age-matched, sex-specific, or pathology-specific). This will lead to more precise diagnostic and prognostic evaluations in neurology and neuroradiology, improve the accuracy of longitudinal studies tracking disease progression (e.g., Alzheimer's, MS), enhance the precision of pre-surgical planning by providing patient-tailored anatomical models, and facilitate the discovery of more robust biomarkers by ensuring that image analysis is performed against the most anatomically appropriate template.

Abstract

Deformable templates, or atlases, are images that represent a prototypical anatomy for a population, and are often enhanced with probabilistic anatomical label maps. They are commonly used in medical image analysis for population studies and computational anatomy tasks such as registration and segmentation. Because developing a template is a computationally expensive process, relatively few templates are available. As a result, analysis is often conducted with sub-optimal templates that are not truly representative of the study population, especially when there are large variations within this population. We propose a machine learning framework that uses convolutional registration neural networks to efficiently learn a function that outputs templates conditioned on subject-specific attributes, such as age and sex. We also leverage segmentations, when available, to produce anatomical segmentation maps for the resulting templates. The learned network can also be used to register subject images to the templates. We demonstrate our method on a compilation of 3D brain MRI datasets, and show that it can learn high-quality templates that are representative of populations. We find that annotated conditional templates enable better registration than their unlabeled unconditional counterparts, and outperform other templates construction methods.
