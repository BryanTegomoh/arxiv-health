Patient-specific AI for generation of 3D dosimetry imaging from two 2D-planar measurements

arXiv ID: 2510.21362v1
Published: 2025-10-24
Authors: Alejandro Lopez-Montes, Robert Seifert, Astrid Delker, Guido Boening, Jiahui Wang, Christoph Clement, Ali Afshar-Oromieh, Axel Rominger, Kuangyu Shi
Categories: physics.med-ph, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.98 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper proposes a novel patient-specific reinforced learning approach to generate 3D activity maps for dosimetry from just two 2D planar scintigraphy images (anterior and posterior). By developing patient-specific datasets and utilizing AI models like 3DUnet and diffusion models, the method aims to replace expensive and time-consuming 3D SPECT acquisitions with fast planar imaging. The study demonstrates enhanced accuracy and organ delineation, offering a paradigm shift for nuclear medicine dosimetry.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to nuclear medicine as it provides a potentially cheaper, faster, and more accessible method for 3D dosimetry, which is critical for personalized radionuclide therapy (e.g., 177Lu-PSMA). It could significantly improve patient management by enabling more frequent and comprehensive dosimetry studies.

AI Health Application

The AI application involves using patient-specific deep learning models (3DUnet, diffusion models, reinforcement learning) to reconstruct or generate high-fidelity 3D radiation dose distribution maps (activity maps) from limited 2D planar scintigraphy images. This allows for accurate 3D dosimetry quantification without the need for time-consuming and expensive 3D SPECT scans, thereby improving therapy planning, monitoring, and overall patient management in nuclear medicine treatments.

Key Points

  • Addresses the challenge of generating 3D activity maps from two 2D planar images, a problem unachievable by conventional methods for nuclear medicine dosimetry (e.g., 177Lu-PSMA).
  • Proposes a patient-specific reinforced learning methodology that includes generating a unique dataset of possible 3D uptake maps tailored to an individual patient's anatomy.
  • Explores both 3DUnet and diffusion models as AI approaches for reconstructing 3D activity maps from the 2D planar inputs.
  • Demonstrates significant performance improvements with patient-specific reinforcement learning, including an approximate 20% reduction in Mean Absolute Error (MAE) and a ~5% increase in Structural Similarity Index Measure (SSIM).
  • Highlights that diffusion models, especially when combined with patient-specific training, lead to better organ delineation and anatomical representation.
  • Achieves an SSIM of 0.89 against ground truth in simulations and 0.73 in real planar acquisitions when compared to a SPECT scan performed 30 minutes later.
  • Aims to eliminate the need for costly and time-consuming SPECT acquisitions, thereby facilitating multi-time point dosimetry studies and enabling 3D quantification using only planar scintigraphy.

Methodology

The methodology involves two primary steps: first, the creation of a patient-specific dataset comprising potential 3D uptake maps of radiopharmaceuticals within the individual's anatomy. Second, an AI approach, specifically investigating 3DUnet and diffusion models, is employed to generate 3D activity maps from two 2D planar images (anterior and posterior). The method was validated using both simulated data and real-world planar acquisitions.

Key Findings

Patient-specific reinforcement learning led to enhanced results, showing an approximately 20% reduction in MAE and a 5% increase in SSIM. Diffusion models, when combined with patient-specific training, delivered superior organ delineation and anatomical detail. Quantitatively, the method achieved an SSIM of 0.89 in simulations compared to ground truth, and 0.73 when comparing real planar reconstructions to a SPECT acquisition performed 30 minutes later.

Clinical Impact

This methodology has the potential to revolutionize nuclear medicine dosimetry by enabling accurate 3D quantification of radiopharmaceutical distribution using only fast and cost-effective 2D planar scintigraphy, obviating the need for expensive and time-consuming SPECT scans. This would make multi-time point dosimetry studies more feasible and accessible, ultimately leading to more precise and personalized radionuclide therapies and improved treatment outcomes.

Abstract

In this work we explored the use of patient specific reinforced learning to generate 3D activity maps from two 2D planar images (anterior and posterior). The solution of this problem remains unachievable using conventional methodologies and is of particular interest for dosimetry in nuclear medicine where approaches for post-therapy distribution of radiopharmaceuticals such as 177Lu-PSMA are typically done via either expensive and long 3D SPECT acquisitions or fast, yet only 2D, planar scintigraphy. Being able to generate 3D activity maps from planar scintigraphy opens the gate for new dosimetry applications removing the need for SPECT and facilitating multi-time point dosimetry studies. Our solution comprises the generation of a patient specific dataset with possible 3D uptake maps of the radiopharmaceuticals withing the anatomy of the individual followed by an AI approach (we explored both the use of 3DUnet and diffusion models) able to generate 3D activity maps from 2D planar images. We have validated our method both in simulation and real planar acquisitions. We observed enhanced results using patient specific reinforcement learning (~20% reduction on MAE and ~5% increase in SSIM) and better organ delineation and patient anatomy especially when combining diffusion models with patient specific training yielding a SSIM=0.89 compared to the ground truth for simulations and 0.73 when compared to a SPECT acquisition performed half an hour after the planar. We believe that our methodology can set a change of paradigm for nuclear medicine dosimetry allowing for 3D quantification using only planar scintigraphy without the need of expensive and time-consuming SPECT leveraging the pre-therapy information of the patients.
