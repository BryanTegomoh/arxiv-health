Severity Detection Tool for Patients with Infectious Disease

Published: 2019-12-10

Summary

This paper presents a machine learning-based tool for the automatic detection of Autonomic Nervous System Dysfunction (ANSD) severity in patients suffering from serious infectious diseases like Hand, Foot and Mouth Disease (HFMD) and tetanus. By analyzing physiological data (ECG and PPG waveforms) from low-cost wearable sensors, the approach extracts novel time and frequency domain features, outperforming standard HRV analysis, and shows promising results in classifying ANSD levels. This innovation aims to facilitate early diagnosis and treatment in resource-limited settings, thereby improving patient care.

Medical Relevance

Early and accurate detection of autonomic nervous system dysfunction (ANSD) is paramount for reducing high mortality rates associated with severe infectious diseases like tetanus and HFMD. This tool provides a non-invasive, cost-effective method for this crucial detection, potentially transforming patient management and resource allocation in vulnerable populations.

AI Health Application

The AI application involves using machine learning (specifically Support Vector Machines) to automatically classify the severity levels of autonomic nervous system dysfunction (ANSD) in patients with infectious diseases (HFMD, Tetanus). This is achieved by extracting features from physiological data (ECG, PPG waveforms) collected using wearable sensors. The goal is to provide an early, automated diagnostic tool to facilitate timely treatment and improve patient outcomes, particularly in resource-constrained settings.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical challenge of early detection of Autonomic Nervous System Dysfunction (ANSD), which is the primary cause of death in HFMD and tetanus patients, especially in low and middle-income countries.
  • Proposes a machine learning framework to automatically classify ANSD levels using easily collectible physiological patient data from low-cost wearable sensors (Electrocardiogram - ECG and Photoplethysmogram - PPG).
  • Utilizes efficient, novel features extracted from the time and frequency domains of ECG and PPG waveforms, designed to encode subtle variations indicative of ANSD.
  • Employs a Support Vector Machine (SVM) as the core classification algorithm to categorize different ANSD severity levels.
  • Validated on real-world datasets of HFMD and tetanus patients acquired in Vietnam, demonstrating encouraging performance in ANSD level classification.
  • The proposed features were found to be simple, more generalisable, and significantly outperformed traditional Heart Rate Variability (HRV) analysis for ANSD detection.
  • Aims to enhance the diagnosis and treatment processes for infectious diseases in resource-constrained environments, ultimately leading to improved patient outcomes and more efficient healthcare resource management.

Methodology

The study collects physiological data, specifically Electrocardiogram (ECG) and Photoplethysmogram (PPG) waveforms, using low-cost wearable sensors. From these waveforms, efficient features are extracted that encode variations in both the time and frequency domains. A Support Vector Machine (SVM) is then employed as the machine learning classifier to automatically classify different levels of Autonomic Nervous System Dysfunction (ANSD). The proposed approach was validated using multiple datasets from HFMD and tetanus patients in Vietnam.

Key Findings

The study successfully demonstrated an encouraging performance in classifying ANSD levels. A key finding was that the proposed features, derived from ECG and PPG waveforms, are not only simple and more generalisable but also significantly outperformed the standard Heart Rate Variability (HRV) analysis in detecting ANSD.

Clinical Impact

This research has the potential to profoundly impact clinical practice by enabling earlier and more objective detection of ANSD in patients with HFMD and tetanus, particularly in low and middle-income countries. This could lead to more timely and appropriate clinical interventions, potentially reducing mortality and morbidity, optimizing the use of scarce healthcare resources, and improving overall patient care during outbreaks or for severe cases.

Limitations

The abstract describes the work as a "proof-of-principle," implying that while promising, further extensive clinical validation, larger and more diverse datasets, and real-world deployment studies are necessary to fully establish its robustness, generalizability, and widespread clinical utility.

Future Directions

While not explicitly detailed as a separate section, the 'proof-of-principle' nature and the aim to 'facilitate diagnosis and treatment' imply future work. This would likely involve comprehensive clinical trials, integration into existing healthcare workflows, refinement of algorithms for real-time monitoring, and exploration of its applicability to other medical conditions where early ANSD detection is crucial for prognosis and management.

Abstract

Hand, foot and mouth disease (HFMD) and tetanus are serious infectious diseases in low and middle income countries. Tetanus in particular has a high mortality rate and its treatment is resource-demanding. Furthermore, HFMD often affects a large number of infants and young children. As a result, its treatment consumes enormous healthcare resources, especially when outbreaks occur. Autonomic nervous system dysfunction (ANSD) is the main cause of death for both HFMD and tetanus patients. However, early detection of ANSD is a difficult and challenging problem. In this paper, we aim to provide a proof-of-principle to detect the ANSD level automatically by applying machine learning techniques to physiological patient data, such as electrocardiogram (ECG) and photoplethysmogram (PPG) waveforms, which can be collected using low-cost wearable sensors. Efficient features are extracted that encode variations in the waveforms in the time and frequency domains. A support vector machine is employed to classify the ANSD levels. The proposed approach is validated on multiple datasets of HFMD and tetanus patients in Vietnam. Results show that encouraging performance is achieved in classifying ANSD levels. Moreover, the proposed features are simple, more generalisable and outperformed the standard heart rate variability (HRV) analysis. The proposed approach would facilitate both the diagnosis and treatment of infectious diseases in low and middle income countries, and thereby improve overall patient care.
