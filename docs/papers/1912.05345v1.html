Severity Detection Tool for Patients with Infectious Disease

Published: 2019-12-10
Authors: Girmaw Abebe Tadesse, Tingting Zhu, Nhan Le Nguyen Thanh, Nguyen Thanh Hung, Ha Thi Hai Duong, Truong Huu Khanh, Pham Van Quang, Duc Duong Tran, LamMinh Yen, H Rogier Van Doorn, Nguyen Van Hao, John Prince, Hamza Javed, DaniKiyasseh, Le Van Tan, Louise Thwaites, David A. Clifton

Summary

This paper presents a proof-of-principle for an automated severity detection tool designed to identify autonomic nervous system dysfunction (ANSD) levels in patients with infectious diseases like Hand, foot and mouth disease (HFMD) and tetanus. Leveraging machine learning on physiological data (ECG, PPG) from low-cost wearable sensors, the tool extracts novel features and uses a Support Vector Machine, demonstrating encouraging classification performance and outperforming standard Heart Rate Variability (HRV) analysis.

Medical Relevance

Early and accurate detection of Autonomic Nervous System Dysfunction (ANSD) is crucial for reducing mortality and morbidity in severe infectious diseases like tetanus and HFMD, especially in low-resource environments where intensive treatment is challenging. This tool offers a non-invasive, cost-effective method to achieve this.

AI Health Application

The AI application involves using machine learning (specifically Support Vector Machines) to automatically detect and classify levels of Autonomic Nervous System Dysfunction (ANSD) based on physiological patient data (ECG and PPG waveforms) collected via low-cost wearable sensors. This AI-powered tool aims to provide an early warning system for a life-threatening complication in infectious disease patients, thereby assisting in timely diagnosis and treatment to improve patient outcomes.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical challenge of early ANSD detection, a major cause of death in HFMD and tetanus, particularly in resource-limited settings.
  • Utilizes physiological data (ECG and PPG waveforms) collectable via low-cost wearable sensors, making it suitable for low and middle-income countries.
  • Employs a machine learning approach, specifically a Support Vector Machine (SVM), for the automatic classification of ANSD levels.
  • Introduces efficient, custom-extracted features from time and frequency domains of the waveforms, designed to encode relevant physiological variations.
  • Achieved encouraging performance in classifying ANSD levels, validated on multiple datasets of HFMD and tetanus patients in Vietnam.
  • Demonstrated that the proposed features are simple, more generalizable, and superior in performance compared to standard Heart Rate Variability (HRV) analysis.
  • Aims to facilitate earlier diagnosis and optimized treatment for severe infectious diseases, thereby improving patient care and resource management.

Methodology

The study collects physiological patient data, specifically electrocardiogram (ECG) and photoplethysmogram (PPG) waveforms, using low-cost wearable sensors. Efficient features are then extracted from these waveforms, capturing variations in both the time and frequency domains. A Support Vector Machine (SVM) algorithm is subsequently employed to classify the detected ANSD levels. The proposed approach was validated using datasets from HFMD and tetanus patients in Vietnam.

Key Findings

The proposed machine learning-based approach achieved encouraging performance in automatically classifying ANSD levels in HFMD and tetanus patients. A key finding was that the newly introduced features are simple, exhibit greater generalizability, and significantly outperformed the standard Heart Rate Variability (HRV) analysis methods in detecting ANSD.

Clinical Impact

This tool has the potential to significantly improve clinical outcomes by enabling early and automatic detection of ANSD, a critical prognostic indicator, in patients with severe infectious diseases. This could lead to more timely and targeted medical interventions, better allocation of healthcare resources, and ultimately reduce mortality rates, particularly in low and middle-income countries where resources are often strained.

Limitations

The abstract describes the work as providing a "proof-of-principle," implying it is an initial demonstration. No explicit limitations regarding data size, diversity, or specific clinical trial phases are mentioned within the abstract itself.

Future Directions

Not explicitly mentioned in the abstract, but the 'proof-of-principle' nature implicitly suggests future work would involve broader clinical validation, larger and more diverse patient cohorts, refinement of the model, and potential integration into real-time monitoring systems for practical clinical use.

Abstract

Hand, foot and mouth disease (HFMD) and tetanus are serious infectious diseases in low and middle income countries. Tetanus in particular has a high mortality rate and its treatment is resource-demanding. Furthermore, HFMD often affects a large number of infants and young children. As a result, its treatment consumes enormous healthcare resources, especially when outbreaks occur. Autonomic nervous system dysfunction (ANSD) is the main cause of death for both HFMD and tetanus patients. However, early detection of ANSD is a difficult and challenging problem. In this paper, we aim to provide a proof-of-principle to detect the ANSD level automatically by applying machine learning techniques to physiological patient data, such as electrocardiogram (ECG) and photoplethysmogram (PPG) waveforms, which can be collected using low-cost wearable sensors. Efficient features are extracted that encode variations in the waveforms in the time and frequency domains. A support vector machine is employed to classify the ANSD levels. The proposed approach is validated on multiple datasets of HFMD and tetanus patients in Vietnam. Results show that encouraging performance is achieved in classifying ANSD levels. Moreover, the proposed features are simple, more generalisable and outperformed the standard heart rate variability (HRV) analysis. The proposed approach would facilitate both the diagnosis and treatment of infectious diseases in low and middle income countries, and thereby improve overall patient care.
