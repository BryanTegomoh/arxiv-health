Severity Detection Tool for Patients with Infectious Disease

Published: 2019-12-10
Authors: Girmaw Abebe Tadesse, Tingting Zhu, Nhan Le Nguyen Thanh, Nguyen Thanh Hung, Ha Thi Hai Duong, Truong Huu Khanh, Pham Van Quang, Duc Duong Tran, LamMinh Yen, H Rogier Van Doorn, Nguyen Van Hao, John Prince, Hamza Javed, DaniKiyasseh, Le Van Tan, Louise Thwaites, David A. Clifton

Summary

This paper proposes a machine learning-based tool for automatic detection of Autonomic Nervous System Dysfunction (ANSD) levels in patients with Hand, Foot and Mouth Disease (HFMD) and tetanus. By extracting efficient time and frequency domain features from low-cost ECG and PPG data and employing a Support Vector Machine, the approach demonstrated encouraging performance and superior results compared to standard HRV analysis. This method aims to improve diagnosis and treatment in resource-limited settings.

Medical Relevance

Early and accurate detection of Autonomic Nervous System Dysfunction (ANSD) is paramount for managing severe infectious diseases like HFMD and tetanus, which carry high mortality rates and demand intensive resources. This low-cost, automated machine learning tool offers a significant advancement in facilitating timely diagnosis and guiding targeted treatment, particularly in underserved regions.

AI Health Application

The paper proposes using machine learning (Support Vector Machine) on physiological signals (ECG and PPG) from wearable sensors to automatically detect and classify the severity levels of Autonomic Nervous System Dysfunction (ANSD) in patients with infectious diseases (HFMD and tetanus). This AI application aims to provide an early diagnostic tool to prevent severe outcomes, facilitate timely treatment, and improve patient management in clinical settings.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical need for early and automatic detection of Autonomic Nervous System Dysfunction (ANSD), a leading cause of death in HFMD and tetanus patients, particularly in low and middle-income countries.
  • Proposes a proof-of-principle utilizing machine learning (Support Vector Machine) to classify ANSD levels based on physiological patient data.
  • Leverages readily collectable physiological signals, specifically electrocardiogram (ECG) and photoplethysmogram (PPG) waveforms, which can be acquired using low-cost wearable sensors.
  • Employs efficient feature extraction methods that encode variations in the ECG and PPG waveforms across both time and frequency domains.
  • Validated the approach on multiple datasets derived from HFMD and tetanus patients in Vietnam, demonstrating its applicability in real-world scenarios.
  • Achieved encouraging performance in accurately classifying ANSD levels, suggesting significant potential for clinical utility.
  • Demonstrated that the proposed features are simple, possess greater generalizability, and notably outperformed traditional Heart Rate Variability (HRV) analysis for ANSD detection.

Methodology

The study utilized machine learning techniques, specifically a Support Vector Machine (SVM), for classifying ANSD levels. It involved extracting efficient features from electrocardiogram (ECG) and photoplethysmogram (PPG) waveform data, encompassing variations in both time and frequency domains. These features were then used to train and validate the SVM classifier on datasets of HFMD and tetanus patients.

Key Findings

The proposed machine learning approach achieved encouraging performance in classifying Autonomic Nervous System Dysfunction (ANSD) levels in HFMD and tetanus patients. A key finding was that the novel features extracted from ECG and PPG data proved to be simpler, more generalizable, and significantly outperformed traditional Heart Rate Variability (HRV) analysis in detecting ANSD.

Clinical Impact

This tool has the potential to significantly improve patient care by enabling earlier and more precise diagnosis of Autonomic Nervous System Dysfunction (ANSD) in critical infectious disease cases. This would allow for more timely and tailored treatment interventions, potentially reducing mortality rates, optimizing healthcare resource allocation, and ultimately leading to better patient outcomes, especially beneficial in low and middle-income countries.

Limitations

The abstract presents this work as a 'proof-of-principle,' indicating that while promising, it requires further extensive validation, clinical trials, and potentially larger and more diverse datasets before widespread clinical deployment. Specific limitations regarding dataset size, diversity, or generalizability beyond the Vietnamese patient population are not detailed in the abstract.

Future Directions

While not explicitly outlined, the 'proof-of-principle' nature of the work implies a future direction toward extensive validation and refinement of the tool. This includes scaling up testing with larger and more diverse patient populations, integrating the algorithms into clinically deployable wearable systems for real-time monitoring, and assessing its impact on clinical decision-making and patient outcomes in various healthcare settings.

Abstract

Hand, foot and mouth disease (HFMD) and tetanus are serious infectious diseases in low and middle income countries. Tetanus in particular has a high mortality rate and its treatment is resource-demanding. Furthermore, HFMD often affects a large number of infants and young children. As a result, its treatment consumes enormous healthcare resources, especially when outbreaks occur. Autonomic nervous system dysfunction (ANSD) is the main cause of death for both HFMD and tetanus patients. However, early detection of ANSD is a difficult and challenging problem. In this paper, we aim to provide a proof-of-principle to detect the ANSD level automatically by applying machine learning techniques to physiological patient data, such as electrocardiogram (ECG) and photoplethysmogram (PPG) waveforms, which can be collected using low-cost wearable sensors. Efficient features are extracted that encode variations in the waveforms in the time and frequency domains. A support vector machine is employed to classify the ANSD levels. The proposed approach is validated on multiple datasets of HFMD and tetanus patients in Vietnam. Results show that encouraging performance is achieved in classifying ANSD levels. Moreover, the proposed features are simple, more generalisable and outperformed the standard heart rate variability (HRV) analysis. The proposed approach would facilitate both the diagnosis and treatment of infectious diseases in low and middle income countries, and thereby improve overall patient care.
