The Impact of Past Epidemics on Future Disease Dynamics

arXiv ID: 0910.2008v1
Published: 2009-10-12
Authors: Shweta Bansal, Lauren Ancel Meyers
Categories: q-bio.PE, q-bio.QM
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00

Summary

This paper develops mathematical models using percolation theory to analyze the long-term dynamics of consecutive seasonal outbreaks of partially-immunizing infections in populations with contact heterogeneity. It demonstrates how past epidemics restructure the epidemiologically active host population, limiting future outbreak potential and potentially driving evolutionary pressure for increased pathogen infectiousness.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for understanding the long-term epidemiological patterns of recurrent infectious diseases, informing public health strategies for vaccine deployment, optimal timing of interventions, and pandemic preparedness, especially for highly variable pathogens like influenza.

Design early warning systems that analyze dynamic immune landscapes and contact patterns to detect potential increases in pathogen infectiousness or shifts in outbreak potential, contributing to biosecurity preparedness.

Key Points

  • Addresses the critical gap in understanding the long-term epidemic or endemic transmission dynamics of pathogens within contact networks.
  • Focuses on seasonal, partially-immunizing infections (e.g., influenza) in populations characterized by heterogeneous contact patterns.
  • Employs two distinct mathematical approaches based on percolation theory to model the dynamics of consecutive seasonal outbreaks.
  • Considers two specific models for acquired immunity: 'leaky immunity' (all previously infected individuals gain partial immunity) and 'perfect immunity' (a fraction of previously infected individuals acquire full immunity).
  • Demonstrates that prior epidemics fundamentally restructure the epidemiologically active portion of the host population by generating immunity.
  • This restructuring effect limits the potential size and severity of future outbreaks due to a reduced susceptible host pool.
  • Speculates that these dynamics can result in evolutionary pressure on pathogens to increase their infectiousness to overcome the diminished susceptible population.

Methodology

The study develops two mathematical approaches leveraging methods from percolation theory. These models are designed to simulate consecutive seasonal outbreaks of a partially-immunizing infection within a population exhibiting contact heterogeneity. Two distinct immunity scenarios are explored: 'leaky immunity' (where all previously infected individuals gain partial protection) and 'perfect immunity' (where a fraction of previously infected individuals become fully immune).

Key Findings

Past epidemics significantly reshape the composition of the epidemiologically active host population by inducing immunity, thereby inherently limiting the potential for subsequent large-scale outbreaks. The authors speculate that this dynamic reduction in susceptible hosts may exert an evolutionary pressure on pathogens, potentially leading to increased infectiousness.

Clinical Impact

This work can enhance the accuracy of predictions for outbreak severity and timing for seasonal infectious diseases. It can inform the design of more effective and targeted vaccination campaigns by considering the long-term dynamics of population immunity and contact structures, potentially guiding the development of vaccines against evolving pathogens.

Limitations

The assertion regarding evolutionary pressure for increased infectiousness is presented as a speculation, indicating a need for further theoretical or empirical validation. The mathematical models represent simplifications of complex biological and social systems, which may not capture all real-world complexities.

Future Directions

The speculation about evolutionary pressure for increased infectiousness highlights a significant area for future research, likely involving more complex evolutionary models or empirical studies. Further investigation could also explore the impact of specific public health intervention strategies (e.g., varying vaccine efficacy or coverage) within these dynamic network contexts.

Abstract

Many pathogens spread primarily via direct contact between infected and susceptible hosts. Thus, the patterns of contacts or contact network of a population fundamentally shapes the course of epidemics. While there is a robust and growing theory for the dynamics of single epidemics in networks, we know little about the impacts of network structure on long term epidemic or endemic transmission. For seasonal diseases like influenza, pathogens repeatedly return to populations with complex and changing patterns of susceptibility and immunity acquired through prior infection. Here, we develop two mathematical approaches for modeling consecutive seasonal outbreaks of a partially-immunizing infection in a population with contact heterogeneity. Using methods from percolation theory we consider both leaky immunity, where all previously infected individuals gain partial immunity, and perfect immunity, where a fraction of previously infected individuals are fully immune. By restructuring the epidemiologically active portion of their host population, such diseases limit the potential of future outbreaks. We speculate that these dynamics can result in evolutionary pressure to increase infectiousness.
