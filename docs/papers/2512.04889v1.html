FENCE: Flexible Electric Noise Cancellation Endo-shield for the Suppression of Electromagnetic Interference in Low-Field MRI

Published: 2025-12-04
Authors: Julia Pfitzer, Martin Uecker, Hermann Scharfetter

Summary

This paper introduces FENCE (Flexible Electric Noise Cancellation Endo-shield), a novel, low-cost solution designed to mitigate electromagnetic interference (EMI) in low-field MRI systems operating without traditional Faraday cages. FENCE, implemented as flexible PCB shields placed inside RF coils, effectively blocks capacitive coupling, demonstrating significant improvements in signal-to-noise ratio (SNR) in both phantom and in-vivo head imaging experiments. This enhances image quality, maintains system portability, and improves accessibility of low-field MRI.

Medical Relevance

By significantly reducing electromagnetic interference, FENCE enhances the diagnostic capabilities and image quality of portable and accessible low-field MRI systems. This innovation makes MRI more practical for use in resource-limited settings, at the point of care, or in mobile clinics, thereby broadening access to crucial medical imaging.

Key Points

  • Low-field MRI systems without Faraday shields face significant electromagnetic interference (EMI), hindering portability and accessibility.
  • The FENCE approach is based on the hypothesis that EMI primarily couples capacitively from the patient's body to the RF coil.
  • FENCE (Flexible Electric Noise Cancellation Endo-shield) utilizes flexible PCB shields placed inside the RF coil to block this capacitive coupling, and it can be retrofitted to existing RF coils.
  • The study also investigated the complementary effect of capacitive segmentation of the RF coil to further reduce EMI coupling.
  • Finite Element (FE) simulations were used to predict shielding performance and assess the minimal impact on RF coil losses (Q factor) prior to practical implementation.
  • Phantom experiments demonstrated FENCE's effectiveness, increasing SNR by up to a factor of 9 and reducing EMI levels to near-baseline, with only a 9% reduction in coil Q factor.
  • In-vivo head imaging confirmed these benefits across diverse electromagnetic environments, showing SNR increases up to a factor of 2, despite an 18% decrease in Q factor, validating its real-world applicability.

Methodology

The methodology involved an initial phase of Finite Element (FE) simulations to predict the shielding performance of FENCE and estimate its impact on RF coil quality factor (Q factor). Subsequently, practical implementation and testing were conducted through phantom experiments to quantify SNR and EMI reduction, followed by in-vivo head imaging in various realistic scenarios to validate efficacy under diverse electromagnetic conditions.

Key Findings

FENCE, particularly when combined with capacitively segmented coils, effectively suppresses EMI by blocking capacitive coupling. Phantom experiments demonstrated a substantial SNR increase of up to a factor of 9 and EMI reduction to near-baseline levels, with a modest 9% reduction in coil Q factor. In-vivo head imaging confirmed an SNR increase of up to a factor of 2, accompanied by an 18% decrease in Q factor, validating FENCE's performance in realistic clinical settings and showing good agreement with FE simulation predictions.

Clinical Impact

FENCE offers a simple, low-cost, and retrofittable solution to improve image quality in low-field MRI systems. This innovation removes the need for expensive, dedicated Faraday-shielded rooms, significantly enhancing the portability, accessibility, and practical utility of MRI for diagnostic purposes in a wider range of clinical environments, including underserved areas or point-of-care applications.

Abstract

Electromagnetic interference (EMI) is a significant challenge for low-field MRI systems operating without conventional Faraday-shielded rooms. Traditional EMI mitigation approaches include external shields, subject grounding via electrodes, or active noise cancellation requiring synchronized receive channels. These methods either limit portability, introduce patient discomfort, or demand expensive hardware. In this work, we start from the hypothesis that EMI primarily couples capacitively from the body to the RF coil. We investigated two methods of blocking capacitive coupling while preserving inductive MRI signal detection: First, we employed capacitive segmentation of the RF coil and studied its effect on EMI coupling. Second, we present FENCE (Flexible Electromagnetic Noise Cancellation Endo-shield), a novel approach blocking capacitive coupling using flexible PCB shields placed inside the RF coil. FENCE can be retrofitted to existing RF coils. Finite element (FE) simulations were used to estimate the expected shielding performance and the impact on RF coil losses prior to practical implementation. Testing in various realistic scenarios then demonstrated that the combination of FENCE with segmented coils is effective against both environmental noise sources and controlled EMI. In phantom experiments, FENCE increased SNR by up to a factor of 9 and reduced EMI levels to near-baseline levels with 9% reduction in coil quality factor (Q factor), showing good agreement with the predictions from the FE simulations. In-vivo head imaging confirmed these results across diverse electromagnetic environments where SNR increased by up to a factor 2 while showing an 18% decrease in Q factor. FENCE's simple design provides a low-cost solution to EMI in low-field MRI, enhancing image quality while maintaining system portability and accessibility.
