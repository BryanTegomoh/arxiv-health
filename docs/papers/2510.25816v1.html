Summary

This paper introduces the Clinical Entity Augmented Retrieval (CLEAR) method, which uses entity-aware retrieval to overcome the challenges of semantic question answering from unstructured clinical documentation within EHRs. CLEAR demonstrated significant improvements in both accuracy and computational efficiency, outperforming traditional methods and offering particular advantages for long clinical notes. The study also developed a reusable evaluation platform for clinical QA systems.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to healthcare as it addresses a fundamental challenge in extracting actionable insights from the vast amount of unstructured clinical data in EHRs. By enabling more accurate and efficient semantic question answering, it can significantly improve clinical decision support, accelerate medical research, enhance patient safety, and optimize healthcare operations.

AI Health Application

The research develops and evaluates AI methods (specifically entity-aware retrieval, large context inference, and retrieval augmented generation) to enhance semantic question answering and information retrieval from clinical notes within Electronic Health Records (EHRs). This application aims to improve the efficiency and accuracy of accessing critical clinical information, thereby supporting healthcare professionals, potentially aiding in clinical decision-making, and optimizing the utility of digital health records.

  • The Clinical Entity Augmented Retrieval (CLEAR) method utilizes entity-aware retrieval to improve understanding of nuanced clinical relationships.
  • CLEAR achieved an F1 score of 0.90, surpassing embedding-based retrieval (0.86), and used over 70% fewer tokens in initial evaluations.
  • A Clinical Notes QA Evaluation Platform was developed to validate CLEAR against zero-shot large context inference and traditional chunk-based retrieval augmented generation.

Methodology

The study involved developing the Clinical Entity Augmented Retrieval (CLEAR) method, which is based on entity-aware retrieval. This method was validated using a newly developed Clinical Notes QA Evaluation Platform. The platform compared CLEAR's performance against two baseline approaches: zero-shot large context inference and traditional chunk-based retrieval augmented generation. Evaluation was conducted on 12 diverse clinical notes ranging from 10,000 to 65,000 tokens, representing realistic EHR content. Key performance metrics included F1 score, win rate, average semantic similarity, and token usage.

Key Findings

CLEAR achieved an F1 score of 0.90, surpassing embedding-based retrieval (0.86). In comprehensive testing, it obtained a 58.3% win rate and an average semantic similarity of 0.878 against competing methods. Crucially, CLEAR used 78% fewer tokens than wide context processing, indicating significant computational efficiency gains. The method demonstrated its strongest performance on long clinical notes (exceeding 65,000 tokens), achieving a 75% win rate, confirming the benefits of entity-aware retrieval for both accuracy and efficiency in clinical NLP.

Clinical Impact

The CLEAR method has the potential to revolutionize how healthcare professionals and systems interact with clinical data. It can enable more precise and rapid retrieval of patient information, aiding in diagnosis, treatment planning, and risk assessment. Reduced token usage translates to lower computational costs and faster processing, making advanced NLP solutions more scalable and economically viable for healthcare organizations. The evaluation platform itself offers a standardized tool for future development, fostering innovation in clinical question answering systems critical for advancing evidence-based medicine.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations of the CLEAR method or the study. However, the evaluation was conducted on a specific set of 12 clinical notes; while described as 'realistic,' the generalizability of these findings across all medical specialties, diverse patient populations, or different EHR systems would require further validation.

Future Directions

The developed Clinical Notes QA Evaluation Platform serves as a "reusable and transparent benchmark for assessing clinical question answering systems." This implies a future direction for standardizing the evaluation of new clinical NLP methods, encouraging further research and development in areas where semantic precision and computational efficiency are crucial. Future work could also involve expanding the test set to include a wider variety of clinical notes, specialties, and potentially multi-lingual contexts.

Abstract

Electronic Health Records (EHR) store clinical documentation as base64 encoded attachments in FHIR DocumentReference resources, which makes semantic question answering difficult. Traditional vector database methods often miss nuanced clinical relationships. The Clinical Entity Augmented Retrieval (CLEAR) method, introduced by Lopez et al. 2025, uses entity aware retrieval and achieved improved performance with an F1 score of 0.90 versus 0.86 for embedding based retrieval, while using over 70 percent fewer tokens. We developed a Clinical Notes QA Evaluation Platform to validate CLEAR against zero shot large context inference and traditional chunk based retrieval augmented generation. The platform was tested on 12 clinical notes ranging from 10,000 to 65,000 tokens representing realistic EHR content. CLEAR achieved a 58.3 percent win rate, an average semantic similarity of 0.878, and used 78 percent fewer tokens than wide context processing. The largest performance gains occurred on long notes, with a 75 percent win rate for documents exceeding 65,000 tokens. These findings confirm that entity aware retrieval improves both efficiency and accuracy in clinical natural language processing. The evaluation framework provides a reusable and transparent benchmark for assessing clinical question answering systems where semantic precision and computational efficiency are critical.
