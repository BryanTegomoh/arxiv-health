Early warning signals in plant disease outbreaks

Published: 2018-02-21

Summary

This paper introduces a lattice-based epidemic model coupled with a stochastic process to analyze plant disease outbreaks, specifically fungal infections in forests. The study identifies a critical transition from local disease confinement to widespread epiphytotic outbreaks, a shift directly influenced by the density of susceptible hosts. Crucially, it demonstrates that early-warning indicators can successfully forecast this critical transition before the system enters an epidemic regime.

Medical Relevance

While focused on plant diseases, this research introduces a generalizable framework for modeling infectious disease dynamics and detecting critical transitions, which holds significant potential for adaptation to human infectious disease epidemiology, enabling proactive public health interventions and pandemic preparedness.

Key Points

  • Infectious plant disease outbreaks, particularly fungal diseases in forests, pose significant threats to ecosystems, agriculture, and food trade, with current prediction and control being challenging.
  • A novel lattice-based epidemic model, coupled with a simplified stochastic process simulating host-pathogen interaction, was developed to study disease spread.
  • The propagation velocity of the pathogen on susceptible hosts was measured to quantify disease spread within the model.
  • Quantitative results indicate a critical transition between two stable phases: local disease confinement and an extended epiphytotic outbreak.
  • This critical transition is quantitatively dependent on the density of susceptible individuals within the host population.
  • Early-warning indicators, a framework widely applied in dynamical systems for impending regime shifts, successfully predicted the critical transition into the epiphytotic regime prior to its onset.
  • The research suggests that these early-warning indicators, integrated into mathematical and computational models, could be instrumental for predicting forest disease epidemics and adaptable to more specific pathogen-host-environmental interactions.

Methodology

The study employed a lattice-based epidemic model, integrated with a stochastic process designed to mimic simplified host-pathogen interactions. Disease propagation was quantified by measuring the pathogen's velocity across susceptible hosts. Early-warning indicators, a technique derived from the analysis of dynamical systems, were then applied to predict critical shifts in disease dynamics.

Key Findings

['Identification of a critical transition point in disease dynamics, shifting from localized infection to a widespread epiphytotic outbreak, whose occurrence is dictated by the density of susceptible hosts.', 'Successful demonstration that early-warning indicators can accurately forecast the impending onset of this critical epiphytotic regime before its full manifestation, providing a predictive lead time.']

Clinical Impact

This modeling approach and the successful application of early-warning indicators provide a robust theoretical foundation for developing practical predictive tools for infectious disease outbreaks. Such tools could enable timely public health interventions, optimized resource allocation for disease control (e.g., vaccination campaigns, quarantines), and enhanced biosecurity measures, not only in agriculture but also potentially for human health crises.

Limitations

The model mimics the interaction between hosts and pathogen in a "very simplified way," suggesting a potential limitation in fully capturing the intricate complexities of real-world biological systems and environmental factors.

Future Directions

The authors suggest extending the research by developing mathematical and computational models that incorporate more specific pathogen-host-environmental interactions, aiming to enhance the predictive accuracy and utility of these early-warning indicators for real-world forest disease epidemics.

Abstract

Summary 1. Infectious disease outbreaks in plants threaten ecosystems, agricultural crops and food trade. Currently, several fungal diseases are affecting forests worldwide, posing a major risk to tree species, habitats and consequently ecosystem decay. Prediction and control of disease spread are difficult, mainly due to the complexity of the interaction between individual components involved. 2. In this work, we introduce a lattice-based epidemic model coupled with a stochastic process that mimics, in a very simplified way, the interaction between the hosts and pathogen. We studied the disease spread by measuring the propagation velocity of the pathogen on the susceptible hosts. Quantitative results indicate the occurrence of a critical transition between two stable phases: local confinement and an extended epiphytotic outbreak that depends on the density of the susceptible individuals. 3. Quantitative predictions of epiphytotics are performed using the framework early-warning indicators for impending regime shifts, widely applied on dynamical systems. These signals forecast successfully the outcome of the critical shift between the two stable phases before the system enters the epiphytotic regime. 4. Synthesis: Our study demonstrates that early-warning indicators could be useful for the prediction of forest disease epidemics through mathematical and computational models suited to more specific pathogen-host-environmental interactions.
