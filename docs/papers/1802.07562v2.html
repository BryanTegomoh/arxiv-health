Early warning signals in plant disease outbreaks

arXiv ID: 1802.07562v2
Published: 2018-02-21
Authors: S. Orozco-Fuentes, G. Griffiths, M. J. Holmes, R. Ettelaie, J. Smith, A. W. Baggaley, N. G. Parker

Summary

This research introduces a lattice-based epidemic model with a stochastic component to study disease spread in host populations, identifying a critical transition between localized containment and widespread outbreaks. The study successfully applies early-warning indicators, derived from dynamical systems theory, to forecast these critical shifts before the onset of an epidemic. This methodology demonstrates a promising approach for predicting infectious disease outbreaks using computational models.

Medical Relevance

Although focused on plant disease, the developed methodology of using epidemic models and early-warning indicators for critical transition prediction is highly relevant to human and animal infectious disease epidemiology. It provides a generalized framework for forecasting outbreaks in various biological systems, enabling potential preemptive public health interventions.

AI Health Application

While the paper does not explicitly mention AI, its focus on developing and applying 'mathematical and computational models' and 'early-warning indicators' for predicting disease outbreaks is a direct application area for AI and machine learning in public health and biosecurity. These techniques are used to build predictive tools for disease surveillance, forecasting, and early intervention, applicable across human, animal, and plant health systems, thereby contributing to overall health and biosecurity efforts.

Key Points

  • Infectious disease outbreaks, particularly fungal diseases in plants, pose significant threats to ecosystems and agriculture, with current prediction and control being challenging.
  • A novel lattice-based epidemic model, coupled with a simplified stochastic process, was developed to simulate host-pathogen interactions and disease propagation.
  • The model revealed a critical transition between two stable phases: local pathogen confinement and an extended epiphytotic (epidemic) outbreak.
  • This critical transition is directly dependent on the density of susceptible individuals within the host population.
  • The study successfully applied early-warning indicators (EWIs), widely used for impending regime shifts in dynamical systems, to predict this critical transition.
  • These EWIs forecasted the shift from local confinement to an extended epiphytotic outbreak effectively, *before* the system entered the widespread epidemic regime.
  • The research synthesizes that early-warning indicators hold potential for predicting forest disease epidemics and can be adapted for more complex, specific pathogen-host-environmental interactions.

Methodology

The study employed a lattice-based epidemic model, integrated with a simplified stochastic process, to simulate host-pathogen interactions. Disease spread was quantified by measuring the pathogen's propagation velocity on susceptible hosts. Early-warning indicators, typically applied to dynamical systems for forecasting regime shifts, were then utilized to predict critical transitions within the simulated system.

Key Findings

The primary finding is the identification of a critical transition point in disease dynamics, shifting from local pathogen confinement to an extended epiphytotic outbreak, a transition crucially dependent on susceptible host density. Crucially, early-warning indicators were demonstrated to successfully forecast this impending critical shift *before* the widespread outbreak occurs.

Clinical Impact

While not directly clinical for human patients, the framework offers significant potential for public health and animal health. The ability to forecast critical epidemic shifts proactively allows for earlier implementation of containment strategies, resource allocation, and preventative measures (e.g., vaccination campaigns, culling, quarantine) in human and animal populations, thus mitigating the scale and impact of outbreaks.

Limitations

The abstract mentions that the stochastic process mimics interactions in a 'very simplified way,' suggesting the current model might not capture the full complexity of real-world pathogen-host dynamics. The framework is also primarily mathematical and computational, and requires adaptation to 'more specific pathogen-host-environmental interactions' for practical application.

Future Directions

Future research should focus on adapting and applying these early-warning indicators within mathematical and computational models that are more specifically tailored to complex pathogen-host-environmental interactions, thereby increasing their utility for real-world disease prediction and management.

Abstract

Summary 1. Infectious disease outbreaks in plants threaten ecosystems, agricultural crops and food trade. Currently, several fungal diseases are affecting forests worldwide, posing a major risk to tree species, habitats and consequently ecosystem decay. Prediction and control of disease spread are difficult, mainly due to the complexity of the interaction between individual components involved. 2. In this work, we introduce a lattice-based epidemic model coupled with a stochastic process that mimics, in a very simplified way, the interaction between the hosts and pathogen. We studied the disease spread by measuring the propagation velocity of the pathogen on the susceptible hosts. Quantitative results indicate the occurrence of a critical transition between two stable phases: local confinement and an extended epiphytotic outbreak that depends on the density of the susceptible individuals. 3. Quantitative predictions of epiphytotics are performed using the framework early-warning indicators for impending regime shifts, widely applied on dynamical systems. These signals forecast successfully the outcome of the critical shift between the two stable phases before the system enters the epiphytotic regime. 4. Synthesis: Our study demonstrates that early-warning indicators could be useful for the prediction of forest disease epidemics through mathematical and computational models suited to more specific pathogen-host-environmental interactions.
