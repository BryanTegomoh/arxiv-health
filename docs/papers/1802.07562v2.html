Early warning signals in plant disease outbreaks

arXiv ID: 1802.07562v2
Published: 2018-02-21
Authors: S. Orozco-Fuentes, G. Griffiths, M. J. Holmes, R. Ettelaie, J. Smith, A. W. Baggaley, N. G. Parker
Categories: q-bio.PE
Relevance Score: 0.85 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces a lattice-based epidemic model coupled with a stochastic process to study plant disease spread, revealing a critical transition from local confinement to widespread outbreaks dependent on susceptible host density. It successfully demonstrates that early-warning indicators can forecast these critical shifts, offering a predictive framework applicable to broader infectious disease epidemiology.

Medical Relevance

While focused on plant disease, this research presents a fundamental methodological advance directly transferable to human and animal infectious disease epidemiology. The framework of early-warning signals for critical transitions can provide a crucial tool for forecasting major outbreaks (pandemics/epidemics) in human populations, enabling proactive public health interventions.

AI Health Application

The paper's emphasis on mathematical and computational models for predicting disease spread and identifying early-warning indicators for critical transitions aligns directly with areas where AI and machine learning are increasingly applied in health. AI could enhance the accuracy and real-time application of such indicators for epidemic forecasting, outbreak detection, and decision support systems in public health contexts, moving from 'mathematical and computational models' to more advanced predictive AI algorithms for human disease.

Key Points

  • Infectious disease outbreaks in plants, particularly fungal diseases affecting forests, pose significant ecological and economic threats due to the complexity of host-pathogen interactions.
  • A lattice-based epidemic model, integrated with a simplified stochastic process for host-pathogen interaction, was developed to simulate and study disease propagation velocity.
  • The model identified a critical transition between two stable phases: local disease confinement and an extended epiphytotic (epidemic) outbreak, with this transition dependent on the density of susceptible individuals.
  • The study successfully applied the framework of early-warning indicators for impending regime shifts to quantitatively predict these critical transitions.
  • These early-warning signals were shown to forecast the outcome of the critical shift before the system fully enters the widespread epiphytotic regime.
  • The research demonstrates the potential utility of early-warning indicators for predicting forest disease epidemics, suggesting their broader applicability to more specific pathogen-host-environmental interaction models.
  • The methodology provides a transferable framework for understanding and predicting critical transitions in infectious disease dynamics across various host systems, including human and animal health.

Methodology

The study employed a lattice-based epidemic model combined with a stochastic process to simulate host-pathogen interactions and disease spread. Disease progression was quantified by measuring the propagation velocity of the pathogen on susceptible hosts. The primary predictive tool utilized was the framework of early-warning indicators for impending regime shifts, a quantitative approach widely applied in dynamical systems.

Key Findings

The research identified a critical transition in disease dynamics, contingent on the density of susceptible hosts, that shifts the system from localized disease confinement to widespread epiphytotic outbreaks. Crucially, early-warning indicators were demonstrated to successfully forecast the impending onset of these critical shifts *before* the system entered an epidemic state, providing a predictive lead time.

Clinical Impact

Although not directly 'clinical' in a traditional human medicine sense, this work has profound public health implications. By providing a robust method for forecasting large-scale disease outbreaks (epidemics) significantly in advance, it could enable earlier implementation of control measures, resource allocation, and policy decisions for *any* infectious disease, thereby mitigating health crises, reducing morbidity and mortality, and lessening societal burden.

Limitations

The abstract explicitly states that the stochastic process modeling host-pathogen interaction is 'very simplified.' Additionally, the authors imply that the indicators would be 'useful for the prediction of forest disease epidemics through mathematical and computational models suited to more specific pathogen-host-environmental interactions,' suggesting the current model might lack the specificity required for direct real-world application without further refinement.

Future Directions

The authors suggest that future research should focus on applying these early-warning indicators within mathematical and computational models that are specifically tailored to more intricate and realistic pathogen-host-environmental interactions, thereby enhancing their practical utility for predicting specific disease scenarios.

Abstract

Summary 1. Infectious disease outbreaks in plants threaten ecosystems, agricultural crops and food trade. Currently, several fungal diseases are affecting forests worldwide, posing a major risk to tree species, habitats and consequently ecosystem decay. Prediction and control of disease spread are difficult, mainly due to the complexity of the interaction between individual components involved. 2. In this work, we introduce a lattice-based epidemic model coupled with a stochastic process that mimics, in a very simplified way, the interaction between the hosts and pathogen. We studied the disease spread by measuring the propagation velocity of the pathogen on the susceptible hosts. Quantitative results indicate the occurrence of a critical transition between two stable phases: local confinement and an extended epiphytotic outbreak that depends on the density of the susceptible individuals. 3. Quantitative predictions of epiphytotics are performed using the framework early-warning indicators for impending regime shifts, widely applied on dynamical systems. These signals forecast successfully the outcome of the critical shift between the two stable phases before the system enters the epiphytotic regime. 4. Synthesis: Our study demonstrates that early-warning indicators could be useful for the prediction of forest disease epidemics through mathematical and computational models suited to more specific pathogen-host-environmental interactions.
