Summary

This paper leverages lessons from the COVID-19 pandemic in the U.S. and other jurisdictions to define the essential requirements for an effective infectious disease surveillance and forecasting system. It details the specific data elements necessary to inform evidence-based public health decision-making, calibrate transmission-dynamic models, and support various levels of health authorities, ultimately aiming to enhance health equity.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for fortifying national public health infrastructure, enabling more rapid and effective responses to future infectious disease outbreaks, and ensuring that public health interventions are informed by robust data to achieve equitable health outcomes.

AI Health Application

The paper implies the need for advanced computational methods, which often include AI/ML, for building effective surveillance systems, forecasting and modeling disease spread, analyzing complex surveillance data to inform public health decisions, and developing early warning systems for outbreaks. AI could be used for pattern recognition in diverse data types, predictive analytics for resource allocation, and optimizing public health interventions during pandemics.

Key Points

  • The COVID-19 pandemic exposed significant deficiencies in U.S. infectious disease surveillance, forecasting, and modeling capabilities.
  • The paper delineates specific requirements for an effective surveillance system designed to support critical decision-making during future pandemics.
  • It identifies the precise data elements required to inform public health guidance and policies, as well as to accurately calibrate inputs and outputs of transmission-dynamic models.
  • Data needs are explicitly defined to cater to the diverse decision-making processes of state, territorial, local, and tribal health authorities.
  • The analysis draws insights from both U.S. and international jurisdictions regarding the utility and value of specific types of surveillance data.
  • Actionable steps are outlined to ensure the consistent availability of the necessary surveillance data for a robust public health response.
  • A key consideration of the proposed surveillance enhancements is their potential contribution to improving health equity across populations.

Methodology

The paper employs a qualitative, analytical approach, synthesizing lessons learned from the COVID-19 pandemic experience in the U.S. and globally. It involves defining system requirements, identifying specific data elements, and conceptualizing actionable steps for surveillance system improvement, rather than conducting empirical research or data collection.

Key Findings

The primary findings are the detailed articulation of surveillance needs and specific data requirements essential for an effective pandemic response system. These include defining the range of decisions requiring surveillance data, identifying necessary data elements for informing decisions and calibrating transmission models, and specifying data types relevant to different levels of health authorities, all informed by critical lessons from COVID-19.

Improved and standardized surveillance data will lead to more timely and accurate clinical guidance, better resource allocation (e.g., hospital capacity, therapeutics, vaccines), and more effective public health interventions. This will ultimately result in better patient management, reduced disease transmission, and decreased morbidity and mortality during infectious disease outbreaks.

Abstract

The COVID-19 pandemic has highlighted the need to upgrade systems for infectious disease surveillance and forecasting and modeling of the spread of infection, both of which inform evidence-based public health guidance and policies. Here, we discuss requirements for an effective surveillance system to support decision making during a pandemic, drawing on the lessons of COVID-19 in the U.S., while looking to jurisdictions in the U.S. and beyond to learn lessons about the value of specific data types. In this report, we define the range of decisions for which surveillance data are required, the data elements needed to inform these decisions and to calibrate inputs and outputs of transmission-dynamic models, and the types of data needed to inform decisions by state, territorial, local, and tribal health authorities. We define actions needed to ensure that such data will be available and consider the contribution of such efforts to improving health equity.
