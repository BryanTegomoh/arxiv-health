Summary

This paper outlines the critical needs for upgrading infectious disease surveillance, forecasting, and modeling systems in the United States, leveraging lessons learned from the COVID-19 pandemic. It defines the necessary data elements and system requirements to support evidence-based public health decision-making across various levels of authority, while also addressing health equity.

Medical Relevance

This analysis is crucial for modernizing public health infrastructure, enabling more effective and equitable responses to future infectious disease outbreaks and pandemics, thereby directly impacting patient care coordination, resource allocation, and population health outcomes.

AI Health Application

The paper highlights the need for 'forecasting and modeling of the spread of infection' and 'calibrating inputs and outputs of transmission-dynamic models.' These tasks are prime applications for AI and machine learning, which can be used to analyze surveillance data, predict disease outbreaks, optimize resource allocation, develop early warning systems, and inform public health interventions. While not explicitly stating 'AI,' the emphasis on data-driven modeling and decision support in infectious disease surveillance strongly implicates medical AI applications in epidemiology and public health.

Key Points

  • The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted an urgent need to significantly upgrade U.S. infectious disease surveillance and modeling capabilities.
  • The primary objective of enhanced surveillance is to provide robust data for evidence-based public health guidance and policy decisions during pandemics.
  • The analysis draws specific lessons from the U.S. experience with COVID-19 and evaluates valuable data types from other jurisdictions.
  • The paper defines the spectrum of public health decisions requiring surveillance data, along with the precise data elements needed for both decision-making and the calibration of transmission-dynamic models.
  • It identifies distinct data requirements specific to state, territorial, local, and tribal health authorities.
  • Concrete actions are proposed to ensure the consistent availability of required surveillance data.
  • The proposed surveillance system improvements are considered in terms of their potential contribution to improving health equity.

Methodology

The paper employs a conceptual and analytical framework, synthesizing experiences and lessons from the COVID-19 pandemic within the U.S. and from international jurisdictions. It involves defining system requirements, outlining data elements, and identifying necessary actions through a discussion-based approach.

Key Findings

The paper identifies that effective pandemic response necessitates significantly upgraded surveillance systems capable of informing diverse public health decisions. It concludes that specific data elements are crucial for both operational decision support and the calibration of transmission models, and that tailored data strategies are required for different levels of health authority. Furthermore, it highlights that proactive steps are needed to ensure data availability and improve health equity through these surveillance efforts.

Clinical Impact

Implementing the proposed surveillance upgrades would directly improve clinical decision-making by providing more accurate insights into disease prevalence and trends, informing resource allocation (e.g., hospital capacity, vaccine distribution), guiding the development of evidence-based clinical guidelines, and enabling earlier, targeted interventions to protect healthcare workers and the public.

Future Directions

Future work involves implementing the defined actions to ensure data availability, developing robust mechanisms for data integration and sharing across various health authorities, and actively assessing how these improved surveillance efforts contribute to better health equity outcomes and more effective pandemic responses.

Abstract

The COVID-19 pandemic has highlighted the need to upgrade systems for infectious disease surveillance and forecasting and modeling of the spread of infection, both of which inform evidence-based public health guidance and policies. Here, we discuss requirements for an effective surveillance system to support decision making during a pandemic, drawing on the lessons of COVID-19 in the U.S., while looking to jurisdictions in the U.S. and beyond to learn lessons about the value of specific data types. In this report, we define the range of decisions for which surveillance data are required, the data elements needed to inform these decisions and to calibrate inputs and outputs of transmission-dynamic models, and the types of data needed to inform decisions by state, territorial, local, and tribal health authorities. We define actions needed to ensure that such data will be available and consider the contribution of such efforts to improving health equity.
