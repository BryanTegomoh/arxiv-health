Summary

This review paper analyzes the use of edible polysaccharides as versatile stabilizers and carriers for microencapsulating phenolic compounds and pigments in food and nutraceutical formulations. It details how various polysaccharides and encapsulation techniques improve the stability, bioaccessibility, and bioavailability of these bioactive substances by forming protective matrices through specific physical interactions.

Medical Relevance

This research is crucial for advancing the delivery of health-benefiting phenolic compounds and pigments, potentially enhancing their efficacy in functional foods and nutraceuticals, which can contribute to improved disease prevention, management, and overall human health by ensuring better absorption and utilization in the body.

Key Points

  • Edible polysaccharides, including starch, maltodextrin, alginate, pectin, inulin, chitosan, and gum arabic, are effective wall materials for microencapsulation.
  • They stabilize bioactive compounds (polyphenols, pigments) during storage and passage through the gastrointestinal tract, thereby improving their bioaccessibility and bioavailability.
  • Common encapsulation techniques discussed include spray drying, freeze drying, extrusion, emulsification, and coacervation for microparticle production.
  • Polysaccharides entrap compounds via physical interactions (e.g., hydrogen bonds, electrostatic interactions), forming a protective barrier or binding directly to the bioactive core.
  • Encapsulation efficiency can reach up to 90%, largely governed by the strength of intermolecular binding within the wall matrix and with the encapsulated compounds.
  • Combining different wall polysaccharides enhances the microencapsulate's solubility, storage stability, bioaccessibility, and overall bioactivity.
  • A significant limitation identified is the scarcity of clinical trials demonstrating the bioefficacy of polyphenols and pigments loaded in polysaccharide microparticles, warranting further research.

Methodology

This is a comprehensive review paper synthesizing existing literature. It discusses various types of edible polysaccharides used as wall materials, describes major microencapsulation techniques (spray drying, freeze drying, extrusion, emulsification, coacervation), and analyzes the structural interactions and physicochemical properties that benefit the encapsulation of polyphenols and pigments.

Clinical Impact

The technology offers significant potential for creating enhanced functional foods and nutraceuticals that more effectively deliver beneficial compounds to the human body. This could lead to improved nutritional interventions, better management of chronic diseases through dietary means, and more effective prophylactic strategies, once clinical efficacy is robustly established.

Limitations

The primary limitation highlighted is the scarcity of clinical trials evaluating the bioefficacy of polyphenols and pigments loaded in polysaccharide microparticles in human subjects.

Further human clinical trials are explicitly required to provide robust evidence reinforcing the use and demonstrating the bioefficacy of this polysaccharide-based microencapsulation technology for delivering phenolic compounds and pigments in food and nutraceutical applications.

Abstract

Food polysaccharides have emerged as suitable carriers of active substances and as additives to food and nutraceutical formulations, showing potential to stabilize bioactive compounds during the storage of microencapsulate preparations, even in the gastrointestinal tract following the intake of bioactive compounds, thereby improving their bioaccessibility and bioavailability. This review provides a comprehensive overview of the main polysaccharides employed as wall materials, including starch, maltodextrin, alginate, pectin, inulin, chitosan, and gum arabic, and discusses how structural interactions and physicochemical properties can benefit the microencapsulation of polyphenols and pigments. The main findings and principles of the major encapsulation techniques, including spray drying, freeze drying, extrusion, emulsification, and coacervation, related to the production of microparticles, were briefly described. Polysaccharides can entrap hydrophilic and hydrophobic compounds by physical interactions, forming a barrier around the nucleus or binding to the bioactive compound. Intermolecular binding between polysaccharides in the wall matrix, polyphenols, and pigments in the nucleus can confer up to 90% of encapsulation efficiency, governed mainly by hydrogen bonds and electrostatic interactions. The mixture of wall polysaccharides in the microparticles synthesis favors the encapsulation solubility, storage stability, bioaccessibility, and bioactivity of the microencapsulate compounds. Clinical trials on the bioefficacy of polyphenols and pigments loaded in polysaccharide microparticles are scarce and require further evidence to reinforce the use of this technology.
