Delta-XAI: A Unified Framework for Explaining Prediction Changes in Online Time Series Monitoring

arXiv ID: 2511.23036v1
Published: 2025-11-28
Authors: Changhun Kim, Yechan Mun, Hyeongwon Jang, Eunseo Lee, Sangchul Hahn, Eunho Yang
Categories: cs.LG, cs.AI
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper introduces Delta-XAI, a unified framework that adapts 14 existing XAI methods for explaining prediction changes in online time series monitoring, addressing the oversight of temporal dependencies in prior work. It also proposes a principled evaluation suite and a novel method, Shifted Window Integrated Gradients (SWING). Key findings include that adapted classical gradient-based methods like Integrated Gradients can outperform recent XAI approaches, and SWING consistently demonstrates superior effectiveness by systematically capturing temporal dependencies.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to medicine as it directly addresses the critical need for interpretable AI models in healthcare for online time series monitoring, such as patient vital signs or continuous glucose monitoring, where understanding *why* a prediction changes is crucial for timely and accurate clinical decision-making, patient safety, and building trust in AI systems.

AI Health Application

This research directly contributes to enhancing the explainability (XAI) of AI models used for online time series monitoring in healthcare. For instance, in an Intensive Care Unit (ICU) or for remote patient monitoring, an AI system might continuously monitor vital signs (time series data) and predict a patient's deteriorating condition. Delta-XAI and SWING would allow clinicians to understand *why* the AI's prediction changed, identifying specific temporal patterns or features that led to the change, thus providing critical insights for medical intervention. This improves trust, accountability, and the interpretability of AI-driven clinical decision support systems.

Key Points

  • **Problem Addressed**: Existing XAI methods for time series often analyze time steps independently, overlooking temporal dependencies, making it difficult to explain prediction changes and leverage online dynamics in critical domains.
  • **Delta-XAI Framework**: Introduces Delta-XAI, a wrapper function that adapts 14 diverse existing XAI methods to effectively analyze prediction changes in online time series monitoring.
  • **Principled Evaluation Suite**: Proposes a novel and principled evaluation suite specifically for online XAI, assessing critical aspects such as faithfulness, sufficiency, and coherence.
  • **Shifted Window Integrated Gradients (SWING)**: Introduces a novel gradient-based explanation method, SWING, which systematically incorporates past observations into the integration path to capture temporal dependencies and mitigate out-of-distribution effects.
  • **Performance of Adapted Classical Methods**: Experiments reveal that classical gradient-based methods, particularly Integrated Gradients (IG), can surprisingly outperform more recent XAI approaches when appropriately adapted for temporal analysis.
  • **Effectiveness of SWING**: Extensive experiments consistently demonstrate SWING's superior effectiveness across diverse settings and metrics, validating its ability to capture complex temporal dynamics.
  • **Healthcare Relevance**: Emphasizes the crucial role of explainable online time series monitoring in sensitive domains like healthcare, where temporal and contextual prediction dynamics underpin critical clinical decisions.

Methodology

Delta-XAI is proposed as a unified framework that serves as a wrapper to adapt 14 diverse existing XAI methods for explaining prediction changes in online time series. It introduces a principled evaluation suite to assess explainability across metrics like faithfulness, sufficiency, and coherence in the online setting. Building on experimental insights that highlight the strength of gradient-based methods when adapted, the paper then proposes Shifted Window Integrated Gradients (SWING), a novel method that extends Integrated Gradients by systematically incorporating past observations into its integration path to capture temporal dependencies and mitigate out-of-distribution effects.

Key Findings

The experiments revealed two main findings: firstly, classical gradient-based methods, particularly Integrated Gradients (IG), can surprisingly outperform more recent XAI approaches when appropriately adapted for temporal analysis in online settings. Secondly, the proposed Shifted Window Integrated Gradients (SWING) consistently demonstrated superior effectiveness across various experiments and metrics, successfully capturing temporal dependencies and mitigating out-of-distribution effects for explaining prediction changes.

Clinical Impact

By providing more faithful, sufficient, and coherent explanations for prediction changes in online time series data, this research can significantly enhance clinician trust and adoption of AI-powered monitoring systems. This leads to earlier and more informed clinical interventions, improved patient safety, reduced diagnostic errors in dynamic scenarios, and more efficient resource allocation in critical care, ultimately improving the quality and safety of healthcare delivery.

Abstract

Explaining online time series monitoring models is crucial across sensitive domains such as healthcare and finance, where temporal and contextual prediction dynamics underpin critical decisions. While recent XAI methods have improved the explainability of time series models, they mostly analyze each time step independently, overlooking temporal dependencies. This results in further challenges: explaining prediction changes is non-trivial, methods fail to leverage online dynamics, and evaluation remains difficult. To address these challenges, we propose Delta-XAI, which adapts 14 existing XAI methods through a wrapper function and introduces a principled evaluation suite for the online setting, assessing diverse aspects, such as faithfulness, sufficiency, and coherence. Experiments reveal that classical gradient-based methods, such as Integrated Gradients (IG), can outperform recent approaches when adapted for temporal analysis. Building on this, we propose Shifted Window Integrated Gradients (SWING), which incorporates past observations in the integration path to systematically capture temporal dependencies and mitigate out-of-distribution effects. Extensive experiments consistently demonstrate the effectiveness of SWING across diverse settings with respect to diverse metrics. Our code is publicly available at https://anonymous.4open.science/r/Delta-XAI.
