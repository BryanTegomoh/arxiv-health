Machine-Learning Based Detection of Coronary Artery Calcification Using Synthetic Chest X-Rays

arXiv ID: 2511.11093v1
Published: 2025-11-14
Authors: Dylan Saeed, Ramtin Gharleghi, Susann Bier, Sonit Singh
Categories: cs.CV
Relevance Score: 0.95 / 1.00
View on arXiv Download PDF

Summary

This paper pioneers the systematic evaluation of Digitally Reconstructed Radiographs (DRRs) as a synthetic, label-rich training domain for machine learning models to detect Coronary Artery Calcification (CAC) from chest X-ray-like images. The study demonstrates that lightweight Convolutional Neural Networks, when optimized with super-resolution, contrast enhancement, and curriculum learning, achieve a mean AUC of 0.754, establishing DRRs as a scalable foundation for CAC detection competitive with or exceeding prior real CXR-based methods.

Medical Relevance

Early and cost-effective detection of Coronary Artery Calcification is crucial for identifying individuals at high risk for cardiovascular events, enabling timely preventive interventions. This research proposes a method to leverage readily available chest X-rays for this purpose, potentially democratizing cardiovascular risk assessment and reducing reliance on more expensive and less accessible CT scans for screening.

AI Health Application

The paper describes the application of machine learning (specifically CNNs) for automated detection of Coronary Artery Calcification (CAC) from chest X-rays. It innovatively uses synthetic chest X-rays (Digitally Reconstructed Radiographs - DRRs) derived from CT scans to create a label-rich training environment, addressing a major challenge in medical AI development (lack of ground truth labels for real CXRs). The ultimate goal is to facilitate scalable, inexpensive screening for CAC, thereby improving early detection of cardiovascular disease risk.

Key Points

  • Coronary Artery Calcification (CAC) is a strong predictor of cardiovascular events, but CT-based detection is costly for large-scale screening, while real CXRs lack reliable ground truth labels for deep learning.
  • Digitally Reconstructed Radiographs (DRRs), generated from CT volumes, are introduced as a scalable surrogate training domain that inherits precise CAC labels for deep learning model development.
  • The study systematically evaluated DRRs generated from 667 CT scans (COCA dataset) to assess model capacity, super-resolution fidelity, preprocessing, and training strategies for CAC detection.
  • Lightweight CNNs trained from scratch on DRRs outperformed larger, pre-trained networks for the task.
  • Significant performance gains were observed when super-resolution techniques were paired with contrast enhancement during preprocessing.
  • Curriculum learning was identified as a crucial strategy to stabilize model training, particularly under conditions of weak supervision.
  • The best model configuration achieved a mean Area Under the Curve (AUC) of 0.754, demonstrating performance comparable to or exceeding prior studies utilizing real CXRs for CAC detection.
  • The research establishes DRRs as a viable and scalable foundation for CAC detection, paving the way for future transfer learning and domain adaptation to real chest X-rays.

Methodology

The study utilized 667 CT scans from the COCA dataset to generate synthetic Digitally Reconstructed Radiographs (DRRs), which are CXR-like images inheriting precise CAC labels from the original CT volumes. A systematic evaluation was conducted on these DRRs, involving the assessment of various lightweight Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) architectures, the impact of super-resolution techniques, different image preprocessing strategies (e.g., contrast enhancement), and advanced training methodologies such as curriculum learning, all aimed at optimizing CAC detection.

Key Findings

Lightweight CNNs, trained from scratch on synthetic DRRs, demonstrated superior performance compared to larger, pre-trained networks. A key finding was that combining super-resolution with contrast enhancement significantly boosted detection accuracy. Furthermore, curriculum learning proved effective in stabilizing training under weak supervision. The optimal model configuration achieved a mean AUC of 0.754, indicating its competitive performance against existing CXR-based CAC detection methods.

Clinical Impact

This work has the potential to significantly impact clinical practice by enabling the development of an inexpensive and scalable screening tool for Coronary Artery Calcification using routinely acquired chest X-rays. Such a tool could facilitate earlier identification of individuals at risk for cardiovascular events, allowing for timely lifestyle modifications or medical interventions, thereby improving patient outcomes and potentially alleviating the burden on healthcare systems from advanced cardiovascular disease.

Limitations

The primary limitation is that the models are trained and evaluated on synthetic Digitally Reconstructed Radiographs (DRRs), not real chest X-rays. While DRRs mimic CXRs, a domain gap exists that needs to be bridged for real-world application. The use of weak supervision in training also presents challenges that required specific strategies like curriculum learning.

Future Directions

The research explicitly lays the foundation for future work focused on transfer learning and domain adaptation techniques to bridge the gap between synthetic DRRs and real chest X-rays, which is essential for the clinical deployment of these models.

Abstract

Coronary artery calcification (CAC) is a strong predictor of cardiovascular events, with CT-based Agatston scoring widely regarded as the clinical gold standard. However, CT is costly and impractical for large-scale screening, while chest X-rays (CXRs) are inexpensive but lack reliable ground truth labels, constraining deep learning development. Digitally reconstructed radiographs (DRRs) offer a scalable alternative by projecting CT volumes into CXR-like images while inheriting precise labels. In this work, we provide the first systematic evaluation of DRRs as a surrogate training domain for CAC detection. Using 667 CT scans from the COCA dataset, we generate synthetic DRRs and assess model capacity, super-resolution fidelity enhancement, preprocessing, and training strategies. Lightweight CNNs trained from scratch outperform large pretrained networks; pairing super-resolution with contrast enhancement yields significant gains; and curriculum learning stabilises training under weak supervision. Our best configuration achieves a mean AUC of 0.754, comparable to or exceeding prior CXR-based studies. These results establish DRRs as a scalable, label-rich foundation for CAC detection, while laying the foundation for future transfer learning and domain adaptation to real CXRs.
