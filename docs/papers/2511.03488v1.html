NAP: Attention-Based Late Fusion for Automatic Sleep Staging

Summary

This paper introduces NAP (Neural Aggregator of Predictions), an attention-based deep learning model designed for automatic sleep staging from heterogeneous polysomnography (PSG) data. NAP employs a novel tri-axial attention mechanism to intelligently combine prediction streams from frozen, pretrained single-channel models, achieving state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization across diverse datasets. This approach addresses the variability in PSG signal composition, channel availability, and acquisition protocols, enhancing the robustness and adaptability of automated sleep staging.

Medical Relevance

This research is highly relevant to sleep medicine as it provides a robust, adaptable, and accurate method for automated sleep staging, which is critical for diagnosing and managing sleep disorders. By overcoming the challenges of data heterogeneity, NAP can improve diagnostic consistency and efficiency in diverse clinical settings.

AI Health Application

The paper presents NAP, an attention-based AI model designed to automate and improve the accuracy of sleep staging from polysomnography data. This application assists in the diagnosis and management of sleep disorders by providing an automated, state-of-the-art method for analyzing complex physiological signals.

Key Points

  • Addresses the significant heterogeneity in polysomnography signals across different modalities, channels, and acquisition protocols, which limits the effectiveness of existing fixed-subset models.
  • Introduces NAP (Neural Aggregator of Predictions), an attention-based model for late fusion of predictions from multiple sources.
  • Utilizes a tri-axial attention mechanism to capture and integrate temporal, spatial, and predictor-level dependencies from input streams.
  • NAP aggregates outputs from *frozen, pretrained single-channel models*, allowing it to adapt to varying input dimensions and leverage existing specialized predictors.
  • Consistently outperforms individual single-channel predictors and simpler ensemble methods in automatic sleep staging.
  • Achieves state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization across multiple unseen datasets, demonstrating high robustness and adaptability to real-world clinical variability.
  • The proposed approach, while demonstrated for sleep staging, is presented as extensible to other multimodal physiological applications.

Methodology

The methodology centers on NAP, a neural aggregator model that operates on a late fusion principle. It takes prediction outputs from multiple *frozen, pretrained single-channel models* as its input. A key component is a tri-axial attention mechanism, which learns to weigh and combine these predictions by considering dependencies across time, spatial channels, and the individual predictor sources, making the system adaptable to different input dimensions.

Key Findings

The main findings are that NAP consistently surpasses the performance of individual single-channel predictors and basic ensemble methods in automated sleep staging. Crucially, it achieves state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization across various datasets, indicating its superior ability to perform accurately on new, unseen data without requiring specific retraining for each dataset.

Clinical Impact

The clinical impact is significant, as NAP can substantially improve the accuracy, consistency, and efficiency of sleep disorder diagnosis by providing more reliable automated sleep staging. Its zero-shot generalization capability is particularly valuable, enabling seamless deployment across different clinical sites with varying PSG equipment and protocols, thereby reducing human scoring burden and standardizing diagnoses.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations of the NAP model itself. However, as an approach relying on aggregating outputs from pretrained single-channel models (late fusion), it inherently depends on the quality and diversity of these individual predictors. The explicit scope of the paper, as presented in the abstract, focuses on its strengths and generalizability.

Future Directions

The authors suggest that the proposed attention-based late fusion approach, demonstrated effectively in automated sleep staging from polysomnography, could be extended and applied to other multimodal physiological applications beyond sleep analysis.

Abstract

Polysomnography signals are highly heterogeneous, varying in modality composition (e.g., EEG, EOG, ECG), channel availability (e.g., frontal, occipital EEG), and acquisition protocols across datasets and clinical sites. Most existing models that process polysomnography data rely on a fixed subset of modalities or channels and therefore neglect to fully exploit its inherently multimodal nature. We address this limitation by introducing NAP (Neural Aggregator of Predictions), an attention-based model which learns to combine multiple prediction streams using a tri-axial attention mechanism that captures temporal, spatial, and predictor-level dependencies. NAP is trained to adapt to different input dimensions. By aggregating outputs from frozen, pretrained single-channel models, NAP consistently outperforms individual predictors and simple ensembles, achieving state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization across multiple datasets. While demonstrated in the context of automated sleep staging from polysomnography, the proposed approach could be extended to other multimodal physiological applications.
