NAP: Attention-Based Late Fusion for Automatic Sleep Staging

Published: 2025-11-05
Authors: Alvise Dei Rossi, Julia van der Meer, Markus H. Schmidt, Claudio L. A. Bassetti, Luigi Fiorillo, Francesca Faraci

Summary

This paper introduces NAP (Neural Aggregator of Predictions), an attention-based deep learning model designed for robust automatic sleep staging from polysomnography (PSG) signals. NAP addresses the inherent heterogeneity of PSG data by learning to combine prediction streams from frozen, pretrained single-channel models, achieving state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization across diverse datasets.

Medical Relevance

This research significantly advances the automation of sleep staging, a cornerstone for diagnosing sleep disorders. By providing a highly adaptable and accurate model, it can improve diagnostic consistency, reduce the labor-intensive burden of manual scoring, and facilitate more widespread and efficient screening for sleep-related health issues.

AI Health Application

The AI application is the automated analysis and staging of sleep from polysomnography data. This can assist clinicians in diagnosing sleep disorders (e.g., sleep apnea, insomnia, narcolepsy) more efficiently and accurately, reduce manual workload, and provide more consistent diagnostic results across different clinical sites and patient populations.

Key Points

  • Addresses the challenge of highly heterogeneous polysomnography signals, which vary in modality, channel availability, and acquisition protocols, limiting the utility of fixed-subset models.
  • Introduces NAP, an attention-based model, which employs a tri-axial attention mechanism to capture temporal, spatial, and predictor-level dependencies for combining multiple prediction streams.
  • NAP operates as a late fusion model, aggregating outputs from frozen, pretrained single-channel models, allowing it to adapt to different input dimensions and channel availability.
  • Demonstrated its effectiveness in automated sleep staging, a critical task for diagnosing sleep disorders.
  • Consistently outperforms individual, single-channel predictors and simple ensemble methods, showcasing its superior aggregation capabilities.
  • Achieves state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization across multiple, distinct polysomnography datasets, indicating high adaptability and robustness to unseen data characteristics.
  • The proposed approach is broadly applicable and could be extended to other multimodal physiological applications beyond sleep staging.

Methodology

The authors developed NAP, a neural network model utilizing an attention-based late fusion approach. It aggregates prediction streams, derived from frozen, pretrained single-channel models (e.g., EEG, EOG, ECG), using a tri-axial attention mechanism. This mechanism captures dependencies across time, spatial channels, and individual predictors, allowing the model to dynamically weigh and combine information while adapting to varying input dimensions and channel availability.

Key Findings

NAP consistently demonstrated superior performance compared to individual single-channel predictors and traditional simple ensemble methods for automatic sleep staging. Crucially, it achieved state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization across multiple, diverse polysomnography datasets, indicating its robustness and ability to perform well on data it was not explicitly trained on, irrespective of variations in acquisition protocols or channel availability.

Clinical Impact

The development of NAP offers a robust solution for automated sleep staging that can integrate heterogeneous PSG data from various clinical sites. This could lead to more standardized, accurate, and efficient diagnosis of sleep disorders, reducing human error and resource allocation in sleep labs. Its zero-shot generalization capability implies that a single model could be deployed across different hospitals without extensive retraining, accelerating clinical adoption and improving accessibility to advanced diagnostics.

Limitations

While the abstract highlights strengths, it primarily focuses on the demonstration of NAP within the context of automated sleep staging. Explicit limitations regarding model interpretability, computational demands, or performance under extreme data degradation are not detailed in the abstract.

Future Directions

The authors suggest extending the proposed attention-based late fusion approach to other multimodal physiological applications beyond sleep staging, indicating its potential utility in various clinical monitoring and diagnostic tasks involving complex, heterogeneous biosignals.

Abstract

Polysomnography signals are highly heterogeneous, varying in modality composition (e.g., EEG, EOG, ECG), channel availability (e.g., frontal, occipital EEG), and acquisition protocols across datasets and clinical sites. Most existing models that process polysomnography data rely on a fixed subset of modalities or channels and therefore neglect to fully exploit its inherently multimodal nature. We address this limitation by introducing NAP (Neural Aggregator of Predictions), an attention-based model which learns to combine multiple prediction streams using a tri-axial attention mechanism that captures temporal, spatial, and predictor-level dependencies. NAP is trained to adapt to different input dimensions. By aggregating outputs from frozen, pretrained single-channel models, NAP consistently outperforms individual predictors and simple ensembles, achieving state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization across multiple datasets. While demonstrated in the context of automated sleep staging from polysomnography, the proposed approach could be extended to other multimodal physiological applications.
