NAP: Attention-Based Late Fusion for Automatic Sleep Staging

Published: 2025-11-05
Authors: Alvise Dei Rossi, Julia van der Meer, Markus H. Schmidt, Claudio L. A. Bassetti, Luigi Fiorillo, Francesca Faraci

Summary

NAP (Neural Aggregator of Predictions) introduces an attention-based late fusion model designed to address the heterogeneity of polysomnography (PSG) signals for automatic sleep staging. By combining predictions from frozen, pretrained single-channel models using a tri-axial attention mechanism, NAP consistently outperforms individual predictors and simple ensembles, achieving state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization across multiple diverse datasets.

Medical Relevance

Accurate and automated sleep staging is fundamental for the diagnosis and management of sleep disorders. This model provides a robust, adaptable solution that can handle diverse clinical data, reducing the burden of manual scoring and improving diagnostic consistency across different healthcare settings.

AI Health Application

The AI model (NAP) is designed to automate and improve the accuracy of sleep staging from polysomnography data. This enables more efficient and potentially more accessible diagnosis of sleep disorders, which are critical for patient health management.

Key Points

  • Polysomnography (PSG) signals are highly heterogeneous in modality, channel availability, and acquisition protocols, posing significant challenges for existing fixed-subset models.
  • NAP is an attention-based late fusion model that learns to adaptively combine multiple prediction streams.
  • It incorporates a novel tri-axial attention mechanism that captures temporal, spatial (channel/modality), and predictor-level dependencies.
  • NAP aggregates outputs from frozen, pretrained single-channel models, allowing it to adapt to varying input dimensions and exploit existing specialized predictors.
  • The model demonstrates consistent performance improvement, outperforming both individual single-channel predictors and simpler ensemble aggregation methods.
  • It achieves state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization, performing effectively on unseen datasets without specific retraining, indicating robust adaptability to data variability.
  • While primarily demonstrated for automated sleep staging, the underlying attention-based fusion approach is proposed to be extensible to other multimodal physiological applications.

Methodology

NAP utilizes an attention-based late fusion architecture. It takes predictions generated by multiple *frozen, pretrained single-channel deep learning models* as its input streams. A tri-axial attention mechanism then processes these streams, learning to weigh and combine them based on temporal dynamics, spatial relationships (between channels/modalities), and the specific characteristics of each predictor. This adaptive aggregation results in a unified, robust sleep stage prediction.

Key Findings

NAP consistently achieves superior performance compared to both individual single-channel predictors and traditional ensemble methods. Crucially, it exhibits state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization capabilities, demonstrating robust performance on diverse, previously unseen datasets without requiring task-specific fine-tuning, effectively addressing the challenges of data heterogeneity in clinical PSG recordings.

Clinical Impact

This model offers a significant advancement for automated sleep staging, enabling more consistent, scalable, and less labor-intensive diagnosis of sleep disorders. Its adaptability to diverse data acquisition protocols can facilitate broader adoption in various clinical sites, standardize sleep scoring, and free up clinicians' time, ultimately leading to improved patient care.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state limitations. However, it implicitly suggests that the current demonstration is focused solely on sleep staging, though the method has broader applicability. The reliance on pre-trained single-channel models means its performance is partly contingent on the quality and diversity of these foundational predictors.

Future Directions

The proposed attention-based late fusion approach is highly adaptable and could be extended beyond automated sleep staging to a wide array of other applications involving multimodal physiological signal analysis in medicine.

Abstract

Polysomnography signals are highly heterogeneous, varying in modality composition (e.g., EEG, EOG, ECG), channel availability (e.g., frontal, occipital EEG), and acquisition protocols across datasets and clinical sites. Most existing models that process polysomnography data rely on a fixed subset of modalities or channels and therefore neglect to fully exploit its inherently multimodal nature. We address this limitation by introducing NAP (Neural Aggregator of Predictions), an attention-based model which learns to combine multiple prediction streams using a tri-axial attention mechanism that captures temporal, spatial, and predictor-level dependencies. NAP is trained to adapt to different input dimensions. By aggregating outputs from frozen, pretrained single-channel models, NAP consistently outperforms individual predictors and simple ensembles, achieving state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization across multiple datasets. While demonstrated in the context of automated sleep staging from polysomnography, the proposed approach could be extended to other multimodal physiological applications.
