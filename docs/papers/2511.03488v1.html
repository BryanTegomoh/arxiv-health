NAP: Attention-Based Late Fusion for Automatic Sleep Staging

Summary

This paper introduces NAP (Neural Aggregator of Predictions), an attention-based late fusion model designed to overcome the heterogeneity of polysomnography (PSG) signals for automated sleep staging. NAP adaptively combines predictions from multiple frozen, pretrained single-channel models using a novel tri-axial attention mechanism, achieving state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization across diverse datasets and outperforming individual predictors and simple ensembles.

Medical Relevance

This research significantly enhances the reliability and adaptability of automated sleep staging, which is crucial for accurate diagnosis and management of sleep disorders. By overcoming data heterogeneity, NAP facilitates more consistent and widespread application of AI in sleep medicine, potentially improving patient care outcomes.

AI Health Application

The paper develops NAP (Neural Aggregator of Predictions), an attention-based AI model, to automate the process of sleep staging from polysomnography data. This AI application aims to provide more accurate and generalizable automated sleep stage classifications, which are critical for the diagnosis and management of sleep disorders, patient monitoring, and clinical research in sleep medicine.

Key Points

  • Addresses the significant challenge of signal heterogeneity (modality, channels, acquisition protocols) in polysomnography data for sleep staging.
  • Proposes NAP, an attention-based late fusion model, as a solution to robustly combine multimodal physiological signals.
  • Employs a novel tri-axial attention mechanism to capture temporal, spatial, and predictor-level dependencies, enabling adaptive weighting of different prediction streams.
  • Aggregates outputs from frozen, pretrained single-channel models, allowing for adaptability to varying input dimensions and channel availability.
  • Consistently outperforms individual predictors and simple ensemble methods, demonstrating superior performance in automated sleep staging.
  • Achieves state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization across multiple, diverse polysomnography datasets, highlighting its robustness and clinical applicability.
  • The proposed methodology is versatile and could be extended beyond sleep staging to other multimodal physiological signal analysis applications.

Methodology

The authors developed NAP, a Neural Aggregator of Predictions, which is an attention-based late fusion model. It operates by taking prediction streams from 'frozen, pretrained single-channel models' as input. The core of NAP is a 'tri-axial attention mechanism' that learns to weigh these streams based on temporal, spatial, and predictor-level dependencies. The model is designed to adapt to different input dimensions and was trained and evaluated on its ability to generalize across diverse polysomnography datasets without specific retraining, a concept known as zero-shot generalization.

Key Findings

NAP consistently demonstrated superior performance compared to individual single-channel predictors and simple ensemble methods for automatic sleep staging. A crucial finding is its achievement of state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization across multiple heterogeneous polysomnography datasets, indicating its robust adaptability to variations in data composition and acquisition protocols.

Clinical Impact

NAP has the potential to significantly streamline and standardize the diagnosis of sleep disorders by providing a more robust and adaptable automated sleep staging solution. Its ability to generalize across diverse clinical datasets means it could be deployed in various healthcare settings regardless of specific equipment or acquisition protocols, thereby reducing human scoring variability, improving diagnostic efficiency, and potentially increasing access to accurate sleep disorder diagnostics.

Limitations

The abstract does not explicitly state specific limitations of the NAP model itself.

Future Directions

The authors suggest that the proposed attention-based late fusion approach, while demonstrated for automated sleep staging from polysomnography, could be extended to other multimodal physiological applications.

Abstract

Polysomnography signals are highly heterogeneous, varying in modality composition (e.g., EEG, EOG, ECG), channel availability (e.g., frontal, occipital EEG), and acquisition protocols across datasets and clinical sites. Most existing models that process polysomnography data rely on a fixed subset of modalities or channels and therefore neglect to fully exploit its inherently multimodal nature. We address this limitation by introducing NAP (Neural Aggregator of Predictions), an attention-based model which learns to combine multiple prediction streams using a tri-axial attention mechanism that captures temporal, spatial, and predictor-level dependencies. NAP is trained to adapt to different input dimensions. By aggregating outputs from frozen, pretrained single-channel models, NAP consistently outperforms individual predictors and simple ensembles, achieving state-of-the-art zero-shot generalization across multiple datasets. While demonstrated in the context of automated sleep staging from polysomnography, the proposed approach could be extended to other multimodal physiological applications.
